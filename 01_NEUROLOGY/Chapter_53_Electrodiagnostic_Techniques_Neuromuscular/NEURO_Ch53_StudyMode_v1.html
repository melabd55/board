CHAPTER 53 ■ Electrodiagnostic Techniques in Neuromuscular Disease — Key Points

Clinical anchor
  • EMG and nerve-conduction studies (NCS) are now feasible at any PICU bedside thanks to portable equipment.
  • They direct evaluation, give a diagnosis, and identify ICU-acquired peripheral nerve injuries that change prognosis.
  • The "PICU electrodiagnosis triad": pre-existing neuromuscular disease, ICU-acquired weakness, and compressive mononeuropathy from positioning.
ICU layer
  • Whenever a PICU patient cannot wean, develops new weakness, or fails to lift their head — EMG/NCS narrows the differential within hours.
  • Three planning targets at the bedside: warm the limb to ≥32 °C, wash out neuromuscular blocker, secure quiet electrical environment.
  • Genetic testing has replaced electrodiagnosis as first-line for hereditary disease, but EDX remains decisive for acquired disease.
RCPSC relevance
  • 🔥🔥🔥 Examiners frame "unexplained ventilator weaning failure" stems around bedside EDX.
  • Be ready to localize the lesion to one of four compartments (anterior horn cell, peripheral nerve, NMJ, muscle) before naming the disease.

INTRODUCTION

Clinical anchor
  • The chapter scope is the peripheral nervous system — motor axis from anterior horn cell to muscle, sensory axis up to the dorsal root ganglion.
  • Excludes central techniques (SSEP, TMS) used for spinal-cord disease.
  • Frames pediatric NMD into four anatomic compartments: anterior horn cell, peripheral nerve, neuromuscular junction, muscle.
  • Acknowledges that genetic testing reduced EDX use for hereditary disease, MRI replaced EDX as first-line for inflammatory myopathy.
  • Acquired conditions where EDX still drives decisions: poliomyelitis, acute flaccid myelitis (AFM), West Nile, AIDP, infantile botulism, tick paralysis, autoimmune MG, trauma, acute myositis.
  • ICU-acquired: ICU-AW (CIP, CIM, CINM), compressive mononeuropathies from positioning or ischemia.
ICU layer
  • The AANEM consensus reaffirms high utility of EDX in pediatric NMD despite genetic advances.
  • A pragmatic approach: clinical exam first, then EDX to localize, then targeted genetic or serologic confirmation.
  • Always do EDX before "no diagnosis" labels in a ventilator-dependent infant.
RCPSC pearl
  • Memorize the 4-compartment localization framework — it answers ~80 % of stems on this topic.

SIGNAL RECORDING — Equipment

Clinical anchor
  • Modern EMG/NCS equipment is small, portable, well-shielded, and PICU-deployable.
  • Amplifies microvolt (SNAP) to millivolt (CMAP) signals.
  • Bandwidth: ~1–2 Hz to ~20 kHz, customizable for each acquisition.
  • Computerized algorithms measure latency, amplitude, conduction velocity automatically.
  • Superimposed traces are essential for F-wave acquisition.
ICU layer
  • Common-mode rejection breaks down if reference/ground electrodes are loose — check impedance before chasing artifact.
  • ECMO, dialysis, and warming devices add electrical noise — turn off non-essential devices for the duration of the study where safe.
  • 50/60-Hz mains artifact is rare with modern shielded equipment but check skin preparation if seen.
Exam trap
  • Cold limbs slow conduction velocity by ~2 m/s per 1 °C below 32 °C — falsely diagnose demyelination.

SIGNAL RECORDING — Electrodes

Clinical anchor
  • Three classes: surface (cm radius), concentric needle (mm radius), single-fiber needle (~300 μm radius).
  • Surface electrodes record summated CMAPs and SNAPs.
  • Concentric needle records motor unit potentials (MUPs) — used for neuropathic vs. myopathic distinction.
  • Single-fiber (SFEMG) records pairs of fibers in one motor unit — used for NMJ "jitter" and blocking.
RCPSC pearl 🔥🔥🔥
  • Electrode type maps to question — surface for amplitude/velocity, concentric for MUP morphology, single-fiber for NMJ.
  • A normal CMAP does NOT exclude NMJ disease — escalate to RNS or SFEMG.

PERIPHERAL ELECTROPHYSIOLOGY — Stimulation and Recording

Clinical anchor
  • Supramaximal square-wave pulses (typically 0.1 ms duration, current up to ~100 mA).
  • Five core acquisitions: motor NCS (CMAP), sensory NCS (SNAP), repetitive nerve stimulation (RNS), F-wave, H-reflex.
  • AANEM has practice guidelines for stimulation safety and intensity.
ICU layer
  • Adequate sedation is necessary; some children tolerate without sedation if explained.
  • Stimulation does not damage nerve or skin if technique is correct.

Motor Nerve Conduction (CMAP)

Figure 53.2 — Normal motor NCS waveform
  • Biphasic CMAP: onset latency, peak amplitude, area, conduction velocity.
  • Onset latency reflects fastest myelinated fibers.
  • Amplitude reflects functioning motor axons + reinnervated fibers.
  • Conduction velocity calculated between proximal and distal stimulation.
Clinical anchor
  • Low CMAP amplitude: axonal loss OR severe conduction block proximal to stimulus.
  • Slow CV / prolonged distal latency: demyelination.
  • Temporal dispersion + amplitude drop >50 % across a segment: conduction block.
ICU layer
  • Bedside screening: distal CMAP from ulnar / peroneal — amplitude >50 % below normal in a child with progressive weakness signals axonal disease (AMAN, ICU-AW, severe SMA).
  • Serial CMAP amplitudes are the recovery tracker in ICU-AW.

Sensory Nerve Conduction (SNAP)

Clinical anchor
  • Small (μV) responses recorded antidromically or orthodromically.
  • Sural (older children); medial plantar (infants — long distance reduces stimulus artifact).
  • Preserved SNAP with low CMAP localizes proximal to dorsal root ganglion — AHC / ventral root / NMJ / muscle.
  • Reduced or absent SNAP indicates peripheral nerve disease (sensorimotor or sensory neuronopathy).
RCPSC pearl 🔥🔥🔥
  • "Normal SNAP + low CMAP + EMG denervation" = anterior horn cell / acute flaccid myelitis / poliomyelitis fingerprint.
  • Exception: in severe SMA 0/1, dorsal root ganglionopathy may reduce SNAP.

Repetitive Nerve Stimulation (RNS)

Figure 53.3 — RNS decrement and increment
  • Slow rate (2–3 Hz): progressive amplitude decrement (4th vs. 1st CMAP) >10 % is postsynaptic disease (MG, CMS).
  • Rapid rate (20–50 Hz) or post-exercise: marked amplitude increment (>60–100 %) is presynaptic disease (infant botulism, LEMS).
ICU layer 🔥🔥🔥
  • The decisive bedside test for the floppy infant: rapid RNS showing incremental response confirms infant botulism — start BabyBIG immediately.
  • For suspected MG crisis: 3-Hz RNS at ulnar / accessory nerve.
  • For unconscious or sedated patients, tetanic stimulation replaces voluntary contraction in increment testing.
Exam trap
  • A normal RNS does NOT exclude MG — up to 30 % of generalized MG and most ocular MG have negative RNS — go to SFEMG.

F-wave and H-reflex

Clinical anchor — F-wave
  • Late response from antidromic conduction up the motor axon → AHC firing → orthodromic descent.
  • Tests proximal motor pathway integrity.
  • Variable in shape but always present in 20 trials in normal subjects.
  • Absent or prolonged F-wave early in GBS / AIDP — sometimes the only abnormality in the first 7–10 days.
Clinical anchor — H-reflex
  • Electrical analogue of Achilles tendon reflex; tests Ia afferent → α-motor neuron monosynaptic arc.
  • Useful in S1 radiculopathy (lost ipsilaterally) and early peripheral neuropathy.
  • Disappears with age in many limbs other than gastrocnemius — absence not always pathological.
Pearl
  • F-wave is the earliest electrodiagnostic abnormality in AIDP — more sensitive than CSF in the first week.

Performing EMG/NCS in the PICU

Suggested PICU protocol
  • Warm limb to ≥32 °C; wash out NMBA (allow ≥30 min after a rocuronium dose).
  • Step 1: leg SNAP (sural or medial plantar in infants). If normal, sensory neuropathy excluded; arms may not need testing.
  • Step 2: motor NCS — peroneal (from extensor digitorum brevis) or ulnar (from adductor pollicis).
  • Step 3: needle EMG — tibialis anterior first (easily activated by sole stimulation).
  • Step 4: tongue EMG via submental route if AHC pattern suspected.
  • Step 5: SFEMG / stimulation SFEMG of orbicularis oculi if all above normal and NMJ disease possible.
ICU layer
  • Use the smallest gauge (30 g, "facial") needles to minimize discomfort and bleeding risk.
  • Specific phrenic or bulbar muscle sampling on request — bulbar palsies are increasingly common PICU admissions.
Pearl 🔥🔥🔥
  • Never close a ventilator-dependent infant's workup with "normal EDX" — SFEMG of orbicularis oculi catches missed CMS / seronegative MG.

Peripheral Nervous System Disorders in the PICU — Hereditary: Spinal Muscular Atrophy

Clinical anchor
  • 5q SMA — SMN1 deletion / point mutation; most common pediatric genetic NMD.
  • Severity stratification: SMA 0 (prenatal) → SMA 3 (childhood ambulatory).
  • SMA 0/1 present diagnostic difficulty for EDX: dorsal root ganglionopathy may reduce SNAP; reinnervated MUPs may not have developed yet.
  • Fibrillations alone do not confirm SMA — also seen in acute myositis / dystrophy.
  • Genetic testing is invariably positive in severe SMA and rapidly turned around in most centers.
ICU layer 🔥🔥🔥
  • SMN-modifying therapies (nusinersen intrathecal, onasemnogene IV gene therapy, risdiplam oral) make timely diagnosis a critical-illness emergency.
  • Pre-symptomatic newborn-screening detected SMA gives best outcomes with early therapy.
  • NIV bridging while genetic confirmation pending.

SMA with Respiratory Distress (SMARD1)

Clinical anchor
  • IGHMBP2 mutation — rare anterior horn cell variant.
  • Classic phenotype: IUGR baby, normal initial post-natal course, returns at ~3 months with respiratory failure.
  • Often unilateral diaphragmatic involvement at first — mimics diaphragmatic eventration.
  • No SMN-modifying therapy effective; long-term ventilatory support required.
Pearl
  • Always send IGHMBP2 testing in a "diaphragmatic eventration" infant who has IUGR history and bilateral / progressive involvement.
Page 2803

Congenital Myasthenic Syndromes (CMS)

Clinical anchor
  • Genetic NMJ disorders presenting as PICU diagnostic dilemmas.
  • Commonest subtype: epsilon AChR mutation (postsynaptic).
  • Other subtypes: ChAT (presynaptic), COLQ (synaptic acetylcholinesterase deficiency), RAPSN, DOK7, slow-channel, fast-channel.
  • RNS often shows decrement; SFEMG shows increased jitter.
  • Some subtypes worsen with anticholinesterases — subtype identification matters.
ICU layer
  • Slow-channel: fluoxetine / quinidine.
  • DOK7 / COLQ: salbutamol / ephedrine.
  • Postsynaptic AChR-ε / fast-channel: pyridostigmine ± 3,4-diaminopyridine.
  • Edrophonium testing — limited utility in PICU; risk of bradyarrhythmia.
Page 2804

Myotonic Dystrophy

Clinical anchor
  • DM1 — DMPK gene CTG repeat expansion; autosomal-dominant with anticipation.
  • Congenital DM1: very large CTG repeat (>1000), maternally inherited, floppy neonate, polyhydramnios.
  • Mother often has only subclinical grip myotonia — examine her.
  • EMG: classic "dive-bomber" myotonic discharges.
  • Hypotonia improves in childhood, then progressive weakness reappears in adolescence/adulthood.
Pearl 🔥🔥
  • Cardiac conduction defects progress through life — annual ECG mandatory, pacemaker risk.

Clinical anchor
  • The dominant categories: GBS subtypes, AFM, infant/foodborne/iatrogenic botulism, tick paralysis, autoimmune MG, toxic neuropathy, post-infectious mononeuritis multiplex.
  • Each has a discriminating electrodiagnostic profile.

Guillain-Barré Syndrome (GBS / AIDP) — Pathophysiology and Subtypes

Clinical anchor
  • Acute / subacute areflexic ascending paralysis after antecedent infection (Campylobacter, viral, COVID).
  • Subtypes — AIDP (demyelinating), AMAN (axonal motor), AMSAN (axonal sensorimotor), Miller-Fisher (ophthalmoplegia + ataxia + areflexia), Bickerstaff brainstem encephalitis, pharyngo-cervico-brachial.
  • Antiganglioside antibodies (GM1, GD1a, GQ1b) define some subtypes.
ICU layer 🔥🔥🔥
  • Indications for PICU: rapid progression, autonomic instability, bulbar dysfunction, FVC <20 mL/kg, MIP less negative than –30 cmH₂O.
  • Treat with IVIG 2 g/kg over 5 days or plasma exchange 50 mL/kg × 5 sessions.
  • Avoid succinylcholine — risk of hyperkalemia.

GBS — Electrodiagnostic Features

Clinical anchor
  • AIDP: slowed CV, prolonged distal motor latency, absent / prolonged F-waves, conduction block, temporal dispersion. Sensory NCS often abnormal.
  • AMAN: low CMAP amplitudes with preserved velocity; sensory NCS preserved.
  • AMSAN: low CMAP AND SNAP amplitudes.
  • Miller-Fisher: reduced SNAPs, absent H-reflex with preserved motor NCS.
Pearl 🔥🔥🔥
  • CSF cytoalbuminologic dissociation is classic but often absent in week 1 — F-waves are more sensitive early.
  • Three sensory and motor nerves should be tested for full subtype classification.

Acute Flaccid Myelitis (AFM)

Clinical anchor
  • Outbreak association with enterovirus D68 (biennial peaks in late summer/fall).
  • Asymmetric flaccid limb weakness with T2 hyperintensity in cord gray matter.
  • EDX indistinguishable from poliomyelitis — normal SNAPs, low CMAPs, EMG denervation.
  • Diagnosis confirmed by clinical + MRI + viral PCR (NP, stool, CSF).
ICU layer
  • Other viral causes of AFP — Japanese encephalitis, Murray Valley, St Louis, Russian spring-summer, West Nile, Zika.
  • No proven disease-modifying therapy; supportive + aggressive rehab.
  • Nerve transfer surgery considered at 6–12 months for residual paralysis.

Botulism — Infant and Foodborne

Clinical anchor
  • Mechanism: presynaptic NMJ block — botulinum toxin cleaves SNARE proteins, preventing ACh release.
  • Infant botulism (2 weeks–12 months): C. botulinum spores germinate in gut.
  • Presents with constipation, weak cry, hypotonia, ptosis, sluggish/fixed pupils, descending paralysis.
  • EDX: low resting CMAP, >60 % incremental response on rapid RNS, brief small-amplitude MUPs (BSAPs).
ICU layer 🔥🔥🔥
  • Treat empirically with BabyBIG (Human Botulism Immune Globulin) — do not wait for stool toxin.
  • Every day of delay extends ventilator days.
  • Avoid aminoglycosides — worsen presynaptic block.
  • Foodborne / adult: equine heptavalent botulism antitoxin (BAT) — skin test before infusion.
Page 2810

Tick Paralysis

Clinical anchor
  • Mechanism: tick salivary neurotoxin — presynaptic Na-channel + Ca-release dysfunction.
  • Acute ascending paralysis, areflexia, often ataxia; CSF normal.
  • EDX: reduced CMAP amplitudes with relatively preserved velocity; minimal RNS decrement.
Pearl 🔥🔥
  • Full body inspection for an attached tick (scalp, axillae, groin, behind ears) is mandatory in any pediatric AFP from a relevant geographic region.
  • Tick removal is diagnostic AND curative — weakness reverses within hours.
Page 2811

Autoimmune Myasthenia Gravis (JMG)

Clinical anchor
  • Postsynaptic AChR or MuSK antibody binding.
  • Fatigable weakness, ocular involvement, bulbar, proximal limb, respiratory.
  • Pupils are spared (unlike botulism).
  • RNS at 3 Hz: >10 % decrement.
  • SFEMG: increased jitter ± blocking — most sensitive electrodiagnostic test.
ICU layer
  • Crisis: respiratory failure ± severe bulbar dysfunction.
  • Treat with IVIG 2 g/kg or PLEX; pyridostigmine; steroids; thymectomy if indicated.
  • Drug avoidance list: aminoglycosides, macrolides, fluoroquinolones, beta-blockers, magnesium, phenytoin, lithium, D-penicillamine, hydroxychloroquine, contrast.

Conditions Acquired in the PICU — Critical Illness Neuromyopathy / ICU-AW

Definitions
  • Umbrella term ICU-AW includes critical illness polyneuropathy (CIP), critical illness myopathy (CIM), critical illness neuromyopathy (CINM, overlap), prolonged neuromuscular blockade.
  • CIP: axonal sensorimotor neuropathy with reduced CMAP + SNAP, preserved velocity.
  • CIM: primary myosin loss with reduced CMAP, normal SNAP, low neCMAP/dmCMAP ratio (<0.5).
  • CINM: combined features.
Risk factors
  • Sepsis / SIRS, multi-organ failure, sustained hyperglycemia, prolonged immobilization, corticosteroid exposure, neuromuscular blocker exposure, prolonged mechanical ventilation, severity-of-illness.
Diagnostic criteria (consensus)
  • Generalized weakness developing after onset of critical illness.
  • Symmetric, flaccid, sparing cranial nerves usually.
  • MRC sum score <48/60 (or pediatric proxy) on two examinations ≥24 h apart.
  • Ventilator dependence.
  • Other causes of weakness excluded.
ICU layer — prevention bundle
  • Daily sedation interruption.
  • Minimize NMBA exposure.
  • Avoid sustained hyperglycemia (target ~6–10 mmol/L).
  • Early mobilization (PT/OT) from day 1 of stability.
  • Minimize corticosteroid duration.
  • Treat sepsis source aggressively.
  • Protein-adequate nutrition.
  • Routine MRC sum-score screening on rounds.
Pearl 🔥🔥🔥
  • No drug therapy works for ICU-AW. Prevention is the exam answer.

COVID-19 and ICU-Acquired Weakness

Clinical anchor
  • COVID-era ICU survivors show increased ICU-AW rates.
  • Multifactorial: prolonged sedation, prone position, steroids, prolonged mechanical ventilation, direct viral nerve effects.
  • Some develop a post-COVID GBS-like syndrome — electrodiagnosis discriminates.
ICU layer
  • Apply standard prevention bundle aggressively; engagement with early mobility programs even when proned.
  • Outcomes broadly similar to other sepsis-related ICU-AW.
Page 2817

Mononeuropathies That Develop in the PICU

Clinical anchor
  • Mechanism: pressure / ischemia from prolonged positioning, especially during long surgery, in the cardiac PICU, on ECMO.
  • Common patterns: peroneal at fibular head (foot drop), ulnar at elbow, brachial plexus stretch (head-turned positioning), sciatic post intramuscular injection.
ICU layer
  • EDX: after 7–10 days, conduction block or low CMAP across the lesion; denervation on EMG.
  • Splint to prevent contracture; document early; most recover in weeks–months.
  • Severe / persistent cases may need surgical decompression.
Pearl 🔥🔥
  • New foot drop in a child waking from a long cardiac surgery → peroneal compression at the fibular head — common exam stem.

CONCLUSION

Take-home messages
  • Electrodiagnostic studies localize the lesion to one of four compartments — AHC / nerve / NMJ / muscle.
  • They distinguish demyelinating from axonal disease.
  • They are the most sensitive single test for early GBS, MG, infant botulism, and ICU-AW.
  • Bedside deployment is now feasible in any PICU — deploy early, not late.

Figure 53.1 — Electrode types
  • Recording radius shrinks from surface (cm) → concentric (mm) → single-fiber (~300 μm).
  • Choice of electrode determines question answered: summated amplitude vs. MUP morphology vs. single-fiber jitter.
  • Trap: confusing CMAP amplitude (axon count) with MUP amplitude (motor-unit size after reinnervation).
Figure 53.2 — Normal motor NCS waveform
  • Onset latency = myelin function.
  • Amplitude = axon count.
  • Area = axon count corrected for dispersion.
  • CV = (distance / latency difference) between two stimulation sites.
  • Trap: conduction block can drop amplitude at one site without slowing CV.
Figure 53.3 — RNS decrement vs. increment
  • Slow 3-Hz train decrement >10 % = postsynaptic NMJ (MG, CMS).
  • Rapid 50-Hz train increment >60–100 % = presynaptic NMJ (botulism, LEMS).
  • Trap: normal RNS does not exclude MG — escalate to SFEMG.
Figure 53.A — Neuropathic vs. Myopathic EMG Patterns
  • Schematic of motor unit potential morphology and recruitment.
  • Neuropathic — large, long, polyphasic with reduced recruitment.
  • Myopathic — small, short, polyphasic with early recruitment ("SLEEP" mnemonic).
Figure 53.4 — F-wave acquisition
  • Multiple sweeps superimposed; variable F-wave morphology with consistent M-response.
  • Absent / prolonged F-wave is the earliest AIDP marker.
  • Trap: F-wave variability is normal; absence (in >20 trials) is the abnormal finding.

  • Submaximal tibial stimulation; recording over gastrocnemius shows H-wave at ~30 ms.

Equations

Conduction velocity (CV)
CV (m/s) = Distance (mm) ÷ [Proximal latency − Distal latency] (ms)
Pediatric norms reach adult values (~50 m/s lower limb, ~55 m/s upper limb) by age 3–5 years.
RNS decrement
Decrement (%) = [(A1 − A4) ÷ A1] × 100. >10 % = postsynaptic NMJ disease.
Rapid increment
Increment (%) = [(A_post − A_pre) ÷ A_pre] × 100. >60–100 % = presynaptic NMJ disease.
F-wave latency
F_min ≈ (2 × proximal–distal distance ÷ CV) + central delay (~1 ms). Absence or prolongation above age-norm is abnormal.

① AANEM Position / Consensus Statement on Pediatric Electrodiagnostic Studies

  • Authoring body: American Association of Neuromuscular & Electrodiagnostic Medicine (AANEM)
  • Source: Muscle & Nerve (AANEM official journal) · 2021 (with subsequent updates 2022–2023)
  • Link: aanem.org/practice/practice-guidance · Journal: Muscle & Nerve (Wiley)
  • Key findings:
    • Despite advances in genetic and MRI diagnostics, EDX studies retain high utility in acquired pediatric NMD.
    • EDX remains decisive for ventilator-dependent weakness of unclear cause.
    • Recommends bedside-friendly protocol (sural/medial plantar SNAP → peroneal CMAP → tibialis anterior EMG → SFEMG of orbicularis oculi as needed).
    • Endorses minimum 30 °C limb skin temperature for valid CV measurement.
  • ICU impact: Codifies early bedside testing in any PICU when weakness drives ventilation; legitimizes the streamlined PICU protocol on examiner audits.
  • RCPSC relevance: Reference statement for examiner-level discussions of test selection and indication.

② EAN / PNS Guideline on Guillain-Barré Syndrome

  • Authoring bodies: European Academy of Neurology (EAN) + Peripheral Nerve Society (PNS)
  • Source: Journal of the Peripheral Nervous System · 2023 · Van den Berg B, Walgaard C, Drenthen J, Fokke C, Jacobs BC, van Doorn PA (lead authors of foundational work; 2023 update led by PNS/EAN task force)
  • Link: DOI 10.1111/jns.12567 (PNS guideline DOI series) · PNS site: pnsociety.com · EAN guideline portal: ean.org/research/ean-guidelines
  • Key findings:
    • IVIG 2 g/kg (over 2–5 days) or plasma exchange 50 mL/kg × 5 sessions as first-line in adults and children.
    • No benefit from combining IVIG + PLEX.
    • Corticosteroids not recommended.
    • Treatment-related fluctuation: repeat course of same agent is reasonable.
    • Early NCS for prognostication, especially when AMAN suspected.
  • ICU impact: Anchors IVIG vs. PLEX choice; defines TRF management.
  • RCPSC relevance: Core reference for management answers.

③ California Infant Botulism Treatment and Prevention Program — BabyBIG (Human Botulism Immune Globulin)

  • Authoring body: California Department of Public Health · Infant Botulism Treatment and Prevention Program (IBTPP)
  • Source: Original pivotal RCT — Arnon SS, Schechter R, Maslanka SE, Jewell NP, Hatheway CL. N Engl J Med. 2006;354:462–471 · with subsequent registry updates 2010–2023
  • Link: Program/24-hour hotline: infantbotulism.org · Pivotal trial: DOI 10.1056/NEJMoa051926 · PMID 16452559
  • Key findings:
    • Single IV BIG-IV dose shortens hospital stay by ~3 weeks and mechanical ventilation by ~2 weeks compared with placebo.
    • Greatest benefit when given within ≤3 days of admission — argues for empiric dosing on clinical suspicion.
    • Adverse-event rate low; aluminum hydroxide concern in renal failure managed by hydration.
    • Registry confirms efficacy across infants <1 year with toxin types A, B, and others.
  • ICU impact: Empiric treatment on clinical suspicion + supportive RNS is the standard. Do not delay for stool toxin confirmation.
  • RCPSC relevance: Examiners expect BabyBIG named on infant-botulism stems.

④ WHO Global Polio Eradication Initiative — AFP Surveillance

  • Authoring body: World Health Organization (WHO) + Global Polio Eradication Initiative (GPEI)
  • Source: WHO Standard Procedures for AFP Surveillance · 2018 update, with annual surveillance bulletins through 2024
  • Link: polioeradication.org · WHO AFP standards: who.int/teams/immunization-vaccines-and-biologicals/diseases/poliomyelitis
  • Key findings:
    • Any child <15 y with AFP must be reported.
    • Two stool samples 24–48 h apart, refrigerated, sent to a WHO-accredited polio reference laboratory.
    • Adequate sample = both samples taken within 14 days of onset.
    • Surveillance performance indicators: ≥2 / 100 000 children <15 y; ≥80 % adequate stool sampling.
  • ICU impact: Public-health reporting is a duty even when GBS or AFM is the working diagnosis.
  • RCPSC relevance: Stool-sampling requirement is a high-yield exam factoid.

⑤ CDC Acute Flaccid Myelitis Surveillance & Clinical Management

  • Authoring body: US Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) + AFM Task Force
  • Source: CDC AFM website + MMWR reports · 2014–2024 surveillance data · Murphy OC, Messacar K, Benson L et al. Lancet. 2021;397(10271):334–346 (clinical management review)
  • Link: cdc.gov/acute-flaccid-myelitis · Clinical review DOI: 10.1016/S0140-6736(20)32723-9
  • Key findings:
    • Biennial outbreak pattern (peaks 2014, 2016, 2018) linked to enterovirus D68.
    • Asymmetric flaccid limb weakness with cord T2 hyperintensity in the gray matter.
    • EDX: preserved SNAP, low CMAP, EMG denervation — indistinguishable from poliomyelitis.
    • No proven disease-modifying therapy; supportive + aggressive rehabilitation.
    • Nerve transfer surgery at 6–12 months for residual upper-limb paralysis improves function.
  • ICU impact: Distinguishing AFM from AIDP avoids futile IVIG/PLEX and prioritizes rehab.
  • RCPSC relevance: Examiners use late-summer enterovirus stems with asymmetric weakness.

⑥ SMA Newborn Screening + SMN-Modifying Therapy

  • Authoring bodies: US Recommended Uniform Screening Panel (RUSP, since 2018) · Cure SMA · European Reference Network on Neuromuscular Disorders
  • Key trials:
    • NURTURE (onasemnogene/AVXS-101 pre-symptomatic) — Strauss KA et al. Nat Med. 2022;28:1390–1397 · DOI 10.1038/s41591-022-01867-3
    • SPR1NT (pre-symptomatic onasemnogene) — Mendell JR et al. N Engl J Med. 2017;377:1713–1722 (Phase 1) · DOI 10.1056/NEJMoa1706198
    • SUNFISH (risdiplam type 2/3) — Mercuri E et al. N Engl J Med. 2023;388:1366–1376 · DOI 10.1056/NEJMoa2009965
    • FIREFISH (risdiplam type 1) — Darras BT et al. N Engl J Med. 2021;385:427–435 · DOI 10.1056/NEJMoa2102047
    • CHERISH (nusinersen later-onset) — Mercuri E et al. N Engl J Med. 2018;378:625–635 · DOI 10.1056/NEJMoa1710504
  • Links: curesma.org · HRSA RUSP
  • Key findings:
    • Pre-symptomatic treatment (via newborn screening detection) gives near-normal motor outcomes.
    • Symptomatic infants benefit substantially but rarely reach normal milestones.
    • Combination therapy is increasingly used in incomplete responders.
  • ICU impact: A floppy infant with respiratory failure should have SMN1 testing turned around within hours, not days.
  • RCPSC relevance: Naming the three modifying agents and their administration route is a standard exam beat.

⑦ ESPNIC / PALISI Consensus on ICU-Acquired Weakness in Children

  • Authoring bodies: European Society of Paediatric and Neonatal Intensive Care (ESPNIC) + Pediatric Acute Lung Injury and Sepsis Investigators (PALISI)
  • Source: Intensive Care Medicine / Pediatric Critical Care Medicine · 2023 consensus statement (built on adult ICU-AW criteria of Stevens RD et al. 2009)
  • Link: ESPNIC: espnic-online.org · PALISI: palisi.org · Adult criteria origin: DOI 10.1097/CCM.0b013e3181b6e64c (PMID 19623052)
  • Key findings:
    • Criteria — MRC sum score <48/60 (or pediatric proxy) on two examinations ≥24 h apart, ventilator dependence, electrodiagnostic confirmation when feasible.
    • Prevention bundle — daily sedation interruption, minimize NMBA, glycemic control 6–10 mmol/L, early mobilization from day 1, minimize corticosteroid duration, treat sepsis, protein-adequate nutrition.
    • Mobilization protocols feasible on ECMO.
    • No drug therapy works.
  • ICU impact: Codifies screening + confirmation + prevention pathway.
  • RCPSC relevance: Bundle items and MRC threshold are common stem ingredients.

⑧ International Congenital Myasthenic Syndromes Working Group

  • Authoring body: International CMS Working Group · Engel AG (Mayo), Beeson D (Oxford) and collaborators
  • Source: Engel AG, Shen XM, Selcen D, Sine SM. Lancet Neurol. 2015;14(4):420–434 (foundational review); subsequent updates 2020–2024 in Neuromuscular Disorders
  • Link: Foundational review DOI: 10.1016/S1474-4422(14)70201-7 · PMID 25792100 · Updated CMS resource: Muscle Gene Table
  • Key findings:
    • Pharmacotherapy is subtype-specific — pyridostigmine + 3,4-DAP for ε-AChR / RAPSN / fast-channel; salbutamol or ephedrine for DOK7 / COLQ; fluoxetine or quinidine for slow-channel.
    • Anticholinesterases worsen slow-channel, DOK7, and COLQ subtypes — confirm subtype before treating.
    • Genetic panel + SFEMG drive the correct choice.
  • ICU impact: A floppy infant who paradoxically worsens on pyridostigmine deserves CMS subtype testing.
  • RCPSC relevance: Subtype-specific drug direction is the high-yield CMS answer.

⑨ Cochrane Review — Plasma Exchange vs. IVIG for GBS

  • Authoring body: Cochrane Neuromuscular Group
  • Source: Hughes RA, Swan AV, van Doorn PA. Cochrane Database Syst Rev. 2014 (last update 2017) — IVIG; Chevret S et al. 2017 — plasma exchange
  • Link: Cochrane Library: DOI 10.1002/14651858.CD002063.pub6 · PMID 25238327
  • Key findings:
    • IVIG and plasma exchange are equally effective at hastening recovery in adults; pediatric data support similar effect size.
    • Combining IVIG + PLEX shows no advantage.
    • Corticosteroids do not improve outcomes.
  • ICU impact: Choose by patient factor — line access, hemodynamic stability, IgA status.

⑩ COVID-19–associated GBS systematic reviews

  • Source: Multiple systematic reviews 2021–2024; representative: Filosto M et al. J Neurol Neurosurg Psychiatry. 2021;92:751–756 · DOI 10.1136/jnnp-2020-324837
  • Key findings:
    • Modest increase in GBS risk after SARS-CoV-2 infection.
    • Vaccine association is weak and outweighed by infection prevention benefit.
    • Clinical course and treatment unchanged from non-COVID parainfectious GBS.
  • ICU impact: Same management algorithm applies regardless of antecedent infection.

5. ICU Decision Engine

IF PICU child has unexplained weakness or weaning failure with normal central imaging
THEN
  • Do bedside NCS + EMG within 24 h.
  • Start with sural / medial plantar SNAP and peroneal / ulnar CMAP.
  • Warm limb ≥32 °C and ensure NMBA washout before interpreting.
IF SNAP preserved + CMAP reduced + EMG denervation
THEN
  • Lesion is at the anterior horn cell / ventral root.
  • Differentials: SMA, AFM, polio, severe GBS axonal variant.
  • Send SMN1 testing, MRI cord, infectious workup (enterovirus PCR).
IF SNAP reduced + CMAP reduced + EMG denervation
THEN
  • Peripheral nerve disease (axonal): GBS axonal variant (AMSAN), critical illness polyneuropathy, toxic neuropathy.
  • Treat etiology; IVIG/PLEX if AMSAN.
IF CMAP slow / dispersed + F-wave absent + EMG normal early
THEN
  • Demyelinating GBS (AIDP).
  • IVIG 2 g/kg or PLEX; intubate proactively if FVC <20 mL/kg.
IF CMAP normal + RNS decrement at 3 Hz + SFEMG jitter
THEN
  • Postsynaptic NMJ disease (MG / CMS).
  • Confirm Ab status, treat per crisis pathway, avoid worsening drugs.
IF Low CMAP at rest + >60 % increment with rapid RNS + brief small MUPs
THEN
  • Presynaptic NMJ disease (infant botulism / LEMS).
  • Start BabyBIG immediately if infant.
  • Avoid aminoglycosides.
IF EMG short-duration low-amplitude polyphasic MUPs with early recruitment, normal SNAP and velocity
THEN
  • Myopathy — inflammatory, dystrophic, or critical illness.
  • MRI muscle, CK, biopsy if needed.
IF Failure to wean >7 days after sepsis with reduced CMAP and normal SNAP
THEN
  • Critical illness myopathy.
  • Minimize NMBA and steroids; mobilize aggressively; plan slow wean.
IF New unilateral foot drop after long surgery
THEN
  • Peroneal mononeuropathy at fibular head.
  • Splint, document, recover over weeks–months.

6. SAQ Bank — 40 Questions

SAQ 1. Name the four anatomic compartments of pediatric NMD with one discriminating EDX clue each.
Answer.
  • AHC — preserved SNAP + low CMAP + EMG denervation.
  • Peripheral nerve — reduced SNAP and CMAP; slow velocity (demyelinating) or low amplitude (axonal).
  • NMJ — RNS decrement (postsynaptic) or rapid-RNS increment (presynaptic).
  • Muscle — short-duration, low-amplitude, polyphasic MUPs with early recruitment + normal SNAP/velocity.
SAQ 2. Why is SNAP preserved in AHC disease, and what is the exception?
Answer.
  • Sensory cell body sits in dorsal root ganglion — peripheral to AHC.
  • Pure motor AHC disease leaves DRG intact.
  • Exception — severe SMA 0/1 dorsal root ganglionopathy may reduce SNAP.
SAQ 3. Define conduction block.
Answer.
  • ≥50 % drop in CMAP amplitude (or area) between distal and proximal stimulation sites.
  • Without significant temporal dispersion.
  • Signifies focal demyelination causing impulse failure across that segment.
SAQ 4. Ventilator-mechanics thresholds for intubation in pediatric GBS.
Answer.
  • FVC <20 mL/kg.
  • MIP less negative than –30 cmH₂O.
  • MEP <40 cmH₂O.
  • Rapidly declining trend, bulbar weakness with secretion failure, autonomic instability.
  • Intubate proactively, not emergently.
SAQ 5. Infant botulism presentation and bedside confirmation.
Answer.
  • Age 2 weeks–12 months.
  • Constipation, weak cry, poor feeding, hypotonia, ophthalmoplegia, descending paralysis.
  • RNS: low resting CMAP, >60 % incremental response at 50 Hz / post-exercise.
  • Treat immediately with BabyBIG.
SAQ 6. Why avoid aminoglycosides in suspected infant botulism?
Answer.
  • Aminoglycosides block presynaptic ACh release.
  • Compound the existing presynaptic NMJ failure of botulinum toxin.
  • Risk precipitating respiratory arrest.
SAQ 7. Differentiate AIDP from AMAN electrodiagnostically.
Answer.
  • AIDP — slow CV, prolonged distal latency, absent / prolonged F-waves, conduction block, temporal dispersion, preserved CMAP amplitudes early.
  • AMAN — low CMAP amplitudes from outset, near-normal velocity and latency, EMG acute denervation, F-waves variable.
SAQ 8. Five risk factors for ICU-AW.
Answer.
  • Sepsis / SIRS.
  • Multi-organ failure.
  • Sustained hyperglycemia.
  • Corticosteroid exposure.
  • NMBA exposure (especially prolonged).
  • Prolonged mechanical ventilation and immobility.
SAQ 9. Single best preventive intervention for ICU-AW.
Answer.
  • Early mobilization paired with daily sedation interruption.
  • Minimal NMBA exposure.
  • Glycemic control.
  • No pharmacotherapy has proven benefit.
SAQ 10. Distinguish CIP from CIM at the bedside.
Answer.
  • CIP — reduced CMAP AND SNAP with preserved velocity.
  • CIM — reduced CMAP, normal SNAP, low nerve-evoked CMAP / direct-muscle-stimulation ratio (<0.5).
  • EMG in CIM: small short-duration MUPs ± fibrillations.
SAQ 11. Three common PICU compressive mononeuropathies and clues.
Answer.
  • Peroneal at fibular head — foot drop after prolonged lateral positioning.
  • Ulnar at elbow — paresthesias / 4th-5th finger clawing.
  • Brachial plexus stretch — arm weakness after head-turned positioning, ECMO cannulation.
SAQ 12. What is SFEMG and what does it quantify?
Answer.
  • Specialized concentric needle records action potentials from a pair of muscle fibers in the same motor unit.
  • Variability of the inter-potential interval is "jitter" — quantified as mean consecutive difference.
  • Increased jitter ± blocking indicates NMJ failure.
SAQ 13. Preferred muscle for stimulation SFEMG in a sedated child.
Answer.
  • Orbicularis oculi via stimulation SFEMG.
  • Accessible; does not require voluntary contraction.
  • Highly sensitive for generalized MG / CMS.
SAQ 14. Equation for nerve conduction velocity.
Answer.
  • CV (m/s) = distance (mm) ÷ (proximal − distal latency in ms).
  • Distance along the nerve path between two stimulation points.
  • Latencies measured to onset of CMAP.
SAQ 15. Why does temperature change pediatric NCS results?
Answer.
  • Cold slows axonal sodium-channel kinetics.
  • Prolongs distal latencies and slows CV ~2 m/s per 1 °C below 32 °C.
  • Maintain skin temperature ≥32 °C before interpreting velocity.
SAQ 16. Why are pediatric CV norms age-dependent up to ~5 years?
Answer.
  • Myelination is incomplete at birth and matures progressively.
  • Infants have ~25–35 m/s velocities.
  • Adult values (~50–55 m/s) reached by ~3–5 years.
  • Adult cutoffs in a neonate misdiagnose physiological slowing.
SAQ 17. MRI role in suspected inflammatory myopathy.
Answer.
  • STIR / T2 hyperintensity shows muscle edema and atrophy.
  • Guides biopsy site.
  • First-line in juvenile dermatomyositis.
  • EDX confirms myopathic process and excludes neuropathic mimics.
SAQ 18. AANEM position on EDX in the genetic-testing era.
Answer.
  • Genetics has replaced EDX as first-line for hereditary disease (SMA, muscular dystrophy).
  • EDX retains high utility for acquired disease, ventilator-dependent weakness, and when genetic testing is non-diagnostic.
SAQ 19. Define myotonic discharge and a pathognomonic disease.
Answer.
  • Repetitive, waxing-and-waning "dive-bomber" discharges of muscle fiber origin.
  • Pathognomonic for myotonic dystrophy (DM1, DM2).
  • Also seen in non-dystrophic myotonias and some K-channel disorders.
SAQ 20. Four causes of fibrillation potentials.
Answer.
  • Axonal neuropathy / motor neuronopathy (SMA, ALS, AFM).
  • Inflammatory myopathy.
  • Severe necrotizing myopathy.
  • Late stages of muscular dystrophy.
  • Fibrillations are not specific to neurogenic disease.
SAQ 21. Typical CSF finding in GBS and its temporal pitfall.
Answer.
  • Cytoalbuminologic dissociation — elevated protein (often >0.45 g/L) with normal cell count.
  • Up to 50 % of CSF studies in week 1 are normal.
  • EDX is more sensitive than CSF early.
SAQ 22. Approach to a floppy infant with descending paralysis.
Answer.
  • Stabilize airway and ventilation.
  • Suspect infant botulism.
  • Send stool for botulinum toxin and PCR.
  • Bedside RNS expecting low CMAP with marked rapid-RNS increment.
  • Treat empirically with BabyBIG.
  • Avoid aminoglycosides.
  • Anticipate 2–4 weeks of ventilator support.
SAQ 23. Miller-Fisher syndrome features and associated antibody.
Answer.
  • Triad — ophthalmoplegia, ataxia, areflexia.
  • Often post-Campylobacter / viral.
  • Anti-GQ1b IgG positive in ~90 %.
  • Treatment supportive ± IVIG.
SAQ 24. Why might SNAP be reduced in severe SMA 0/1?
Answer.
  • Severe SMA may have dorsal root ganglionopathy with sensory axon loss.
  • Can mimic peripheral neuropathy on NCS.
  • Confirm with SMN1 deletion testing.
SAQ 25. Two EDX features distinguishing AFM from AIDP.
Answer.
  • AFM — preserved SNAPs, reduced CMAPs with normal velocity, denervation on EMG, asymmetric distribution.
  • AIDP — slow CV, prolonged distal latencies, absent F-waves, conduction block; sensory NCS often abnormal; symmetric distribution.
SAQ 26. EDX role in tick paralysis.
Answer.
  • Low-amplitude CMAPs with relatively preserved velocity.
  • Minimal RNS decrement.
  • Rarely necessary — full body skin inspection and tick removal are diagnostic + curative.
SAQ 27. SFEMG finding in autoimmune MG.
Answer.
  • Increased jitter (mean consecutive difference > age-specific upper limit, typically >35 μs).
  • Intermittent blocking.
  • Sensitivity >95 % for generalized MG and most sensitive for ocular MG.
SAQ 28. Differential of failure to wean requiring EDX.
Answer.
  • ICU-AW (CIP / CIM).
  • Residual neuromuscular blockade.
  • Unrecognized GBS.
  • Undiagnosed MG / CMS.
  • Infant botulism.
  • Occult SMA / SMARD.
  • Phrenic nerve injury.
  • Cervical myelopathy.
  • Metabolic / endocrine myopathies (hypophosphatemia, hypothyroid, steroid myopathy).
SAQ 29. H-reflex utility.
Answer.
  • Electrical equivalent of Achilles reflex.
  • Ia afferent stimulation → α-motor neuron monosynaptic response.
  • Useful in S1 radiculopathy (lost ipsilaterally) and early peripheral neuropathy.
SAQ 30. Three pitfalls producing false-positive EDX in the PICU.
Answer.
  • Cold limb → false slowing.
  • Residual neuromuscular blockade → false RNS decrement.
  • Electrical interference (ECMO, dialysis, warming devices) → spurious spontaneous activity.
SAQ 31. Direct muscle stimulation (DMS) and CIM vs. CIP.
Answer.
  • DMS bypasses the nerve and stimulates muscle directly.
  • CIM — both nerve-evoked and muscle-evoked CMAPs reduced (low neCMAP/dmCMAP >0.5 indicates myopathy).
  • CIP — nerve-evoked reduced but DMS preserved (ratio <0.5).
SAQ 32. Post-exercise facilitation — disease implication.
Answer.
  • Brief voluntary contraction or 10 s tetanic stimulation transiently increases CMAP amplitude >60 %.
  • Indicates presynaptic NMJ disease — LEMS or infant botulism.
  • Use tetanic RNS when sedation prevents voluntary contraction.
SAQ 33. Standard PICU NCS/EMG protocol for an unexplained ventilator-dependent infant.
Answer.
  • Warm to 32 °C; ensure NMBA washout.
  • Sural or medial plantar SNAP.
  • Peroneal motor NCS with F-wave.
  • Ulnar motor NCS with F-wave + 3 Hz and 50 Hz RNS.
  • Needle EMG of tibialis anterior.
  • Submental tongue EMG if AHC pattern.
  • SFEMG / StimSFEMG of orbicularis oculi if above normal.
SAQ 34. Contraindications and cautions to needle EMG in PICU.
Answer.
  • Contraindications — severe uncorrected thrombocytopenia or coagulopathy; overlying skin infection.
  • Cautions — anticoagulation (small needles, single passes); valvular disease (sterile technique; no formal endocarditis prophylaxis indication).
SAQ 35. EMG pattern in juvenile dermatomyositis.
Answer.
  • ↑ insertional activity with fibrillations and positive sharp waves.
  • Short-duration, low-amplitude, polyphasic MUPs with early recruitment.
  • Occasionally myotonic-like discharges.
  • Pattern is non-specific — MRI muscle and autoantibodies (anti-Mi2, anti-MDA5) confirm.
SAQ 36. EDX profile of acute axonal nerve injury and evolution.
Answer.
  • Immediately — low / absent CMAP on distal stimulation; normal proximal CMAP (Wallerian degeneration not yet complete).
  • By 7–10 days — distal CMAP loss complete.
  • 2–3 weeks — EMG fibrillations and positive sharp waves (acute denervation).
  • Months later — reinnervation produces large, long, polyphasic MUPs.
SAQ 37. EDX role in suspected phrenic nerve injury after cardiac surgery.
Answer.
  • Phrenic nerve conduction with surface recording over the diaphragm.
  • Fluoroscopic or ultrasound sniff test.
  • Needle EMG of the diaphragm in expert hands.
  • Confirms phrenic neuropathy and may prompt diaphragmatic plication.
SAQ 38. Why is early SFEMG decisive when other tests are normal?
Answer.
  • SFEMG is far more sensitive than RNS for NMJ disease.
  • Increased jitter / blocking can be the only EDX abnormality in CMS or seronegative MG.
  • Both are treatable — missing them costs years of ventilator dependence.
SAQ 39. Three drug classes that worsen NMJ transmission.
Answer.
  • Aminoglycosides (gentamicin, tobramycin).
  • Macrolides (erythromycin, azithromycin).
  • Magnesium sulfate.
  • Fluoroquinolones.
  • Beta-blockers, lithium, non-depolarizing NMBAs (prolonged effect).
SAQ 40. ICU-AW prevention bundle.
Answer.
  • Daily sedation interruption / lightest effective sedation.
  • Minimize NMBA exposure.
  • Avoid sustained hyperglycemia (target ~6–10 mmol/L).
  • Early mobilization with PT/OT from day 1.
  • Minimize corticosteroid duration.
  • Treat sepsis source aggressively.
  • Protein-adequate nutrition.
  • Routine MRC sum-score screening on rounds.

7. Case Bank — 30 Interpretation Cases

Case 1 — Floppy newborn.
Term infant, 3 weeks old, profound hypotonia, areflexia, weak cry, tongue fasciculations, respiratory failure. EDX: normal SNAPs, severely reduced CMAPs, EMG fibrillations and large reinnervated MUPs in legs and tongue.
Q. Localize, diagnose, next steps.
Answer.
  • Lesion at AHC.
  • Likely SMA type 1 (5q-SMN1 deletion).
  • Urgent SMN1 deletion + SMN2 copy number testing.
  • Supportive ventilation, swallow assessment, family counselling.
  • Initiate modifying therapy (nusinersen / onasemnogene / risdiplam) ASAP.
Case 2 — Failure to wean post-sepsis.
8-year-old, ventilated 14 days, rocuronium 4 days, methylprednisolone. Fails 3 SBTs. Symmetric weakness, diminished reflexes.
Q. EDX picture, plan.
Answer.
  • Likely CIM ± CIP.
  • EDX — low CMAP, normal SNAP if isolated CIM; EMG short MUPs.
  • DMS confirms CIM if neCMAP/dmCMAP <0.5.
  • Stop NMBA, taper steroids, optimize glycemia, mobilize, plan tracheostomy if prolonged wean.
Case 3 — Classic AIDP.
12-year-old, 2 weeks post-Campylobacter, ascending weakness over 4 days, areflexia, FVC 18, autonomic lability. CSF protein 0.6 g/L, cells 2.
Q. Subtype, EDX, treatment.
Answer.
  • AIDP.
  • EDX — prolonged distal latencies, slow CV, absent / prolonged F-waves, conduction block.
  • Intubate (FVC <20). Avoid succinylcholine.
  • IVIG 2 g/kg over 5 days or PLEX.
  • Treat autonomic instability with short-acting agents.
Case 4 — Infant botulism.
5-month-old, 5 days of constipation, poor feeding, weak cry, descending paralysis, ophthalmoplegia. Bedside RNS — low CMAP with 80 % increment at 50 Hz.
Q. Treatment, drug to avoid.
Answer.
  • Infant botulism.
  • BabyBIG immediately.
  • Avoid aminoglycosides.
  • Supportive ICU 2–4 weeks; swallow restoration gates extubation.
Case 5 — AFM.
3-year-old, asymmetric flaccid limb weakness after URI. MRI cord gray matter T2 hyperintensity. EDX — normal SNAPs, low CMAPs, EMG with fibrillations.
Q. Diagnosis, management.
Answer.
  • AFM (likely enterovirus D68).
  • Send NP / stool / CSF for enterovirus PCR.
  • Supportive care; IVIG sometimes given (weak evidence).
  • Aggressive rehab; nerve transfer at 6–12 mo for residual.
  • Report to public health.
Case 6 — Ventilator-dependent toddler, normal MRI.
2-year-old, ventilator-dependent. MRI brain / spine normal. SMA negative. NCS / EMG normal. RNS at 3 Hz unremarkable.
Q. Next test.
Answer.
  • Single-fiber EMG (or stimulation SFEMG of orbicularis oculi).
  • Most sensitive for NMJ disease.
  • Catches CMS and seronegative MG when RNS is normal.
Case 7 — Post-cardiac surgery foot drop.
14-year-old post-Fontan, new right foot drop POD 1, lateral decubitus during 9 h surgery.
Q. Diagnosis, plan.
Answer.
  • Peroneal mononeuropathy at fibular head.
  • EDX after 7–10 days — conduction block, denervation in tibialis anterior and peroneus longus.
  • Splint, document, expect weeks–months for recovery.
Case 8 — Adolescent MG with ptosis and dysphagia.
16-year-old female, fluctuating ptosis, fatigable proximal weakness, dysphagia, FVC 20.
Q. EDX, plan.
Answer.
  • RNS at 3 Hz — >10 % decrement.
  • SFEMG — increased jitter ± blocking.
  • Manage as crisis — intubate proactively, IVIG / PLEX, pyridostigmine cautiously, start steroids with monitoring for transient worsening.
Case 9 — Floppy baby with maternal grip-myotonia.
Term neonate, hypotonia, club feet, polyhydramnios history. Mother shakes hands slowly with delayed release.
Q. Diagnosis, EMG in mother.
Answer.
  • Congenital DM1 (CTG repeat >1000, maternally inherited).
  • Mother's EMG — myotonic "dive-bomber" discharges.
  • Genetic DMPK testing.
  • Counsel for autosomal-dominant inheritance with anticipation.
Case 10 — Tick paralysis.
4-year-old after camping, 24 h ascending paralysis and ataxia. CSF normal.
Q. Crucial step, expected EDX.
Answer.
  • Full body skin inspection (scalp, axillae, groin, behind ears).
  • EDX — low CMAP, preserved velocity.
  • Tick removal — both diagnostic and curative.
Case 11 — SMARD1.
3-month-old, IUGR at birth, tachypnea, unilateral diaphragmatic elevation on CXR.
Q. Differential, confirmatory test.
Answer.
  • SMARD1 vs. eventration vs. acquired phrenic injury.
  • Phrenic NCS + intercostal / diaphragm EMG.
  • IGHMBP2 gene testing for confirmation.
  • Long-term ventilator support if SMARD1.
Case 12 — Steroid + NMBA myopathy.
10-year-old asthma exacerbation, intubated 10 days, paralyzed 5 days with high-dose methylprednisolone. Fails to wean.
Q. EDX, differentiator.
Answer.
  • Acute necrotizing / steroid + NMBA myopathy.
  • EDX — low CMAP, normal SNAP, myopathic MUPs ± fibrillations, low neCMAP/dmCMAP.
  • CK elevated.
  • Distinguished from CIP by normal SNAP and intact DMS response.
Case 13 — Miller-Fisher syndrome.
5-year-old develops areflexia, ataxia, ophthalmoplegia 10 days after viral illness.
Q. Diagnosis, antibody.
Answer.
  • Miller-Fisher syndrome (GBS variant).
  • Anti-GQ1b IgG positive in >90 %.
  • EDX — reduced SNAPs and absent H-reflex with preserved motor NCS.
  • Supportive ± IVIG.
Case 14 — Organophosphate poisoning.
8-year-old farm child, miosis, hypersalivation, fasciculations, respiratory failure. CMAPs show repetitive discharges after single supramaximal stimulus.
Q. Mechanism, treatment.
Answer.
  • Organophosphate — AChE inhibition with ACh persistence at NMJ.
  • Repetitive CMAPs reflect persistent ACh.
  • Atropine, pralidoxime, decontamination.
  • Avoid succinylcholine — markedly prolonged paralysis.
Case 15 — Periodic paralysis.
9-year-old, episodic limb weakness after exercise, normal between episodes, family history.
Q. Differential, EDX test.
Answer.
  • Hyper/hypo-kalemic periodic paralysis or Andersen-Tawil.
  • McManis long-exercise test — CMAP drop >40 % after prolonged exercise.
  • Confirm by genetic testing (CACNA1S, SCN4A, KCNJ2).
Case 16 — CIP vs. AIDP overlap.
11-year-old post-septic shock at day 21, persistent flaccid quadriparesis. EDX — ↓ SNAPs and ↓ CMAPs symmetrically, normal CV, EMG denervation.
Q. CIP or AIDP — features pointing to CIP.
Answer.
  • Normal velocity (no demyelination).
  • Symmetric distal axonal pattern.
  • Sepsis history; no preceding infection or autonomic features.
  • Treat as CIP — prevention bundle, rehab, no IVIG.
  • Reassess if velocity slows or F-waves disappear.
Case 17 — Phrenic palsy post Norwood.
2-month-old post-Norwood fails extubation 3×. Right hemidiaphragm elevated. Sniff test paradoxical.
Q. EDX, plication role.
Answer.
  • Phrenic NCS — absent / low CMAP on the affected side.
  • Plication if persistent at 2–4 weeks and weaning failed.
  • EDX provides objective support for surgery.
Case 18 — Mononeuritis multiplex.
14-year-old vasculitis, painful stepwise individual nerve palsies. EDX — conduction block at random sites.
Q. Pattern, mechanism.
Answer.
  • Mononeuritis multiplex — vasculitic axonal neuropathy from infarction of vasa nervorum.
  • Look for systemic vasculitis (ANCA-associated, PAN).
  • Immunosuppression; EDX documents affected nerves.
Case 19 — Acute myositis with rhabdomyolysis.
7-year-old, influenza, severe leg pain, CK 60 000, dark urine.
Q. EDX necessary?
Answer.
  • Usually not necessary — clinical + CK + viral PCR sufficient.
  • If done — small-amplitude short-duration polyphasic MUPs with fibrillations.
  • EDX adds value if presentation atypical or MG / GBS in differential.
Case 20 — MG worsened by macrolide.
MG patient receives erythromycin for pneumonia, more weak, requires intubation.
Q. Mechanism, action.
Answer.
  • Macrolides interfere with NMJ transmission.
  • Stop drug, support ventilation, treat as crisis (IVIG / PLEX).
  • Add to home/ICU avoid-list.
Case 21 — Paradoxical CMS response.
Infant improves on pyridostigmine then worsens dramatically.
Q. Likely subtype, why paradoxical.
Answer.
  • Slow-channel or DOK7 / COLQ CMS — anticholinesterases worsen these subtypes.
  • Switch — fluoxetine / quinidine (slow channel) or salbutamol / ephedrine (DOK7).
  • Subtype-specific therapy requires genetic panel.
Case 22 — Diaphragm fatigue post-ECMO.
9-year-old after VA-ECMO for meningococcal sepsis, persistent hypercapnia despite normal lungs. Diaphragm thin on ultrasound.
Q. Mechanism, role of EDX.
Answer.
  • Diaphragm disuse atrophy + CIP affecting phrenic nerve.
  • Phrenic NCS shows reduced CMAP.
  • Progressive weaning, diaphragm training, NIV bridges, nutrition.
Case 23 — Persistent denervation in AMAN.
10-year-old, 4 months post-AMAN GBS, remains quadriplegic, fibrillations on EMG, no reinnervated MUPs.
Q. Prognosis, next step.
Answer.
  • Persistent denervation without reinnervation at 4 mo = poor prognosis for that nerve.
  • Nerve transfer surgery for upper-limb function.
  • Aggressive rehab; vocational planning.
  • EDX informs family counselling.
Case 24 — Functional weakness.
15-year-old, sudden bilateral leg weakness after argument. EDX entirely normal. Reflexes present, give-way pattern.
Q. Approach.
Answer.
  • Functional / dissociative motor disorder.
  • Normal EDX + inconsistent signs (Hoover, dragging gait).
  • Multidisciplinary PT and mental health support.
  • Avoid invasive workup once diagnosis secure.
Case 25 — Post-COVID GBS.
8-year-old, 2 weeks post-COVID, areflexic ascending paralysis. EDX demyelinating.
Q. Diagnosis, management.
Answer.
  • Post-COVID AIDP.
  • IVIG 2 g/kg or PLEX; ICU monitoring for respiratory and autonomic failure.
  • Pediatric outcomes similar to other parainfectious GBS.
Case 26 — Immune-mediated necrotizing myopathy.
12-year-old, severe symmetric proximal weakness, CK 25 000, EMG abundant fibrillations + small short-duration MUPs.
Q. Differential, antibodies.
Answer.
  • Immune-mediated necrotizing myopathy.
  • Anti-HMGCR or anti-SRP antibodies.
  • High-dose steroids, IVIG, methotrexate or rituximab.
  • EDX confirms myopathic; MRI directs biopsy.
Case 27 — CIP in cardiac PICU.
5-year-old after prolonged ECMO for refractory cardiomyopathy. Weak, areflexic, ICU day 30.
Q. EDX, rehab.
Answer.
  • CIP — reduced SNAPs and CMAPs, preserved velocity, EMG denervation.
  • Aggressive PT/OT, sit-up by day 1 of stability, daily sedation interruption.
  • Tracheostomy if prolonged ventilator course.
  • Vitamin / nutrition support; partial recovery over months expected.
Case 28 — Tetanus.
8-year-old, contaminated wound, trismus, opisthotonus, reflex spasms.
Q. EDX feature distinguishing tetanus from other paralytic disorders.
Answer.
  • Continuous motor unit activity at rest.
  • Loss of normal silent period after a contraction.
  • Reflects failure of Renshaw / glycinergic inhibition.
  • Treat — metronidazole, human tetanus IG, benzodiazepines, NMBA if needed, intensive supportive care.
Case 29 — Diphtheric neuropathy.
8-year-old from a low-vaccination region, palatal paralysis at week 3 after pharyngeal pseudomembrane illness, peripheral neuropathy by week 6.
Q. Diagnosis, treatment.
Answer.
  • Diphtheric polyneuropathy — toxin demyelinates cranial nerves first, then peripheral.
  • Diphtheria antitoxin ASAP.
  • Erythromycin or penicillin to eradicate.
  • Vaccinate household contacts.
Case 30 — Serial CMAP tracking ICU recovery.
PICU 6-week stay; serial CMAP amplitudes from peroneal nerve declining over 2 weeks despite improving sedation scores.
Q. Interpretation, action.
Answer.
  • Progressive axonal loss — evolving CIP / CIM.
  • Tighten prevention bundle (mobilization, sedation interruption, glycemia, NMBA review).
  • Evaluate for ongoing sepsis or steroid burden.
  • Plan slower weaning strategy with rehab integration.

③ ICU management steps

  • Stabilize airway / ventilation; protect from autonomic instability.
  • Warm limb to 32 °C, wash out NMBA, then perform bedside NCS / EMG.
  • Match SNAP–CMAP–EMG pattern to the 4-bucket framework.
  • Pull the trigger on treatable causes — IVIG / PLEX for GBS, BabyBIG for infant botulism, anti-AChE / IVIG for MG.
  • Run the ICU-AW prevention bundle from day 1.
  • Plan rehab and follow-up EDX in 2–4 weeks; serial CMAP amplitude is the recovery tracker.

④ Critical thresholds

  • FVC <20 mL/kg → intubate.
  • MIP not more negative than –30 cmH₂O → intubate.
  • RNS decrement >10 % @ 3 Hz → postsynaptic NMJ.
  • RNS increment >60–100 % @ 50 Hz → presynaptic NMJ → BabyBIG if infant.
  • Skin temperature <32 °C → warm before interpreting velocity.
  • neCMAP/dmCMAP <0.5 → muscle inexcitability → CIM.

⑤ Red flags

  • Asymmetric flaccid paralysis with normal SNAPs → AFM until proven otherwise.
  • Bulbar weakness with autonomic features → GBS, MG, or botulism — escalate.
  • "Normal EDX" in a ventilator-dependent infant → SFEMG mandatory.
  • Foot drop after surgery → peroneal compression; document early.
  • New weakness on macrolide / aminoglycoside in known MG → stop the drug.

⑥ Exam pearls

🔥🔥🔥 Localize to AHC / nerve / NMJ / muscle BEFORE naming the disease.
🔥🔥🔥 Normal SNAP + low CMAP + denervation = AHC.
🔥🔥🔥 BabyBIG empirically — do not wait for stool toxin.
🔥🔥 F-waves are the earliest demyelination marker in AIDP.
🔥🔥 SFEMG of orbicularis oculi catches what RNS misses.
🔥 Always warm the limb before reading conduction velocity.
