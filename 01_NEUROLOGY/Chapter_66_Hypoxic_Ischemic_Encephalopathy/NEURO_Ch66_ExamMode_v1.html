Chapter 66 — Hypoxic-Ischemic Encephalopathy — EXAM MODE

Rogers' Textbook of Pediatric Intensive Care, Chapter 66 (Fink, Manole, Herrmann, Clark). Classification: DISEASE / ICU. Adaptive distribution: 40 SAQs + 40 PICU cases. EXAM MODE: answers hidden. Time yourself in Blocks A and B.

How to use:
  • Block A — 10 timed mock RCPSC SAQs (12 minutes).
  • Block B — 5 sequential oral cases (40 minutes). Self-grade with Block E rubric.
  • Block C — 30 randomized SAQs (drill mode).
  • Block D — 35 randomized PICU cases (drill mode).
  • Block E — examiner feedback rubric.

Block A — Mock RCPSC SAQ (10 questions, 12 minutes)

A1. List the four phases of cerebral blood flow after cardiac arrest and explain why each matters for management.

Show answer
  • No-reflow (immediate post-ROSC): microvascular failure from endothelial swelling, leukocyte plugging, microthrombi.
  • Hyperemia (~15 min): reactive high CBF risking edema and hemorrhagic conversion.
  • Delayed hypoperfusion (~30 min to 24 h): sustained low CBF mismatched with metabolic demand — defend MAP here to maintain CPP.
  • Recovery (≥24 h): CBF returning toward baseline; ongoing risk of secondary insult.
  • Duration of delayed hypoperfusion correlates with severity of injury in animal models.

A2. Name the canonical selectively vulnerable brain regions in HIE.

Show answer
  • Hippocampal CA1 region (Sommer sector).
  • Cortical layers 3 and 5 (pyramidal neurons).
  • Basal ganglia (caudate and putamen).
  • Cerebellar Purkinje cells.
  • Thalamus (lateral and ventral nuclei).
  • Watershed (border) zones in hypotension-driven injury.

A3. An 8-year-old after out-of-hospital cardiac arrest is comatose 30 min after ROSC. Outline the post-resuscitation bundle per the AHA 2019 scientific statement.

Show answer
  • SpO₂ 94–99%; avoid hyperoxia and hypoxia.
  • PaCO₂ 35–45 mm Hg; avoid prophylactic hyperventilation.
  • MAP ≥ 5th percentile for age; avoid hypotension.
  • TTM 32–34°C or 36–37.5°C for ≥72 h; avoid fever ≥38°C for at least 5 days.
  • Glucose 3.3–10 mmol/L.
  • cEEG ≥24 h; treat clinical and electrographic seizures.
  • MRI 3–7 days post-arrest.
  • Multimodal prognostication delayed to ≥72 h with confounders managed.

A4. Explain the THAPCA-OH and THAPCA-IH trials.

Show answer
  • THAPCA-OH (Moler NEJM 2015) — pediatric out-of-hospital arrest; hypothermia 33°C vs normothermia 36.8°C for 48 h; no significant difference in 1-year survival with favorable function.
  • THAPCA-IH (Moler NEJM 2017) — in-hospital arrest cohort; similar negative result.
  • Both strategies superior to allowing fever — temperature management bundle, not specifically hypothermia, is the key.
  • Either approach is reasonable per AHA 2019.

A5. A neonate at 38 weeks gestation has Sarnat II HIE at 4 hours of life. Outline therapeutic hypothermia eligibility and protocol.

Show answer
  • Eligibility: ≥36 weeks gestation, ≤6 h postnatal age, evidence of perinatal hypoxic-ischemic event (acidosis, low Apgar, need for resuscitation), moderate-severe encephalopathy (Sarnat II–III).
  • Cool to 33.5°C × 72 h.
  • Slow rewarming 0.25–0.5°C/h.
  • Continue strict normothermia 36–37.5°C through 5 days total.
  • Sedation, seizure management, cEEG, MRI at days 4–7.
  • Evidence: CoolCap, NICHD, TOBY trials; ILCOR 2020 endorsement.

A6. Discuss why both hyperoxia and prophylactic hyperventilation should be avoided post-ROSC.

Show answer
  • Hyperoxia drives ROS production during reperfusion, worsening oxidative injury to vulnerable neurons.
  • Target SpO₂ 94–99%; wean FiO₂ promptly.
  • Hyperventilation lowers PaCO₂ → cerebral vasoconstriction → reduced CBF.
  • Worsens the delayed hypoperfusion phase already underway.
  • Target PaCO₂ 35–45 mm Hg (normocapnia); brief hyperventilation only as rescue for impending herniation.

A7. Outline a multimodal approach to pediatric HIE prognostication at 72 hours.

Show answer
  • Clinical exam off sedation: pupillary, corneal, motor responses; status myoclonus history.
  • Continuous EEG: background, reactivity, malignant patterns (burst suppression, isoelectric, refractory SE).
  • MRI 3–7 days: DWI restriction in selectively vulnerable regions, loss of grey-white differentiation, basal ganglia/thalamus injury.
  • SSEPs: bilateral absent N20 cortical response is a strong predictor (extrapolated from adults).
  • Biomarkers (NSE, S100B, GFAP) — investigational.
  • No single marker definitive — integrate; account for sedation, TTM, hemodynamic confounders.
  • Multidisciplinary family meeting with palliative care.

A8. A 9-year-old is post-arrest day 7 with no brainstem reflexes and no respiratory drive. Outline the pediatric brain death determination process.

Show answer
  • Prerequisites: known irreversible cause; correction of confounders (sedation, paralytics, hypothermia rewarmed to ≥36°C, BP adequate, electrolytes corrected).
  • Two independent clinical exams by qualified physicians separated by required interval (24 h for term–30 days; 12 h for >30 days per Nakagawa 2011 / Greer 2023).
  • Each exam: documented coma, absent brainstem reflexes (pupillary, corneal, oculocephalic/oculovestibular, gag, cough), apnea test.
  • Apnea test: PaCO₂ rises to ≥60 mm Hg or 20 mm Hg above baseline with no respiratory drive.
  • Ancillary studies if exam confounded (cerebral perfusion scan, CTA, EEG).
  • ODO involvement for organ donation discussion, separate from determination team.

A9. Why is post-ROSC hypotension associated with worse outcomes?

Show answer
  • Cerebral autoregulation is impaired after HIE.
  • CPP becomes pressure-passive; low MAP directly reduces CBF.
  • Delayed hypoperfusion phase compounds the injury.
  • Topjian 2018 CCM and JAMA Peds — each hypotension episode independently linked to worse survival to discharge with favorable outcome.
  • Management: aggressive MAP support with volume, vasopressors, inotropes for myocardial stunning.

A10. Explain why erythropoietin is NOT recommended as a routine adjunct to therapeutic hypothermia in neonatal HIE.

Show answer
  • HEAL trial (Wu NEJM 2022) — RCT of EPO + hypothermia vs placebo + hypothermia.
  • No reduction in death or neurodevelopmental impairment.
  • Trial high quality, multicenter, adequately powered.
  • EPO removed from routine recommendation.
  • Research continues in older pediatric cohorts and post-arrest contexts.

Block B — Mock RCPSC Oral Cases (5 sequential, 40 minutes)

B1. A 7-year-old previously well child arrives in the PICU after out-of-hospital cardiac arrest. Witnessed collapse, bystander CPR; ROSC achieved at 18 min by EMS. On arrival: intubated, GCS 3, BP 80/45, HR 110, SpO₂ 99% on FiO₂ 1.0, temp 36.4°C.

B1a — First-hour priorities.
  • ABCs: confirm tube placement, capnography, secure ventilation.
  • Wean FiO₂ to target SpO₂ 94–99%.
  • Adjust ventilation to PaCO₂ 35–45 mm Hg.
  • IV access; bedside glucose; full labs (CBC, electrolytes, glucose, lactate, blood gas, troponin, coags).
  • Vasopressor (epinephrine or norepinephrine) to MAP ≥ age-appropriate threshold.
  • Treat reversible causes (Hs and Ts) — ECG, echo, sepsis workup as indicated.
  • Initiate TTM strategy decision (33°C vs 36–37.5°C) and start within 6 h.
  • Begin cEEG ASAP.
B1b — Choose between 33°C hypothermia and 36–37.5°C normothermia.
  • Per THAPCA-OH and THAPCA-IH: both strategies non-inferior; the goal is fever avoidance.
  • Institutional protocol typically dictates.
  • Hypothermia advantages: theoretical neuroprotection at low core temperature.
  • Normothermia advantages: simpler logistics, fewer shivering and coagulopathy issues.
  • Either choice — maintain for ≥72 h with slow rewarming; strict normothermia thereafter for 5 days.
B1c — Hour 6: cEEG shows electrographic seizures. Manage.
  • Initiate ASM tier: lorazepam 0.1 mg/kg IV; load levetiracetam 40–60 mg/kg IV.
  • Add fosphenytoin 20 mg PE/kg if persistent.
  • If refractory: anesthetic infusion (midazolam 0.05–2 mg/kg/h); continue cEEG to titrate.
  • Search for electrolyte abnormalities, glucose extremes, fever.
  • Continue ≥24 h cEEG.
B1d — Day 4: family asks about prognosis. Off sedation, GCS 5, MRI shows diffuse cortical + basal ganglia DWI restriction. Approach.
  • Integrate clinical exam (off sedation), cEEG (background, reactivity), MRI findings, SSEPs if available.
  • This combination (poor exam + extensive DWI + ≥72 h) supports poor prognostic assessment.
  • Multidisciplinary family meeting with palliative care, social work, chaplaincy.
  • Honest, compassionate communication; acknowledge remaining uncertainty.
  • Discuss goals of care: tracheostomy/PEG vs WLST.
  • Allow time and revisit; document discussions.
B1e — Family decides on WLST. Outline comfort care.
  • Multidisciplinary support: palliative care, social work, chaplaincy.
  • Withdraw vasopressors, paralytics, life-sustaining interventions per family wishes.
  • Continue ASMs to prevent seizure-related distress.
  • Pain and anxiety control (opioids, benzodiazepines).
  • Family presence; allow rituals; bereavement support.
  • Organ donation consideration through ODO (separate from medical team).
  • If donation declined, comfort care continues until death.

B2. A term newborn delivered after prolonged fetal distress has cord pH 6.8, Apgar 1 at 5 min, requiring resuscitation. By 1 hour of life: lethargic, hypotonic, weak reflexes, GCS-equivalent low. Brought to NICU.

B2a — Diagnosis and immediate management.
  • Moderate–severe neonatal HIE (Sarnat II likely; need full exam).
  • Stabilize: airway, ventilation, hemodynamics, normoglycemia.
  • Confirm eligibility for cooling: ≥36 wk, ≤6 h postnatal age, perinatal event, moderate-severe encephalopathy.
  • Initiate therapeutic hypothermia 33.5°C × 72 h within 6 h of life.
  • aEEG/cEEG; treat seizures (phenobarbital 20 mg/kg first-line in neonates).
  • MRI between days 4–7 for prognostic data.
B2b — Day 1 develops seizures. Treatment?
  • Phenobarbital 20 mg/kg IV first-line; can repeat 10 mg/kg if seizures persist.
  • Avoid benzodiazepines as first-line in neonates (GABA may be excitatory due to NKCC1 dominance).
  • Second-line: levetiracetam 40–60 mg/kg IV or fosphenytoin 20 mg PE/kg.
  • If still refractory: midazolam infusion.
  • Empiric pyridoxine 100 mg IV trial if persistent.
  • Continuous cEEG throughout cooling.
B2c — Family asks about role of erythropoietin.
  • HEAL trial (Wu NEJM 2022) demonstrated no benefit of EPO + hypothermia vs placebo + hypothermia for neonatal HIE.
  • EPO is NOT recommended as routine adjunct.
  • Therapeutic hypothermia remains the only proven neuroprotective intervention.
  • Other adjuncts (melatonin, allopurinol, xenon, stem cells) remain investigational.
B2d — MRI on day 5 shows extensive basal ganglia and thalamic injury. Prognostic discussion.
  • Basal ganglia and thalamic pattern in neonatal HIE is associated with severe motor disability (often dystonic cerebral palsy).
  • Cognitive outcomes variable.
  • Honest discussion with family acknowledging uncertainty.
  • Early intervention program enrollment.
  • Long-term follow-up: neurology, developmental pediatrics, rehab.
  • Bayley assessment at 18–24 months for definitive neurodevelopmental classification.

B3. A 4-year-old submerged in a pool for ~15 minutes. EMS achieved ROSC after 25 min CPR. On arrival: comatose, GCS 3, hypothermic at 32°C, hyperkalemic 6.5, severe acidosis pH 6.95.

B3a — Initial resuscitation.
  • ABCs; secure airway; lung-protective ventilation (anticipate ARDS).
  • Treat hyperkalemia: calcium gluconate, insulin/glucose, bicarbonate, β-agonist.
  • Correct acidosis with adequate ventilation and perfusion; cautious bicarbonate if severe.
  • Volume resuscitation; vasopressors for MAP support.
  • Initial hypothermia at 32°C — manage carefully; do not aggressively rewarm in initial hour given drowning-related hypothermia may be neuroprotective.
  • cEEG initiation; treat seizures.
B3b — TTM decision at 6 h.
  • Given drowning hypothermia at 32°C, can maintain at 32–34°C × 72 h per TTM protocol.
  • Alternatively, gradually warm to 36–37.5°C for normothermia strategy.
  • Either is reasonable per THAPCA data.
  • Avoid fever for ≥5 days total.
  • cEEG continuously.
B3c — Develops ARDS day 2 with PaO₂/FiO₂ 100.
  • Lung-protective ventilation: tidal volume 5–6 mL/kg ideal body weight; PEEP titration.
  • Permissive hypercapnia within safe range; avoid extremes that worsen brain injury.
  • Prone positioning if refractory hypoxemia.
  • Consider ECMO if persistent severe ARDS.
  • Maintain MAP for cerebral perfusion despite ventilation strategies.
B3d — Day 7 prognosis discussion.
  • Drowning post-arrest HIE typically poor when CPR >25 min and submersion >10 min.
  • Integrate clinical exam, cEEG, MRI.
  • Cold-water submersion may modify prognosis somewhat; reassess serially.
  • Multidisciplinary family meeting with palliative care.
  • Discuss goals of care with realistic outcome expectations.

B4. A 6-year-old after cardiac surgery for tetralogy of Fallot suffers intraoperative arrest with 18 min CPR before ROSC. Now in CICU.

B4a — Initial post-ROSC management.
  • Post-arrest bundle: SpO₂ 94–99%, normocapnia, MAP support.
  • Inotropic and chronotropic support for myocardial stunning (epi + milrinone).
  • Echo for residual cardiac lesions or RV failure.
  • Consider TTM (33°C or 36–37.5°C); coordinate with surgical team for bleeding risk.
  • cEEG; treat seizures.
  • MRI when stable for prognostic information.
B4b — Why is post-cardiac-surgery HIE common?
  • Cardiopulmonary bypass effects: hypoperfusion, embolic microparticles, inflammation, hypothermia/rewarming injury.
  • Pre-existing cyanotic heart disease may compound vulnerability.
  • Watershed and basal ganglia injury common patterns.
  • Mitigation: improved bypass techniques, controlled rewarming, near-infrared spectroscopy monitoring.
B4c — Day 3 develops worsening hemodynamics, lactate rising.
  • Echo for cardiac function and tamponade.
  • Consider ECMO for refractory cardiogenic shock.
  • Sepsis workup; broad-spectrum antibiotics if febrile or unstable.
  • Address surgical complications (residual VSD, RVOT obstruction).
  • Maintain neuroprotective measures throughout.
B4d — Family asks about long-term neurologic outcome.
  • Congenital heart disease + post-bypass arrest carries significant neurodevelopmental risk.
  • Reassess at 72 h with multimodal data.
  • Long-term: cardiac neurodevelopmental follow-up program.
  • Early intervention if deficits emerge.
  • Honest communication with cardiology team.

B5. A 14-year-old after fentanyl overdose with respiratory arrest. ROSC after naloxone + 6 min CPR. Now in PICU intubated, GCS 6.

B5a — Initial management priorities.
  • Post-arrest bundle: SpO₂ 94–99%, normocapnia, MAP support.
  • Naloxone infusion if respiratory depression recurs (long-acting opioids).
  • Continue ventilation until reliable spontaneous respiration.
  • Drug screen, salicylate, acetaminophen, ethanol panel.
  • ECG for arrhythmia.
  • cEEG within hours; treat seizures.
  • TTM (33°C or 36–37.5°C) × 72 h.
B5b — Develops shivering in TTM at 33°C.
  • Increase sedation (fentanyl, midazolam, dexmedetomidine).
  • Add neuromuscular blockade (rocuronium infusion) if shivering uncontrolled.
  • Optimize Mg, electrolytes.
  • Maintain cEEG (paralyzed patient cannot show clinical seizures).
B5c — Day 2 awakens following commands. Plan?
  • Good neurologic recovery — encouraging sign.
  • Continue normothermia.
  • Wean sedation; assess for extubation when criteria met.
  • Mental health and addiction services consult.
  • Plan for outpatient addiction follow-up; naloxone home kit; counseling.
  • Family meeting addressing substance use.
B5d — Discharge planning and prevention of recurrence.
  • Addiction medicine consult; treatment program enrollment.
  • Naloxone home kits with family education.
  • School and mental health support.
  • Pediatric primary care follow-up.
  • If neurologic deficits: neuropsychology, rehab.
  • Consider toxicology screen for other substances.

Block C — Randomized SAQ Drill (30 questions)

C1. Define hypoxic-ischemic encephalopathy.

Show answer
  • Brain injury from hypoxia and/or ischemia with or without reperfusion.
  • Outcomes determined by age, developmental stage, regional vulnerability, etiology, and post-arrest care quality.

C2. Four CBF phases post-ROSC.

Show answer
  • No-reflow; hyperemia; delayed hypoperfusion; recovery.
  • Depth and duration of delayed hypoperfusion correlate with severity.

C3. Selectively vulnerable brain regions in HIE.

Show answer
  • CA1 hippocampus, cortical layers 3 and 5, basal ganglia, cerebellar Purkinje cells, thalamus, watershed zones.

C4. Main cell death pathways in HIE.

Show answer
  • Excitotoxicity (glutamate, NMDA), calcium overload, mitochondrial collapse, ROS/RNS, inflammation, apoptosis (caspase), necroptosis (RIPK1/3/MLKL).

C5. SpO₂ target post-ROSC.

Show answer
  • 94–99%; avoid hypoxia and hyperoxia.

C6. PaCO₂ target post-ROSC.

Show answer
  • 35–45 mm Hg (normocapnia).
  • Avoid prophylactic hyperventilation.

C7. MAP target post-pediatric arrest.

Show answer
  • ≥5th percentile for age.
  • Avoid hypotension (Topjian 2018 — each episode worsens outcome).

C8. THAPCA-OH main finding.

Show answer
  • Hypothermia 33°C vs normothermia 36.8°C — no significant difference in 1-y favorable outcome.
  • Both superior to allowing fever.

C9. TTM duration post-pediatric arrest.

Show answer
  • ≥72 h, then strict normothermia for ≥5 days total.

C10. Glucose target post-arrest.

Show answer
  • 3.3–10 mmol/L.
  • Avoid extremes; avoid tight glycemic control.

C11. cEEG indication post-arrest.

Show answer
  • All comatose pediatric post-arrest patients (AHA 2019).
  • ≥24 h minimum; 30–47% have post-arrest seizures.

C12. Neonatal HIE cooling eligibility.

Show answer
  • ≥36 wk gestation, ≤6 h postnatal age.
  • Perinatal hypoxic-ischemic event evidence.
  • Moderate–severe encephalopathy (Sarnat II–III).

C13. Neonatal HIE cooling protocol.

Show answer
  • 33.5°C × 72 h.
  • Slow rewarming 0.25–0.5°C/h.
  • Continue normothermia post-rewarming.

C14. Sarnat staging — what stage is eligible for cooling?

Show answer
  • Sarnat II (moderate) and Sarnat III (severe).
  • Sarnat I (mild) — not eligible.

C15. HEAL trial result.

Show answer
  • Wu NEJM 2022 — EPO + hypothermia vs placebo + hypothermia in neonatal HIE.
  • No reduction in death or neurodevelopmental impairment.
  • EPO not recommended routine adjunct.

C16. Why don't we cool preterm neonates <36 weeks?

Show answer
  • Higher risk of IVH and other complications.
  • Limited evidence of benefit.
  • Laptook 2017 — risk > benefit in preterm.

C17. First-line ASM in neonatal SE.

Show answer
  • Phenobarbital 20 mg/kg IV.
  • Repeat 10 mg/kg if persistent.
  • BZDs less reliable due to immature GABA system.

C18. Multimodal prognostication tools.

Show answer
  • Clinical exam off sedation.
  • cEEG.
  • MRI 3–7 days.
  • SSEPs (bilateral absent N20).
  • Biomarkers (NSE, S100B — investigational).
  • Defer ≥72 h.

C19. EEG patterns predicting poor outcome.

Show answer
  • Isoelectric / low-voltage background.
  • Burst suppression >24 h.
  • Refractory status epilepticus.
  • Lack of reactivity; no sleep architecture.
  • Alpha coma pattern.

C20. MRI features predicting poor outcome.

Show answer
  • Diffuse cortical DWI restriction.
  • Basal ganglia + thalamic involvement.
  • Loss of grey-white differentiation.
  • Diffuse cerebral edema.

C21. Pediatric brain death exam components.

Show answer
  • Coma with known irreversible cause.
  • Absent brainstem reflexes (pupillary, corneal, oculocephalic/oculovestibular, gag, cough).
  • Apnea test (PaCO₂ ≥60 or +20 from baseline with no respiratory drive).
  • Two independent exams separated per age.

C22. Brain death observation interval by age (Nakagawa 2011).

Show answer
  • Term newborn to 30 days: 24 h.
  • >30 days to 18 years: 12 h.

C23. Ancillary tests for pediatric brain death.

Show answer
  • Cerebral perfusion scan.
  • CT angiography.
  • EEG (electrocerebral silence).
  • Transcranial Doppler (variable utility).

C24. Drowning HIE pearls.

Show answer
  • Outcome relates to submersion time and time to BLS.
  • Cold-water submersion may permit extended resuscitation.
  • Watch for ARDS, electrolyte imbalance, aspiration.

C25. CO poisoning treatment.

Show answer
  • 100% O₂ via non-rebreather.
  • Hyperbaric O₂ for severe (LOC, GCS <15, COHb >25%, pregnancy, ischemia).
  • Watch for delayed neuropsychiatric syndrome.

C26. Cyanide poisoning treatment.

Show answer
  • Hydroxocobalamin 70 mg/kg IV (max 5 g).
  • Or sodium thiosulfate.
  • Consider in fire victims with coma and acidosis.

C27. ECPR indications in pediatrics.

Show answer
  • Witnessed in-hospital arrest with refractory rhythm and reversible cause.
  • Cardiac surgery patients post-bypass.
  • Severe myocarditis, sudden severe respiratory failure.
  • AHA PALS 2020 supports consideration in selected cases.

C28. Pediatric Cerebral Performance Category (PCPC).

Show answer
  • Global functional outcome scale.
  • 1 = normal, 2 = mild disability, 3 = moderate, 4 = severe, 5 = coma/vegetative, 6 = brain death.
  • Used in pediatric arrest research.

C29. Status myoclonus <24 h post-arrest — significance.

Show answer
  • Strong poor-prognostic marker.
  • Treat (lev, propofol, midazolam) — do not equate with futility.
  • Integrate with multimodal assessment.

C30. Endogenous neuroprotective mechanisms.

Show answer
  • Heat shock proteins, HIF-1α, BDNF/NGF, anti-oxidant systems.
  • Rationale for ischemic preconditioning research.
