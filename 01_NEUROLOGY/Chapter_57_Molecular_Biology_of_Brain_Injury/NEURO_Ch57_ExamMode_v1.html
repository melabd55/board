Mock RCPSC Block A — 10 SAQs (timed)

⏱ 30 min total · 3 min per SAQ
SAQ A-1
Define the two types of secondary brain injury.
Show Answer
  • Type 1 — endogenous cascades (excitotoxicity, oxidative stress, apoptosis, inflammation) unfolding over hours to weeks.
  • Type 2 — secondary insults (hypotension, hypoxia, hyperthermia, hyper-/hypoglycemia, hypocapnia, raised ICP, seizures).
  • Type 2 prevention has the highest immediate ICU impact.
SAQ A-2
Outline the CBF ischemic thresholds.
Show Answer
  • ~50 mL/100 g/min — normal.
  • ~30 — protein synthesis failure.
  • ~20 — electrical silence.
  • ~10 — ion pump failure and cell death.
  • Penumbra — salvageable tissue between functional silence and irreversible damage.
SAQ A-3
Outline the intrinsic apoptosis pathway.
Show Answer
  • Bax/Bak insert into outer mitochondrial membrane.
  • Cytochrome-c release.
  • Cyt-c + dATP + Apaf-1 → apoptosome → caspase-9.
  • Caspase-9 → caspase-3 → apoptosis.
  • Inhibited by Bcl-2, Bcl-xL.
SAQ A-4
Outline the excitotoxic cascade.
Show Answer
  • Energy failure → Na⁺/K⁺ ATPase failure → depolarization.
  • Glutamate released into synapse.
  • NMDA-receptor opening → Ca²⁺ influx.
  • Ca²⁺ activates proteases, lipases, endonucleases.
  • Mitochondrial Ca²⁺ overload → mPTP → cyt-c release → apoptosis or necrosis.
SAQ A-5
List six brain edema mechanisms.
Show Answer
  • Cellular (cytotoxic).
  • Ionic (BBB intact, Na⁺ Cl⁻ entry).
  • Vasogenic (BBB breakdown).
  • Osmolar (necrotic tissue load).
  • Glymphatic.
  • Hyperemic (increased CBV).
SAQ A-6
What is SUR1/TRPM4 in brain edema?
Show Answer
  • Sulfonylurea receptor 1 / TRPM4 channel.
  • Upregulated after injury.
  • Mediates ionic edema via Na⁺ entry.
  • Target of glibenclamide (BIIB093) — investigational for stroke and TBI.
SAQ A-7
Why is hyperthermia harmful after brain injury?
Show Answer
  • Increases cerebral metabolic rate.
  • Increases glutamate release.
  • Increases oxidative stress.
  • Disrupts BBB.
  • Increases inflammation.
  • Target normothermia 36.0–37.5 °C; aggressively treat fever.
SAQ A-8
Outline THAPCA trial findings.
Show Answer
  • Pediatric in-hospital + out-of-hospital cardiac arrest.
  • Hypothermia 33 °C × 48 h vs. targeted normothermia 36.8 °C × 5 days.
  • No significant improvement in survival with good function with hypothermia.
  • Aggressive fever avoidance is now pediatric post-arrest standard.
SAQ A-9
CRASH-3 trial summary.
Show Answer
  • Tranexamic acid within 3 h of TBI reduces death in mild-to-moderate cases.
  • Mechanism — antifibrinolytic; reduces hematoma expansion.
  • No benefit beyond 3 h or in severe injury with bilateral fixed pupils.
  • Pediatric extrapolation supported.
SAQ A-10
Why is tight glucose control avoided in pediatric brain injury?
Show Answer
  • Hypoglycemia limits substrate and is more harmful than mild hyperglycemia.
  • NICE-SUGAR / GIST-UK adult evidence.
  • Target 5.5–10 mmol/L (100–180 mg/dL).
  • Avoid >180 mg/dL and <80 mg/dL.

Mock RCPSC Block B — 5 Sequential ICU Cases

⏱ 30 min total · 6 min per case
CASE B-1
8-year-old after MVC, GCS 6, ICP 25 mmHg on sedation and head-of-bed elevation.
Q. Management cascade.
Show Answer
  • Maintain age-appropriate CPP.
  • Hypertonic saline (3 % continuous or 23.4 % bolus).
  • Aggressive normothermia.
  • Continuous EEG.
  • Avoid sustained hyperventilation.
  • If refractory — escalate hyperosmolar therapy; consider decompressive craniectomy.
CASE B-2
6-year-old after drowning, ROSC at 25 min, intubated and sedated.
Q. Temperature management.
Show Answer
  • Targeted normothermia 36.0–37.5 °C (THAPCA).
  • Aggressive fever avoidance.
  • Continuous EEG.
  • Avoid hypotension, hypoxia, hyperoxia, hypocapnia.
  • Avoid tight glucose control.
CASE B-3
10-year-old with moderate TBI (GCS 10), arrived 1.5 h after MVC. CT shows small contusions.
Q. Tranexamic acid use?
Show Answer
  • Yes — within 3 h, mild-to-moderate severity.
  • CRASH-3 supports TXA reduces death.
  • Pediatric extrapolation supported.
  • Avoid in severe injury (GCS 3) with bilateral fixed dilated pupils.
CASE B-4
3-year-old with pneumococcal meningitis develops focal seizures.
Q. Mechanism and management.
Show Answer
  • Pneumolysin → selective dentate hilar neuron vulnerability.
  • Pyroptosis and inflammatory cell death.
  • Treat seizures (benzodiazepine + fosphenytoin / levetiracetam).
  • Continue antibiotics + dexamethasone.
  • Continuous EEG.
  • Long-term — hippocampal sclerosis and temporal lobe epilepsy risk.
CASE B-5
Toddler with severe TBI develops diffuse cerebral edema on CT.
Q. Mechanism and management.
Show Answer
  • Mixed cytotoxic + ionic + vasogenic edema.
  • Hypertonic saline 3 % continuous + 23.4 % boluses.
  • Head-of-bed 30°, midline.
  • Sedation + analgesia; consider NMBA.
  • Normothermia; avoid hypocapnia.
  • Surgical decompression if refractory.

Block C — Remaining SAQs (randomized)

Q C-1
List five regulated necrosis pathways.
Show Answer
  • Apoptosis (intrinsic and extrinsic).
  • Necroptosis (RIPK1/RIPK3/MLKL).
  • Ferroptosis (iron + lipid peroxidation; GPX4).
  • Pyroptosis (NLRP3 → caspase-1 → gasdermin D).
  • Parthanatos (PARP overactivation).
  • Autophagy.
Q C-2
Describe ferroptosis.
Show Answer
  • Iron-dependent lipid peroxidation.
  • GPX4 normally prevents this.
  • Failure → polyunsaturated fatty acid oxidation → membrane disruption.
  • Relevant to TBI, ICH, HIE, stroke.
  • Inhibitors — ferrostatin-1, liproxstatin-1, deferoxamine.
Q C-3
List four endogenous neuroprotectants.
Show Answer
  • Adenosine — inhibits glutamate release.
  • Heat shock proteins (Hsp70, Hsp90).
  • Bcl-2 family anti-apoptotic proteins.
  • Erythropoietin.
  • Hypothermia (induced).
Q C-4
Outline secondary insults that worsen outcome.
Show Answer
  • Hypotension.
  • Hypoxemia.
  • Hyperthermia.
  • Hyper-/hypoglycemia.
  • Hypocapnia (or extreme hypercapnia).
  • Seizures.
  • Raised ICP.
Q C-5
Describe selective neuronal vulnerability.
Show Answer
  • Hippocampal CA1, dentate hilar neurons.
  • Basal ganglia.
  • Cerebellar Purkinje cells.
  • Watershed cortex (parieto-occipital).
  • Thalamus.
  • Mechanisms — high NMDA density, low antioxidant capacity, watershed perfusion.
Q C-6
Describe necroptosis.
Show Answer
  • RIPK1/RIPK3/MLKL programmed necrosis.
  • Triggered by TNF when caspase-8 is blocked.
  • MLKL phosphorylation → membrane pore.
  • Inflammatory cell death.
  • Inhibitor — necrostatin-1.
Q C-7
Outline pyroptosis.
Show Answer
  • NLRP3 inflammasome activation.
  • Caspase-1 cleaves gasdermin D → membrane pore.
  • IL-1β / IL-18 release.
  • Driver of inflammatory cell death after CNS infection.
  • Therapeutic targets — anakinra, NLRP3 inhibitors.
Q C-8
Parthanatos mechanism.
Show Answer
  • DNA damage → PARP-1 activation.
  • Excessive PARP depletes NAD⁺ and ATP.
  • AIF translocates to nucleus.
  • Chromatinolysis.
  • PARP inhibitors investigational.
Q C-9
Apoptosis-inducing factor (AIF) pathway.
Show Answer
  • AIF released from mitochondria.
  • Translocates to nucleus → chromatinolysis.
  • Caspase-independent.
Q C-10
ER stress apoptosis pathway.
Show Answer
  • Misfolded protein accumulation triggers UPR.
  • PERK, IRE1α, ATF6 signaling.
  • Prolonged stress → CHOP → caspase-12.
  • Apoptosis if chronic.
Q C-11
Outline neuroinflammation cascade.
Show Answer
  • DAMPs (HMGB1, S100B, HSPs) released from damaged cells.
  • Microglial activation via TLR2/TLR4, RAGE.
  • Astrocyte reactivity.
  • Cytokines — TNF-α, IL-1β, IL-6, IL-10.
  • Peripheral immune cells cross disrupted BBB.
  • Janus-faced — harmful early; may support repair later.
Q C-12
Axonal injury cascade.
Show Answer
  • Primary stretch / shearing.
  • Secondary axotomy — Ca²⁺ influx, mitochondrial dysfunction, cytoskeletal disruption.
  • Wallerian degeneration of distal axon.
  • Oligodendrocyte injury → demyelination.
Q C-13
Pre-oligodendrocyte vulnerability in preterm PVL.
Show Answer
  • Pre-oligodendrocyte highly vulnerable to glutamate excitotoxicity and oxidative stress.
  • Limited antioxidant defense.
  • Periventricular white matter — watershed vascular territory.
  • Periventricular leukomalacia results.
Q C-14
Hypertonic saline mechanism for cerebral edema.
Show Answer
  • Osmotic gradient draws water from brain across intact BBB.
  • 3 % continuous or 23.4 % bolus.
  • Less rebound vs. mannitol.
  • Maintains intravascular volume.
  • Monitor Na and osmolality; avoid rapid sodium correction.
Q C-15
Glibenclamide rationale for cerebral edema.
Show Answer
  • Blocks SUR1/TRPM4 channel upregulated after injury.
  • Reduces ionic edema.
  • GAMES-RP adult trial — reduced edema in malignant large hemispheric stroke.
  • BIIB093 (IV glibenclamide) in development.
Q C-16
Cyclosporine A rationale in brain injury.
Show Answer
  • Inhibits mPTP.
  • Reduces cyt-c release and apoptosis.
  • Animal models show benefit; pediatric trials investigational.
Q C-17
CTE — what is it?
Show Answer
  • Chronic traumatic encephalopathy.
  • Linked to repetitive sub-concussive and concussive head impacts.
  • p-tau pathology in perivascular distribution at sulcus depths.
  • Currently autopsy diagnosis.
  • Drives return-to-play protocols in youth contact sports.
Q C-18
Aquaporin-4 role in brain edema.
Show Answer
  • Astrocyte water channel at end-feet.
  • Promotes cytotoxic edema and resolves vasogenic edema.
  • Target of anti-AQP4 antibody in NMOSD.
Q C-19
V1a receptor in brain edema.
Show Answer
  • Vasopressin 1a receptor contributes to edema after TBI / stroke.
  • Antagonists (conivaptan) reduce edema in animal models.
  • Clinical relevance in hyponatremia management.
Q C-20
Hyperoxia caution in brain injury.
Show Answer
  • Sustained PaO₂ >300 mmHg may increase oxidative injury.
  • Free radical generation worsens secondary damage.
  • Target PaO₂ 80–100 mmHg.
  • High FiO₂ acceptable acutely; titrate down once stable.
Q C-21
Heightened excitotoxicity in developing brain.
Show Answer
  • Higher NMDA receptor density.
  • NR2B-rich subunit (longer Ca²⁺ flux).
  • Reduced inhibitory tone before GABA switch.
  • Immature antioxidant defenses.
  • Higher synaptic metabolic demand.
Q C-22
Heat shock protein role.
Show Answer
  • Hsp70, Hsp90 — molecular chaperones.
  • Refold damaged proteins.
  • Antiapoptotic — bind cyt-c, Apaf-1, AIF.
  • Induced by injury, hypothermia, ischemic preconditioning.
Q C-23
Subclinical seizures in pediatric brain injury.
Show Answer
  • Common after TBI, post-arrest, asphyxia, meningitis.
  • Often electrographic-only — missed without continuous EEG.
  • Increase metabolic demand and excitotoxicity.
  • Continuous EEG monitoring; aggressive seizure management.
Q C-24
Why does CRASH-3 cap benefit at 3 h?
Show Answer
  • Hematoma expansion mostly occurs in the first hours.
  • TXA is antifibrinolytic — reduces hematoma growth.
  • Beyond 3 h, hematoma evolution complete; no benefit.
  • Severe injury with bilateral fixed pupils — primary injury too severe to benefit.
Q C-25
Autophagy in brain injury.
Show Answer
  • Lysosomal degradation of cellular components.
  • Mild — protective (organelle clearance).
  • Excessive — cell death.
  • Cross-talk with apoptosis and necrosis.
  • Modulation under investigation.
Q C-26
HELIX trial implication.
Show Answer
  • Hypothermia for HIE in low-resource settings INCREASED mortality.
  • Different placental insufficiency mechanisms suspected.
  • Resource setting and underlying pathophysiology matter.
Q C-27
Future directions in pediatric neuroprotection.
Show Answer
  • Combination therapies addressing multiple cell death pathways.
  • Adaptive trial designs.
  • Biomarker-stratified precision medicine.
  • BBB-targeted drug delivery (nanoparticles, focused ultrasound).
  • Stem-cell / gene therapy.
  • Pediatric brain-injury consortia.
Q C-28
Outline pediatric TBI guideline thresholds.
Show Answer
  • Age-appropriate CPP.
  • Hyperosmolar — hypertonic saline first-line.
  • Normothermia (36.0–37.5 °C).
  • Avoid sustained hyperventilation.
  • Glucose 100–180 mg/dL.
  • Treat clinical / electrographic seizures.
Q C-29
Why is normothermia preferred over hypothermia in pediatric post-arrest?
Show Answer
  • THAPCA-IH and THAPCA-OH showed no significant benefit of 33 °C × 48 h over targeted normothermia 36.8 °C.
  • Aggressive fever avoidance is the core principle.
  • Neonatal HIE hypothermia is a separate well-supported evidence base.
Q C-30
Define osmotic demyelination syndrome.
Show Answer
  • Central pontine myelinolysis from rapid sodium correction in chronic hyponatremia.
  • Correction limit — 8–10 mEq/L per 24 h.
  • If over-corrected, re-lower with D5W / desmopressin.
  • Long-term — quadriparesis, dysphagia, "locked-in."

Block D — Remaining Cases (randomized)

CASE D-1
12-year-old with TBI develops T 39.5 °C on day 2.
Q. Approach.
Show Answer
  • Hyperthermia worsens outcome — treat aggressively.
  • Acetaminophen ± ibuprofen if no bleeding risk.
  • External cooling — cooling pads, ice packs, blanket.
  • Surface temperature feedback device for refractory.
  • Target 36.0–37.5 °C.
  • Investigate infection source.
CASE D-2
10-year-old with primary brain tumor and worsening edema.
Q. First-line therapy.
Show Answer
  • Dexamethasone — reduces tumor vasogenic edema by stabilizing BBB.
  • Not effective for cytotoxic edema (TBI / stroke).
  • Definitive — tumor surgery / radiation / chemotherapy.
CASE D-3
14-year-old after near-hanging with anoxic injury, comatose.
Q. Brain protection priorities.
Show Answer
  • Targeted normothermia.
  • Maintain CPP; avoid hypotension.
  • Avoid hyperoxia, hypocapnia.
  • Continuous EEG.
  • Avoid tight glucose control.
  • Watch for delayed edema and posterior watershed pattern on MRI.
CASE D-4
26-week preterm with grade IV IVH and post-hemorrhagic hydrocephalus.
Q. Management.
Show Answer
  • Germinal matrix vulnerability — pressure-passive CBF, thin-walled vessels.
  • Avoid hyperosmolar boluses, fluctuating CBF.
  • Serial head US; drain CSF via reservoir / subgaleal shunt.
  • Long-term VP shunt if persistent.
  • Ferroptosis from heme iron may contribute to white matter injury.
CASE D-5
9-year-old with severe TBI has glucose 220 mg/dL on admission.
Q. Approach.
Show Answer
  • Target glucose 100–180 mg/dL.
  • Insulin if >180 mg/dL sustained.
  • Avoid tight control.
  • Avoid hypoglycemia <80 mg/dL.
  • Frequent monitoring.
CASE D-6
7-year-old SCD with acute right hemiparesis.
Q. Acute brain protection.
Show Answer
  • Exchange transfusion to HbS <30 %.
  • Normothermia; treat fever.
  • Avoid hypotension, hypoxia, hypocapnia.
  • Avoid tight glucose control.
  • Continuous EEG if depressed mental status.
  • Consider tPA / thrombectomy in selected cases.
CASE D-7
5-year-old with new-onset refractory status epilepticus (NORSE) on midazolam infusion.
Q. Mechanisms and escalation.
Show Answer
  • Excitotoxicity + neuroinflammation drive seizures and neuronal injury.
  • Pentobarbital coma — burst suppression target.
  • Ketogenic diet.
  • Immunotherapy (steroids, IVIG, PLEX) if autoimmune.
  • Anakinra for FIRES.
  • Continuous EEG.
CASE D-8
15-year-old high school football player with third concussion this season.
Q. Long-term risk and management.
Show Answer
  • Increased CTE risk.
  • Sub-concussive impacts also contribute.
  • Strict return-to-play protocol — symptom-free, normal cognitive testing, graduated exertion.
  • Family discussion about cumulative risk and sport choice.
  • Concussion clinic follow-up.
CASE D-9
Adolescent with chronic hyponatremia (Na 118) corrected too quickly.
Q. Complication.
Show Answer
  • Osmotic demyelination syndrome.
  • Correction limit 8–10 mEq/L per 24 h.
  • If over-corrected, re-lower with D5W / desmopressin.
  • Quadriparesis, dysphagia, "locked-in" risk.
CASE D-10
14-year-old with anti-NMDA receptor encephalitis in refractory status epilepticus.
Q. Mechanism and approach.
Show Answer
  • Auto-antibodies internalize NMDA receptors → glutamate hypofunction.
  • Paradoxical seizures despite hypofunction (network instability).
  • Immunotherapy — steroids + IVIG or PLEX first-line; rituximab + cyclophosphamide refractory.
  • Tumor search (ovarian teratoma).
  • ICU support for autonomic / respiratory failure.
CASE D-11
Term neonate with HIE has glucose 14 mmol/L (250 mg/dL).
Q. Approach.
Show Answer
  • Avoid tight glucose control.
  • Target ~5.5–10 mmol/L.
  • Insulin infusion if >180 mg/dL sustained.
  • Avoid hypoglycemia — limits substrate.
  • Frequent monitoring; correct gradually.
CASE D-12
Adolescent TBI with refractory ICP >30 mmHg despite tier 1 + tier 2 therapy.
Q. Surgical option.
Show Answer
  • Decompressive craniectomy reduces ICP refractory to medical management.
  • Pediatric data and adult extrapolation (DECRA, RESCUEicp) — improves survival but functional outcome variable.
  • Family discussion about quality-of-life outcomes.
  • Subsequent cranioplasty months later.
CASE D-13
Discussion of CsA in pediatric TBI.
Q. Status of evidence.
Show Answer
  • Strong animal model evidence for mPTP inhibition.
  • Pediatric TBI trials investigational.
  • Not currently standard of care.
  • Translation challenges — BBB penetration, dosing.
