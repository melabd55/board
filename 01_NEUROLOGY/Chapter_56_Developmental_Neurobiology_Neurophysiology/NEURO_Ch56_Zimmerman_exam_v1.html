ZIMMERMAN Ch 58 — Structure, Function & Development — EXAM MODE

SOURCE: ZIMMERMAN   Companion to: ZIMMERMAN_58 STUDY MODE

Block A — Mock RCPSC SAQ (10 questions, 12 min)

A1. Explain the developmental switch of GABA from excitatory to inhibitory and its clinical implication.

Show answer
  • Early development: NKCC1 dominant → high intracellular Cl⁻ → GABA-A opens Cl⁻ channel → Cl⁻ EFFLUX → depolarization → GABA EXCITATORY.
  • Mature pattern: KCC2 expression increases → low intracellular Cl⁻ → Cl⁻ INFLUX → hyperpolarization → GABA INHIBITORY.
  • Switch occurs gradually over first months of life.
  • Clinical: phenobarbital first-line in neonatal SE; BZDs less reliable in neonate.
  • Bumetanide (NKCC1 inhibitor) — investigational adjunct.

A2. Outline normal CBF values across pediatric development and their clinical relevance.

Show answer
  • Term newborn ~40 mL/100 g brain/min.
  • Peak at 4–8 years ~100 mL/100 g/min — mirrors peak CMRO₂ during synaptic pruning.
  • Older adolescent/adult ~50 mL/100 g/min.
  • Implications: age-stratified CPP targets (≥ 40 infant, ≥ 50 school age, ≥ 60 adolescent per BTF).
  • Higher metabolic demand makes 4–8 y children vulnerable to even brief hypoperfusion.

A3. List the major glial cell types and one critical-care-relevant function of each.

Show answer
  • Microglia — immune surveillance, synaptic pruning; activated in HIE/TBI/infection; therapeutic target.
  • Astrocytes — BBB formation via end-feet; K⁺/glutamate homeostasis; lactate shuttle to neurons; GFAP biomarker.
  • Oligodendrocytes — CNS myelin; vulnerable to hypoxia in PVL.
  • Schwann cells — PNS myelin; demyelinated in GBS.
  • Ependymal — line ventricles; produce CSF (choroid plexus); disrupted in IVH/ventriculitis.

A4. Describe glutamate receptor types and how they participate in excitotoxicity.

Show answer
  • NMDA — Ca²⁺-permeable; Mg²⁺ block at rest; requires glutamate + glycine; central to LTP and excitotoxicity.
  • AMPA — Na⁺-permeable; fast excitatory; depolarization relieves NMDA Mg block.
  • Kainate — modulatory.
  • Metabotropic mGluR 1–8 — G-protein coupled; slower signaling.
  • Excitotoxicity: excess glutamate → NMDA/AMPA overactivation → Ca²⁺ overload → mitochondrial collapse → cell death.
  • Targets: NMDA antagonism (ketamine), Mg supplementation, antioxidants.

A5. A 14-year-old after severe TBI develops central diabetes insipidus. Explain the anatomic basis.

Show answer
  • Posterior pituitary (a circumventricular organ — BBB deficient) secretes ADH from hypothalamic magnocellular neurons.
  • TBI can damage hypothalamic neurons or pituitary stalk → ADH deficiency.
  • Treatment: DDAVP; replace free water deficit cautiously over 48 h.
  • Screen anterior pituitary axes (cortisol, T4).
  • DI may co-exist with brain death but does NOT establish it.

A6. Map the major arterial territories of the brain.

Show answer
  • ACA: medial frontal/parietal; leg motor cortex.
  • MCA: lateral hemisphere (most face/arm motor, language in dominant hemisphere, neglect in non-dominant).
  • PCA: occipital lobe (visual cortex), medial temporal lobe, splenium of corpus callosum.
  • Basilar: pons, midbrain, cerebellum via PICA, AICA, SCA.
  • Circle of Willis collateralizes anterior and posterior circulations.

A7. List the selectively vulnerable brain regions in HIE.

Show answer
  • Hippocampal CA1 region (Sommer sector).
  • Cortical layers 3 and 5 (pyramidal neurons).
  • Basal ganglia (caudate, putamen).
  • Cerebellar Purkinje cells.
  • Thalamus.
  • Watershed zones in border zones of major vascular territories.

A8. Describe spinal cord blood supply and ASA syndrome.

Show answer
  • Anterior spinal artery (single midline) supplies anterior 2/3 — motor + spinothalamic tracts.
  • Posterior spinal arteries (paired) supply posterior 1/3 — dorsal columns.
  • Artery of Adamkiewicz at T9–T12 reinforces lumbar enlargement.
  • ASA syndrome: motor loss + spinothalamic loss + preserved proprioception/vibration.
  • Causes: aortic surgery, vasculitis, atherosclerosis.
  • Treatment: maintain MAP for cord perfusion; CSF drainage in select cases.

A9. Explain pediatric synaptic density development and how it shapes critical-care neurology.

Show answer
  • Rapid synaptogenesis prenatally and in first months.
  • Peak synaptic density at 1–4 years (region-specific).
  • Use-dependent pruning extending into adolescence; frontal lobes mature last (early 20s).
  • High plasticity in critical periods (visual, language).
  • Implications: prolonged sedation, immobility, and isolation impair plasticity (PICS-p risk).
  • Early rehabilitation and family engagement leverage plasticity for recovery.
  • FDA warning on repeated anesthesia < 3 years — coordinate care to minimize cumulative exposure.

A10. List the circumventricular organs and one functional role of each.

Show answer
  • Area postrema — chemoreceptor trigger zone for vomiting.
  • Median eminence — hypothalamic-pituitary regulation.
  • Subfornical organ — thirst, Ang II sensing.
  • OVLT (organum vasculosum lamina terminalis) — osmoreceptor.
  • Pineal gland — melatonin secretion.
  • Posterior pituitary — ADH, oxytocin secretion.
  • Subcommissural organ.
  • BBB-deficient regions sense plasma signals to coordinate autonomic/endocrine responses.

Block B — Oral Cases (5 sequential, 35 min)

B1. 3-day-old term neonate with refractory seizures.

B1a — First-line therapy and rationale.
  • Phenobarbital 20 mg/kg IV first-line.
  • Rationale: NKCC1 dominant in neonates → GABA depolarizing/excitatory → BZDs less reliable.
  • Phenobarbital acts on GABA-A but also has other mechanisms.
  • Empiric pyridoxine 100 mg IV trial (pyridoxine-dependent epilepsy).
  • Folinic acid 5 mg if pyridoxine-resistant.
B1b — Continued seizures despite phenobarbital.
  • Repeat phenobarbital 10 mg/kg.
  • Levetiracetam 40–60 mg/kg or fosphenytoin 20 PE/kg.
  • Midazolam infusion if refractory.
  • cEEG mandatory.
  • Send glucose, electrolytes, ammonia, lactate, plasma AA, urine OA, acylcarnitine.
  • HSV PCR, septic workup.
B1c — Develops persistent burst suppression on cEEG.
  • Concerning for severe brain injury.
  • Investigate underlying cause (HIE, IEM, structural).
  • Continue cEEG; titrate sedation if iatrogenic.
  • Family discussion regarding prognosis (avoid premature definitive statement).
B1d — Family asks about long-term neurodevelopmental outcome.
  • Avoid early prognostication; reassess clinical exam off sedation, EEG, imaging.
  • MRI when stable.
  • Early Intervention program enrollment.
  • Pediatric neurology and developmental pediatrics follow-up.
  • Family education on plasticity and early stimulation.

B2. 26-week premature infant in NICU develops grade IV IVH.

B2a — Pathophysiology.
  • Germinal matrix is highly vascularized, immature.
  • Vulnerable to BP fluctuations, hypoxia, hypercarbia, acidosis.
  • Grade IV = parenchymal extension with hemorrhagic infarction.
  • White matter (oligodendrocyte precursors) particularly vulnerable.
B2b — Management.
  • Stabilize hemodynamics; avoid BP fluctuations.
  • Targeted oxygen saturation (avoid hyperoxia).
  • Watch for post-hemorrhagic hydrocephalus.
  • Serial ultrasound monitoring.
  • Neurosurgery consultation for progressive ventriculomegaly.
B2c — Develops post-hemorrhagic hydrocephalus.
  • Serial taps via reservoir or VAD.
  • VP shunt when stable weight (typically > 2 kg) and CSF clears of blood.
  • Long-term neurodevelopmental follow-up.
  • Anticipate motor (cerebral palsy from PVL) and cognitive sequelae.
B2d — Long-term plan.
  • Early intervention services.
  • Developmental pediatric and neurology follow-up.
  • Audiology, vision.
  • PT/OT for motor delay.
  • Family education and support.

B3. 10-year-old with severe TBI developing cerebral edema and rising ICP despite tier-1 measures.

B3a — Mechanisms of cerebral edema after TBI.
  • Cytotoxic: cellular swelling from energy failure (Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase failure); predominantly astrocyte.
  • Vasogenic: BBB disruption with extravasation of fluid.
  • Osmotic shifts.
  • Hyperosmolar therapy targets both cytotoxic + vasogenic components.
B3b — Age considerations.
  • Peak CBF/CMRO₂ phase (4–8 y) → high metabolic demand.
  • Narrower BP autoregulation range → vulnerable to hypotension.
  • CPP target ≥ 50 mm Hg in school-age child.
  • Open fontanelle (if applicable) does NOT prevent herniation.
B3c — NMDA antagonism rationale.
  • Prolonged seizure/excitotoxicity causes GABA-A internalization (BZD resistance).
  • NMDA receptors traffic to membrane → glutamate hypersensitivity.
  • Ketamine (NMDA antagonist) becomes rational in refractory states.
  • Also useful in hemodynamically unstable patient.
B3d — Plan continued care.
  • Tier-2 measures: barbiturate coma, controlled hyperventilation with multimodal monitoring, decompressive craniectomy.
  • Multimodal monitoring (PbtO₂, microdialysis if available).
  • cEEG.
  • Aggressive normothermia.
  • Family meeting on prognosis.

B4. 14-year-old after thoracic aortic surgery develops acute paralysis with preserved proprioception and vibration sense.

B4a — Diagnosis.
  • Anterior spinal artery syndrome.
  • Motor + spinothalamic loss; dorsal columns preserved.
  • Spinal cord vascular injury common after aortic surgery (artery of Adamkiewicz).
B4b — Acute management.
  • Maintain MAP ≥ 85–90 mm Hg for spinal cord perfusion (similar to acute SCI).
  • CSF drainage may help in select cases.
  • Steroids generally not recommended (per AANS/CNS pediatric extrapolation).
  • Imaging MRI cord.
B4c — Long-term plan.
  • Inpatient rehabilitation.
  • Bladder/bowel management.
  • Skin care for sensory loss.
  • Mobility aids; family support.
  • PT/OT lifelong.
B4d — Recurrence prevention.
  • Maintain BP postoperatively.
  • Address underlying vascular pathology.
  • Genetic counseling if connective tissue disease underlying.

B5. 6-year-old with HIE post-arrest, MRI shows bilateral basal ganglia + cortical DWI restriction.

B5a — Interpret imaging in developmental context.
  • Selectively vulnerable regions affected — typical HIE pattern.
  • Cortical layers 3/5, basal ganglia, hippocampus CA1, cerebellar Purkinje cells.
  • Reflects energy-dependent vulnerability with high baseline metabolic demand.
  • Severe injury pattern; poor prognostic indicator.
B5b — Post-arrest management.
  • Apply AHA 2019 post-arrest bundle.
  • TTM 32–34°C or 36–37.5°C × 72 h.
  • cEEG ≥ 24 h.
  • Strict normothermia for 5 days.
  • Defer prognostication until ≥ 72 h with multimodal data.
B5c — Family meeting at 72 h.
  • Integrate clinical exam (off sedation), cEEG, MRI.
  • Multidisciplinary meeting with palliative care, social work, chaplaincy.
  • Honest communication acknowledging uncertainty.
  • Discuss goals of care: tracheostomy/PEG vs WLST.
  • Allow time and revisit.
B5d — If family chooses continued aggressive care.
  • Maintain ICU support.
  • Treat any electrographic seizures.
  • Tracheostomy/PEG planning.
  • Inpatient rehab referral.
  • PICS-Family resources.
  • Long-term plan for chronic complex care.

Block C — Randomized SAQ Drill (20)

C1. CBF developmental peak.

Show
  • 4–8 years at ~100 mL/100 g brain/min.

C2. GABA switch — which transporters?

Show
  • NKCC1 (early, excitatory GABA) → KCC2 (mature, inhibitory GABA).

C3. Excitatory and inhibitory amino acid neurotransmitters.

Show
  • Glutamate (excitatory); GABA, glycine (inhibitory).

C4. Microglia activators.

Show
  • LPS (infection), thrombin (injury), DAMPs, cytokines.

C5. CNS vs PNS myelin source.

Show
  • CNS: oligodendrocytes (multiple axons per cell). PNS: Schwann cells (one cell per axon segment).

C6. Pediatric myelination timeline.

Show
  • Begins in utero; major spurt first 2 years; continues to adolescence; frontal lobes last.

C7. BBB components.

Show
  • Tight junctions of capillary endothelium; astrocyte end-feet; pericytes; selective transporters (GLUT1).

C8. Circumventricular organs — vomiting center.

Show
  • Area postrema (chemoreceptor trigger zone).

C9. Selectively vulnerable regions in HIE.

Show
  • CA1 hippocampus, cortical layers 3/5, basal ganglia, cerebellar Purkinje cells, thalamus, watershed zones.

C10. Cerebrovascular reactivity at birth.

Show
  • CO₂ and O₂ reactivity functional from birth in term newborns; BP-autoregulation narrower range in younger children.

C11. PVL mechanism.

Show
  • Oligodendrocyte precursor vulnerability to hypoxia-ischemia in premature white matter; spastic diplegia.

C12. Anterior spinal artery syndrome features.

Show
  • Motor + spinothalamic loss with preserved proprioception/vibration.

C13. Circle of Willis components.

Show
  • ACoA + 2 ACAs + 2 ICAs + 2 PCoAs + 2 PCAs + basilar terminus; ~50% incomplete.

C14. Synaptic density peak age.

Show
  • 1–4 years (region-specific); pruning continues to adolescence.

C15. FDA pediatric anesthesia warning age cutoff.

Show
  • Repeated/prolonged general anesthesia in children < 3 years.

C16. Cranial nerves arising from medulla.

Show
  • IX (glossopharyngeal), X (vagus), XI (accessory), XII (hypoglossal).

C17. Posterior circulation arteries.

Show
  • Vertebral → basilar → PCA; supplies brainstem, cerebellum, occipital lobe, medial temporal.

C18. Reticular activating system function.

Show
  • Arousal/consciousness; projects brainstem → thalamus → cortex; lesions cause coma.

C19. Spinal cord termination by age.

Show
  • Adult L1–L2; newborn L2–L3.

C20. Neuroplasticity mechanisms.

Show
  • LTP (NMDA-dependent strengthening); LTD (weakening); BDNF-mediated; impaired by critical illness, sedation, malnutrition.

Block D — Randomized Theory-Applied Cases (15)

D1. Neonate w/ status epilepticus.

Show
  • Phenobarbital 20 mg/kg first-line; NKCC1-dominant GABA-excitatory; pyridoxine trial.

D2. 6-yo TBI w/ rising ICP, high CMRO₂ phase.

Show
  • Age 4-8 peak CBF/CMRO₂; CPP ≥ 50; aggressive normothermia; NMDA antagonism rational.

D3. 28-wk premature IVH.

Show
  • Germinal matrix fragility; avoid BP fluctuations; oligodendrocyte PVL risk; serial US.

D4. Adolescent TBI with new central DI.

Show
  • Posterior pituitary dysfunction; DDAVP; replace FWD over 48 h; screen anterior axis.

D5. 10-yo MELAS stroke-like episode.

Show
  • Mitochondrial energy failure; L-arginine 0.5 g/kg; avoid valproate; mitochondrial cocktail.

D6. Infant post-arrest with cortical + BG DWI.

Show
  • HIE in selectively vulnerable regions; post-arrest bundle; multimodal prognostication ≥ 72 h.

D7. Child with ADEM/MS-like demyelination.

Show
  • Oligodendrocyte injury; MRI white matter lesions; methylpred + IVIG.

D8. GBS with progressive weakness.

Show
  • Schwann cell demyelination (PNS); IVIG/PLEX; watch respiratory failure (VC, NIF).

D9. ASA syndrome post-aortic surgery.

Show
  • Motor + STT loss, proprioception preserved; maintain MAP for cord perfusion; CSF drainage select cases.

D10. Adolescent ACoA aneurysm SAH.

Show
  • Most common adult site (pediatric distribution different); nimodipine; coiling vs clipping.

D11. Toddler with chemo-induced vomiting.

Show
  • Area postrema CTZ; 5-HT3 antagonist, steroids, NK1 antagonist.

D12. Patient on prolonged propofol infusion.

Show
  • GABA-A action; risk PRIS > 4 mg/kg/h or > 48 h; acidosis, rhabdo, cardiac failure.

D13. Child < 3 y with repeated anesthesia for cancer.

Show
  • FDA warning; minimize cumulative exposure; do NOT delay essential treatment; discuss with family.

D14. Adolescent athlete concussion.

Show
  • Diffuse axonal injury; frontal lobes vulnerable; cognitive rest then graduated RTP (CISG).

D15. Prolonged immobilization + sedation in PICU.

Show
  • Impairs plasticity; early mobility, minimize sedation, sleep promotion, family engagement.

Top 5 traps
