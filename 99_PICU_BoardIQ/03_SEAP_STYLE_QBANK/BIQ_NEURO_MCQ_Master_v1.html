MCQ 1 MODERATE
A 5-year-old boy is admitted after a fall from a tree, intubated for GCS 6. CT shows diffuse swelling with compressed basal cisterns. An EVD reads ICP 22 mm Hg with serial pulse waveform analysis showing P2 > P1. PRx is +0.4. Optimal CPP analysis suggests CPPopt 65 mm Hg; current CPP 58.

What single intervention best addresses both the waveform abnormality and the autoregulation status?

  1. Initiate barbiturate coma immediately
  2. Increase MAP with norepinephrine to target CPPopt 65 while continuing optimized tier-1 therapy
  3. Aggressive hyperventilation to PaCO₂ 25 mm Hg
  4. Loop diuretic to reduce intravascular volume
  5. Stop CSF drainage to allow physiologic ICP rise
Show answer + reasoning
Answer: B — Increase MAP to target CPPopt 65 within optimized tier-1.

P2 > P1 on the ICP waveform indicates reduced intracranial compliance. A positive PRx (> 0.25) confirms impaired pressure autoregulation. The CPPopt concept (Czosnyka, Brady, Aries) individualizes the perfusion target to where PRx is most negative. Driving MAP to the patient's individualized CPPopt rather than a fixed adult-pattern target reduces secondary injury without supraphysiologic overshoot.

Why others are wrong:
  • A: Barbiturate coma is a tier-2 maneuver; jumping directly skips optimization and risks hypotension.
  • C: Aggressive hyperventilation risks ischemia and is contraindicated without PbtO₂/SjvO₂ monitoring.
  • D: Diuresis worsens cerebral perfusion in a patient with impaired autoregulation.
  • E: CSF diversion is a tier-1 cornerstone; stopping it raises ICP and harms.
Trap: Picking fixed CPP targets (e.g., "MAP to 90") instead of individualized CPPopt based on PRx.
Source: Rogers Ch 62 / Zimmerman Ch 118; Aries 2012 (Crit Care Med — CPPopt); Czosnyka PRx framework.
MCQ 2 MODERATE
A 3-year-old (15 kg) post-MVC has severe TBI. ICP 22, MAP 70. The fellow proposes a CPP target of ≥ 60 mm Hg, citing adult data.

What CPP target best fits current pediatric BTF guidance for a 3-year-old?

  1. ≥ 30 mm Hg
  2. ≥ 40 mm Hg (acceptable minimum)
  3. ≥ 60 mm Hg
  4. ≥ 70 mm Hg
  5. ≥ 80 mm Hg
Show answer + reasoning
Answer: B — ≥ 40 mm Hg.

Pediatric CPP targets are age-stratified because cerebral autoregulation, baseline MAP, and cerebrovascular resistance differ by age. Current consensus is: ≥ 40 in infants/toddlers, ≥ 50 in school-age, ≥ 60 in adolescents. Higher CPP via vasopressors and fluids does not improve outcome and is associated with ARDS.

Why others are wrong:
  • A: Below the safety floor; risks ischemia.
  • C, D, E: Adult-pattern targets; supraphysiologic CPP in young children does not improve outcome and increases the risk of TRALI/ARDS and hemorrhagic conversion.
Trap: Adult thinking applied to a pediatric vignette. Examiners deliberately seed adult numbers as distractors.
Source: Rogers Ch 62 / Zimmerman Ch 118; Kochanek BTF 2019.
MCQ 3 HARD
An 8-year-old child arrives after a high-speed MVC. GCS 5, fixed dilated left pupil, HR 140, BP 70/40, SpO₂ 91% on NRB. The trauma team is ready to intubate. A PIV is in place; norepinephrine is being prepared.

Which induction agent is most appropriate for this patient?

  1. Propofol 2 mg/kg
  2. Thiopental 5 mg/kg
  3. Ketamine 2 mg/kg
  4. Midazolam 0.3 mg/kg alone
  5. Etomidate 0.3 mg/kg
Show answer + reasoning
Answer: C — Ketamine.

Ketamine preserves hemodynamics in a hypotensive trauma patient. Older teaching that ketamine raises ICP has been largely refuted in mechanically ventilated patients with controlled CO₂; current pediatric neurocritical care literature accepts ketamine in TBI with secured airway. In a hypotensive child with herniation signs, avoiding any further drop in MAP is the priority — hypotension after TBI doubles mortality.

Why others are wrong:
  • A: Propofol bolus causes deep vasodilation and hypotension — contraindicated in shock.
  • B: Thiopental causes profound myocardial depression and hypotension.
  • D: Midazolam alone produces inadequate intubating conditions and hypotensive in shock.
  • E: Etomidate is hemodynamically neutral but causes single-dose adrenal suppression; ketamine is preferred when hypotension is the dominant issue, though etomidate is a reasonable alternative.
Trap: Picking etomidate because it is "neutral" — in a hypotensive trauma child with imminent herniation, ketamine's sympathomimetic preload preservation is preferred.
Source: Zimmerman Ch 118 Table 118.2 (RSI strategy by hemodynamics); BTF 2019.
MCQ 4 MODERATE
A 9-year-old with severe TBI has refractory ICP 30 mm Hg despite optimized tier-1 therapy. The team considers therapeutic hypothermia 32–34°C.

Which statement best reflects current evidence on hypothermia in pediatric TBI?

  1. Prophylactic hypothermia 32°C × 48 h improves long-term outcome
  2. Hypothermia 33°C reduces mortality in severe pediatric TBI
  3. Prophylactic hypothermia is not recommended
  4. Hypothermia replaces hyperosmolar therapy as first-line ICP treatment
  5. Rewarming should occur at 2°C per hour
Show answer + reasoning
Answer: C — Prophylactic hypothermia is not recommended; targeted hypothermia is an option for refractory ICH.

Hutchison 2008 (NEJM) and Adelson Cool Kids 2013 (Lancet Neurology) both showed no benefit and trends toward harm with prophylactic hypothermia. BTF 2019 reflects this: do not use prophylactic hypothermia for neuroprotection; hypothermia 32–34°C may be considered as a Tier-2 option for refractory ICH, with slow rewarming (< 0.5°C/h).

Why others are wrong:
  • A, B: Directly contradicted by Hutchison and Cool Kids.
  • D: Hyperosmolar therapy remains a tier-1 cornerstone.
  • E: Rapid rewarming risks rebound ICH; slow rewarming < 0.5°C/h is recommended.
Trap: Adult cardiac-arrest TTM literature applied to TBI. Mechanisms and trials differ.
Source: Rogers Ch 62 / Zimmerman Ch 118; Hutchison 2008 NEJM; Adelson 2013 Lancet Neurol; Kochanek BTF 2019.
MCQ 5 HARD
A 12-year-old with severe TBI undergoes CT showing effaced basal cisterns and a 3 mm midline shift. No surgical mass lesion. The radiologist describes the appearance.

Using the Marshall CT classification, this is most consistent with which category?

  1. Marshall I — diffuse injury, no visible pathology
  2. Marshall II — diffuse injury, cisterns present, shift 0–5 mm
  3. Marshall III
  4. Marshall IV — diffuse injury, shift > 5 mm
  5. Marshall V — evacuated mass lesion
Show answer + reasoning
Answer: C — Marshall III.

Marshall III is defined by diffuse injury with swelling: compressed or absent basal cisterns with midline shift 0–5 mm. This radiographic pattern correlates with elevated ICP and worse outcomes. Recognition of compressed cisterns is one of the most important imaging predictors of intracranial hypertension before invasive monitoring.

Why others are wrong:
  • A: No abnormality is visible; here cisterns are effaced.
  • B: Cisterns are present in grade II; the vignette describes effacement.
  • D: Shift exceeds 5 mm in grade IV; here it is 3 mm.
  • E: No surgical mass lesion was evacuated.
Trap: Mixing up grade III (swelling) and grade IV (shift) — the discriminator is shift magnitude.
Source: Zimmerman Ch 118 Table 118.3.
MCQ 6 MODERATE
A 7-year-old with focal seizures arrives in active status epilepticus. He has received midazolam 0.2 mg/kg IM by EMS and a second dose of lorazepam 0.1 mg/kg IV on arrival, with continued seizing. The team prepares to give a second-line agent.

Based on the ESETT trial (NEJM 2019), which of the following second-line agents performs comparably to the others?

  1. Fosphenytoin only
  2. Levetiracetam only
  3. Valproate only
  4. Fosphenytoin, levetiracetam, and valproate — comparable efficacy
  5. Continuous midazolam infusion as second-line
Show answer + reasoning
Answer: D — Fosphenytoin, levetiracetam, and valproate were comparable in ESETT.

ESETT (Established Status Epilepticus Treatment Trial) randomized adult and pediatric patients with benzodiazepine-refractory convulsive status to fosphenytoin, levetiracetam, or valproate. All three were comparable in efficacy (~half achieved seizure cessation and improved responsiveness at 60 min) and safety. Choice is now individualized by patient comorbidity, allergy, and side-effect profile.

Why others are wrong:
  • A, B, C: ESETT did not show superiority of any one agent.
  • E: Continuous midazolam infusion is third-line (refractory SE), not second.
Trap: Choosing fosphenytoin out of habit. ESETT changed practice: levetiracetam is often preferred for fewer drug interactions and rapid administration.
Source: Rogers Ch 64; ESETT (Kapur NEJM 2019).
MCQ 7 MODERATE
A 10-year-old with refractory convulsive status epilepticus is intubated and started on midazolam infusion. Clinical seizures stop. Over the next 24 h she remains comatose without spontaneous movement.

What is the single most important next step?

  1. Wean midazolam infusion
  2. Order MRI brain
  3. Continuous EEG monitoring
  4. Lumbar puncture
  5. Brain death exam
Show answer + reasoning
Answer: C — Continuous EEG monitoring.

After convulsive status epilepticus, non-convulsive seizures and non-convulsive status epilepticus are common (up to 30–50% in critically ill children). The clinical exam alone cannot exclude ongoing electrographic seizures, especially in a sedated patient. cEEG identifies persistent electrographic seizures or NCSE that warrant continued treatment, and also confirms burst suppression if pharmacologic coma is being titrated.

Why others are wrong:
  • A: Weaning sedation before ruling out ongoing seizures risks recurrence.
  • B: MRI is appropriate for etiology workup but not the immediate priority.
  • D: LP for CSF analysis is appropriate after exclusion of mass effect but does not address the immediate seizure question.
  • E: Brain death determination requires absence of confounders including sedation; premature here.
Trap: Equating absence of motor activity with seizure freedom in a paralyzed/sedated patient.
Source: Rogers Ch 64; pediatric cEEG consensus.
MCQ 8 HARD
A 6-year-old with super-refractory status epilepticus is on midazolam 2 mg/kg/h, pentobarbital infusion, levetiracetam, and fosphenytoin. Continuous EEG shows persistent electrographic seizures. The fellow proposes adding ketamine.

The mechanistic rationale for ketamine in super-refractory status epilepticus is best described as:

  1. Enhancing GABA-A receptor sensitivity
  2. Blocking voltage-gated sodium channels
  3. Antagonizing NMDA receptors trafficked to the synapse during prolonged seizure activity
  4. Reducing glutamate release by inhibiting calcium channels
  5. Increasing CSF GABA concentration
Show answer + reasoning
Answer: C — NMDA antagonism.

During prolonged seizure activity, GABA-A receptors are internalized (reducing GABAergic drug efficacy) while NMDA receptors are trafficked to the synapse, making the brain "glutamate-hypersensitive." NMDA antagonism by ketamine targets this maladaptive plasticity. This is the rationale for ketamine in refractory and super-refractory status.

Why others are wrong:
  • A: BZDs (midazolam) act via this mechanism — already being used.
  • B: Sodium-channel blockade describes phenytoin/lacosamide, already used.
  • D: Refers to gabapentinoids/calcium-channel modulators — not the dominant ketamine effect.
  • E: Not the mechanism.
Trap: Confusing ketamine's general anesthetic property with the specific NMDA receptor pathophysiology relevant in prolonged seizure.
Source: Rogers Ch 64; pediatric refractory SE consensus (Brophy 2012; Trinka 2015).
MCQ 9 MODERATE
An 8-year-old previously healthy boy presents with acute right hemiparesis and aphasia. MRI confirms a left MCA infarct. There is no cardiac source or prothrombotic state identified, but MRA shows irregular narrowing of the left M1 segment.

Which arteriopathy is most likely?

  1. Moyamoya disease
  2. Focal cerebral arteriopathy of childhood (FCA)
  3. Primary CNS vasculitis
  4. Arterial dissection
  5. Atherosclerotic stenosis
Show answer + reasoning
Answer: B — Focal cerebral arteriopathy of childhood (FCA).

FCA is the single most common cause of arterial ischemic stroke in previously healthy children, accounting for approximately one third of cases (VIPS study, 2016). It typically affects the unilateral M1 or distal ICA, often follows recent infection (especially varicella), and is monophasic or progresses transiently. Distinguishing FCA from primary CNS vasculitis (multifocal, progressive) and moyamoya (bilateral, characteristic appearance) is critical because management differs.

Why others are wrong:
  • A: Moyamoya is bilateral with characteristic "puff of smoke" collaterals — not described.
  • C: Primary CNS vasculitis is typically multifocal, progressive, and may need immunosuppression.
  • D: Arterial dissection shows long-segment irregular narrowing with intramural hematoma, often after trauma or connective tissue disorder.
  • E: Atherosclerosis is exceedingly rare in healthy children.
Trap: Picking moyamoya — common but bilateral; FCA is unilateral and more common overall.
Source: Rogers Ch 65 / Zimmerman Ch 66 supplement; VIPS study (Wintermark 2016).
MCQ 10 MODERATE
A 9-year-old with sickle cell disease HbSS presents with acute right hemiparesis. CT excludes hemorrhage. Hemoglobin is 8 g/dL and HbS is 80% on baseline electrophoresis.

What is the most urgent therapeutic step?

  1. Aspirin 5 mg/kg
  2. Simple transfusion of packed red blood cells
  3. Exchange transfusion to reduce HbS to < 30%
  4. Hydroxyurea bolus
  5. Therapeutic hypothermia
Show answer + reasoning
Answer: C — Exchange transfusion to reduce HbS to < 30%.

Acute ischemic stroke in HbSS is a medical emergency. Rapid exchange transfusion to reduce HbS below 30% (preferred < 20–30%) restores oxygen delivery to ischemic brain and prevents propagation. STOP and SWiTCH trials established the role of chronic transfusion for primary and secondary stroke prevention. Hydroxyurea (TWiTCH, SIT) has a role in chronic management but not acute.

Why others are wrong:
  • A: Antiplatelet therapy has no role in acute sickle stroke.
  • B: Simple transfusion raises Hb but does not rapidly reduce HbS percentage — risk of hyperviscosity.
  • D: Hydroxyurea is for chronic stroke prevention.
  • E: No indication for hypothermia in sickle stroke.
Trap: Simple transfusion — raises total Hb (and viscosity) without diluting HbS quickly enough. Exchange is the answer.
Source: Rogers Ch 65 / Zimmerman Ch 66; STOP/SWiTCH/TWiTCH/SIT trials.
MCQ 11 MODERATE
A 4-year-old with mastoiditis develops progressive headache, vomiting, and a seizure. MRI/MRV demonstrates thrombosis of the right transverse and sigmoid sinuses with a small parenchymal venous infarct showing minimal hemorrhagic transformation.

Which is the best management approach for the venous thrombosis itself?

  1. Observation only because of intracranial hemorrhage
  2. Antiplatelet therapy only
  3. Anticoagulation with low-molecular-weight heparin
  4. Mechanical thrombectomy as first-line
  5. IV tPA
Show answer + reasoning
Answer: C — Anticoagulation with LMWH.

Pediatric cerebral sinovenous thrombosis (CSVT) is treated with anticoagulation (LMWH or UFH) even in the presence of a small hemorrhagic infarct, because mortality and propagation risk without anticoagulation exceed the risk of bleeding. Treat the underlying source (mastoiditis, IV antibiotics, surgical drainage as needed). Endovascular thrombolysis is reserved for select refractory cases.

Why others are wrong:
  • A: Mortality without anticoagulation is significant; small hemorrhage is not an absolute contraindication.
  • B: Antiplatelet therapy is not the standard for CSVT.
  • D: Mechanical thrombectomy is investigational/refractory option only.
  • E: IV tPA is for acute arterial ischemic stroke (and rarely used in pediatrics).
Trap: Withholding anticoagulation because of "any hemorrhage." For small hemorrhagic conversion in CSVT, anticoagulation is still preferred.
Source: Rogers Ch 65 / Zimmerman Ch 66 supplement; American Heart Association pediatric stroke statement.

A 7-year-old is admitted 6 hours after a witnessed in-hospital cardiac arrest (downtime 14 min). She is on TTM at 33°C, off sedation for the last 18 hours, normothermic baseline now restored. On post-arrest day 4: pupils 3 mm reactive, motor response withdrawal to noxious. cEEG day 1 showed burst-suppression; day 3 background continuous slow with reactivity. MRI on day 4 shows scattered cortical DWI restriction in watershed zones but no basal ganglia or thalamic involvement. Family requests prognostic information.

Which statement is most accurate regarding multimodal neuroprognostication at this time point?

  1. The MRI pattern alone establishes poor prognosis — recommend WLST
  2. The presence of a continuous reactive EEG and withdrawal motor response are favorable signs

Continuous reactive EEG background and motor withdrawal off sedation are favorable prognostic features. Scattered watershed DWI without deep grey matter (basal ganglia/thalamus) involvement is a more favorable imaging pattern than diffuse cortical-deep-grey injury. Pediatric prognostication post-arrest requires multimodal integration over days; the Topjian AHA pediatric post-arrest statement explicitly cautions against single-modality determination and against early prognostication. MCQ 14 MODERATE
A 10-year-old is undergoing apnea testing as part of brain-death determination. Pre-test PaCO₂ is 38 mm Hg. After 8 minutes off the ventilator he has no respiratory effort.

What PaCO₂ change confirms a positive apnea test?

  1. PaCO₂ ≥ 45 mm Hg
  2. PaCO₂ ≥ 55 mm Hg with a rise of ≥ 10 from baseline
  3. PaCO₂ ≥ 60 mm Hg AND a rise of ≥ 20 from baseline
  4. PaCO₂ ≥ 80 mm Hg with arterial pH < 7.20
  5. Any PaCO₂ rise is sufficient if no respiratory effort
Show answer + reasoning
Answer: C — PaCO₂ ≥ 60 mm Hg AND rise ≥ 20 from baseline with no respiratory effort.

Current Canadian (Shemie 2023) and Nakagawa 2011 pediatric criteria specify a target of PaCO₂ ≥ 60 mm Hg AND a rise of ≥ 20 mm Hg over baseline, with arterial pH typically < 7.30. The apnea test must be performed safely (pre-oxygenation, hemodynamic stability) and stopped early if instability develops; in those cases ancillary testing (cerebral blood flow imaging) is required.

Why others are wrong:

  • D: Excessive PaCO₂ without an absolute requirement of pH.
  • E: Apnea test requires defined CO₂ thresholds.
Trap: Confusing adult-style thresholds with pediatric-specific requirements; remember the "AND" — both absolute and delta thresholds must be met.
Source: Rogers Ch 68; Shemie 2023 Canadian Brain-Based Death Guideline; Nakagawa 2011.
MCQ 15 HARD
A 5-year-old after severe TBI is on the second clinical exam for brain-death determination. The patient is normothermic (36.5°C) but received vecuronium 4 hours ago and propofol infusion was discontinued 8 hours ago. Train-of-four shows 0/4 twitches.

What is the most appropriate next action?

  1. Proceed with examination immediately
  2. Delay until train-of-four shows 4/4 twitches and adequate clearance of propofol
  3. Reverse vecuronium with sugammadex and proceed
  4. Order ancillary cerebral blood flow imaging
  5. Skip apnea test and rely on clinical exam alone
Show answer + reasoning
Answer: B — Delay until train-of-four is 4/4 and propofol is cleared.

Neuromuscular blockade and CNS depressants are absolute confounders to brain-death determination. The exam cannot proceed until train-of-four demonstrates full recovery (4/4 twitches) and sedative levels are sub-therapeutic. If, despite waiting, confounders cannot be eliminated (e.g., persistent metabolite or impaired clearance), ancillary cerebral blood flow imaging is mandated.

Why others are wrong:

  • D: Acceptable later if confounders persist, not first step here.
  • E: Apnea test is mandatory unless ancillary testing replaces it.
Trap: Performing brain-death determination in the presence of unresolved confounders.

MCQ 16 MODERATE
A 12-year-old with GBS is intubated 24 h ago. Today: FVC 22 mL/kg, NIF –30, MEP 40, alert, secretions easily cleared. Daily SBT for 30 min with PSV 6/PEEP 5 is well tolerated. Vital sign labile with episodes of HR 40 lasting 10 s and BP swings 90/40 to 170/100.

Which factor most strongly argues against extubation today despite the favorable respiratory mechanics?

  1. FVC of 22 mL/kg
  2. NIF of –30 cmH₂O
  3. Autonomic instability with paroxysmal bradycardia and BP lability
  4. Duration of intubation of 24 h
  5. Patient age
Show answer + reasoning
Answer: C — Autonomic instability.

Autonomic dysfunction is a leading cause of mortality in GBS and a strong relative contraindication to extubation. Paroxysmal bradycardia (with risk of asystole), labile blood pressure, and cardiac dysrhythmia complicate post-extubation management. The respiratory mechanics are at the threshold of acceptability but the dominant risk is hemodynamic. MCQ 18 MODERATE
An 8-year-old boy with delayed motor milestones (no formal diagnosis) is brought to the ED after a fall and requires intubation for respiratory failure. The team administers succinylcholine 1.5 mg/kg. Within 3 minutes, ECG shows peaked T-waves and then wide-complex tachycardia degenerating to VF. Defibrillation × 2 has failed. K = 8.3 mmol/L.

What is the most appropriate next action?

  1. Continue defibrillation alone until ROSC
  2. Calcium chloride 20 mg/kg IV, insulin/glucose, sodium bicarbonate, then repeat defibrillation
  3. Sodium thiopental infusion
  4. Empiric dantrolene 2.5 mg/kg
  5. Sugammadex 16 mg/kg
Show answer + reasoning
Answer: B — Calcium + insulin/glucose + sodium bicarbonate + repeat defibrillation.

The clinical picture is hyperkalemic cardiac arrest from succinylcholine in an undiagnosed muscular dystrophy. Defibrillation alone will fail until the myocardial membrane is stabilized. Sequence: calcium chloride (or gluconate) first for membrane stabilization, then K-lowering (insulin/glucose, β-agonist, bicarbonate if acidemic), then repeat defibrillation. Send K periodically and consider dialysis if refractory.

Why others are wrong:
  • A: Hyperkalemic arrhythmia is refractory to defibrillation without K correction.
  • C: Thiopental has no role here.
  • D: Dantrolene treats malignant hyperthermia, not hyperkalemic arrest. MH presents with hypercarbia, rigidity, hyperthermia.
  • E: Sugammadex reverses aminosteroid NMB (rocuronium/vecuronium), not succinylcholine.
Trap: Confusing the clinical picture with MH. Immediate hyperkalemic arrest after sux in a child with myopathy is the pattern.
Source: Rogers Ch 60; PALS H's and T's.
