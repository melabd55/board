Mock RCPSC Block A — 10 SAQs (timed)

⏱ 30 min total · 3 min per SAQ
SAQ A-1
A 6-month-old develops constipation, hypotonia, weak cry, shallow respirations. Outline diagnostic and therapeutic priorities.
Show Answer
  • Stabilize airway / ventilation first.
  • Suspect infant botulism.
  • Stool for botulinum toxin and PCR.
  • Bedside RNS: low resting CMAP with marked increment at 50 Hz / post-exercise.
  • Empiric BabyBIG immediately — do not wait for stool result.
  • Avoid aminoglycosides.
  • Anticipate 2–4 weeks of ventilatory support.
SAQ A-2
Name the four anatomic compartments framing pediatric neuromuscular weakness with one discriminating EDX clue each.
Show Answer
  • Anterior horn cell — preserved SNAP + low CMAP + EMG denervation.
  • Peripheral nerve — reduced SNAP and CMAP; slow CV (demyelinating) or low amplitude (axonal).
  • Neuromuscular junction — RNS decrement at 3 Hz (postsynaptic) or increment at 50 Hz (presynaptic).
  • Muscle — short-duration, low-amplitude, polyphasic MUPs with early recruitment, normal SNAP/velocity.
SAQ A-3
Differentiate AIDP from AMAN electrodiagnostically.
Show Answer
  • AIDP — slowed CV; prolonged distal motor latency; absent / prolonged F-waves; conduction block; temporal dispersion; preserved CMAP amplitudes early.
  • AMAN — low CMAP amplitudes from outset; near-normal velocity and latency; EMG acute denervation; F-waves variable.
SAQ A-4
Outline the ICU-AW prevention bundle.
Show Answer
  • Daily sedation interruption / lightest effective sedation.
  • Minimize NMBA exposure.
  • Avoid sustained hyperglycemia (target ~6–10 mmol/L).
  • Early mobilization with PT / OT from day 1 of stability.
  • Minimize corticosteroid duration.
  • Aggressive sepsis source control.
  • Protein-adequate nutrition.
  • Routine MRC sum-score screening on rounds.
SAQ A-5
Ventilator-mechanics thresholds prompting intubation in pediatric GBS.
Show Answer
  • FVC <20 mL/kg.
  • MIP less negative than –30 cmH₂O.
  • MEP <40 cmH₂O.
  • Rapidly declining trend.
  • Bulbar weakness with secretion failure.
  • Autonomic instability.
  • Intubate proactively, not emergently.
SAQ A-6
Why is SNAP preserved in anterior horn cell disease and what is the SMA exception?
Show Answer
  • The sensory cell body sits in the dorsal root ganglion, peripheral to the AHC.
  • Pure motor AHC disease leaves the DRG intact, preserving SNAP.
  • Exception — severe SMA 0/1 may have dorsal root ganglionopathy producing reduced SNAP; confirm with SMN1 testing.
SAQ A-7
Why avoid aminoglycosides in suspected infant botulism?
Show Answer
  • Aminoglycosides block presynaptic acetylcholine release.
  • This compounds the existing presynaptic NMJ failure caused by botulinum toxin.
  • Risk of precipitating acute respiratory arrest.
SAQ A-8
Direct muscle stimulation: what it adds, and how it discriminates CIM from CIP.
Show Answer
  • DMS bypasses the nerve and stimulates muscle directly.
  • CIM — both nerve-evoked CMAP and direct-muscle-evoked CMAP are reduced; neCMAP/dmCMAP ratio >0.5 indicates myopathy.
  • CIP — nerve-evoked CMAP reduced but DMS preserved (ratio <0.5).
SAQ A-9
Why does temperature change pediatric NCS results, and what target should be maintained?
Show Answer
  • Cold slows axonal sodium-channel kinetics.
  • Prolongs distal latencies and slows CV by ~2 m/s per 1 °C below 32 °C.
  • Maintain skin temperature ≥32 °C with a warming blanket or warmed room before interpreting velocity.
SAQ A-10
List three drugs that worsen NMJ transmission and should be avoided in MG / botulism / CMS.
Show Answer
  • Aminoglycosides (gentamicin, tobramycin).
  • Macrolides (erythromycin, azithromycin).
  • Magnesium sulfate.
  • Also — fluoroquinolones, beta-blockers, lithium, iodinated contrast, non-depolarizing NMBAs.

Mock RCPSC Block B — 5 Sequential ICU Cases

⏱ 30 min total · 6 min per case
CASE B-1
Term infant, 3 weeks old, profound hypotonia, areflexia, weak cry, tongue fasciculations, respiratory failure. EDX: normal SNAPs, severely reduced CMAPs, EMG with widespread fibrillations and large reinnervated MUPs in legs and tongue.
Q. Localize the lesion, name the most likely diagnosis, outline next steps.
Show Answer
  • Lesion: anterior horn cell.
  • Most likely: SMA type 1 (5q-SMN1 deletion).
  • Urgent SMN1 deletion + SMN2 copy number testing.
  • Supportive ventilation, swallow assessment, NG feeds, family counselling.
  • Rapid initiation of modifying therapy — onasemnogene (if <2 y and antibody titer permits), nusinersen, or risdiplam per local protocol.
CASE B-2
8-year-old with septic shock from S. aureus pneumonia, ventilated 14 days, prolonged rocuronium 4 days, methylprednisolone. Fails 3 SBTs. Reflexes diminished; symmetric weakness.
Q. Most likely EDX picture and management.
Show Answer
  • Critical illness myopathy ± polyneuropathy (CINM).
  • EDX — low CMAP amplitudes with preserved CV; sensory NCS depends on neuropathic overlap.
  • EMG short-duration MUPs with early recruitment.
  • DMS confirms myopathy if neCMAP/dmCMAP <0.5.
  • Management — stop NMBA, taper steroids, optimize glycemia, early mobilization, plan tracheostomy if expected prolonged wean.
CASE B-3
12-year-old, two weeks post-Campylobacter gastroenteritis, ascending weakness over 4 days, areflexia, FVC 18 mL/kg, autonomic lability. CSF protein 0.6 g/L, 2 cells/μL.
Q. Likely subtype, EDX features, treatment.
Show Answer
  • AIDP (most common pediatric GBS).
  • EDX — prolonged distal latencies, slowed CV, absent / prolonged F-waves, conduction block.
  • Intubate (FVC <20 mL/kg).
  • IVIG 2 g/kg over 5 days or PLEX.
  • Treat autonomic instability — short-acting agents only; atropine pre-suctioning.
  • Avoid succinylcholine (hyperkalemia risk).
CASE B-4
3-year-old, acute right arm and bilateral leg weakness after a febrile URI. MRI: T2 hyperintensity in cord gray matter. EDX: normal SNAPs, low CMAPs, EMG with fibrillations.
Q. Diagnosis and management.
Show Answer
  • Acute flaccid myelitis (AFM); likely enterovirus D68.
  • Send NP / stool / CSF for enterovirus PCR.
  • Supportive care; IVIG sometimes given (weak evidence).
  • Aggressive rehabilitation.
  • Consider nerve transfer surgery at 6–12 months for residual paralysis.
  • Report to public health.
CASE B-5
2-year-old admitted for failure to thrive, becomes ventilator-dependent. MRI brain / spine normal, genetic SMA negative. NCS / EMG normal. RNS at 3 Hz unremarkable.
Q. What single additional test, and why?
Show Answer
  • Single-fiber EMG (or stimulation SFEMG of orbicularis oculi).
  • Most sensitive electrodiagnostic test for NMJ disease.
  • Catches congenital myasthenic syndromes and seronegative MG missed by RNS.
  • Both are treatable, so missing them costs years of ventilator dependence.

Block C — Remaining SAQs (randomized)

Q C-1
Define conduction block.
Show Answer
  • ≥50 % drop in CMAP amplitude (or area) between distal and proximal stimulation sites.
  • Without significant temporal dispersion.
  • Signifies focal demyelination causing impulse failure across that segment.
Q C-2
List four conditions producing fibrillation potentials.
Show Answer
  • Axonal neuropathy or motor neuronopathy (SMA, AFM, polio, ALS).
  • Inflammatory myopathy.
  • Severe necrotizing myopathy.
  • Late stages of muscular dystrophy.
  • Fibrillations alone are not specific to neurogenic disease.
Q C-3
What is post-exercise facilitation and what disease does it suggest?
Show Answer
  • Brief voluntary contraction or 10 s tetanic stimulation transiently increases CMAP amplitude >60 %.
  • Indicates presynaptic NMJ disease — LEMS or infant botulism.
  • Use tetanic RNS when sedation prevents voluntary contraction.
Q C-4
AANEM consensus position on EDX in the genetic-testing era.
Show Answer
  • Genetics has replaced EDX as first-line for confirmed hereditary disease (SMA, muscular dystrophy).
  • EDX retains high utility for acquired pediatric NMD, ventilator-dependent weakness, and when genetic testing is non-diagnostic.
Q C-5
Describe the SFEMG finding in autoimmune MG.
Show Answer
  • Increased jitter — mean consecutive difference > age-specific upper limit, typically >35 μs.
  • Intermittent blocking.
  • Sensitivity >95 % for generalized MG.
  • Most sensitive electrodiagnostic test for ocular MG.
Q C-6
Differential of failure to wean that should be electrodiagnostically investigated.
Show Answer
  • ICU-AW (CIP / CIM / CINM).
  • Residual neuromuscular blockade.
  • Unrecognized GBS.
  • Undiagnosed MG or CMS.
  • Infant botulism.
  • Occult SMA / SMARD1.
  • Phrenic nerve injury.
  • Cervical myelopathy.
  • Metabolic / endocrine myopathies — hypophosphatemia, hypothyroidism, steroid myopathy.
Q C-7
Define myotonic discharge and give a pathognomonic disease.
Show Answer
  • Repetitive, waxing-and-waning, "dive-bomber" discharges of muscle fiber origin.
  • Pathognomonic for myotonic dystrophy (DM1, DM2).
  • Also seen in non-dystrophic myotonias and some K-channel disorders.
Q C-8
List five risk factors for ICU-acquired weakness.
Show Answer
  • Sepsis / SIRS.
  • Multi-organ failure.
  • Sustained hyperglycemia.
  • Corticosteroid exposure.
  • Neuromuscular blocker exposure.
  • Prolonged mechanical ventilation.
  • Prolonged immobilization.
  • Severity-of-illness score.
Q C-9
Equation for nerve conduction velocity.
Show Answer
  • CV (m/s) = distance (mm) ÷ (proximal latency − distal latency in ms).
  • Distance is along the nerve path between two stimulation points.
  • Latencies are measured to the onset of the CMAP.
Q C-10
Define single-fiber EMG and the parameter it quantifies.
Show Answer
  • Specialized concentric needle records action potentials from a pair of muscle fibers in the same motor unit.
  • Variability of the inter-potential interval is "jitter" — quantified as the mean consecutive difference.
  • Increased jitter ± blocking indicates NMJ failure.
Q C-11
Two EDX features distinguishing AFM from AIDP.
Show Answer
  • AFM — preserved SNAPs, reduced CMAPs with normal latency / velocity, denervation on EMG, asymmetric distribution.
  • AIDP — slow CV, prolonged distal latencies, absent F-waves, conduction block; sensory NCS often abnormal; symmetric distribution.
Q C-12
Role of MRI in suspected inflammatory myopathy.
Show Answer
  • STIR / T2 hyperintensity shows muscle edema and atrophy.
  • Guides biopsy site.
  • First-line in juvenile dermatomyositis.
  • EDX confirms myopathic process and excludes neuropathic mimics.
Q C-13
Miller-Fisher syndrome features and antibody.
Show Answer
  • Triad — ophthalmoplegia, ataxia, areflexia.
  • Often post-Campylobacter or viral infection.
  • Anti-GQ1b IgG positive in ~90 %.
  • Treatment supportive ± IVIG.
  • EDX — reduced SNAPs, absent H-reflex, preserved motor NCS.
Q C-14
Typical CSF finding in GBS and its temporal pitfall.
Show Answer
  • Cytoalbuminologic dissociation — elevated protein with normal cell count.
  • Up to 50 % of CSF studies in week 1 are normal.
  • EDX is more sensitive than CSF early.
Q C-15
Three pitfalls causing false-positive EDX findings in the PICU.
Show Answer
  • Cold limb → falsely slow CV.
  • Residual neuromuscular blockade → false RNS decrement.
  • Electrical interference (ECMO, dialysis, warming devices) → spurious spontaneous activity.
Q C-16
Why are pediatric CV norms age-dependent up to ~5 years?
Show Answer
  • Myelination is incomplete at birth and matures progressively.
  • Infants have ~25–35 m/s velocities.
  • Adult values (~50–55 m/s) reached by ~3–5 years.
  • Adult cutoffs misdiagnose physiological slowing in a neonate.
Q C-17
EDX evolution of an acute axonal nerve injury.
Show Answer
  • Immediately — low / absent distal CMAP; normal proximal CMAP (Wallerian degeneration not yet complete).
  • 7–10 days — distal CMAP loss complete.
  • 2–3 weeks — EMG fibrillations and positive sharp waves (acute denervation).
  • Months later — reinnervation gives large, long, polyphasic MUPs.
Q C-18
EMG pattern in juvenile dermatomyositis.
Show Answer
  • ↑ insertional activity with fibrillations and positive sharp waves.
  • Short-duration, low-amplitude, polyphasic MUPs with early recruitment.
  • Occasionally myotonic-like discharges.
  • Pattern non-specific — MRI muscle and autoantibodies (anti-Mi2, anti-MDA5) confirm.
Q C-19
Preferred muscle for stimulation SFEMG in an uncooperative or sedated child.
Show Answer
  • Orbicularis oculi via stimulation SFEMG.
  • Accessible.
  • Does not require voluntary contraction.
  • Highly sensitive for generalized MG and CMS.
Q C-20
Role of EDX in tick paralysis.
Show Answer
  • Low CMAP amplitudes with preserved velocity.
  • Minimal RNS decrement.
  • Rarely necessary — full skin inspection and tick removal are diagnostic and curative.
Q C-21
Three common pediatric ICU compressive mononeuropathies and clues.
Show Answer
  • Peroneal at fibular head — foot drop after prolonged lateral positioning.
  • Ulnar at elbow — paresthesias and 4th–5th finger clawing.
  • Brachial plexus stretch — arm weakness after head-turned positioning, ECMO cannulation, prolonged anesthesia.
Q C-22
Sensible PICU EMG/NCS protocol for an unexplained ventilator-dependent infant.
Show Answer
  • Warm to 32 °C; ensure NMBA washout.
  • Sural or medial plantar SNAP.
  • Peroneal motor NCS with F-wave.
  • Ulnar motor NCS with F-wave and 3 Hz / 50 Hz RNS.
  • Needle EMG of tibialis anterior.
  • Submental tongue EMG if AHC pattern.
  • SFEMG / StimSFEMG of orbicularis oculi if all prior normal.
Q C-23
Contraindications and cautions to needle EMG in PICU children.
Show Answer
  • Contraindications — severe uncorrected thrombocytopenia or coagulopathy; overlying skin infection.
  • Cautions — anticoagulation (small needles, single passes); valvular disease (sterile technique; no formal endocarditis prophylaxis indication).
Q C-24
Why might SNAP be reduced in severe SMA 0/1?
Show Answer
  • Severe SMA may have dorsal root ganglionopathy with sensory axon loss.
  • Mimics peripheral neuropathy on NCS.
  • Confirm with SMN1 deletion testing.
Q C-25
H-reflex utility.
Show Answer
  • Electrical equivalent of the Achilles reflex.
  • Ia afferent stimulation → monosynaptic α-motor neuron response.
  • Useful in S1 radiculopathy (lost ipsilaterally) and early peripheral neuropathy.
Q C-26
What does early SFEMG of orbicularis oculi add when CMAP, SNAP, RNS, and EMG are all normal in a ventilator-dependent infant?
Show Answer
  • Far more sensitive than RNS for NMJ disease.
  • Increased jitter or blocking can be the only EDX abnormality in CMS or seronegative MG.
  • Both are treatable — high-impact detection.
Q C-27
EDX role in suspected phrenic injury after cardiac surgery.
Show Answer
  • Phrenic nerve conduction with surface recording over the diaphragm.
  • Fluoroscopic or ultrasound sniff test.
  • Needle EMG of the diaphragm in expert hands.
  • Confirms phrenic neuropathy and may prompt diaphragmatic plication.
Q C-28
Single best preventive intervention for ICU-AW.
Show Answer
  • Early mobilization paired with daily sedation interruption.
  • Minimal NMBA exposure.
  • Glycemic control.
  • No drug therapy works — prevention is the answer.
Q C-29
Distinguish CIP from CIM electrodiagnostically.
Show Answer
  • CIP — reduced CMAP and SNAP, preserved velocity (sensorimotor axonal).
  • CIM — reduced CMAP with normal SNAP and low neCMAP/dmCMAP <0.5.
  • EMG in CIM — short-duration MUPs ± fibrillations.
Q C-30
Infant botulism presentation and bedside confirmation.
Show Answer
  • Age 2 weeks–12 months.
  • Constipation, weak cry, poor feeding, hypotonia, ophthalmoplegia, descending paralysis.
  • Bedside RNS — low resting CMAP, >60 % incremental response at 50 Hz or post-exercise facilitation.
  • Treat with BabyBIG immediately.

Block D — Remaining Case Bank (randomized)

CASE D-1
14-year-old post-Fontan completion has new right foot drop on POD 1. Lateral decubitus during surgery 9 h.
Q. Diagnosis, expected EDX, management.
Show Answer
  • Peroneal mononeuropathy at fibular head.
  • EDX after 7–10 days — conduction block or low CMAP across fibular head; denervation in tibialis anterior and peroneus longus.
  • Splint to prevent contracture, position care, follow recovery; most resolve over weeks–months.
CASE D-2
16-year-old female with fluctuating ptosis, fatigable proximal weakness, dysphagia, FVC 20 mL/kg. AChR antibody pending.
Q. Expected EDX and ICU plan.
Show Answer
  • RNS at 3 Hz — >10 % decrement.
  • SFEMG — increased jitter ± blocking.
  • Manage as myasthenic crisis — intubate proactively, IVIG 2 g/kg or PLEX.
  • Pyridostigmine cautiously.
  • Start steroids with monitoring for transient worsening, steroid-sparing agent for maintenance.
CASE D-3
Term neonate with profound hypotonia, club feet, polyhydramnios history. Mother shakes hands slowly with delayed release.
Q. Diagnosis, expected EMG, parental implication.
Show Answer
  • Congenital myotonic dystrophy (DM1) inherited from mother.
  • EMG in mother — myotonic "dive-bomber" discharges.
  • Neonate often has hypotonia without classic myotonia.
  • Genetic DMPK CTG-repeat testing.
  • Counsel for autosomal-dominant inheritance with anticipation.
CASE D-4
4-year-old after camping presents with 24 h of ascending paralysis, areflexia, ataxia. CSF normal.
Q. Crucial step and expected EDX.
Show Answer
  • Full body skin inspection for an attached tick (scalp, axillae, groin, behind ears).
  • EDX — low CMAP amplitudes with preserved velocity.
  • Tick removal is both diagnostic and curative; weakness reverses within hours.
CASE D-5
3-month-old, ex-32-weeker, with worsening tachypnea and unilateral diaphragmatic elevation on CXR. Birth weight <3rd centile.
Q. Differential, EDX, confirmatory test.
Show Answer
  • SMARD1 (IGHMBP2) vs. diaphragmatic eventration vs. acquired phrenic injury.
  • Phrenic nerve conduction with intercostal / diaphragm EMG.
  • IGHMBP2 gene testing for confirmation.
  • SMARD1 mandates long-term ventilatory support.
CASE D-6
10-year-old with severe asthma exacerbation, intubated 10 days, paralyzed 5 days, high-dose methylprednisolone, fails to wean. Proximal weakness, normal reflexes.
Q. EDX picture and key differentiator.
Show Answer
  • Acute necrotizing / steroid + NMBA myopathy.
  • EDX — low CMAP, normal SNAP, myopathic MUPs ± fibrillations, low neCMAP/dmCMAP ratio.
  • CK elevated.
  • Distinguished from CIP by normal SNAP and intact direct muscle response.
CASE D-7
5-year-old develops areflexia, ataxia, ophthalmoplegia 10 days after viral illness.
Q. Diagnosis and confirmatory antibody.
Show Answer
  • Miller-Fisher syndrome (GBS variant).
  • Anti-GQ1b IgG positive in >90 %.
  • EDX — reduced SNAPs and absent H-reflex with preserved motor NCS.
  • Most recover with supportive care ± IVIG.
CASE D-8
8-year-old farm child presents with miosis, hypersalivation, fasciculations, and respiratory failure. CMAPs show repetitive discharges after single supramaximal stimulus.
Q. Diagnosis, mechanism, treatment.
Show Answer
  • Organophosphate poisoning — acetylcholinesterase inhibition with persistent ACh at the NMJ.
  • Repetitive CMAP firing reflects persistent ACh.
  • Atropine titration; pralidoxime; decontamination.
  • Avoid succinylcholine (markedly prolonged paralysis).
  • Monitor for intermediate syndrome 24–96 h later.
CASE D-9
9-year-old with episodic limb weakness after exercise, normal between episodes. Family history of similar episodes.
Q. Differential and useful EDX test.
Show Answer
  • Periodic paralysis (hypoKPP, hyperKPP, Andersen-Tawil).
  • McManis long-exercise test — CMAP drop >40 % after prolonged exercise.
  • Genetic testing — CACNA1S, SCN4A, KCNJ2.
  • Acute treatment depends on serum K direction.
CASE D-10
11-year-old post-septic shock at day 21 has persistent flaccid quadriparesis. EDX: ↓ SNAPs and ↓ CMAPs symmetrically, normal CV, EMG denervation.
Q. CIP or AIDP — what features point to CIP?
Show Answer
  • Normal velocity (no demyelination).
  • Symmetric distal axonal pattern.
  • Sepsis history; no preceding infection or autonomic features.
  • Treat as CIP — prevention bundle, rehab, no IVIG.
  • Reassess if velocity slows or F-waves disappear.
CASE D-11
2-month-old post-Norwood fails extubation 3 times. Right hemidiaphragm elevated. Sniff test paradoxical movement.
Q. EDX and role of plication.
Show Answer
  • Phrenic nerve conduction shows absent or low CMAP on the affected side.
  • If persistent at 2–4 weeks and weaning failed, consider diaphragmatic plication.
  • EDX provides objective documentation supporting the surgical decision.
CASE D-12
14-year-old with vasculitic illness develops painful, stepwise individual nerve palsies. EDX shows individual nerves with conduction block at random sites.
Q. Pattern recognition and underlying mechanism.
Show Answer
  • Mononeuritis multiplex — vasculitic axonal neuropathy from ischemic infarction of vasa nervorum.
  • Look for systemic vasculitis — ANCA-associated, polyarteritis nodosa.
  • Treat with immunosuppression.
  • EDX documents each affected nerve.
CASE D-13
7-year-old with influenza, severe leg pain, CK 60 000 U/L, dark urine.
Q. Is EDX necessary, and what would it show?
Show Answer
  • Usually not necessary — clinical + CK + viral PCR sufficient.
  • If done — small-amplitude short-duration polyphasic MUPs with fibrillations (myopathy).
  • EDX adds value if presentation atypical or if MG / GBS in the differential.
CASE D-14
Known MG patient receives erythromycin for pneumonia, becomes more weak, requires intubation.
Q. Mechanism and remedy.
Show Answer
  • Macrolides interfere with NMJ transmission.
  • Stop offending drug; support ventilation; treat as crisis with IVIG or PLEX.
  • Add to home and ICU avoid-list.
CASE D-15
Infant with persistent weakness improves on pyridostigmine then worsens dramatically.
Q. Likely CMS subtype and why the paradoxical response.
Show Answer
  • Slow-channel CMS or DOK7 / COLQ CMS — anticholinesterases worsen these subtypes.
  • Switch to fluoxetine / quinidine (slow channel) or salbutamol / ephedrine (DOK7).
  • Genetic panel confirms subtype before tailoring therapy.
CASE D-16
9-year-old weaned from venoarterial ECMO after meningococcal sepsis. Persistent hypercapnia despite normal lungs. Diaphragm thin on ultrasound.
Q. Mechanism and EDX role.
Show Answer
  • Diaphragm disuse atrophy plus CIP affecting phrenic nerve.
  • Phrenic conduction shows reduced CMAP.
  • Progressive weaning with diaphragm training (T-piece trials, NIV bridges), nutritional optimization.
CASE D-17
10-year-old, 4 months post-AMAN GBS, remains quadriplegic with severe distal weakness, fibrillations on EMG, no reinnervated MUPs.
Q. Prognostic implication and next step.
Show Answer
  • Persistent denervation without reinnervation at 4 months indicates poor prognosis for that nerve.
  • Consider nerve transfer surgery for upper-limb function.
  • Aggressive rehab and vocational planning.
  • EDX informs realistic family counselling.
CASE D-18
15-year-old develops sudden bilateral leg weakness after argument. EDX entirely normal. Reflexes present, give-way pattern.
Q. Approach.
Show Answer
  • Functional / dissociative motor disorder.
  • Normal EDX plus inconsistent exam signs (Hoover, dragging gait).
  • Multidisciplinary management with PT and mental health support.
  • Avoid invasive workup once diagnosis is secure.
CASE D-19
8-year-old, 2 weeks post-SARS-CoV-2 infection, develops areflexic ascending paralysis. EDX shows demyelinating features.
Q. Diagnosis and management.
Show Answer
  • Post-COVID AIDP.
  • IVIG 2 g/kg or PLEX; ICU monitoring for respiratory and autonomic failure.
  • Pediatric outcomes similar to other parainfectious GBS.
CASE D-20
12-year-old presents with severe symmetric proximal weakness, CK 25 000, EMG with abundant fibrillations plus small short-duration MUPs.
Q. Differential and antibodies.
Show Answer
  • Immune-mediated necrotizing myopathy.
  • Anti-HMGCR or anti-SRP antibodies.
  • High-dose steroids, IVIG, methotrexate or rituximab.
  • EDX confirms myopathic pattern; MRI directs biopsy.
CASE D-21
5-year-old after prolonged ECMO for refractory cardiomyopathy. Weak, areflexic in all limbs, ICU day 30.
Q. EDX features and rehab plan.
Show Answer
  • CIP — reduced SNAPs and CMAPs with preserved velocity, EMG denervation.
  • Aggressive PT/OT; sit up by day 1 of stability; daily sedation interruption.
  • Tracheostomy if prolonged ventilator course.
  • Vitamin / nutrition support.
  • Expect partial recovery over months.
CASE D-22
8-year-old after a contaminated wound develops trismus, opisthotonus, and reflex spasms.
Q. EDX feature distinguishing tetanus from other paralytic disorders.
Show Answer
  • Continuous motor unit activity at rest with loss of normal silent period after a contraction.
  • Reflects failure of Renshaw / glycinergic inhibition.
  • Treat — metronidazole, human tetanus IG, benzodiazepines, NMBA if needed, intensive supportive care.
CASE D-23
PICU 6-week stay; on extubation review, serial CMAP amplitudes from peroneal nerve have declined over 2 weeks despite improving sedation scores.
Q. Interpretation and action.
Show Answer
  • Progressive axonal loss — likely evolving CIP or CIM.
  • Tighten ICU-AW prevention bundle.
  • Evaluate for ongoing sepsis or steroid burden.
  • Plan slower weaning strategy with rehab integration.
