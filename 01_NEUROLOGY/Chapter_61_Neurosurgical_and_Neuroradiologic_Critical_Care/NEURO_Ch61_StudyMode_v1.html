CHAPTER 61 ■ Neurosurgical and Neuroradiologic Critical Care — Key Points

Key points distilled
  • The PICU is a direct extension of the neurosurgical OR and interventional neuroradiology suite — communication is paramount.
  • Familiarity with neuromonitoring (ICP, EVD, NIRS, EEG, TCD) is mandatory.
  • Postoperative dysnatremias (SIADH, CSW, DI) are the highest-yield complications.
  • Special challenges — brain tumor surgery, hydrocephalus, cerebrovascular surgery, epilepsy surgery, craniosynostosis.
RCPSC relevance 🔥🔥🔥
  • Examiners frame postoperative complications and dysnatremias as a multi-part stem — recognize, distinguish, treat.
Pages 3174–3180 · Anesthetic and Intraoperative Considerations

Anesthetic Considerations

Anesthetic goals
  • Stable hemodynamics, adequate gas exchange, immobility, analgesia.
  • Cerebral protection — maintain CPP, normocapnia (PaCO₂ 35–40 mmHg), avoid hyperthermia and hypoxia.
  • Smooth emergence — avoid coughing, bucking, hypertension that may trigger bleeding.
Choice of agents
  • Volatile anesthetics — dose-dependent vasodilation; cerebral autoregulation impaired >1 MAC.
  • Propofol — cerebral vasoconstrictor; reduces CMR; ideal for brain-protective infusion in selected cases.
  • Opioids — short-acting (remifentanil) preferred to allow rapid neuro exam after surgery.
  • Avoid succinylcholine if chronic immobilization or denervation (hyperkalemia).
  • Avoid prolonged anesthesia in <3 y per FDA developmental neuroapoptosis warning.
Trap
  • Nitrous oxide — avoid in posterior fossa surgery (tension pneumocephalus risk after dural closure).
  • Hypothermia from prolonged surgery — increases coagulopathy and infection risk; maintain normothermia.

Intraoperative Hemodynamic Management

  • Maintain age-appropriate MAP and CPP.
  • Avoid hypotension during induction (vagal stimulation, vasodilation, hypovolemia).
  • Vasopressors — phenylephrine, norepinephrine first-line; avoid pure α-agonists in raised ICP because reflex bradycardia ↓ CPP.
  • Blood loss can be brisk in posterior fossa / scoliosis / craniosynostosis — large IV access, blood products available.
  • Hyperoxia avoided routinely; FiO₂ titrated to PaO₂ 80–100 mmHg.
Pages 3181–3187 · Imaging and Surgical Approach

Intraoperative + Postoperative Imaging

  • Intraoperative MRI / CT (iMRI / iCT) — guides resection extent, identifies residual tumor.
  • Neuronavigation — preoperative MRI / DTI integrated into surgical microscope.
  • Awake mapping for eloquent cortex in older cooperative children.
  • Postoperative CT — first-pass for hemorrhage, hydrocephalus, mass effect.
  • Postoperative MRI within 48 h — gold standard for residual tumor assessment (avoid postsurgical enhancement after 72 h).

Surgical Considerations

  • Positioning — prone (posterior fossa, spine), sitting (posterior fossa in older child — venous air embolism risk), supine (most supratentorial).
  • Mayfield pins in <2 y — caution given thin skull; use horseshoe headrest.
  • Venous air embolism (sitting position) — sudden ↓ ETCO₂, hypotension, arrhythmia; treat with flooding field, Trendelenburg, left lateral decubitus, aspirate from central venous catheter.
  • Surgical bleeding — bipolar cautery, hemostatic agents (FloSeal, Surgicel, Gelfoam).
Pages 3188–3192 · Emergence and Delayed Awakening

Emergence Considerations

Smooth emergence priorities
  • Avoid coughing / bucking — triggers hypertension, increases bleeding risk.
  • Lidocaine 1 mg/kg IV, remifentanil-titrated, dexmedetomidine bridge — all reduce cough.
  • Allow rapid neuro exam — minimal residual sedation when possible.
  • Adequate analgesia — undertreated pain → hypertension → bleeding.

Delayed Awakening — Differential

Factors to consider
  • Residual anesthetic / sedative / opioid effect.
  • Residual neuromuscular blockade — confirm with train-of-four.
  • Hypothermia — slows drug metabolism.
  • Hyper- or hypoglycemia, hyponatremia, hyperammonemia.
  • Postoperative hemorrhage / edema / pneumocephalus / hydrocephalus.
  • Cerebral ischemia (vascular injury, vasospasm, embolus).
  • Seizure — postictal state, NCSE.
  • Posterior fossa syndrome (cerebellar mutism syndrome).
  • Pre-existing encephalopathy.

Intentional Delayed Emergence

  • Indications — anticipated airway swelling, severe hemodynamic instability, intentional therapeutic coma (refractory ICP, status), prolonged complex surgery.
  • Maintain sedation in PICU; plan extubation when stable.
  • Continuous EEG and ICP monitoring during sedation.
Pages 3193–3197 · Structured Handoff and Postop Care

Structured Handoff (OR → PICU)

Essential elements
  • Procedure performed, intraoperative findings, extent of resection.
  • Anesthesia agents and reversal status.
  • Hemodynamic course — blood loss, transfusions, vasoactives.
  • Fluid balance, lines and drains, dressings.
  • Anticipated complications — bleeding, seizure, dysnatremia, hydrocephalus.
  • Postoperative goals — sedation plan, CPP target, ventilation, EVD output.
  • Pain management plan.
  • Family communication and follow-up imaging timing.

Routine Postoperative Care

ICU layer
  • Head-of-bed 30°; midline head position.
  • Normothermia (36–37.5 °C); aggressive fever control.
  • Normoglycemia (target 5.5–10 mmol/L); avoid tight control.
  • Normocapnia (PaCO₂ 35–40 mmHg).
  • Avoid hypotension and hyperoxia.
  • Seizure prophylaxis case-by-case — supratentorial tumor resection, cortical AVM.
  • VTE prophylaxis — mechanical first, pharmacologic when bleeding risk acceptable.
  • DVT screening in immobile / paretic patients.
  • Antibiotic prophylaxis usually 24–48 h.
Pages 3198–3204 · Brain Tumor Surgery + Hydrocephalus

Postoperative Brain Tumor Care

  • Posterior fossa syndrome (cerebellar mutism syndrome) — 8–31 % after midline posterior fossa surgery; appears 1–4 days postoperatively.
  • Features — mutism, hypotonia, ataxia, behavioral changes, swallowing dysfunction.
  • Mostly transient (weeks–months); residual speech / cognitive impairment in some.
  • Treatment — supportive; speech therapy; some report benefit from fluoxetine, bromocriptine, dextroamphetamine.
  • Hydrocephalus from tumor obstruction — may need preoperative or postoperative CSF drainage.
  • Endocrine complications — pan-hypopituitarism after pituitary / craniopharyngioma surgery.
  • Diabetes insipidus common after pituitary / craniopharyngioma.

Hydrocephalus Treatment

Options
  • External ventricular drain (EVD) — temporary; allows ICP monitoring and CSF drainage.
  • VP (ventriculoperitoneal) shunt — most common chronic option.
  • VA (ventriculoatrial) shunt — alternative when peritoneal cavity not suitable.
  • Lumbo-peritoneal shunt — communicating hydrocephalus only.
  • Endoscopic third ventriculostomy (ETV) — obstructive (aqueductal stenosis); reduces shunt dependence.
  • ETV + choroid plexus cauterization (CPC) — increasing use in infants.
Shunt complications
  • Infection — 5–15 % rate; staphylococci common; treat with antibiotics + externalization.
  • Obstruction — proximal (ventricular catheter), distal (peritoneum), or valve.
  • Disconnection / fracture.
  • Overdrainage — slit ventricle syndrome, subdural hematoma.
  • Migration, abdominal pseudocyst.
  • Catheter-related thrombosis (VA shunt).
Pearl 🔥🔥🔥
  • Shunt malfunction in a child with headache + vomiting + altered mental status → emergent CT and neurosurgery consult.
  • Shunt series X-ray — disconnection / fracture along the tubing.
  • "Shunt tap" via reservoir provides ICP and CSF for analysis.
Pages 3205–3210 · Cerebrovascular and Epilepsy Surgery

Cerebrovascular Surgery

AVM resection / embolization
  • Risk of normal perfusion pressure breakthrough (NPPB) — hyperemia in previously hypoperfused tissue → hemorrhage.
  • Postoperative BP control — avoid hypertension; nicardipine or labetalol infusion.
  • Continuous EEG — seizure risk.
  • Watch for vasospasm in the surrounding territory.
Moyamoya revascularization
  • Direct bypass — STA-MCA anastomosis (older children).
  • Indirect bypass — encephaloduroarteriosynangiosis (EDAS), pial synangiosis (younger children).
  • Avoid hypotension, hyperventilation, dehydration, hyperthermia — all trigger ischemia.
  • Liberal IV fluids; aspirin postoperatively.
  • Treat pain to avoid sympathetic activation.
Aneurysm coiling / clipping
  • Pediatric aneurysms rare; often infectious, traumatic, dissecting.
  • Postoperative — monitor for vasospasm (TCD), rebleeding, hydrocephalus.
  • Nimodipine for vasospasm prophylaxis (extrapolated from adult).

Epilepsy Surgery

  • Indications — refractory focal epilepsy with localized substrate.
  • Procedures — temporal lobectomy, lesionectomy, hemispherectomy, corpus callosotomy, vagal nerve stimulation, responsive neurostimulation (RNS), deep brain stimulation (DBS).
  • Postoperative — continuous EEG, anticipate seizures, watch for stroke / hemorrhage / hydrocephalus.
  • Hemispherectomy — fluid balance challenges, infection risk, may need EVD.
  • Withdraw AEDs only after sustained seizure freedom; not in immediate postop period.
Pages 3211–3215 · Craniosynostosis + Spinal Surgery

Craniosynostosis Surgery

  • Premature fusion of one or more cranial sutures.
  • Single suture (non-syndromic) — sagittal (scaphocephaly, commonest), coronal (plagiocephaly), metopic (trigonocephaly), lambdoid (rare).
  • Multi-suture / syndromic — Crouzon, Apert, Pfeiffer, Saethre-Chotzen, Muenke.
  • Procedures — strip craniectomy + helmet (<6 mo), open cranial vault remodeling, endoscopic strip with helmet, spring-assisted, distraction osteogenesis.
  • Major perioperative concerns — blood loss (can exceed circulating volume), air embolism, hypothermia.
  • Strategies — cell saver, tranexamic acid, careful fluid management, blood product availability.

Spinal Surgery (Neurosurgical Aspects)

  • Scoliosis correction in NMD — large blood loss, intraoperative neurophysiologic monitoring (SSEP, MEP).
  • Wake-up test less used now; SSEP / MEP are mainstay.
  • Postoperative ileus and atelectasis common; aggressive bowel and pulmonary regimens.
  • Cord injury after correction is rare but devastating — emergent MRI and decompression if signal changes.

Trap 🔥🔥🔥
  • Misdiagnosing CSW as SIADH and restricting fluid worsens cerebral perfusion and can precipitate infarction.
  • Volume status is the key discriminator — examine, weigh, check CVP, hematocrit, BUN.
  • Rapid sodium correction risks osmotic demyelination — limit to 8–10 mEq/L per 24 h.

Triple-Phase Response (after pituitary / craniopharyngioma surgery)

  • Phase 1 (first ~24 h) — central DI from posterior pituitary stalk injury.
  • Phase 2 (days 2–7) — release of stored ADH → transient SIADH; hyponatremia risk if continued DDAVP.
  • Phase 3 (after day 7) — permanent DI as stored ADH depleted; ongoing DDAVP needed.
  • Recognize phases — sudden change in urine output / sodium guides therapy adjustment.

Neuroprotective Measures for TBI

  • Same principles as Chapter 57 — age-appropriate CPP, normocapnia, normothermia, normoglycemia, hyperosmolar therapy.
  • Tier 1 — sedation, analgesia, HOB elevation, hyperosmolar.
  • Tier 2 — neuromuscular blockade, mild hypothermia, EVD, decompressive craniectomy.
  • Detailed in Chapter 57 (Molecular Biology) and Chapter 59 (Monitoring).

Equations & thresholds

CPP = MAP − ICP. Age-adjusted targets — infant ≥40, toddler ≥50, child ≥60, adolescent ≥70 mmHg.
Sodium correction limit. ≤8–10 mEq/L per 24 h. Faster correction risks osmotic demyelination.
Free water deficit (acute hypernatremia from DI). Free water deficit (L) = total body water × ([Na actual / Na desired] − 1). Replace half over 24 h.
DDAVP dosing. 0.05–0.4 mcg IV/SC/IN per dose; titrate to urine output and sodium response.
3 % hypertonic saline. 3–5 mL/kg over 10–15 min for symptomatic hyponatremia; raise Na ~3–5 mEq/L per dose, repeat as needed.

③ Pediatric VP Shunt Care — HCRN (Hydrocephalus Clinical Research Network)

  • Source: Kulkarni AV et al. J Neurosurg Pediatr. 2017; HCRN registry publications
  • Link: HCRN: hcrn.org (Seattle)
  • Key findings:
    • Antibiotic-impregnated catheters and chlorhexidine bundle reduce shunt infection.
    • ETV + choroid plexus cauterization viable alternative to VP shunt in selected infants.

④ Moyamoya Pediatric Consensus

  • Source: Smith ER et al. (BCH Boston experience); Scott RM, Smith ER. N Engl J Med. 2009;360:1226–1237 (foundational); recent updates 2020–2024
  • Link: DOI 10.1056/NEJMra0804622
  • Key findings:
    • Revascularization indicated to prevent stroke.
    • Direct STA-MCA bypass in older / collaboration; indirect (EDAS, pial synangiosis) in younger.
    • Perioperative care — avoid hypotension, hyperventilation, dehydration, hyperthermia, sympathetic activation.
    • Aspirin postoperatively.

⑤ Cerebral Salt Wasting in Pediatric Neurosurgery

  • Source: Cerda-Esteve M et al. (CSW reviews); Berkenbosch JW. Pediatr Crit Care Med. 2002 (foundational)
  • Key findings:
    • CSW occurs in 11–45 % of pediatric TBI / subarachnoid hemorrhage / postneurosurgical patients.
    • Often misdiagnosed as SIADH; restricting fluid is dangerous.
    • Volume status is key discriminator.
    • Treatment — sodium and volume replacement; fludrocortisone for refractory.

⑥ Tranexamic Acid in Pediatric Craniosynostosis Surgery

  • Source: Goobie SM et al. Anesthesiology. 2011;114:862–871; subsequent pediatric series
  • Link: DOI 10.1097/ALN.0b013e318210fd8f
  • Key findings:
    • Tranexamic acid significantly reduces blood loss and transfusion requirement in pediatric craniosynostosis surgery.
    • Now part of routine care in many centers.
    • Cell saver adjunct; preoperative anemia management.

⑦ Pediatric Pituitary Surgery — Triple-Phase Response

  • Source: Müller HL et al. Pediatr Blood Cancer. 2019 (craniopharyngioma); Wise-Faberowski L. Best Pract Res Clin Anaesthesiol. 2007
  • Key findings:
    • Triphasic response in 1–3 % of pituitary surgery — early DI, transient SIADH, permanent DI.
    • Frequent monitoring of urine output, sodium, osmolality.
    • DDAVP and fluid management adjusted at each phase transition.
    • Anterior pituitary hormone replacement evaluated postoperatively.

⑧ Pediatric Aneurysm and AVM Management

  • Source: Sorteberg A, Dahlberg D. Childs Nerv Syst. 2013; pediatric ICH guidelines AHA 2019
  • Key findings:
    • Pediatric aneurysms rare; often infectious, traumatic, dissecting.
    • AVM management — endovascular embolization, microsurgery, radiosurgery, or staged combination.
    • Normal perfusion pressure breakthrough risk after AVM resection — strict BP control.

5. ICU Decision Engine

IF Postoperative neurosurgical patient with rising urine output and rising Na
THEN
  • Suspect diabetes insipidus (especially pituitary / craniopharyngioma surgery).
  • Check urine specific gravity (<1.005), urine osm (<300), serum osm (high).
  • DDAVP 0.05–0.4 mcg IV/SC/IN per dose, titrate.
  • Match urine output losses with hypotonic fluid (D5W).
  • Monitor Na q2–4 h; correct slowly.
IF Postoperative hyponatremia
THEN
  • Assess volume status — euvolemic (SIADH) vs. hypovolemic (CSW).
  • SIADH — fluid restrict; 3 % saline only if symptomatic.
  • CSW — replace sodium AND volume; fludrocortisone if refractory.
  • Misdiagnosis is dangerous — examine, weigh, check CVP, BUN, Hct.
IF Postoperative VP shunt patient with new headache, vomiting, altered LOC
THEN
  • Suspect shunt malfunction.
  • Emergent CT and shunt series X-ray.
  • Neurosurgery consult.
  • Consider shunt tap for ICP and CSF analysis.
  • Empiric antibiotics if infection signs.
IF Pediatric moyamoya patient pre/postoperative
THEN
  • Avoid hypotension, hyperventilation, dehydration, hyperthermia.
  • Liberal IV fluids.
  • Aspirin postoperatively.
  • Treat pain to avoid sympathetic activation.
  • Monitor for ischemic stroke — frequent neurological exams.
IF Posterior fossa surgery patient with new mutism + hypotonia + ataxia 1–4 days postop
THEN
  • Posterior fossa syndrome (cerebellar mutism).
  • Mostly transient (weeks–months).
  • Supportive care, speech therapy.
  • Family counseling about typical course.
  • Some report benefit from fluoxetine, bromocriptine, dextroamphetamine.

6. SAQ Bank — 40 Questions

SAQ 1. Outline the structured handoff bundle from OR to PICU.
Answer.
  • Procedure performed, intraoperative findings, extent of resection.
  • Anesthesia agents and reversal status.
  • Hemodynamic course — blood loss, transfusions, vasoactives.
  • Fluid balance, lines and drains, dressings.
  • Anticipated complications.
  • Postoperative goals — sedation plan, CPP target, ventilation, EVD output.
  • Pain management plan.
  • Family communication and follow-up imaging timing.
SAQ 2. Distinguish SIADH from cerebral salt wasting.
Answer.
  • SIADH — euvolemic/hypervolemic; UOP normal or low; urine Na >40; treat with fluid restriction.
  • CSW — hypovolemic; UOP elevated (polyuria); urine Na >100; BUN ↑, Hct ↑; treat with sodium AND volume replacement; fludrocortisone for refractory.
  • Volume status is the key discriminator.
  • Restricting fluid in CSW is dangerous (cerebral hypoperfusion).
SAQ 3. Triple-phase response after pituitary / craniopharyngioma surgery.
Answer.
  • Phase 1 (0–24 h) — central DI from posterior pituitary stalk injury.
  • Phase 2 (days 2–7) — release of stored ADH → transient SIADH.
  • Phase 3 (after day 7) — permanent DI as stored ADH depleted.
  • Adjust DDAVP and fluids at each transition.
  • Anterior pituitary hormones also need replacement evaluation.
SAQ 4. Diagnose diabetes insipidus postoperatively.
Answer.
  • Polyuria (UOP >4 mL/kg/h sustained).
  • Urine specific gravity <1.005 (dilute).
  • Urine osmolality <300 mOsm/kg.
  • Serum sodium and osmolality rising.
  • DDAVP 0.05–0.4 mcg IV/SC/IN per dose; titrate.
  • Match urine output losses with hypotonic fluid (D5W).
SAQ 5. Posterior fossa syndrome (cerebellar mutism).
Answer.
  • Mutism / reduced speech + cerebellar / brainstem signs after midline posterior fossa surgery.
  • Incidence 8–31 %.
  • Onset 1–4 days postoperatively.
  • Features — mutism, hypotonia, ataxia, behavioral changes, dysphagia.
  • Mostly transient (weeks–months); residual deficits in some.
  • Supportive care, speech therapy.
SAQ 6. VP shunt malfunction recognition and workup.
Answer.
  • Symptoms — headache, vomiting, irritability, altered mental status, papilledema, gaze palsies, bradycardia (Cushing's).
  • Workup — emergent CT (ventricle size), shunt series X-ray (disconnection, fracture).
  • Shunt tap for ICP and CSF analysis.
  • Neurosurgery consult.
  • Empiric antibiotics if infection signs.
SAQ 7. Common shunt complications.
Answer.
  • Infection (5–15 %) — staphylococci common; treat with antibiotics + externalization.
  • Obstruction — proximal, distal, or valve.
  • Disconnection / fracture.
  • Overdrainage — slit ventricle, subdural hematoma.
  • Migration, abdominal pseudocyst.
  • Catheter-related thrombosis (VA shunt).
SAQ 8. Moyamoya perioperative care.
Answer.
  • Avoid hypotension, hyperventilation, dehydration, hyperthermia, sympathetic activation.
  • Liberal IV fluids.
  • Aspirin postoperatively.
  • Treat pain aggressively.
  • Monitor for ischemic stroke — frequent neuro exams.
  • Revascularization — direct (STA-MCA) or indirect (EDAS, pial synangiosis).
SAQ 9. Venous air embolism management.
Answer.
  • Sudden ↓ ETCO₂, hypotension, arrhythmia, mill-wheel murmur.
  • Risk — sitting posterior fossa surgery.
  • Flood surgical field with saline.
  • Trendelenburg + left lateral decubitus.
  • Aspirate air from central venous catheter.
  • FiO₂ 100 %.
  • Support hemodynamics.
SAQ 10. Tiered ICP management cascade.
Answer.
  • Baseline — HOB 30°, midline head, sedation, normothermia, normocapnia, normoglycemia.
  • Tier 1 — CSF drainage (EVD); hypertonic saline; mannitol; deepen sedation; treat seizures.
  • Tier 2 — NMBA; brief hyperventilation (with PbtO₂ monitoring); mild hypothermia 35 °C; pentobarbital coma; decompressive craniectomy.
  • Throughout — re-image to rule out evolving lesion.
SAQ 11. Craniosynostosis types and surgery.
Answer.
  • Sagittal — scaphocephaly (most common).
  • Coronal — anterior plagiocephaly.
  • Metopic — trigonocephaly.
  • Lambdoid — posterior plagiocephaly (rare).
  • Syndromic — Crouzon, Apert, Pfeiffer, Saethre-Chotzen, Muenke.
  • Procedures — strip craniectomy + helmet (<6 mo), open vault remodel, endoscopic, spring-assisted.
SAQ 12. Major perioperative concerns in craniosynostosis surgery.
Answer.
  • Blood loss — can exceed circulating volume.
  • Venous air embolism.
  • Hypothermia.
  • Strategies — cell saver, tranexamic acid, careful fluid management, blood products available.
SAQ 13. AVM surgery normal perfusion pressure breakthrough.
Answer.
  • After AVM resection — hyperemia in previously hypoperfused tissue.
  • Risk of hemorrhage and edema.
  • Strict BP control postoperatively (nicardipine, labetalol).
  • Continuous EEG for seizures.
  • Watch for vasospasm.
SAQ 14. Anesthetic agent considerations in neurosurgery.
Answer.
  • Volatile anesthetics — vasodilation; autoregulation impaired >1 MAC.
  • Propofol — cerebral vasoconstrictor; reduces CMR.
  • Opioids — short-acting (remifentanil) preferred for rapid neuro exam.
  • Avoid succinylcholine in chronic immobilization / denervation.
  • Avoid prolonged anesthesia <3 y (FDA warning).
  • Avoid nitrous oxide in posterior fossa (tension pneumocephalus).
SAQ 15. Postoperative care priorities after neurosurgery.
Answer.
  • HOB 30°; midline head.
  • Normothermia, normoglycemia, normocapnia.
  • Avoid hypotension and hyperoxia.
  • Seizure prophylaxis case-by-case.
  • VTE prophylaxis — mechanical first.
  • Antibiotic prophylaxis 24–48 h.
  • Pain management.
SAQ 16. Differential for delayed awakening after neurosurgery.
Answer.
  • Residual anesthetic / sedative / opioid effect.
  • Residual NMBA.
  • Hypothermia.
  • Metabolic — glucose, sodium, ammonia.
  • Postoperative hemorrhage / edema / pneumocephalus / hydrocephalus.
  • Cerebral ischemia (vascular injury, vasospasm).
  • Seizure / NCSE.
  • Posterior fossa syndrome.
  • Pre-existing encephalopathy.
SAQ 17. Indications for ETV / CPC.
Answer.
  • Obstructive hydrocephalus (aqueductal stenosis, tumor).
  • Endoscopic third ventriculostomy creates CSF outflow via floor of third ventricle.
  • Choroid plexus cauterization reduces CSF production.
  • Reduces lifelong shunt dependence.
  • Increasing use in infants — selected based on imaging and prognostic score.
SAQ 18. Hemispherectomy postoperative concerns.
Answer.
  • Fluid balance challenges — large dead space, evaporative losses.
  • Infection risk; may need EVD.
  • Hemiparesis, hemianopia — expected; rehabilitation.
  • Continuous EEG for residual seizures.
  • AED management — taper later, not in immediate postop.
SAQ 19. Intraoperative neurophysiologic monitoring in spinal surgery.
Answer.
  • SSEP — somatosensory evoked potentials (posterior columns).
  • MEP — motor evoked potentials (corticospinal tract).
  • EMG — nerve root monitoring.
  • Wake-up test largely replaced by SSEP/MEP.
  • Changes trigger immediate intervention (release distraction, transfuse, MAP support).
SAQ 20. Pediatric aneurysm postoperative care.
Answer.
  • Monitor for vasospasm — TCD daily.
  • Nimodipine for vasospasm prophylaxis (extrapolated from adult).
  • Maintain euvolemia and adequate CPP.
  • Watch for rebleeding (first 24 h) and hydrocephalus.
  • Continuous EEG for seizures.
SAQ 21. Why is hyponatremia common after neurosurgery?
Answer.
  • SIADH from surgical stress, opioids, pain.
  • CSW from brain natriuretic peptide release.
  • Hypotonic IV fluids (avoid maintenance D5/0.225 % NaCl in this population).
  • Pituitary surgery — triphasic response transient SIADH.
  • Recognition + correct diagnosis crucial.
SAQ 22. Limit on sodium correction rate.
Answer.
  • ≤8–10 mEq/L per 24 h.
  • Faster correction risks osmotic demyelination (central pontine myelinolysis).
  • If over-corrected, can re-lower with D5W ± DDAVP.
  • Long-term — quadriparesis, dysphagia, locked-in syndrome.
SAQ 23. Symptomatic hyponatremia treatment.
Answer.
  • 3 % hypertonic saline 3–5 mL/kg over 10–15 min.
  • Repeat as needed to raise Na ~3–5 mEq/L per dose.
  • Target Na rise ≤8–10 mEq/L per 24 h.
  • Treat seizures with benzodiazepines.
  • Investigate cause — SIADH vs. CSW vs. hypotonic IV fluid.
SAQ 24. Hyperosmolar therapy comparison (HTS vs. mannitol).
Answer.
  • Hypertonic saline (3 % continuous or 23.4 % bolus) — first-line in pediatric TBI.
  • Less rebound; maintains intravascular volume; better in shock.
  • Mannitol 0.5–1 g/kg — osmotic diuresis; may cause hypovolemia.
  • Both need intact BBB; monitor osmolality and renal function.
SAQ 25. Outline emergence considerations after neurosurgery.
Answer.
  • Avoid coughing / bucking — hypertension and bleeding risk.
  • Lidocaine 1 mg/kg IV, remifentanil titration, dexmedetomidine.
  • Allow rapid neuro exam.
  • Adequate analgesia — undertreated pain → hypertension → bleeding.
SAQ 26. Indications for intentional delayed extubation.
Answer.
  • Anticipated airway swelling.
  • Severe hemodynamic instability.
  • Intentional therapeutic coma (refractory ICP, status).
  • Prolonged complex surgery.
  • Continuous EEG and ICP monitoring during sedation.
SAQ 27. Posterior fossa surgery — special anesthetic concerns.
Answer.
  • Position — prone (most common); sitting (older child, posterior fossa exposure).
  • Venous air embolism risk (sitting).
  • Avoid nitrous oxide (tension pneumocephalus after dural closure).
  • Brainstem manipulation — bradycardia, hypotension.
  • Posterior fossa syndrome risk.
SAQ 28. Postoperative seizure prophylaxis after neurosurgery.
Answer.
  • Case-by-case — supratentorial tumor resection, cortical AVM, epilepsy surgery, severe TBI.
  • Levetiracetam first-line — fewer interactions, well tolerated.
  • Phenytoin/fosphenytoin if needed urgently.
  • Duration usually 7 days unless seizures occur.
  • Continuous EEG for high-risk patients.
SAQ 29. CSF leak postoperative management.
Answer.
  • Recognize — clear / pink drainage from wound, rhinorrhea, otorrhea.
  • Confirm with β2-transferrin.
  • Conservative — bed rest, head elevation, lumbar drain.
  • Surgical repair if persistent.
  • Risk of meningitis — empiric antibiotics if signs.
SAQ 30. Endocrine evaluation after pituitary / craniopharyngioma surgery.
Answer.
  • Anterior pituitary hormones — cortisol, thyroid, growth hormone, gonadotropins, prolactin.
  • Posterior pituitary — ADH function (DI screening).
  • Stress-dose hydrocortisone perioperatively if known or suspected hypopituitarism.
  • Replace deficient hormones — cortisol, levothyroxine, GH, sex steroids as needed.
  • Endocrinology consult.
SAQ 31. Postoperative imaging timing after neurosurgery.
Answer.
  • Immediate CT — hemorrhage, hydrocephalus, mass effect.
  • MRI within 48 h — residual tumor (avoid postsurgical enhancement after 72 h).
  • Follow-up MRI per oncology protocol.
  • Stat imaging for any clinical deterioration.
SAQ 32. Why avoid nitrous oxide in posterior fossa surgery?
Answer.
  • N₂O is 30× more soluble than nitrogen.
  • Diffuses into air-filled spaces and expands them.
  • After dural closure, residual air in supratentorial space can cause tension pneumocephalus.
  • Postoperative — delayed awakening, seizures, focal deficits.
  • Substitute with air-oxygen or volatile anesthetic.
SAQ 33. Pediatric epilepsy surgery options.
Answer.
  • Temporal lobectomy — for mesial temporal sclerosis.
  • Lesionectomy — focal cortical dysplasia, tumor.
  • Hemispherectomy — hemispheric epilepsy (Rasmussen, hemimegalencephaly).
  • Corpus callosotomy — drop attacks.
  • Vagal nerve stimulation, responsive neurostimulation, deep brain stimulation — palliative.
SAQ 34. Tranexamic acid in pediatric craniosynostosis.
Answer.
  • Antifibrinolytic agent.
  • Significantly reduces blood loss and transfusion requirement.
  • Standard dose 10–15 mg/kg load + 1 mg/kg/h infusion or 10 mg/kg q4–6h.
  • Routine in many pediatric craniosynostosis programs.
  • Cell saver, preoperative anemia management, blood product availability as adjuncts.
SAQ 35. EVD care principles.
Answer.
  • Sterile insertion with chlorhexidine bundle and antibiotic-impregnated catheter.
  • Zero at level of tragus / external auditory canal.
  • Drain to specified level above tragus (e.g., 10 cm) — gravity-dependent.
  • Daily CSF sampling for cell count, glucose, protein, culture.
  • Infection risk increases after day 5 — limit duration.
  • Tunneled or antibiotic-impregnated for prolonged use.
SAQ 36. AVM treatment options.
Answer.
  • Endovascular embolization — feeders and nidus.
  • Microsurgical resection — accessible eloquent or non-eloquent locations.
  • Stereotactic radiosurgery — small, deep, eloquent.
  • Often staged combination.
  • Postoperative — strict BP control, EEG, vasospasm watch.
SAQ 37. Postoperative pain management after neurosurgery.
Answer.
  • Acetaminophen scheduled — opioid sparing.
  • Avoid NSAIDs in immediate postop (bleeding risk).
  • Opioids — short-acting (fentanyl), titrated to allow neuro exam.
  • Avoid sedation that masks deterioration.
  • Local anesthetic infiltration / scalp blocks reduce opioid need.
  • Dexmedetomidine adjunct.
SAQ 38. Why is hyperthermia harmful postoperatively?
Answer.
  • Increases cerebral metabolic rate.
  • Increases glutamate release and oxidative stress.
  • Disrupts BBB.
  • Each 1 °C above normal worsens secondary injury.
  • Aggressively treat — acetaminophen, surface cooling, target normothermia 36.0–37.5 °C.
SAQ 39. VTE prophylaxis after pediatric neurosurgery.
Answer.
  • Mechanical first — sequential compression devices.
  • Pharmacologic — LMWH when bleeding risk acceptable (typically 24–48 h post-op).
  • Risk-stratify — adolescent, immobile, paretic, cancer.
  • DVT screening with ultrasound in high-risk.
  • Mobilization as soon as safe.
SAQ 40. Future directions in pediatric neurosurgical critical care.
Answer.
  • Multimodal neuromonitoring integration (CPPopt, PbtO₂, NIRS, cEEG).
  • Robotic / minimally invasive procedures.
  • Real-time intraoperative imaging (iMRI, fluorescence-guided).
  • Endoscopic / awake / less-invasive approaches.
  • Standardized handoff and care bundles.
  • Machine learning for postoperative complication prediction.

7. Case Bank — 40 PICU Cases

Case 1 — Postop DI after craniopharyngioma.
12-year-old POD 1 after craniopharyngioma resection; UOP 8 mL/kg/h, Na 152, urine SG 1.002.
Q. Management.
Answer.
  • Central DI — Phase 1 of triple-phase response.
  • DDAVP 0.05–0.1 mcg IV.
  • Match UOP losses with D5W.
  • Monitor Na q2–4 h.
  • Anticipate Phase 2 (SIADH) on day 2–7 — reduce or hold DDAVP as urine output drops.
Case 2 — Postop hyponatremia.
14-year-old POD 3 after midline posterior fossa tumor resection; Na 124, UOP 4 mL/kg/h, urine Na 130, BUN 22, Hct 41 %.
Q. Diagnosis and management.
Answer.
  • Cerebral salt wasting — hypovolemic hyponatremia with high urine Na and elevated BUN/Hct.
  • Replace sodium AND volume — isotonic or 3 % saline.
  • Fludrocortisone for refractory.
  • Do NOT fluid restrict — worsens cerebral hypoperfusion.
Case 3 — Posterior fossa syndrome.
8-year-old POD 2 after medulloblastoma resection; previously alert, now mute, hypotonic, with ataxic features.
Q. Diagnosis and management.
Answer.
  • Posterior fossa syndrome (cerebellar mutism).
  • Typical onset 1–4 days postoperatively.
  • Supportive — speech therapy, PT/OT.
  • Family counseling — mostly transient (weeks–months).
  • Consider fluoxetine / bromocriptine / dextroamphetamine if prolonged.
Case 4 — VP shunt malfunction.
10-year-old VP shunt patient with new headache, vomiting, diplopia.
Q. Workup.
Answer.
  • Emergent CT — assess ventricle size.
  • Shunt series X-ray — disconnection / fracture.
  • Neurosurgery consult.
  • Consider shunt tap for ICP and CSF.
  • Manage raised ICP (HOB, hyperosmolar) while definitive intervention planned.
Case 5 — VP shunt infection.
3-year-old with VP shunt placed 2 weeks ago; fever, lethargy, cell count from shunt tap 800 with low glucose.
Q. Management.
Answer.
  • Shunt infection (often staphylococci).
  • Externalize shunt — replace with EVD.
  • Empiric vancomycin + cefepime or meropenem (gram-negative cover).
  • CSF culture and sensitivities.
  • Intrathecal antibiotics if needed.
  • Re-insert shunt after CSF sterilized.
Case 6 — Moyamoya post-op.
7-year-old POD 1 after STA-MCA bypass for moyamoya; develops dehydration, hypotension during sleep.
Q. Action.
Answer.
  • Avoid hypotension, hyperventilation, dehydration, hyperthermia — all trigger ischemia.
  • Liberal IV fluids; treat hypovolemia.
  • Aspirin postoperatively.
  • Treat pain.
  • Frequent neuro exams for stroke detection.
Case 7 — Venous air embolism.
9-year-old in sitting position for pineal tumor resection; sudden ↓ ETCO₂ from 35 to 18, SBP drops, arrhythmia.
Q. Diagnosis and management.
Answer.
  • Venous air embolism.
  • Flood surgical field with saline.
  • Trendelenburg + left lateral decubitus.
  • Aspirate air from central venous catheter.
  • FiO₂ 100 %.
  • Support hemodynamics with fluids and vasopressors.
Case 8 — AVM resection with NPPB.
16-year-old POD 0 after large AVM resection; new headache, hypertension, new focal weakness.
Q. Diagnosis and management.
Answer.
  • Normal perfusion pressure breakthrough.
  • Emergent CT — rule out hemorrhage.
  • Strict BP control — nicardipine or labetalol infusion.
  • Continuous EEG.
  • Manage cerebral edema if present.
Case 9 — Craniosynostosis blood loss.
6-month-old undergoing open cranial vault remodeling; estimated blood loss 200 mL of 700 mL circulating volume.
Q. Strategies.
Answer.
  • Tranexamic acid — load + infusion.
  • Cell saver if available.
  • Blood products in OR — PRBC, FFP, platelets ready.
  • Maintain temperature (warmer, fluid warmers).
  • Coagulation factor replacement guided by labs / point-of-care testing.
Case 10 — Hemispherectomy.
5-year-old POD 1 after hemispherectomy for Rasmussen encephalitis; fluid shifts and electrolyte changes.
Q. Postop care.
Answer.
  • Strict fluid balance — large dead space.
  • Monitor sodium closely.
  • Continuous EEG.
  • EVD if hydrocephalus.
  • Anticipate hemiparesis, hemianopia.
  • Pain control without oversedation.
  • AED continuation (don't taper).
Case 11 — Posterior fossa hydrocephalus.
4-year-old POD 0 after posterior fossa tumor resection; develops worsening LOC and bradycardia.
Q. Action.
Answer.
  • Acute hydrocephalus from blood / debris / edema obstructing CSF flow.
  • Emergent CT.
  • EVD placement.
  • Manage ICP (HOB, hyperosmolar) en route.
  • Neurosurgery consult.
Case 12 — Delayed awakening.
14-year-old POD 0 after meningioma resection fails to wake; pupils equal and reactive.
Q. Differential and workup.
Answer.
  • Residual anesthetic, sedative, opioid — confirm reversal status, train-of-four.
  • Hypothermia — warm.
  • Metabolic — glucose, sodium, ammonia.
  • Postop hemorrhage / hydrocephalus / pneumocephalus — emergent CT.
  • Seizure / NCSE — continuous EEG.
  • Pre-existing encephalopathy.
Case 13 — Symptomatic hyponatremia with seizures.
8-year-old POD 2 with seizure; Na 122.
Q. Acute management.
Answer.
  • 3 % hypertonic saline 3–5 mL/kg over 10–15 min.
  • Target Na rise ~3–5 mEq/L per dose; repeat as needed for symptom control.
  • Benzodiazepine for seizure.
  • Continuous EEG.
  • Identify cause — SIADH vs. CSW vs. hypotonic IVF.
  • Total correction ≤8–10 mEq/L per 24 h.
Case 14 — Osmotic demyelination.
Adolescent with chronic hyponatremia (Na 118) corrected too rapidly by 18 mEq/L in first 24 h; develops quadriparesis and dysphagia 3 days later.
Q. Diagnosis.
Answer.
  • Osmotic demyelination syndrome (central pontine myelinolysis).
  • Correction limit ≤8–10 mEq/L per 24 h.
  • If over-corrected, re-lower with D5W ± DDAVP.
  • Long-term — quadriparesis, dysphagia, "locked-in."
Case 15 — Postop cerebral edema.
11-year-old POD 0 after glioma resection; CT shows surgical cavity with surrounding edema; ICP 25 on EVD.
Q. Management.
Answer.
  • Baseline measures — HOB 30°, sedation, normocapnia, normothermia.
  • Tier 1 — CSF drainage via EVD; hypertonic saline 3 % bolus; mannitol 0.5–1 g/kg.
  • Dexamethasone if vasogenic edema (tumor).
  • Treat seizures.
  • Tier 2 if refractory — NMBA, mild hypothermia, decompressive craniectomy.
Case 16 — Postop pneumocephalus.
12-year-old POD 0 after posterior fossa surgery; CT shows large supratentorial air collection with mass effect.
Q. Management.
Answer.
  • Tension pneumocephalus — avoid N₂O (worsens).
  • Position with head low / supine.
  • FiO₂ 100 % to wash out N₂.
  • Neurosurgery — consider emergent decompression if symptomatic.
  • Most resolves spontaneously over days.
Case 17 — Pituitary surgery — Phase 2.
Adolescent POD 4 after pituitary tumor resection; previous DI now resolved; Na dropping to 128 with high urine osm.
Q. Diagnosis and adjustment.
Answer.
  • Phase 2 of triple-phase response — transient SIADH from stored ADH release.
  • Reduce or hold DDAVP.
  • Mild fluid restriction.
  • Monitor Na q2–4 h.
  • Anticipate Phase 3 (permanent DI) — UOP will rise again after day 7.
Case 18 — Refractory ICP after TBI surgery.
9-year-old POD 0 after subdural evacuation; ICP 32 despite tier 1 + tier 2 therapy.
Q. Next steps.
Answer.
  • Reconfirm ICP signal validity (waveform, calibration).
  • Optimize CPP per PRx-derived CPPopt.
  • Repeat imaging to rule out evolving lesion.
  • Pentobarbital coma — burst suppression target.
  • Mild hypothermia 35 °C.
  • Decompressive craniectomy.
  • Family meeting with palliative care if refractory.
Case 19 — CSF leak after spinal surgery.
Adolescent POD 3 after Chiari decompression; pillow soaked with clear pinkish fluid.
Q. Workup and management.
Answer.
  • CSF leak — confirm with β2-transferrin.
  • Bed rest, HOB flat or 10°.
  • Lumbar drain to divert CSF.
  • Surgical repair if persistent.
  • Watch for meningitis — empiric antibiotics if signs.
Case 20 — Postop seizure.
14-year-old POD 1 after frontal tumor resection has a generalized tonic-clonic seizure.

Answer.
  • Benzodiazepine to abort.
  • Levetiracetam load + maintenance.
  • Continuous EEG.
  • Emergent CT to rule out new lesion.

Case 21 — Postop infection.
8-year-old POD 7 after posterior fossa surgery; fever, headache, neck stiffness.

Case 22 — Aneurysm post-coiling vasospasm.
15-year-old POD 5 after coiling of ruptured aneurysm; new aphasia and right hemiparesis.

Case 23 — Postop pituitary apoplexy.

Case 24 — Spinal surgery cord signal change.

10. Final One-Page Infographic

🧠 Pediatric Neurosurgical Critical Care — Master Map

① Dysnatremia at-a-glance

  • SIADH — Na ↓, euvolemic, U Na >40 — fluid restrict.
  • CSW — Na ↓, hypovolemic, U Na >100 — replace Na + volume.
  • DI — Na ↑, hypovolemic, dilute urine — DDAVP + free water.
  • Volume status is the key discriminator.

② Triple-phase response (pituitary surgery)

  • Phase 1 (0–24 h) — DI (stalk injury).
  • Phase 2 (days 2–7) — transient SIADH (stored ADH release).
  • Phase 3 (after day 7) — permanent DI.

③ Critical thresholds

  • CPP — infant ≥40, toddler ≥50, child ≥60, adolescent ≥70.
  • Na correction ≤8–10 mEq/L per 24 h.
  • DDAVP 0.05–0.4 mcg IV/SC/IN; titrate.
  • 3 % saline 3–5 mL/kg over 10–15 min for symptomatic hyponatremia.
  • TXA in craniosynostosis — 10–15 mg/kg load + infusion.

④ Red flags

  • Postop polyuria + rising Na = DI.
  • Postop hyponatremia with hypovolemia = CSW (don't fluid restrict!).
  • Sudden ↓ ETCO₂ in sitting surgery = venous air embolism.
  • Post-AVM hypertension = NPPB risk — strict BP control.
  • Shunt patient + headache + vomiting = malfunction.
  • POD 1–4 posterior fossa + mutism = cerebellar mutism syndrome.

⑤ Exam pearls

🔥🔥🔥 Volume status separates SIADH from CSW. Don't restrict CSW.
🔥🔥🔥 Triple-phase response — three transitions to anticipate.
🔥🔥🔥 Avoid N₂O in posterior fossa surgery.
🔥🔥 Moyamoya — avoid hypotension, hyperventilation, dehydration, hyperthermia.
🔥🔥 Sodium correction limit ≤10 mEq/L per 24 h — osmotic demyelination risk.
🔥 Mayfield pins avoided <2 y.
