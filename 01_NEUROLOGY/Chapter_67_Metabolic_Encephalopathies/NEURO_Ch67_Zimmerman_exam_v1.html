Block A — Mock RCPSC SAQ (10 questions, 12 minutes)

A1. Empiric antibiotic regimens by age for suspected bacterial meningitis.

Show answer
  • < 1 month: ampicillin + cefotaxime (or cefepime) ± aminoglycoside (cover GBS, E. coli, Listeria, Klebsiella).
  • 1 month – 5 years: vancomycin + 3rd-gen cephalosporin (ceftriaxone/cefotaxime) — cover S. pneumoniae, N. meningitidis, Hib.
  • > 5 years: vancomycin + 3rd-gen cephalosporin.
  • VP shunt / post-neurosurgical: vancomycin + ceftazidime/cefepime/meropenem (cover Pseudomonas).
  • Add acyclovir 60 mg/kg/day in any patient with altered mental status until HSV excluded.
  • Time-to-antibiotic delay → ~30% increase in poor outcome per hour.

A2. List six classic CSF parameters that distinguish bacterial from viral meningitis.

Show answer
  • WBC count: bacterial > 1000 (PMN); viral 10–500 (lymphocyte).
  • Protein: bacterial > 100 mg/dL; viral normal or mild ↑.
  • Glucose: bacterial < 50% serum value; viral normal.
  • Gram stain: bacterial positive 50–75%; viral negative.
  • Lactate: bacterial > 3.5 mmol/L; viral normal.
  • PCR: bacterial FilmArray ME panel; viral HSV, enterovirus, EBV PCR.
  • Note: enterovirus and mumps can cause hypoglycorrhachia despite viral etiology.

A3. An 8-year-old presents with fever, focal seizure of right face, and temporal lobe T2/FLAIR hyperintensity on MRI. Diagnose and manage.

Show answer
  • HSV encephalitis suspected.
  • Acyclovir 60 mg/kg/day IV divided q8h × 21 days.
  • Start empirically — do not wait for PCR confirmation.
  • Monitor renal function; maintain hydration.
  • cEEG; treat seizures with lev 40–60 mg/kg.
  • Repeat CSF HSV PCR before stopping.
  • Untreated mortality ~70%; treated < 20% but with significant sequelae.

A4. When and how is adjunctive dexamethasone used in bacterial meningitis?

Show answer
  • 0.15 mg/kg IV q6h × 2–4 days (max 10 mg/dose).
  • Strongest evidence in Hib meningitis (Lebel 1988 — reduced sensorineural hearing loss).
  • AAP supports use in pneumococcal meningitis.
  • Give BEFORE or WITH first antibiotic dose for maximal effect.
  • Not used in viral meningitis or neonatal meningitis.

A5. A 14-year-old has subacute psychiatric symptoms, orofacial dyskinesia, seizures, autonomic instability. Discuss.

Show answer
  • Anti-NMDA receptor encephalitis suspected.
  • CSF anti-NMDAR antibody; lymphocytic pleocytosis; MRI often normal.
  • Pelvic US for ovarian teratoma (adolescent female).
  • First-line: methylprednisolone 30 mg/kg/day × 5 + IVIG 2 g/kg or plasma exchange.
  • Second-line: rituximab 375 mg/m²/week × 4; cyclophosphamide.
  • Long ICU course (months); supportive care for autonomic dysfunction; eventual good recovery with sustained treatment.

A6. Outline ADEM clinical features and treatment.

Show answer
  • Acute disseminated encephalomyelitis — monophasic post-infectious/post-vaccination demyelination.
  • Onset 2–4 weeks post-trigger.
  • Encephalopathy + multifocal neurologic deficits.
  • MRI: multifocal poorly demarcated white matter, basal ganglia, thalami, brainstem lesions.
  • CSF: lymphocytic pleocytosis, elevated protein; oligoclonal bands less common.
  • Check anti-MOG antibody.
  • Treatment: methylprednisolone 30 mg/kg/day × 5 d, then oral taper; IVIG 2 g/kg or PLEX if refractory.
  • Recurrence raises suspicion for MS or MOGAD.

A7. 12-year-old with sinusitis develops new headache, vomiting, focal deficit. MRI shows crescent-shaped subdural collection with rim enhancement and DWI restriction.

Show answer
  • Subdural empyema from sinusitis.
  • Surgical emergency — neurosurgery for drainage + ENT for sinus drainage.
  • Empiric: vancomycin + ceftriaxone + metronidazole (Streptococcus milleri group + anaerobes).
  • Anti-epileptic therapy.
  • ICU monitoring; 4–6 weeks IV antibiotics.

A8. Brain abscess imaging signature and treatment.

Show answer
  • MRI: ring-enhancing lesion with central DWI restriction (distinguishes from tumor with central necrosis but no DWI restriction).
  • Surrounding vasogenic edema.
  • Treatment: surgical drainage (CT-guided aspiration or open craniotomy) + 4–8 weeks IV antibiotics.
  • Empiric: vancomycin + ceftriaxone + metronidazole.
  • Source identification: sinusitis, otitis, dental, endocarditis, hematogenous (right-to-left shunt).

A9. 3-year-old with 2 weeks of fever and behavioral change, now with cranial nerve palsies and hydrocephalus. CSF shows lymphocytic pleocytosis, very high protein, low glucose.

Show answer
  • Tuberculous meningitis (TBM).
  • GeneXpert MTB/RIF, AFB stain, TB culture.
  • MRI: basal cistern enhancement, possible infarcts (basal ganglia), tuberculomas.
  • Treatment: 4-drug ATT (isoniazid + rifampin + pyrazinamide + ethambutol) × 2 months, then INH + RIF continuation phase 7–10 months.
  • Adjunctive dexamethasone reduces mortality (Thwaites 2004).
  • Pyridoxine to prevent INH neuropathy.
  • EVD/VP shunt for hydrocephalus.

A10. Post-bacterial-meningitis follow-up — what is essential?

Show answer
  • Audiology assessment at 4 weeks post-discharge (sensorineural hearing loss 10–30% in pneumococcal).
  • Neurodevelopmental assessment at 6 and 12 months.
  • Vaccine review and catch-up.
  • Family education on long-term sequelae (epilepsy, cognitive impairment, motor deficits).
  • Outpatient infectious disease and neurology follow-up.
  • Rehabilitation referral if deficits.

Block B — Oral Cases (5 sequential, 35 min)

B1. 5-year-old presents with 12-hour history of fever, headache, vomiting. On arrival lethargic, GCS 11. Petechial rash noted on lower extremities.

B1a — Initial actions.
  • ABCs, isotonic IVF, blood cultures × 2.
  • Activate sepsis bundle.
  • Empiric ceftriaxone (and vancomycin) within 1 hour — suspect meningococcemia/meningitis.
  • Add acyclovir if any concern for HSV.
  • Bedside glucose; full electrolytes, coags, lactate, blood gas, CBC.
  • Inform infection control (isolation).
B1b — LP timing decision.
  • If no focal signs and patient stable: proceed with LP.
  • If altered mental status or focal signs: CT first to exclude mass effect.
  • Do NOT delay antibiotics for LP.
  • If LP deferred: blood culture and proceed empirically; LP later when safe.
B1c — Develops hypotension and worsening rash.
  • Septic shock from meningococcemia.
  • Aggressive fluid resuscitation (20 mL/kg boluses, balanced crystalloid).
  • Vasopressors early — norepinephrine first-line.
  • Consider hydrocortisone for refractory shock (Waterhouse-Friderichsen).
  • DIC management (cryo, FFP, platelets per labs).
  • Continue ceftriaxone; isolation.
B1d — Family asks about close contacts.
  • Public health notification (notifiable disease).
  • Chemoprophylaxis for household and close contacts (intimate contacts, daycare).
  • Options: rifampin 600 mg q12h × 2 days (weight-based in children); ceftriaxone 250 mg IM × 1 (safe in pregnancy); ciprofloxacin single dose (adults).
  • Coverage period: 7 days before symptom onset to discharge.

B2. 3-week-old neonate with fever 38.3°C, poor feeding, full anterior fontanelle, irritable.

B2a — Initial workup and management.
  • Full septic workup: blood, urine, CSF cultures.
  • Empiric ampicillin + cefotaxime (or cefepime if cefotaxime unavailable).
  • Add acyclovir if vesicles, maternal HSV history, or sepsis-like presentation in young infant.
  • HSV PCR on CSF.
  • HSV-2 maternal history?
  • NICU/PICU admission for close monitoring.
B2b — CSF cell count returns 850 PMN, glucose 1.2 mmol/L, protein 350 mg/dL.
  • Bacterial meningitis confirmed pattern.
  • Continue empiric antibiotics.
  • Avoid dexamethasone in neonate.
  • Repeat LP at 48 h if gram-negative (verify CSF sterility).
  • Audiology pre-discharge.
  • Watch for hydrocephalus, seizures, SIADH.
B2c — Develops seizures on day 3.
  • Phenobarbital 20 mg/kg IV first-line (neonatal SE).
  • cEEG monitoring.
  • Repeat imaging — concern for evolving infarct/empyema/ventriculitis.
  • Check for hyponatremia (SIADH/CSW).
  • Pyridoxine trial if refractory.
B2d — Final culture: E. coli with extended-spectrum β-lactamase. Plan.
  • Switch to meropenem.
  • 3 weeks minimum antibiotic duration for gram-negative neonatal meningitis.
  • Repeat LP to confirm CSF sterility.
  • Audiology before discharge.
  • Neurodevelopmental follow-up.

B3. 10-year-old presents with subacute behavioral changes (psychiatric symptoms, hallucinations) for 3 weeks, then develops orofacial dyskinesias and a generalized seizure.

B3a — Working diagnosis and workup.
  • Anti-NMDA receptor encephalitis suspected.
  • LP for CSF anti-NMDAR antibody, cell count, glucose, protein, viral PCR.
  • Serum anti-NMDAR antibody; paraneoplastic panel.
  • MRI brain with contrast — often normal or mild mesial temporal T2 hyperintensity.
  • cEEG (extreme delta brush pattern can be seen).
  • Pelvic ultrasound for ovarian teratoma (girls).
B3b — Develops autonomic instability and refractory seizures.
  • ICU admission.
  • Start methylprednisolone 30 mg/kg/day × 5 days + IVIG 2 g/kg.
  • Plasma exchange if no early response.
  • Treat seizures aggressively; cEEG continuously.
  • Manage autonomic instability (BP swings, hypoventilation).
  • If ovarian teratoma found, surgical resection.
B3c — Persistent severe symptoms after 2 weeks of first-line immunotherapy.
  • Escalate to second-line: rituximab 375 mg/m²/week × 4 + cyclophosphamide 750 mg/m².
  • Continue IVIG / PLEX as needed.
  • Long ICU course expected (often months).
  • Maintain seizure control; supportive care for autonomic dysfunction.
  • Family meeting and rehab planning.
B3d — Long-term plan after eventual ICU recovery.
  • Maintenance immunosuppression for 12–24 months (rituximab, mycophenolate).
  • Cognitive and psychiatric rehabilitation.
  • Annual surveillance for teratoma recurrence if applicable.
  • Neuropsychology assessment.
  • Relapse risk ~12%.

B4. 6-year-old with VP shunt (placed 8 months ago for congenital hydrocephalus) develops fever, vomiting, irritability.

B4a — Workup.
  • Shunt malfunction or infection.
  • Imaging: head CT (compare to prior).
  • Shunt series for disconnection.
  • Abdomen imaging for distal complications.
  • Neurosurgery consult for shunt tap.
  • CSF from shunt: cell count, Gram stain, culture, glucose, protein, lactate.
B4b — CSF: WBC 250 with lymphocyte predominance, glucose 2.4 mmol/L, protein 80 mg/dL, Gram stain negative.
  • Compatible with shunt infection (often coag-negative staph or other indolent).
  • Empiric: vancomycin + ceftazidime/cefepime/meropenem (cover Pseudomonas).
  • Neurosurgery for shunt externalization.
  • Treat for 10–21 days based on organism.
  • Reinternalize when CSF sterile.
B4c — Culture grows Cutibacterium acnes after 7 days.
  • Common shunt infection organism (especially > 6 months post-insertion).
  • Susceptible to penicillin or vancomycin.
  • Continue treatment 10–14 days post-CSF sterility.
  • Replace shunt when CSF sterile.
B4d — Discuss prevention.
  • Antibiotic-impregnated catheters reduce infection rates.
  • Strict sterile technique.
  • Limit unnecessary shunt access.
  • Pre-op prophylactic antibiotics.
  • Patient/family education on warning signs.

B5. 11-year-old with sudden encephalopathy and multifocal weakness, 3 weeks after viral respiratory illness. MRI shows multifocal subcortical white matter + thalamic lesions.

B5a — Diagnosis and initial management.
  • ADEM (acute disseminated encephalomyelitis).
  • ICU admission.
  • Methylprednisolone 30 mg/kg/day × 5 days then oral taper.
  • LP: lymphocytic pleocytosis, elevated protein.
  • Anti-MOG antibody (MOGAD distinction).
  • Brain MRI with contrast; spine MRI to rule out transverse myelitis.
  • cEEG if seizures.
B5b — Patient deteriorates despite steroids.
  • Add IVIG 2 g/kg total over 2–5 days.
  • If still refractory, plasma exchange.
  • Consider acute hemorrhagic leukoencephalitis (Weston-Hurst) — severe variant; cyclophosphamide may be needed.
  • Rehab consult; supportive care.
B5c — Eventually recovers but with motor deficits.
  • Inpatient rehabilitation.
  • Neurology follow-up.
  • Repeat MRI at 3–6 months to confirm resolution.
  • Recurrence → consider MS or MOGAD diagnosis; serial MRI.
  • If anti-MOG positive, MOGAD-specific monitoring and treatment.
B5d — Family asks about prognosis.
  • ADEM is usually monophasic with good recovery.
  • Most children recover substantially with rehab.
  • Long-term sequelae may include epilepsy, cognitive issues, motor deficits.
  • Recurrence (multiphasic ADEM, MS, or MOGAD) requires lifelong monitoring.

Block C — Randomized SAQ Drill (20)

C1. Classic meningitis triad and frequency in pediatrics.

Show
  • Fever + neck stiffness + altered mental status; present in < 50% of pediatric cases.

C2. Empiric Abx neonate.

Show
  • Ampicillin + cefotaxime (or cefepime) ± aminoglycoside.

C3. Empiric Abx 1 mo – 5 y.

Show
  • Vancomycin + 3rd-gen cephalosporin.

C4. CSF shunt empiric Abx.

Show
  • Vancomycin + ceftazidime/cefepime/meropenem (cover Pseudomonas).

C5. Dexamethasone dosing and timing.

Show
  • 0.15 mg/kg IV q6h × 2–4 d; ideally before or with first antibiotic.

C6. Acyclovir HSV encephalitis dose.

Show
  • 60 mg/kg/day IV divided q8h × 21 days.

C7. HSV MRI signature.

Show
  • Bilateral asymmetric medial temporal + inferior frontal T2/FLAIR ↑; DWI restriction; petechial hemorrhage on GRE.

C8. ADEM treatment.

Show
  • Methylprednisolone 30 mg/kg/day × 5 d, taper; IVIG or PLEX if refractory.

C9. Anti-NMDAR first-line treatment.

Show
  • Methylprednisolone + IVIG; plasma exchange; rituximab/cyclophosphamide second-line; teratoma resection.

C10. Brain abscess MRI vs tumor.

Show
  • Abscess: ring enhancement + central DWI restriction. Tumor: ring enhancement, central necrosis, NO DWI restriction.

C11. Subdural empyema source and organism.

Show
  • Frontal/ethmoidal sinusitis; Streptococcus milleri/intermedius group; surgical drainage + IV antibiotics.

C12. TBM 4-drug regimen.

Show
  • Isoniazid + rifampin + pyrazinamide + ethambutol (or streptomycin) × 2 months; INH + RIF continuation 7–10 months.

C13. TBM adjunctive therapy reducing mortality.

Show
  • Dexamethasone (Thwaites NEJM 2004); also pyridoxine to prevent INH toxicity.

C14. Cryptococcal meningitis treatment.

Show
  • Liposomal amphotericin B + flucytosine induction; fluconazole consolidation + maintenance; therapeutic LPs for ICP.

C15. Time-to-antibiotic significance.

Show
  • ~30% increase in poor outcome per hour of delay; goal < 1 hour from suspicion.

C16. Hib meningitis dexamethasone benefit.

Show
  • Reduced sensorineural hearing loss (Lebel NEJM 1988).

C17. Meningococcal chemoprophylaxis.

Show
  • Rifampin (children/adults); ceftriaxone IM (pregnancy-safe); ciprofloxacin (adults).

C18. Pediatric audiology follow-up post-meningitis.

Show
  • Audiology assessment at 4 weeks post-discharge (10–30% pneumococcal SNHL).

C19. Anti-NMDAR teratoma search.

Show
  • Pelvic ultrasound (and abdominal MRI) in adolescent females; testicular germinoma rarely in males.

C20. Rabies post-exposure prophylaxis.

Show
  • Rabies immunoglobulin 20 IU/kg infiltrated wound + IM; rabies vaccine series day 0, 3, 7, 14; wound care; tetanus prophylaxis.

Show

D15. 17-yo encephalitis, panel pending, refractory seizures.

Show
  • Empiric methylpred + IVIG + continue acyclovir; cEEG; sedation; tumor search; long ICU.

Show

Show

D18. 5-yo meningococcemia + DIC.

Show
  • Aggressive resuscitation; ceftriaxone; DIC products; Waterhouse-Friderichsen cover; public health.

D19. 13-yo refractory NMDAR + ovarian teratoma.

Show
  • Teratoma resection; rituximab + cyclophosphamide; continued methylpred/IVIG/PLEX.

D20. 2-yo dog bite, behavior change 8 weeks later.

Show
  • Symptomatic rabies — fatal; airborne + contact precautions; palliative care; Milwaukee protocol case-by-case.
