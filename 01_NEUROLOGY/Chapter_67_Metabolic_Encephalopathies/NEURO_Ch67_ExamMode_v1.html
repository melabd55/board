Chapter 67 — Metabolic Encephalopathies — EXAM MODE

Rogers' Textbook of Pediatric Intensive Care, Chapter 67 (Ducharme-Crevier, Jouvet, Du Pont-Thibodeau). Classification: DISEASE / ICU. Adaptive distribution: 40 SAQs + 40 PICU cases. EXAM MODE: answers hidden. Time yourself in Blocks A and B.

How to use:

  • Block B — 5 sequential oral cases (40 minutes). Self-grade with Block E rubric.
  • Block C — 30 randomized SAQs (drill mode).

Block A — Mock RCPSC SAQ (10 questions, 12 minutes)

A1. List the three pathophysiologic categories of metabolic encephalopathy with two examples of each.

Show answer
  • Endogenous intoxication: urea cycle disorders, organic acidurias, hepatic encephalopathy, Reye syndrome, Wilson disease.
  • Energy failure: hypoglycemia (hyperinsulinism, GSD, FAOD), mitochondrial defects (Leigh, MELAS, PDH deficiency), thiamine deficiency, BBGD.
  • Water/electrolyte/endocrine: DKA, hyponatremia, hypernatremia, hypocalcemia, hypomagnesemia, thyroid storm, burn encephalopathy.

A2. A 3-week-old neonate has serum ammonia 750 µmol/L and progressive encephalopathy. Outline acute management.

Show answer
  • Stop all protein intake immediately.
  • Provide caloric support: D10 + intralipid; insulin if hyperglycemia develops.
  • Ammonia scavengers IV: sodium benzoate + sodium phenylacetate (Ammonul) loading 250 mg/kg each over 90 min, then maintenance.
  • L-arginine 600 mg/kg IV bolus then infusion (omit if arginase deficiency).
  • L-carnitine; B12 if MMA suspected; biotin trial.
  • Emergent CRRT — indicated for NH₃ > 500 µmol/L OR rapidly rising despite scavengers.
  • Neuroprotection: HOB 30°, hyperosmolar (3% NaCl) for cerebral edema, cEEG.
  • Avoid valproate; treat seizures with phenobarbital or levetiracetam.
  • Engage metabolic genetics; send plasma amino acids, urine organic acids, acylcarnitine profile, urine orotic acid.

A3. An 8-year-old in DKA develops headache, bradycardia, and hypertension 8 hours into treatment. Diagnose and manage.

Show answer
  • Cerebral edema — Cushing's response with bradycardia and hypertension; classic timing 4–12 h into DKA treatment.
  • 3% NaCl 5–10 mL/kg IV over 30 min OR mannitol 0.5–1 g/kg IV.
  • Reduce IV fluid rate by approximately one-third.
  • Head of bed elevated 30°; midline neck.
  • Reduce insulin if hypoglycemia developing.
  • Intubate + brief controlled hyperventilation as rescue if herniation imminent.
  • Urgent CT scan once stable; neurosurgical consult if structural concern.
  • Per ISPAD 2022: gradual fluid resuscitation framework prevents this complication.

A4. A 2-year-old presents with chronic vomiting, lethargy, and a serum Na of 118 mmol/L with a seizure. Manage and explain correction limits.

Show answer
  • Acute symptomatic hyponatremia — 3% NaCl 2–4 mL/kg IV bolus over 10–20 min.
  • Target Na rise of 4–6 mmol/L to relieve symptoms.
  • Continue gradual correction NOT exceeding 8–10 mmol/L in 24 h.
  • Investigate cause: SIADH, CSW, water intoxication, hypotonic IVF, adrenal insufficiency.
  • Risk: osmotic demyelination syndrome (pontine and extrapontine) presents 2–6 days after rapid correction with dysarthria, dysphagia, paraparesis.
  • Higher risk in chronic hyponatremia, hypokalemia, alcoholism, malnutrition, liver disease.

A5. An 11-year-old on long-term TPN develops nystagmus and confusion. Explain the diagnosis and why thiamine must be given before glucose.

Show answer
  • Wernicke encephalopathy from inadequate thiamine in TPN.
  • Classic triad: ophthalmoplegia, ataxia, confusion (full triad in <20% of cases).
  • Glucose alone increases thiamine demand (cofactor for pyruvate dehydrogenase, transketolase) and may precipitate or worsen Wernicke.
  • Thiamine 5–10 mg/kg IV BEFORE glucose; continue daily until resolution.
  • Pediatric risk groups: chronic vomiting, oncology, TPN without supplementation, post-bariatric surgery, refeeding syndrome, malnutrition.
  • MRI may show medial thalamic, mammillary body, periaqueductal lesions.

A6. A 9-year-old develops sudden right hemiparesis. MRI shows cortical lesion crossing vascular boundaries with lactate 8 mmol/L. Discuss working diagnosis and treatment.

Show answer
  • MELAS (Mitochondrial Encephalomyopathy with Lactic Acidosis and Stroke-like episodes).
  • Common mutation: m.3243A>G in MT-TL1.
  • Imaging distinguishes from true ischemic stroke — lesions cross vascular territories.
  • Acute treatment: L-arginine 0.5 g/kg IV bolus + 0.5 g/kg/day continuous infusion for 3–5 days.
  • Maintenance: oral L-arginine.
  • Avoid valproate — mitochondrial toxicity.
  • Mitochondrial cocktail (CoQ10, carnitine, riboflavin, thiamine, vitamin C).
  • Genetic confirmation; family counseling.

A7. A 2-year-old has subacute encephalopathy with bilateral caudate and putamen T2 hyperintensities. Discuss empiric therapy and rationale.

Show answer
  • Empiric trial of biotin 5–10 mg/kg/day + thiamine 20–40 mg/kg/day for biotin-responsive basal ganglia disease (BBGD).
  • SLC19A3 thiamine transporter mutation.
  • Dramatic recovery if treated early; preserves long-term function.
  • Differential includes Leigh syndrome, glutaric aciduria type I, mitochondrial respiratory chain disease, methylmalonic aciduria.
  • Continue workup: plasma AA, urine OA, acylcarnitine, lactate, MRI/MRS.
  • Empiric trial reasonable in any unexplained pediatric encephalopathy with BG lesions.

A8. A 14-year-old with acute liver failure from acetaminophen overdose develops West Haven grade III encephalopathy. Outline ICU management.

Show answer
  • ICU admission; intubate for airway protection.
  • N-acetylcysteine infusion (acetaminophen toxicity).
  • Lactulose to reduce ammonia; rifaximin.
  • Vitamin K for coagulopathy; FFP/cryo only for active bleeding or planned invasive procedure.
  • Hyperosmolar therapy (3% NaCl) for cerebral edema.
  • Head of bed 30°; normothermia; avoid sedatives that mask exam.
  • Refer for liver transplant evaluation EARLY (Wilson, acetaminophen, viral fulminant carry distinct criteria).
  • Avoid invasive procedures unless absolutely necessary due to coagulopathy.

A9. Outline the critical sample for evaluation of pediatric hypoglycemia and explain why timing matters.

Show answer
  • Send during hypoglycemic episode, BEFORE glucose administration if at all possible.
  • Glucose (confirm), insulin, C-peptide, cortisol, growth hormone.
  • Ammonia, lactate, beta-hydroxybutyrate, free fatty acids.
  • Acylcarnitine profile.
  • Urine organic acids, ketones, reducing substances.
  • Pattern recognition: high insulin + low ketones = hyperinsulinism; high NH₃ + low ketones = FAOD; high lactate = GSD or mitochondrial; high ketones + acidosis = organic aciduria.
  • Critical sample is diagnostic gold — values normalize after glucose administration and may delay or preclude diagnosis.

A10. Why is valproate contraindicated in suspected mitochondrial or organic acid disease, and what are the alternatives?

Show answer
  • Valproate inhibits mitochondrial β-oxidation and may precipitate acute liver failure.
  • POLG mutations particularly at risk; cannot reliably exclude in young child with new encephalopathy and seizures.
  • Also inhibits carnitine transport, can worsen secondary carnitine deficiency.
  • Alternatives: levetiracetam, phenobarbital, fosphenytoin, lacosamide.
  • If valproate-induced hyperammonemic encephalopathy: discontinue valproate, supplement L-carnitine, consider underlying urea cycle defect (unmasked).

Block B — Mock RCPSC Oral Cases (5 sequential, 40 minutes)

B1. A 4-day-old term male presents with lethargy, poor feeding, vomiting, and respiratory alkalosis. On arrival: hypotonic, GCS-equivalent low, no response to noxious stimuli. Sepsis workup pending.

B1a — Initial management and differential.
  • Stabilize: airway, ventilation, IV access.
  • Send: glucose, electrolytes, blood gas, ammonia, lactate, plasma amino acids, urine organic acids, acylcarnitine profile, urine orotic acid.
  • Septic workup including CSF (after CT clearance if safe); empiric antibiotics + acyclovir.
  • Differential: neonatal sepsis, inborn error of metabolism, congenital adrenal hyperplasia, hypoxic-ischemic insult.
  • Respiratory alkalosis in encephalopathic neonate is classic for hyperammonemia.
B1b — NH₃ returns at 1,200 µmol/L. Now what?
  • Emergency: stop all protein.
  • D10 + intralipid for catabolic reversal; consider insulin if hyperglycemia develops.
  • Ammonul (Na benzoate + Na phenylacetate) loading IV.
  • L-arginine 600 mg/kg IV bolus then infusion.
  • L-carnitine 100 mg/kg/day; carbamylglutamate if NAGS deficiency considered.
  • Emergent CRRT — NH₃ > 500 is a hard indication; severity demands immediate dialysis.
  • Neuroprotection: HOB 30°, 3% NaCl for cerebral edema, cEEG.
  • Engage metabolic genetics urgently.
B1c — Plasma amino acid panel shows elevated glutamine, low citrulline. Urine orotic acid markedly elevated. Diagnosis?
  • Ornithine transcarbamylase (OTC) deficiency — X-linked, most common urea cycle disorder.
  • Triad: elevated NH₃ + low citrulline + elevated urine orotic acid.
  • Continue acute management; transition to long-term scavenger therapy and protein-restricted diet once stabilized.
  • Family counseling: X-linked inheritance, carrier testing for mother and female siblings.
  • Long-term: consider liver transplant for severe phenotypes.
B1d — After 12 h on CRRT, NH₃ drops to 80 µmol/L. Plan continued care.
  • Wean CRRT carefully — sometimes ammonia rebounds after stopping.
  • Continue Ammonul, arginine, carnitine until oral protocol established.
  • Transition to protein-restricted formula with essential amino acid supplementation.
  • Oral scavenger therapy: glycerol phenylbutyrate or sodium phenylbutyrate.
  • Multidisciplinary follow-up: metabolic genetics, dietitian, neurology, hepatology.
  • Family education on emergency protocols for catabolic stress (illness, fasting).

B2. A 10-year-old with new diagnosis of type 1 diabetes presents in DKA: pH 6.95, glucose 38 mmol/L, ketones 6 mmol/L, severe acidosis. After 6 hours of treatment, develops headache and becomes more lethargic.

B2a — Recognize and manage acute deterioration.
  • Cerebral edema — classic timing 4–12 h after DKA treatment starts.
  • Warning signs: headache, deteriorating mental status, bradycardia, hypertension, papilledema.
  • Immediate action: 3% NaCl 5–10 mL/kg IV over 30 min OR mannitol 0.5–1 g/kg IV.
  • Reduce IV fluid rate by approximately one-third.
  • Elevate head of bed 30°; maintain midline neck.
  • Reduce or hold insulin if hypoglycemia developing.
  • Intubate and brief hyperventilation if herniation imminent.
  • Urgent CT scan; neurosurgery consult if needed.
B2b — Family asks why this happened. Discuss risk factors.
  • Risk factors: younger age (<5 y especially), new diagnosis, severe acidosis with low PCO₂, elevated BUN, treatment with bicarbonate, rapid sodium drop, insulin bolus.
  • Mortality 20–25% in established cerebral edema.
  • Prevention per ISPAD 2022: gradual fluid resuscitation (10 mL/kg over 30 min if shock, then deficit + maintenance over 24–48 h); avoid bicarbonate unless severe acidosis with circulatory compromise; no insulin bolus.
B2c — Outline ongoing DKA management once edema controlled.
  • Continue insulin infusion 0.05–0.1 U/kg/h (avoid bolus).
  • Replace deficit + maintenance over 24–48 h using isotonic crystalloid.
  • K replacement when urine output established (insulin drives K intracellularly).
  • Monitor glucose hourly; add dextrose when glucose < 14–17 mmol/L.
  • Transition to subcutaneous insulin when acidosis resolved, eating well.
  • Diabetes education for family before discharge.
B2d — Day 3 patient recovering. Discuss long-term outcome.
  • Cerebral edema survivors may have residual cognitive deficits.
  • Neuropsychology assessment at 6 weeks and 6 months.
  • School accommodation as needed.
  • Lifetime diabetes care: endocrinology follow-up, glucose monitoring, insulin pump or CGM consideration.
  • Sick-day rules education to prevent future DKA.

B3. A 15-year-old with anorexia nervosa is admitted for refeeding. After 24 h of nutrition reintroduction, she develops respiratory failure requiring intubation. P 0.3 mmol/L, Mg 0.5 mmol/L, K 2.8 mmol/L. Confused and unable to follow commands.

B3a — Recognize syndrome and immediate priorities.
  • Refeeding syndrome with severe hypophosphatemia, hypomagnesemia, hypokalemia.
  • Respiratory failure from phosphate-dependent ATP collapse in respiratory muscles.
  • Encephalopathy from electrolyte derangements + possible Wernicke contribution.
  • Immediate action: thiamine 5–10 mg/kg IV BEFORE any glucose adjustment.
  • IV phosphate replacement cautiously (10–15 mmol over 4–6 h initially); recheck.
  • Mg sulfate 25–50 mg/kg IV.
  • K replacement to safe level.
  • Slow reintroduction of calories (start ~10–20 kcal/kg/day, advance over week).
B3b — Develops nystagmus and confusion despite electrolyte correction. Next step?
  • Wernicke encephalopathy — common in anorexia/malnutrition.
  • Confirm thiamine adequately dosed (5–10 mg/kg IV daily; some protocols up to 500 mg IV daily in adolescents).
  • Continue daily thiamine until resolution; transition to oral.
  • MRI to confirm medial thalamic/mammillary body lesions.
  • Watch for Korsakoff syndrome (memory deficits) if Wernicke not treated promptly.
B3c — Multidisciplinary discharge planning.
  • Psychiatry / eating disorder specialist consult.
  • Nutrition team for ongoing refeeding plan.
  • Family-based therapy consideration.
  • Slow caloric advancement; daily electrolyte monitoring during refeeding phase.
  • Thiamine and multivitamin supplementation continued.
  • Outpatient program or inpatient eating disorder facility transfer.
B3d — Discuss long-term outcome and recurrence prevention.
  • Eating disorders carry high relapse rate; multidisciplinary follow-up essential.
  • Cognitive outcomes generally favorable if Wernicke treated promptly.
  • Bone health: DEXA, calcium, vitamin D.
  • Address comorbid depression, anxiety.
  • Family involvement and education.
  • Pediatric-to-adolescent transition planning.

B4. A 6-year-old with previously well-controlled epilepsy on valproate develops vomiting, lethargy, and progressive obtundation over 24 hours. NH₃ 280 µmol/L, ALT/AST mildly elevated.

B4a — Diagnosis and acute management.
  • Valproate-induced hyperammonemic encephalopathy (VHE).
  • Discontinue valproate immediately.
  • L-carnitine supplementation 100 mg/kg IV bolus then maintenance.
  • Ammonul if persistent hyperammonemia.
  • Switch to alternative ASM: levetiracetam load 40–60 mg/kg.
  • Investigate underlying urea cycle defect (often unmasked by valproate); send plasma AA, urine OA, urine orotic acid.
  • Supportive ICU care; cEEG.
B4b — Urine orotic acid is elevated. Implications?
  • Likely OTC carrier (female heterozygote) or other urea cycle defect unmasked by valproate.
  • Permanent avoidance of valproate.
  • Long-term low-protein diet may be needed.
  • Genetic testing; family counseling.
  • Stress protocol for illness or catabolic events.
B4c — Plan ongoing seizure management.
  • Levetiracetam as primary ASM (no hyperammonemia risk).
  • Alternatives: phenobarbital, lacosamide, lamotrigine (titrate slowly).
  • Avoid valproate permanently.
  • Monitor for adequate seizure control; pediatric neurology follow-up.
  • Family education on avoiding valproate in any future setting.
B4d — Long-term follow-up.
  • Metabolic genetics for definitive diagnosis and management.
  • Neurology for epilepsy management.
  • Dietitian for protein-restricted plan if needed.
  • Carrier testing for female siblings (X-linked OTC).
  • Emergency action plan in case of catabolic stress.

B5. A 12-year-old known DM type 1 presents with progressive ataxia, slurred speech, intermittent confusion lasting hours, no fever. Glucose 8 mmol/L. Episodes triggered by meals.

B5a — Initial workup and differential.
  • Differential: stroke, MELAS, drug intoxication, hypoglycemia rebound, IEM unmasking, D-lactic acidosis (if short bowel history).
  • Send: glucose, electrolytes, blood gas, NH₃, lactate, plasma AA, urine OA.
  • If short bowel history or surgery: send D-lactate specifically.
  • MRI brain to rule out structural cause.
  • Detailed dietary and surgical history.
B5b — Patient had small bowel resection 2 years ago for Crohn's. D-lactate elevated. Manage.
  • D-lactic acidosis from bacterial overgrowth in short bowel.
  • Bowel rest (NPO).
  • Antibiotics: metronidazole or vancomycin for 7–10 days to reduce bacterial overgrowth.
  • Low-carbohydrate diet thereafter.
  • Probiotics controversial.
  • Long-term: dietitian for ongoing dietary management; periodic antibiotic cycling.
B5c — Discuss outpatient management plan.
  • Low-carbohydrate, high-protein diet to limit substrate for D-lactate production.
  • Periodic antibiotic rotation if recurrent episodes.
  • Gastroenterology follow-up.
  • Nutrition consult for ongoing planning.
  • Educate family on triggers (high-carb meals) and warning signs.
B5d — Develops recurrent episodes despite dietary management. Next steps?
  • Reassess: bacterial overgrowth recurrence, dietary adherence, other IEM.
  • Breath hydrogen testing for bacterial overgrowth.
  • Consider longer antibiotic course or different agent.
  • Pediatric gastroenterology for advanced management.
  • Bowel rehabilitation if severe short bowel.
  • Surgical reconstruction options in select cases.

Block C — Randomized SAQ Drill (30 questions)

C1. Define metabolic encephalopathy.

Show answer
  • Global cerebral dysfunction without a structural brain lesion.
  • Caused by intoxication, energy failure, or electrolyte/endocrine disturbance.

C2. Three pathophysiologic categories.

Show answer
  • Endogenous intoxication; energy failure; water/electrolyte/endocrine.

C3. Indications for CRRT in hyperammonemia.

Show answer
  • NH₃ > 500 µmol/L; rapidly rising despite scavengers; persistent > 200 despite therapy; severe encephalopathy.

C4. Mechanism of sodium phenylacetate.

Show answer
  • Conjugates with glutamine → phenylacetylglutamine excreted in urine.
  • Provides alternative nitrogen disposal pathway.

C5. Distinguishing labs for OTC deficiency.

Show answer
  • Elevated NH₃, low citrulline, elevated urine orotic acid.
  • X-linked inheritance.

C6. Mechanism of organic aciduria-induced hyperammonemia.

Show answer
  • Propionyl-CoA inhibits N-acetylglutamate synthase → decreased CPS1 activity → impaired urea cycle.

C7. Reye syndrome features.

Show answer
  • Non-inflammatory encephalopathy with fatty liver after viral illness.
  • Hyperammonemia, hypoglycemia, elevated LFTs, coagulopathy.
  • Avoid aspirin in pediatric viral illnesses.

C8. MSUD biochemistry.

Show answer
  • Branched-chain α-keto acid dehydrogenase deficiency.
  • Leucine, isoleucine, valine accumulate.
  • Sweet-smelling urine.

C9. DKA cerebral edema risk factors.

Show answer
  • Younger age, new diagnosis, severe acidosis, low PCO₂, elevated BUN, bicarbonate use, rapid Na drop, insulin bolus.

C10. DKA cerebral edema treatment.

Show answer
  • 3% NaCl 5–10 mL/kg or mannitol 0.5–1 g/kg.
  • Reduce fluid rate ⅓; HOB 30°.
  • Reduce insulin if hypoglycemia.
  • Intubate + brief HV if herniation.

C11. PECARN DKA FLUID trial result.

Show answer
  • Kuppermann NEJM 2018 — neither rate nor Na content significantly affected neurologic outcome.
  • Gradual replacement remains reasonable.

C12. Why avoid bicarbonate in DKA?

Show answer
  • Associated with worse outcomes including cerebral edema.
  • Paradoxical CNS acidosis.
  • Use only if severe acidosis + circulatory compromise.

C13. Acute symptomatic hyponatremia treatment.

Show answer
  • 3% NaCl 2–4 mL/kg IV bolus.
  • Target rise 4–6 mmol/L.
  • Continue at < 8–10 mmol/L per 24 h thereafter.

C14. Osmotic demyelination risk factors.

Show answer
  • Chronic hyponatremia; rapid correction; hypokalemia; alcoholism; malnutrition; liver disease.

C15. Hypernatremia correction limit.

Show answer
  • ≤ 10–12 mmol/L per 24 h.
  • Calculate free water deficit; replace over 48 h.

C16. Critical sample for hypoglycemia.

Show answer
  • Glucose, insulin, C-peptide, cortisol, GH, NH₃, lactate, β-hydroxybutyrate, FFA, acylcarnitine, urine OA/ketones.
  • Send BEFORE glucose if possible.

C17. Wernicke encephalopathy triad and treatment.

Show answer
  • Ophthalmoplegia, ataxia, confusion (full triad in <20%).
  • Thiamine 5–10 mg/kg IV BEFORE glucose; continue daily.

C18. Biotin-responsive basal ganglia disease — empiric therapy.

Show answer
  • Biotin 5–10 mg/kg/day + thiamine 20–40 mg/kg/day.
  • For unexplained encephalopathy with bilateral basal ganglia lesions.

C19. MELAS acute therapy.

Show answer
  • L-arginine 0.5 g/kg IV bolus + 0.5 g/kg/day infusion × 3–5 days.
  • Avoid valproate; mitochondrial cocktail.

C20. Why avoid valproate in mitochondrial / organic acid disease.

Show answer
  • Inhibits mitochondrial β-oxidation; may precipitate ALF.
  • POLG at particular risk.
  • Use levetiracetam or alternatives.

C21. Leigh syndrome imaging.

Show answer
  • Symmetric bilateral basal ganglia and brainstem T2 hyperintensities.
  • Lactic acidosis; developmental regression.

C22. West Haven HE staging.

Show answer
  • I (mild confusion); II (lethargy + asterixis); III (stupor); IV (coma).

C23. PALF transplant referral criteria.

Show answer
  • Grade III/IV encephalopathy + uncorrected coagulopathy.
  • Worsening trajectory despite supportive care.
  • Wilson disease, acetaminophen, fulminant hepatitis.
  • Refer EARLY.

C24. Wilson disease workup.

Show answer
  • Slit lamp (Kayser-Fleischer rings); ceruloplasmin (low); 24-h urine copper (elevated).
  • D-penicillamine, trientine, zinc; liver transplant for fulminant.

C25. Thyroid storm management.

Show answer
  • Propranolol; PTU/methimazole; iodine (after PTU); steroids; treat hyperthermia.

C26. Myxedema coma management.

Show answer
  • Hydrocortisone FIRST; then IV levothyroxine.
  • Warming; BP and ventilatory support.

C27. Refractory hypocalcemia — check what?

Show answer
  • Magnesium — hypomagnesemia causes refractory hypocalcemia.
  • Replace Mg first, then Ca.

C28. D-lactic acidosis presentation and treatment.

Show answer
  • Short bowel + episodic ataxia/confusion after carbohydrate meals.
  • Standard lactate normal — request D-lactate specifically.
  • Bowel rest, low-carb diet, metronidazole or vancomycin.

C29. Why use isotonic maintenance IV fluids?

Show answer
  • Moritz NEJM 2015 — isotonic prevents hospital-acquired hyponatremia.
  • Hypotonic (D5 ½ NS) → hyponatremia + encephalopathy.

C30. Refractory neonatal seizures empiric trial.

Show answer
  • Pyridoxine 100 mg IV — pyridoxine-dependent epilepsy (ALDH7A1).
  • Then folinic acid 5 mg if no response.

Show plan
  • D10 emergency; critical sample.

Show plan
  • Check NH₃, Na, glucose, lactate.

Show plan
  • Manganese toxicity from TPN.
  • Reduce or remove Mn; check whole blood Mn.
  • Rule out biotin, thiamine deficiency.
