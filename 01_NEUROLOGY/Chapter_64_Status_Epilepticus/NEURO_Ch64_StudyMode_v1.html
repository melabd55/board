1 · Structured Rogers Mirror

KEY POINTS (pages 3342–3346)
  • SE = continuous or intermittent seizure activity ≥5 minutes without recovery of consciousness (operational definition for treatment initiation).
  • Conceptual t1 = 5 min (treatment trigger); t2 = 30 min (potential for neuronal injury) — ILAE 2015 framework.
  • Classification by motor symptoms: convulsive SE (CSE), myoclonic SE, focal motor SE, nonconvulsive SE (NCSE).
  • CSE accounts for 73–98% of pediatric SE.
  • NCSE present in 18–33% of children with unexplained altered consciousness or prolonged postictal state.
  • RSE = failure of one benzodiazepine + one second-line ASM.
  • SRSE = continued or recurrent seizures ≥24 h after anesthetic infusion started, or recurrence on weaning.
  • Pediatric mortality 0–4%; outcome driven by underlying etiology and age.
  • Pharmacoresistance develops with prolonged SE — GABA-A receptor internalization, NMDA upregulation.
  • Etiology dictates adjunctive therapy: CNS infection → antibiotics/antivirals; immune-mediated → immunotherapy; FIRES → ketogenic diet, anakinra/tocilizumab.
ICU layer: The clock starts at 5 minutes — treat first seizure of unknown duration as already in SE territory.
RCPSC angle: Examiners may ask "when does a seizure become status?" — operational definition is 5 min; mechanistic (t1/t2) framework is favored on oral exams.
CLASSIFICATION OF STATUS EPILEPTICUS (pages 3346–3349)

By motor manifestations

  • Convulsive SE (CSE) — generalized tonic, clonic, tonic-clonic, myoclonic; focal motor with or without secondary generalization; hyperkinetic SE.
  • Nonconvulsive SE (NCSE) — without prominent motor signs; requires EEG diagnosis; subcategorized as NCSE with coma (more common in ICU) and NCSE without coma (e.g., absence status, complex partial NCSE).
  • Electrographic-only SE — no motor correlate; identified only on cEEG.

By etiology (ILAE classification)

  • Acute symptomatic (CNS infection, stroke, trauma, metabolic, toxic).
  • Remote symptomatic (prior brain injury, malformation).
  • Progressive (degenerative, neoplastic).
  • Cryptogenic (no identified cause despite thorough investigation).
  • Febrile SE (febrile, otherwise neurologically normal children 6 mo–5 y; FEBSTAT cohort).

By temporal stage

  • Early SE — ~5–30 min.
  • Established SE — ~30–60 min.
  • Refractory SE — failure of 2 ASM tiers.
  • Super-refractory SE — persistent or recurrent ≥24 h on anesthetic.
ICU layer: Always state the SE category, etiology, and stage when discussing management — examiners use this structure.
ETIOLOGY (pages 3349–3354)
  • Most pediatric SE occurs in children WITHOUT a known seizure disorder.
  • Acute symptomatic (~25–40%): CNS infection (meningitis, encephalitis, abscess); ischemic/hemorrhagic stroke; TBI; AHT; hypoxic-ischemic injury; metabolic (hyponatremia, hypoglycemia, hypocalcemia, uremia); toxic (TCA, isoniazid, bupropion).
  • Remote symptomatic (~20%): preexisting cerebral palsy, malformations of cortical development, prior TBI, prior stroke.
  • Febrile SE (~30% in children 6 mo–5 y): part of FEBSTAT cohort; HHV-6 association with mesial temporal sclerosis.
  • Cryptogenic / unknown (~5–10%).
  • Progressive / specific syndromes: NORSE, FIRES, Dravet syndrome, mitochondrial encephalopathies, autoimmune encephalitis (anti-NMDAR).
Trap: Failure to send screening labs (glucose, Na, Ca, Mg, ammonia, tox, infection) within first 15 minutes of presentation.
PATHOPHYSIOLOGY (pages 3354–3361)
  • Seizures arise from imbalance between excitation (glutamate, NMDA, AMPA) and inhibition (GABA-A).
  • Prolonged seizure activity → GABA-A receptor internalization → progressive benzodiazepine resistance.
  • Concurrent NMDA receptor trafficking to membrane → glutamate hypersensitivity.
  • This explains the "20-minute rule" — efficacy of benzodiazepines drops sharply with each passing minute.
  • Developing brain has unique vulnerabilities: NKCC1/KCC2 chloride transporter shift means GABA can be excitatory in neonates (relevance for phenobarbital first-line in neonatal SE).
  • Prolonged SE leads to neuronal injury via excitotoxicity, calcium overload, oxidative stress, and apoptosis/necrosis.
  • SE staging by EEG (Treiman): stage 1 discrete seizures → stage 2 waxing-waning → stage 3 continuous discharges → stage 4 PEDs on flat background → stage 5 PEDs only (terminal).
INITIAL DIAGNOSTIC CONSIDERATIONS (pages 3361–3367)

Initial labs (within first 15 min)

  • Bedside glucose (immediate).
  • Serum Na, Ca, Mg, phosphate, BUN, creatinine, ammonia.
  • CBC with differential.
  • Blood gas (acidosis common after CSE).
  • Toxicology screen (urine and serum).
  • ASM levels if patient on therapy.
  • Lactate, CK (rhabdomyolysis after prolonged CSE).

Imaging

  • Head CT if new SE, focal features, recent trauma, or concern for mass effect.
  • Brain MRI when stable — encephalitis, stroke, structural lesion.

Lumbar puncture

  • Indicated if febrile, encephalopathy, CSF infection suspected (after CT clearance for mass effect).
  • CSF for cell count, glucose, protein, Gram stain, culture, HSV PCR, enterovirus PCR, autoimmune encephalitis panel (anti-NMDAR, anti-LGI1, GAD65, etc.).

cEEG indication

  • Suspected NCSE after CSE not returning to baseline (consider 18–33% incidence).
  • Unexplained altered mental status, unexplained coma.
  • Sedated or paralyzed patient with suspected ongoing seizures.
  • Acute brain injury (HIE, AHT, stroke, encephalitis) with depressed mental status.
  • Continuous EEG ≥24 h — yield increases with duration; first 30 min captures most cases but ~12% require >24 h to detect (Topjian, ACNS guidelines).
TREATMENT — FIRST-LINE BENZODIAZEPINES (pages 3367–3380)

Goal: stop seizure within 5–10 minutes of treatment initiation

  • Lorazepam IV: 0.1 mg/kg (max 4 mg) over 2 min; may repeat once at 5 min if no IV access available, intranasal or buccal midazolam preferred.
  • Midazolam: IV 0.1–0.2 mg/kg; intranasal 0.2 mg/kg (max 10 mg); buccal 0.5 mg/kg; intramuscular 0.2 mg/kg (RAMPART trial supported IM in pre-hospital adults).
  • Diazepam: IV 0.15–0.3 mg/kg; rectal 0.5 mg/kg (pediatric).
  • Repeat dose once if no response in 5 min.
  • Underdosing is the most common error — full dose first.
  • For neonatal SE: phenobarbital 20 mg/kg IV is first-line (not benzodiazepines).
Trap: Sub-therapeutic benzodiazepine dosing → benzodiazepine resistance and progression to RSE.
TREATMENT — SECOND-LINE ASMs (pages 3380–3390)
  • If seizure persists 5–10 min after benzodiazepine, escalate to second-line ASM:
  • Fosphenytoin: 20 mg PE/kg IV (max 1500 mg) over 7–10 min. Monitor HR, BP, ECG. Avoid in cardiac conduction disease.
  • Levetiracetam: 40–60 mg/kg IV (max 4500 mg) over 5 min. Renally adjusted. Well-tolerated; first-line in many centers post-ESETT.
  • Valproate: 20–40 mg/kg IV (max 3000 mg) over 5–10 min. Avoid in <2 y (hepatotoxicity, especially in mitochondrial disease).
  • Phenobarbital: 15–20 mg/kg IV. Used preferentially in neonates and as third-line in older children. Sedating; respiratory depression.
  • ESETT trial (Kapur 2019 NEJM): fosphenytoin vs levetiracetam vs valproate — equipotent in terminating benzodiazepine-refractory SE (~50% efficacy each).
ICU layer: Pick a single second-line agent and dose appropriately — switching mid-stream wastes time and underdoses.
REFRACTORY SE (pages 3390–3398)
  • RSE definition: failure of one benzodiazepine + one second-line ASM.
  • Move promptly to continuous anesthetic infusion within 40–60 minutes of SE onset.
  • Midazolam infusion: bolus 0.2 mg/kg → infusion 0.05–2 mg/kg/h.
  • Pentobarbital infusion: bolus 5 mg/kg → infusion 1–5 mg/kg/h. Titrate to burst suppression on cEEG. Vasopressor support anticipated.
  • Ketamine infusion: bolus 1–3 mg/kg → infusion 1–10 mg/kg/h. NMDA antagonist; preserves hemodynamics. Useful in catecholamine-depleted RSE.
  • Propofol infusion: use cautiously in pediatrics due to propofol infusion syndrome at >4 mg/kg/h or >48 h; reserve for short bridging.
  • Continuous EEG mandatory — titrate to burst suppression (5–10 second interburst interval) or complete seizure cessation.
  • Maintain anesthetic 24–48 h, then attempt wean with cEEG observation.
SUPER-REFRACTORY SE (pages 3398–3401)
  • SRSE = continuing or recurrent SE ≥24 h after anesthetic started, or recurrence on weaning.
  • Mortality 30–50%, severe disability common in survivors.
  • Adjunctive therapies (etiology-directed):
    • Immunotherapy (steroids, IVIG, plasma exchange, rituximab) for autoimmune encephalitis.
    • Ketogenic diet (started within 7 days improves outcomes in FIRES and selected RSE).
    • Anakinra or tocilizumab for FIRES (IL-1, IL-6 antagonism).
    • Pyridoxine 50–100 mg/kg IV trial in neonates and young infants.
    • B-vitamin replacement (biotinidase deficiency).
    • Surgical resection for focal cortical lesion driving SE.
    • Cannabidiol, lacosamide, brivaracetam, perampanel — emerging adjuncts.
  • Therapeutic hypothermia controversial; ENICOR trial negative for benefit.
FIRES / NORSE (pages 3401–3403)
  • NORSE = New-Onset Refractory Status Epilepticus — RSE in patient without prior epilepsy, no clear cause within initial 72 h.
  • FIRES = Febrile Infection-Related Epilepsy Syndrome — subcategory of NORSE with febrile illness 24 h–2 weeks before SE onset.
  • Predominantly affects school-age children (median 8 y).
  • CSF often shows pleocytosis (~50%); autoimmune workup often negative.
  • Postulated mechanism: cytokine storm with IL-1β, IL-6 elevation (Kothur 2015).
  • Management: ASMs as usual + ketogenic diet within 7 days + anakinra (IL-1Ra) or tocilizumab (IL-6) trial.
  • Outcomes: high mortality (~12%), drug-resistant epilepsy in >75% of survivors.
OUTCOMES (pages 3403–3406)
  • Outcome is primarily determined by underlying etiology, not SE duration alone.
  • Cryptogenic or febrile SE: mortality 0–2%.
  • Acute symptomatic SE: mortality 4–16% depending on etiology.
  • FIRES: mortality 12–30%; epilepsy in >75% of survivors.
  • Higher seizure burden on cEEG → worse functional outcome (independent of etiology).
  • Long-term: epilepsy, cognitive impairment, behavioral and learning disorders, mesial temporal sclerosis (after febrile SE — FEBSTAT).

7 · 40 SAQs — Chapter 64 (STUDY MODE — answers visible)

SAQ 1. State the operational definition of status epilepticus and explain the t1/t2 framework.

  • Continuous or intermittent seizures ≥5 minutes without recovery of consciousness — operational treatment trigger.
  • ILAE 2015 (Trinka): t1 = 5 min (point at which seizure unlikely to terminate spontaneously) → treatment threshold.
  • t2 = 30 min — point beyond which neuronal injury is likely.
  • Practical: treat any seizure of unknown duration as SE.

SAQ 2. Classify SE by motor manifestations.

  • Convulsive SE (CSE): generalized tonic, clonic, tonic-clonic, myoclonic; focal motor with or without secondary generalization.
  • Nonconvulsive SE (NCSE): without prominent motor signs; requires EEG diagnosis; with or without coma.
  • Electrographic-only SE: no motor correlate.
  • Myoclonic SE (MSE).
  • Hyperkinetic SE.

SAQ 3. Define refractory and super-refractory SE.

  • RSE: SE failing to terminate after one benzodiazepine + one second-line ASM.
  • SRSE: SE continuing ≥24 h on anesthetic infusion or recurring on weaning.
  • Both carry greater morbidity and mortality.

SAQ 4. List 5 common pediatric SE etiologies.

  • Febrile (especially 6 mo–5 y).
  • CNS infection (meningitis, encephalitis — HSV, enterovirus).
  • Metabolic (hyponatremia, hypoglycemia, hypocalcemia).
  • Toxic (TCA, isoniazid, bupropion).
  • Acute brain injury (AHT, TBI, stroke, HIE).
  • Remote symptomatic (prior brain injury, malformation).
  • NORSE/FIRES, autoimmune encephalitis.

SAQ 5. Explain GABA-A receptor internalization and clinical implication.

  • Prolonged seizure activity causes GABA-A receptors to be removed (endocytosed) from the synaptic membrane.
  • Membrane density of receptors falls → benzodiazepine binding sites reduced → progressive BZD resistance.
  • Concurrently NMDA receptors traffic to membrane → glutamate hypersensitivity.
  • Implication: BZD efficacy declines rapidly with time; ketamine (NMDA antagonist) becomes rational in RSE.

SAQ 6. First-line BZD options and pediatric doses.

  • Lorazepam IV: 0.1 mg/kg (max 4 mg) over 2 min — preferred IV agent.
  • Midazolam: IV 0.1–0.2; IN 0.2 mg/kg; IM 0.2 mg/kg; buccal 0.5 mg/kg.
  • Diazepam: IV 0.15–0.3 mg/kg; rectal 0.5 mg/kg.
  • Neonatal first-line: phenobarbital 20 mg/kg IV.
  • Repeat dose once after 5 min if seizing persists.

SAQ 7. ESETT trial design and result.

  • Kapur et al. NEJM 2019;381:2103 — randomized children and adults with benzodiazepine-refractory SE.
  • Compared levetiracetam vs fosphenytoin vs valproate as second-line.
  • All three equipotent: ~45–47% seizure cessation at 60 min.
  • Implication: choose based on patient factors (age, hepatic/cardiac comorbidity).

SAQ 8. Why avoid valproate in <2-year-olds?

  • Risk of hepatotoxicity, particularly in undiagnosed mitochondrial disease.
  • POLG-related hepatocerebral syndromes can be unmasked.
  • Use levetiracetam or fosphenytoin instead.

SAQ 9. When and how to initiate anesthetic infusion in RSE.

  • If seizure persists after BZD + 1 second-line ASM (around 40 min).
  • Intubate; start cEEG; secure IV access for vasopressor support.
  • Midazolam infusion 0.05–2 mg/kg/h titrated to cessation or burst suppression.
  • Pentobarbital 1–5 mg/kg/h if midazolam fails — burst suppression target.
  • Ketamine 1–10 mg/kg/h — useful for NMDA-driven SE and hemodynamic preservation.

SAQ 10. Define NORSE and FIRES.

  • NORSE: New-Onset Refractory Status Epilepticus in patient without prior epilepsy or active neurologic disease; no clear cause within first 72 h.
  • FIRES: Subcategory of NORSE in patient with febrile illness 24 h–2 weeks before SE onset.
  • Predominantly school-age children (median 8 y).
  • High mortality and post-event drug-resistant epilepsy.

SAQ 11. FIRES management approach.

  • Standard tiered ASM treatment as for any RSE/SRSE.
  • Ketogenic diet within 7 days of SE onset.
  • Immunotherapy: high-dose steroids, IVIG, plasma exchange.
  • Targeted cytokine therapy: anakinra (IL-1Ra) or tocilizumab (IL-6 antagonist) — emerging based on case series (Lai 2020).
  • Comprehensive autoimmune encephalitis panel; comprehensive infection panel.
  • Long-term: drug-resistant epilepsy in >75% — surgical evaluation, vagal nerve stimulator, cannabidiol.

SAQ 12. NCSE incidence in PICU patients.

  • ~18–33% of children with unexplained altered consciousness or prolonged postictal state have NCSE.
  • Higher rates in younger children and after acute brain injury.
  • Justifies low threshold for cEEG.

SAQ 13. Salzburg Criteria for NCSE.

  • Definite NCSE: epileptiform discharges >2.5 Hz on EEG.
  • Possible NCSE: rhythmic/periodic discharges <2.5 Hz PLUS subtle clinical phenomena OR EEG/clinical improvement after IV ASM.
  • Sensitivity 97.7%, specificity 89.6% (Leitinger 2016).

SAQ 14. Indications for cEEG in PICU.

  • CSE not returning to baseline within 20 min.
  • Unexplained coma or altered mental status.
  • Sedated/paralyzed patient with suspected ongoing seizures.
  • Acute brain injury (HIE, AHT, stroke, encephalitis, TBI).
  • Refractory SE on anesthetic infusion.
  • Post-cardiac arrest.
  • Minimum 24 h per ACNS guidelines.

SAQ 15. Why is pentobarbital titrated to burst suppression?

  • Burst suppression = brief bursts of EEG activity separated by ≥5–10 seconds of relative inactivity.
  • Represents near-total cortical suppression — effective seizure termination.
  • Allows weaning attempts to detect breakthrough seizures.
  • Requires continuous cEEG to titrate accurately.

SAQ 16. Risks of pentobarbital coma.

  • Hypotension requiring vasopressors.
  • Ileus / gastric stasis.
  • Bone marrow suppression with prolonged use.
  • Infections (VAP, UTI, line sepsis).
  • Loss of pupillary reflexes complicates neurologic exam.
  • Prolonged elimination half-life delays neurologic assessment.

SAQ 17. Propofol infusion syndrome — risk factors.

  • Doses >4 mg/kg/h.
  • Duration >48 h.
  • Younger children at greater risk.
  • Features: metabolic acidosis, rhabdomyolysis, hyperkalemia, hypertriglyceridemia, cardiac failure.
  • Avoid prolonged propofol in pediatric RSE; reserve for short bridging.

SAQ 18. When to use ketamine in RSE?

  • NMDA-driven SE (later-stage SE with BZD resistance).
  • Hemodynamically unstable patient — preserves BP, even raises it.
  • As adjunct to midazolam infusion.
  • Caution in raised ICP without monitoring (historical concern of ICP rise; modern literature challenges this).

SAQ 19. Initial labs in pediatric SE — within first 15 min.

  • Bedside glucose immediately.
  • Serum Na, K, Ca, Mg, phosphate, BUN, creatinine, ammonia.
  • CBC with differential.
  • Blood gas (anion gap, lactate).
  • Toxicology screen, ASM levels.
  • CK if prolonged CSE (rhabdomyolysis risk).

SAQ 20. CSF studies after head CT clearance.

  • Cell count, glucose, protein.
  • Gram stain, bacterial culture.
  • HSV PCR, enterovirus PCR.
  • Autoimmune encephalitis panel (anti-NMDAR, anti-LGI1, GAD65, anti-VGKC).
  • Cytology if neoplastic concern.
  • Defer LP if focal exam or mass effect on imaging.

SAQ 21. Imaging strategy in pediatric SE.

  • Head CT if new SE, focal features, or concern for trauma/mass.
  • Brain MRI with contrast and DWI/SWI when stable — encephalitis, stroke, structural cause.
  • MRA/MRV if vascular cause suspected.
  • Follow-up imaging at 6–12 months after febrile SE if FEBSTAT criteria (HHV-6, structural abnormality) to detect mesial temporal sclerosis.

SAQ 22. Neonatal SE first-line therapy.

  • Phenobarbital 20 mg/kg IV — first-line in neonates.
  • Rationale: GABA may be excitatory in neonates (NKCC1 chloride transporter dominant); BZDs less reliable.
  • Additional 10 mg/kg may be given for ongoing seizures.
  • Second-line: phenytoin/fosphenytoin or levetiracetam.
  • Empiric pyridoxine 50–100 mg IV in refractory neonatal SE (pyridoxine-dependent epilepsy).

SAQ 23. When to suspect pyridoxine-dependent epilepsy?

  • Neonate or young infant with refractory SE.
  • No clear etiology after standard workup.
  • Treatment: empiric pyridoxine 100 mg IV trial.
  • If seizure cessation occurs → diagnostic.
  • Long-term oral pyridoxine therapy.

SAQ 24. Ketogenic diet in SRSE — when and how.

  • Initiate within 7 days of SE onset.
  • Aim for ketosis (β-hydroxybutyrate >2 mmol/L).
  • Cervenka 2017 showed 60% resolved SE within days.
  • Useful in FIRES, mitochondrial disease, GLUT1 deficiency.
  • Monitor lipids, electrolytes, acid-base status.

SAQ 25. Immunotherapy in SRSE — when to consider.

  • NORSE, FIRES, autoimmune encephalitis (anti-NMDAR, anti-LGI1, etc.).
  • First-line: high-dose methylprednisolone, IVIG.
  • Second-line: plasma exchange, rituximab.
  • Anakinra or tocilizumab if cytokine-driven FIRES.
  • Continue investigating etiology in parallel.

SAQ 26. Mortality in pediatric SE by etiology.

  • Cryptogenic / febrile: 0–2%.
  • Acute symptomatic: 4–16%.
  • FIRES: 12–30%.
  • Encephalitis-driven SE: 10–20%.
  • Underlying etiology is the primary determinant.

SAQ 27. Long-term sequelae of pediatric SE.

  • Drug-resistant epilepsy.
  • Cognitive impairment, learning disability.
  • Behavioral disorders (ADHD-like, autism spectrum features).
  • Mesial temporal sclerosis (after febrile SE — FEBSTAT).
  • Motor deficits if structural injury.

SAQ 28. FEBSTAT key findings.

  • HHV-6 viremia in ~30% of febrile SE cases.
  • Hippocampal abnormalities on acute MRI in ~10–20%.
  • Mesial temporal sclerosis on follow-up imaging in ~30%.
  • Family history of seizures and structural brain abnormality are risk factors.
  • Justifies follow-up imaging at 6–12 months.

SAQ 29. Why is post-cardiac arrest cEEG important?

  • Post-anoxic seizures occur in ~30% of pediatric arrest survivors.
  • Often nonconvulsive — missed without cEEG.
  • Treatable but indicator of worse outcome.
  • AHA 2019 recommends ≥24 h cEEG after pediatric cardiac arrest.

SAQ 30. When to consider transitioning SE management to palliative care.

  • SRSE with poor prognosis despite full management.
  • Diffuse irreversible brain injury on imaging/EEG.
  • Family-centered goals of care discussion.
  • Continue medical comfort care; ASMs may be continued for symptom control.

SAQ 31. SE in patient with known epilepsy — common precipitants.

  • ASM non-adherence (most common).
  • Recent ASM change.
  • Sleep deprivation.
  • Acute illness (febrile, infection).
  • Drug interactions reducing ASM levels.
  • Check ASM levels and address adherence post-event.

SAQ 32. Anti-NMDA receptor encephalitis presentation in children.

  • Subacute behavioral/personality changes, psychosis, dyskinesia.
  • Seizures and SE common.
  • Autonomic instability.
  • CSF anti-NMDAR antibodies; brain MRI often normal or mild T2 hyperintensity.
  • Treatment: steroids + IVIG/plasma exchange first-line; rituximab/cyclophosphamide second-line.
  • Search for teratoma (ovarian in adolescent females).

SAQ 33. Why does benzodiazepine efficacy decline with time?

  • GABA-A receptor internalization with prolonged seizures.
  • Fewer receptors on membrane → less BZD binding.
  • Efficacy >80% if given <5 min; falls to ~40% at 30 min.
  • Implication: do not delay; full dose first time.

SAQ 34. Define burst suppression on EEG.

  • Bursts of EEG activity (high-amplitude, brief) separated by relative inactivity (low-amplitude flat segments).
  • Target interburst interval ≥5–10 seconds for SE management.
  • Represents near-total cortical suppression.
  • Achieved with pentobarbital or high-dose midazolam.

SAQ 35. Define lateralized periodic discharges (LPDs).

  • Repetitive lateralized epileptiform discharges, usually 0.5–2 Hz, on a slow/flat background.
  • Associated with acute focal injury (stroke, encephalitis, abscess, AHT).
  • Linked to seizures and worse outcome.
  • Treat if part of an SE continuum or causing clinical decline.

SAQ 36. Patients with persistent altered mental status post-CSE — approach.

  • Differential: NCSE, prolonged postictal state, ASM toxicity, ongoing structural disease.
  • Start cEEG within 1–2 h to rule out NCSE.
  • Repeat imaging if focal deficit emerges.
  • Check ASM levels, electrolytes, hepatic/renal function.

SAQ 37. Common toxic causes of pediatric SE.

  • Tricyclic antidepressants — Na channel blockade.
  • Bupropion.
  • Isoniazid (treat with pyridoxine).
  • Cocaine, amphetamines.
  • Theophylline.
  • Local anesthetic toxicity (intralipid rescue).
  • Cannabis (synthetic cannabinoids).

SAQ 38. Hypoglycemia in pediatric SE.

  • Check glucose immediately in any seizing child.
  • Treat with D10 2–5 mL/kg IV bolus if <3.3 mmol/L.
  • Consider underlying cause: hyperinsulinism, fatty acid oxidation defect, glycogen storage, sepsis.
  • Glucose may itself precipitate or unmask metabolic disease.

SAQ 39. Hyponatremia and SE.

  • Acute hyponatremia <125 mmol/L can precipitate SE.
  • Treat acute symptomatic hyponatremia with 3% NaCl 2–4 mL/kg bolus.
  • Correct slowly thereafter to avoid osmotic demyelination.
  • Causes: SIADH, water intoxication, salt-wasting, hypotonic IV fluid error.

SAQ 40. Common pitfalls in pediatric SE management.

  • Under-dosing first-line BZD.
  • Delaying second-line ASM.
  • Switching mid-tier rather than escalating.
  • Not initiating cEEG for paralyzed/sedated patient.
  • Missing NCSE in patient with persistent altered mental status.
  • Failure to investigate etiology aggressively.
  • Forgetting pyridoxine trial in neonate/infant.
  • Not initiating ketogenic diet within 7 days for SRSE/FIRES.

Case 1. 5-year-old known epilepsy, generalized tonic-clonic seizing for 8 min on arrival.

  • ABCs, IV access, glucose check.
  • Lorazepam 0.1 mg/kg IV immediately.
  • If no IV: midazolam 0.2 mg/kg IN or 0.5 mg/kg buccal.
  • Repeat BZD at 5 min if seizing.
  • Send labs and ASM levels; check adherence.

Case 2. 7-year-old in SE on arrival, no IV access, no IO yet.

  • Buccal midazolam 0.5 mg/kg or intranasal midazolam 0.2 mg/kg or rectal diazepam 0.5 mg/kg immediately.
  • Establish IO if peripheral fails after 2 attempts.
  • RAMPART trial supports IM midazolam in pre-hospital adults (extrapolated).

Case 3. 3-year-old SE post-BZD x2. Choose second-line.

  • Per ESETT — any of fosphenytoin, levetiracetam, or valproate.
  • Levetiracetam 40–60 mg/kg IV — well-tolerated, no cardiac monitoring needed.
  • Fosphenytoin 20 mg PE/kg — requires ECG monitoring.
  • Avoid valproate in <2 y or mitochondrial concern.

Case 4. Newborn 5-day-old with SE.

  • Phenobarbital 20 mg/kg IV first-line.
  • Check glucose, Na, Ca, Mg.
  • Empiric pyridoxine 100 mg IV trial.
  • Consider sepsis workup; broad-spectrum antibiotics + acyclovir.
  • Send metabolic panel (amino acids, organic acids, acylcarnitine).

Case 5. 10-month-old with febrile illness, seizing for 25 min despite 2 BZD doses.

  • Move to second-line ASM (lev 40–60 mg/kg preferred <2 y).
  • Check glucose, electrolytes, septic workup.
  • Head CT if focal features.
  • If post-second-line ongoing → anesthetic infusion + intubation.

Case 6. 14-year-old known TLE, seizing 12 min, ASM levels low.

  • BZD as per protocol; second-line lev or fosphenytoin.
  • Address ASM non-adherence post-event.
  • Adherence counseling, address barriers (cost, side effects, mental health).

Case 7. 8-year-old SE with low Na 122.

  • Treat acute symptomatic hyponatremia: 3% NaCl 2–4 mL/kg bolus.
  • Concurrent BZD.
  • Correct slowly thereafter (max 8–10 mmol/L in 24 h).
  • Investigate etiology: SIADH, water intoxication, IV fluid error.

Case 8. 3-month-old SE, glucose 1.8 mmol/L.

  • D10 5 mL/kg IV bolus immediately.
  • BZD for ongoing seizure.
  • Investigate cause: hyperinsulinism, fatty acid oxidation defect, GLUT1 deficiency, sepsis.
  • Send labs while treating: insulin, cortisol, ammonia, lactate, acylcarnitine.

Case 9. 2-year-old SE day 1; on midazolam infusion for 18 h. Still not seizing on EEG.

  • Continue infusion 24–48 h total.
  • Then attempt slow wean with continuous cEEG.
  • Add maintenance ASM during wean (lev, fosphenytoin).
  • Investigate underlying cause continues in parallel.

Case 10. 9-year-old SE refractory to midazolam infusion at 2 mg/kg/h.

  • Escalate to pentobarbital infusion — bolus 5 mg/kg, infusion 1–5 mg/kg/h.
  • cEEG to burst suppression with 5–10 sec interburst interval.
  • Vasopressor support anticipated (norepinephrine).
  • Or add ketamine infusion 1–10 mg/kg/h.

Case 11. 5-year-old SRSE — preceded by febrile illness 5 days prior. CSF mild pleocytosis, autoimmune panel negative.

  • FIRES likely.
  • Initiate ketogenic diet within 7 days.
  • Empiric high-dose steroids + IVIG.
  • Consider anakinra (IL-1Ra) or tocilizumab (IL-6 antagonist).
  • Continue ASM escalation, cEEG, full ICU support.
  • Family meeting regarding prognosis.

Case 12. 16-year-old subacute psychiatric symptoms now in SE. MRI normal, CSF lymphocytic pleocytosis.

  • Suspect anti-NMDA receptor encephalitis.
  • Send CSF and serum for anti-NMDAR antibodies, paraneoplastic panel.
  • Pelvic US (ovarian teratoma search in adolescent female).
  • Start high-dose methylprednisolone + IVIG.
  • Plasma exchange if no improvement.
  • Second-line: rituximab, cyclophosphamide.

Case 13. 4-year-old post-CSE, GCS 9, not returning to baseline after 30 min.

  • Suspect NCSE (18–33% incidence).
  • Initiate cEEG within 1 h.
  • If electrographic seizures: continue/repeat ASM until cessation.
  • Continue cEEG ≥24 h.

Case 14. 8-year-old post-arrest, paralyzed/sedated for therapeutic hypothermia. Twitching of right face.

  • Initiate cEEG immediately.
  • Treat any electrographic seizures with full-dose ASM.
  • Maintain cEEG throughout cooling and rewarming.
  • Common post-arrest finding; predicts worse outcome.

Case 15. 12-year-old SE on pentobarbital coma day 3, hypotensive on epinephrine.

  • Optimize fluid status (cautious).
  • Add norepinephrine for vasoconstriction.
  • Daily echo for cardiac function.
  • Consider dose reduction once cEEG shows sustained suppression.
  • Add adjunctive ASMs to facilitate weaning.

Case 16. 6-year-old SE due to TCA overdose.

  • BZD for seizure.
  • Sodium bicarbonate 1–2 mEq/kg IV bolus for QRS widening >100 ms.
  • Avoid phenytoin (Na channel blockade compounds TCA effect).
  • Use levetiracetam or valproate as second-line.
  • Activated charcoal if recent ingestion + protected airway.

Case 17. 5-year-old after isoniazid ingestion seizes.

  • Pyridoxine 70 mg/kg IV (1 g pyridoxine per gram INH ingested).
  • BZD as adjunct.
  • Pyridoxine is the specific antidote.

Case 18. 7-year-old with diabetic ketoacidosis develops seizures.

  • Consider cerebral edema from rapid fluid/glucose shifts.
  • 3% NaCl bolus 5 mL/kg or mannitol 0.5–1 g/kg.
  • Slow IV fluid rate; reduce insulin infusion.
  • Head CT to assess; admit PICU; cEEG.
  • BZD for active seizure.

Case 19. 10-year-old with focal motor SE (right face) for 90 min, lev failed.

  • Focal SE often less responsive to BZD than CSE.
  • Move to fosphenytoin or anesthetic infusion.
  • Send labs and image (CT/MRI) to identify focal structural cause.
  • Consider HSV encephalitis if any febrile prodrome.

Case 20. 11-month-old in PICU on midazolam infusion 5 days, persistent electrographic seizures.

  • Diagnose SRSE.
  • Add pentobarbital infusion or transition to it.
  • Initiate ketogenic diet within 7 days.
  • Empiric immunotherapy trial (steroids + IVIG).
  • Search for underlying genetic, metabolic, or autoimmune etiology.

Case 21. 6-year-old with new-onset SE, CSF normal, MRI normal.

  • NORSE working diagnosis.
  • Aggressive workup: serum/CSF autoimmune encephalitis panel, paraneoplastic panel, infections (HSV, VZV, EBV, mycoplasma, enterovirus), inborn errors of metabolism.
  • Empiric immunotherapy if antibody panel pending.
  • Family genetic history.

Case 22. 9-month-old SE, HSV PCR positive.

  • HSV encephalitis: acyclovir 60 mg/kg/day IV in divided doses.
  • Treat seizure with full ASM tier.
  • cEEG; MRI for temporal lobe involvement.
  • Continue acyclovir 21 days.
  • Repeat PCR before discontinuing.

Case 23. 7-year-old known Dravet syndrome in SE.

  • SCN1A mutation → avoid Na channel blockers (phenytoin, fosphenytoin, lamotrigine, carbamazepine).
  • Use levetiracetam or valproate as second-line.
  • Stiripentol, cannabidiol, fenfluramine for maintenance.
  • Initiate anesthetic infusion if refractory.

Case 24. 15-year-old SE after stopping ASM for weight loss.

  • Common precipitant: ASM non-adherence.
  • Treat acutely per algorithm.
  • Address adherence: psychology, social work, possibly long-acting agents.
  • Document discussion and follow-up plan.

Case 25. 8-year-old in absence status (NCSE without coma).

  • Confusion, slowed thinking, automatisms, often known idiopathic generalized epilepsy.
  • EEG: generalized 3-Hz spike-wave activity continuous or near-continuous.
  • BZD or valproate IV; lev IV alternative.
  • Avoid carbamazepine, phenytoin (may worsen absence).
  • Resume maintenance valproate or lev.

Case 26. 3-year-old SE with prolonged fever 8 days prior.

  • FIRES diagnostic criteria likely met.
  • Standard ASM tier; intubate, anesthetic infusion.
  • Ketogenic diet within 7 days.
  • Cytokine antagonist trial (anakinra).
  • cEEG; CSF cytokine panel if available; autoimmune panel.

Case 27. 10-year-old with NCSE during cardiac arrest cEEG.

  • Post-anoxic NCSE — full-dose ASM (lev 60 mg/kg).
  • Continue therapeutic hypothermia per protocol.
  • Maintain cEEG; goal seizure cessation.
  • Outcome marker but treatable.

Case 28. 5-year-old prolonged SE then ASM toxicity (lev 200 mg/kg over 4 h).

  • Acute toxicity: somnolence, ataxia, behavioral changes.
  • Reduce dose; supportive care.
  • Document medication errors; root cause analysis.
  • Continue cEEG to ensure no breakthrough seizures.

Case 29. 2-year-old NCSE; PT 35 sec on cEEG. ASM choices?

  • NCSE: lorazepam IV 0.1 mg/kg or buccal/IN midazolam first.
  • If persists: levetiracetam IV 40–60 mg/kg.
  • If still persists: anesthetic infusion.
  • Salzburg Criteria for diagnostic confirmation.

Case 30. 12-year-old SE due to bupropion overdose.

  • BZD for seizure.
  • Activated charcoal if recent and airway protected.
  • Sodium bicarbonate if QRS widening.
  • Avoid more bupropion exposure; monitor cardiac rhythm.
  • Lev or valproate as second-line.

Case 31. 4-week-old NCSE on cEEG; persistent burst suppression on EEG.

  • Differential: HIE, drug effect, severe metabolic disease.
  • Investigate underlying cause aggressively.
  • Treat electrographic seizures.
  • Prognostic conversation with family — burst suppression on EEG carries poor prognosis in neonates.

Case 32. 9-year-old SE day 7 on pentobarbital, severe hypotension despite max vasopressors.

  • Reassess seizure activity on cEEG.
  • Reduce pentobarbital dose; transition to alternative agents.
  • Consider continuous ketamine for hemodynamic preservation.
  • Adrenal axis assessment; stress-dose hydrocortisone.

Case 33. 3-year-old known epilepsy, focal motor SE during routine VEEG admission.

  • BZD per protocol; second-line per ESETT.
  • cVEEG continues — characterize semiology.
  • Optimize maintenance ASMs.
  • Consider surgical evaluation if focal lesion confirmed.

Case 34. 7-year-old SRSE, transitioning to comfort care.

  • Multidisciplinary family meeting.
  • Continue ASMs for seizure suppression (symptom control).
  • Palliative care, social work, chaplaincy involved.
  • Withdraw vasopressors and life-sustaining therapies per family wishes.
  • Comfort measures: pain control, anxiolysis, family presence.

Case 35. 11-year-old SE in setting of new HIV diagnosis.

  • CNS opportunistic infections: cryptococcus, toxoplasmosis, CMV, PML, HIV encephalopathy.
  • CSF cryptococcal antigen, India ink, toxoplasma PCR/serology.
  • MRI with contrast.
  • Initiate ART once acute infection controlled.
  • Treat SE per standard algorithm.

Case 36. 6-month-old SE day 3 on midazolam infusion; persistent electrographic seizures.

  • Add pentobarbital infusion.
  • Initiate ketogenic diet within 7 days.
  • Empiric pyridoxine 100 mg IV.
  • Empiric immunotherapy (steroids + IVIG).
  • Comprehensive genetic and metabolic workup.

Case 37. 14-year-old with traumatic brain injury and SE on day 1.

  • BZD then second-line ASM per algorithm.
  • Maintain CPP, normocapnia, normothermia.
  • cEEG continuous.
  • BTF guidelines apply for TBI management.
  • Antiseizure prophylaxis continues for 7 days post-injury.

Case 38. 3-week-old in SE; lab shows ammonia 350 µmol/L.

  • Hyperammonemia — likely inborn error of metabolism (urea cycle defect).
  • Stop protein intake; D10 + intralipid for calories.
  • Sodium benzoate + sodium phenylacetate (ammonul) IV.
  • L-arginine, L-carnitine.

  • Send amino acids, organic acids, urine orotic acid.

Case 39. 8-year-old known epilepsy on cannabidiol therapy, in SE.

  • Treat SE per standard algorithm.
  • Cannabidiol interactions: increases clobazam levels — check level.
  • Adjust maintenance therapy post-event.
  • Consider compliance and recent dose changes.

Case 40. 5-year-old SRSE day 14; family asking about long-term outcomes.

  • SRSE carries high mortality (30–50%) and morbidity.
  • Survivors typically have drug-resistant epilepsy, cognitive impairment, behavioral disorders.
  • Outcome highly etiology-dependent.
  • Honest, compassionate communication; palliative care integration.
  • Continued search for treatable etiology; rehab planning if recovery occurs.

11 · One-Page Infographic — Chapter 64

Pathophysiology Flow

  • Excitation (NMDA/AMPA) ↑ vs Inhibition (GABA-A) ↓ → seizure.
  • Prolonged SE → GABA-A receptor internalization → BZD resistance.
  • NMDA receptor membrane trafficking → glutamate hypersensitivity.
  • Excitotoxicity → calcium overload → apoptosis/necrosis.
  • Neonates: GABA may be excitatory (NKCC1 dominance) → phenobarbital first-line.

Diagnostic Algorithm

  • 0–5 min: ABCs, glucose, IV access, labs.
  • 5–15 min: BZD ×2.
  • 15–40 min: second-line ASM (ESETT — lev = fosphenytoin = valproate).
  • 40 min+: anesthetic infusion + intubation + cEEG.
  • Send: labs, ASM levels, tox, infectious + autoimmune panels.

Critical Thresholds

  • t1 = 5 min, t2 = 30 min.
  • BZD dosing: lorazepam 0.1 mg/kg (max 4 mg); midazolam 0.2 mg/kg IN/IM/buccal.
  • Second-line: lev 40–60 mg/kg; fosphenytoin 20 PE/kg; valproate 20–40 mg/kg.
  • Anesthetic: midazolam 0.05–2 mg/kg/h; pentobarbital 1–5 mg/kg/h; ketamine 1–10 mg/kg/h.
  • cEEG burst suppression: interburst 5–10 sec.

Red Flags

  • SE not responding to BZD within 10 min.
  • Persistent altered mental status post-CSE → NCSE.
  • SE in neonate / young infant — metabolic, infectious, pyridoxine-dependent.
  • Febrile prodrome + new-onset SE → FIRES.
  • Behavioral/psychiatric change + SE → anti-NMDAR encephalitis.

Exam Pearls

  • Treat first BZD at full dose — under-dosing = treatment failure.
  • ESETT: any of lev / fosphenytoin / valproate second-line.
  • Neonate first-line = phenobarbital, NOT BZD.
  • Avoid valproate in <2 y or mitochondrial disease.
  • Ketamine: NMDA antagonist, useful in late RSE.
  • Propofol: caution PRIS in pediatrics.
  • Ketogenic diet in SRSE/FIRES within 7 days.
  • Anakinra/tocilizumab: emerging in FIRES.
  • cEEG ≥24 h — captures ~85–90% of NCSE.
