1 · Major Cell Types — Neurons

Cellular architecture of neurons
  • Soma (cell body): nucleus, ER for protein synthesis, abundant mitochondria for ATP.
  • Dendrites: short local processes specialized to receive input from other neurons.
  • Axon: single output process; carries action potentials to other neurons, muscle, or organs at distances up to meters.
  • Synapses: chemical (most pediatric ICU-relevant) and electrical (gap junctions — allow direct ion passage).
ICU layer: Critical illness disrupts axonal transport, dendritic spine maintenance, and mitochondrial function — basis for "secondary injury" cascade after TBI, HIE, and sepsis.
Action potential and ion channels
  • Resting membrane potential ~ −65 mV maintained by Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase and K⁺ leak channels.
  • Voltage-gated Na⁺ channels open at threshold → rapid depolarization (rising phase).
  • Voltage-gated K⁺ channels open → repolarization.
  • Refractory periods (absolute and relative) prevent reverse propagation.
  • Conduction velocity: depends on axon diameter and myelination (saltatory conduction at nodes of Ranvier).
  • Ion gradient maintenance is highly ATP-dependent — early failure with ischemia or hypoglycemia.
Neurotransmitters — major categories
  • Amino acids: glutamate (main excitatory), GABA and glycine (main inhibitory).
  • Acetylcholine: neuromuscular junction, autonomic ganglia, CNS arousal/attention.
  • Monoamines (catecholamines): dopamine, norepinephrine, epinephrine — modulatory.
  • Serotonin (5-HT): mood, sleep, appetite, autonomic regulation.
  • Histamine: arousal, allergic response.
  • Neuropeptides: substance P, enkephalins, endorphins, oxytocin, vasopressin, hypothalamic releasing factors.
  • Gases: NO, CO — diffusible second messengers in vascular and synaptic signaling.
Glutamate receptors
  • Ionotropic:
    • NMDA — Ca²⁺-permeable; Mg²⁺ block at rest; requires glutamate + glycine; central to LTP and excitotoxicity.
    • AMPA — Na⁺-permeable; primary fast excitatory current; gates depolarization that relieves NMDA Mg block.
    • Kainate — modulatory; some Ca²⁺ permeability.
  • Metabotropic (mGluR): G-protein coupled; eight subtypes; slower signaling.
  • Excitotoxicity: excess glutamate → Ca²⁺ overload → mitochondrial collapse → apoptosis/necrosis (post-arrest, TBI, stroke, status epilepticus).
GABA and glycine receptors
  • GABA-A: ionotropic Cl⁻ channel; benzodiazepine, barbiturate, propofol, volatile anesthetic binding sites.
  • GABA-B: metabotropic; baclofen is selective agonist.
  • Glycine: spinal cord and brainstem inhibition; strychnine blocks.
  • Developmental switch (Rogers Ch 56): NKCC1 dominant in early development (high intracellular Cl⁻ → GABA depolarizing/excitatory); KCC2 expression increases postnatally (low intracellular Cl⁻ → GABA hyperpolarizing/inhibitory).
  • Clinical implication: phenobarbital first-line in neonatal SE (BZDs less reliable).
Acetylcholine
  • Nicotinic receptors (nAChR): ionotropic; NMJ, autonomic ganglia, CNS arousal.
  • Muscarinic receptors (mAChR): M1–M5 metabotropic subtypes; smooth muscle, gland, cardiac, CNS.
  • Acetylcholinesterase (AChE) terminates signal.
  • Pharmacology in ICU: rocuronium (nAChR antagonist for paralysis), neostigmine (AChE inhibitor for reversal), atropine (mAChR antagonist), pyridostigmine (myasthenia).

2 · Glial Cells

Microglia
  • Phagocytic cells derived from peripheral macrophages (yolk-sac origin).
  • Normally quiescent ("resting") in CNS.
  • Activated by LPS (infection), thrombin (injury), DAMPs, cytokines.
  • Activation can be neurotoxic (proinflammatory M1) or neuroprotective (M2).
  • Roles in synaptic pruning during development; complement-mediated removal of weak synapses.
  • Implicated in HIE, TBI, neurodegenerative disease, and infection.
Astrocytes
  • Most abundant CNS glia; star-shaped; form the glia limitans.
  • Functions:
    • Maintenance of extracellular K⁺ and glutamate homeostasis.
    • Provide metabolic substrate (lactate) to neurons via astrocyte–neuron lactate shuttle.
    • Form the blood-brain barrier via end-feet on capillaries.
    • Participate in synaptic pruning and plasticity.
    • Reactive astrogliosis after injury (glial scar formation).
Oligodendrocytes and Schwann cells
  • Oligodendrocytes: form CNS myelin; one oligodendrocyte myelinates multiple axon segments.
  • Schwann cells: form PNS myelin; one Schwann cell per axon segment.
  • Myelination enables saltatory conduction at nodes of Ranvier.
  • Pediatric myelination: begins in utero; major spurt in first 2 years; continues into adolescence (frontal lobes last).
  • Demyelinating diseases: multiple sclerosis (adult), ADEM, transverse myelitis, leukodystrophies, Guillain-Barré (PNS).
Ependymal cells
  • Line the ventricles and central canal of spinal cord.
  • Choroid plexus epithelium produces CSF.
  • Cilia promote CSF flow through ventricular system.
  • Ependymal disruption in IVH, ventriculitis, hydrocephalus.

3 · Major Anatomic Organization

Cerebrum — telencephalon
  • Cerebral cortex: 6 layers; primary motor, sensory, association, frontal, parietal, occipital, temporal.
  • Basal ganglia: caudate, putamen, globus pallidus, subthalamic nucleus, substantia nigra — motor control, behavior.
  • Limbic system: amygdala, hippocampus, cingulate, fornix — emotion, memory.
  • White matter: corpus callosum, internal capsule, anterior commissure.
Diencephalon
  • Thalamus: sensory relay; consciousness gateway.
  • Hypothalamus: autonomic, endocrine, thermoregulation, satiety, sleep, ADH/oxytocin.
  • Subthalamus, epithalamus (pineal gland).
Brainstem
  • Midbrain: cerebral peduncles, tectum (superior/inferior colliculi), cerebral aqueduct, CN III and IV.
  • Pons: cerebellar communication via middle cerebellar peduncle; CN V, VI, VII, VIII; pontine respiratory centers.
  • Medulla: medullary respiratory centers, cardiovascular centers, CN IX–XII; pyramidal decussation; vomiting and swallowing centers.
  • Reticular activating system (RAS): consciousness arousal — projects from brainstem to thalamus to cortex.
Cerebellum
  • Three lobes: anterior, posterior, flocculonodular.
  • Functions: motor coordination, balance, motor learning, cognition/emotion contribution.
  • Peduncles: superior (output), middle (input from pons), inferior (input from spinal cord and brainstem).
  • Tonsillar herniation through foramen magnum → medullary compression (Rogers Ch 62).
Spinal cord
  • Gray matter (H-shaped) and surrounding white matter.
  • Dorsal columns: proprioception, vibration, fine touch (decussate in medulla).
  • Spinothalamic tracts: pain, temperature, crude touch (decussate at level of entry).
  • Corticospinal tracts: voluntary motor (decussate in medulla — pyramidal decussation).
  • Anterior horn cells: lower motor neurons.
  • Central canal continuous with 4th ventricle.
  • Cord ends at L1–L2 in adults, L2–L3 in newborns.
Peripheral nervous system
  • Cranial nerves (12 pairs).
  • Spinal nerves (31 pairs): 8 cervical, 12 thoracic, 5 lumbar, 5 sacral, 1 coccygeal.
  • Autonomic nervous system:
    • Sympathetic: thoracolumbar; postganglionic NE; fight or flight.
    • Parasympathetic: craniosacral; postganglionic ACh; rest and digest.
    • Enteric: independent gut nervous system.
  • Postganglionic sympathetic neurons NE → α/β-adrenergic receptors.

4 · Blood-Brain Barrier and Circumventricular Organs

Blood-brain barrier (BBB)
  • Tight junctions between cerebral capillary endothelial cells.
  • Astrocyte end-feet contribute to maintenance.
  • Pericytes regulate capillary blood flow and BBB integrity.
  • Active transport for glucose (GLUT1), amino acids, and ions.
  • Excludes most large/hydrophilic molecules; lipophilic drugs cross readily.
  • BBB disruption: TBI, stroke, infection (meningitis, encephalitis), tumor, radiation.
Circumventricular organs (CVO) — BBB-deficient regions
  • Area postrema: vomiting center (chemoreceptor trigger zone).
  • Median eminence: hypothalamic-pituitary regulation.
  • Subfornical organ: thirst, fluid balance (senses Ang II).
  • Organum vasculosum of lamina terminalis (OVLT): osmoreceptor.
  • Pineal gland: melatonin.
  • Subcommissural organ.
  • Posterior pituitary: hormone secretion (ADH, oxytocin).
ICU layer: CVO permits direct sensing of plasma signals (osmolality, Ang II, cytokines) that orchestrate autonomic and endocrine responses. Posterior pituitary dysfunction → central diabetes insipidus after TBI/AHT (Rogers Ch 61, 62).

5 · Brain and Spinal Cord Vasculature

Anterior circulation
  • Internal carotid arteries → anterior cerebral (ACA) and middle cerebral (MCA).
  • ACA: medial frontal, parietal; supplies leg motor cortex.
  • MCA: lateral cortex (most of frontal, parietal, temporal); supplies face/arm motor; M1 main stem, M2 superior/inferior division.
  • MCA territory infarct: hemiparesis (face + arm > leg), aphasia (dominant hemisphere), hemineglect (non-dominant).
Posterior circulation
  • Vertebral arteries → basilar artery → posterior cerebral arteries (PCA).
  • PCA: occipital lobe, medial temporal lobe, splenium of corpus callosum.
  • Basilar: pons, midbrain, cerebellum (PICA, AICA, SCA).
  • Vertebrobasilar stroke: ataxia, vertigo, dysarthria, dysphagia, diplopia.
Circle of Willis
  • Anastomotic ring connecting anterior and posterior circulations.
  • Anterior communicating artery (ACoA): connects two ACAs.
  • Posterior communicating arteries (PCoA): connect ICA to PCA bilaterally.
  • Provides collateral flow if one major vessel obstructed.
  • Anatomic variations common (~50% have incomplete circle).
  • Most common aneurysm site: ACoA (adults); pediatric distribution different.
Spinal cord vasculature
  • Anterior spinal artery (ASA): single midline; supplies anterior 2/3 of cord (motor + spinothalamic).
  • Posterior spinal arteries (PSA): paired; supply posterior 1/3 (dorsal columns).
  • Radicular arteries reinforce at multiple levels; artery of Adamkiewicz (largest, T9–T12) supplies lumbar enlargement.
  • ASA syndrome: motor + spinothalamic loss with preserved proprioception (after thoracic aortic surgery, vasculitis).
Venous drainage
  • Superficial cortical veins → superior sagittal, lateral, sigmoid sinuses → internal jugular veins.
  • Deep venous system: internal cerebral veins → vein of Galen → straight sinus → confluence of sinuses (torcular Herophili).
  • CSVT discussion in Rogers Ch 65 and Zimmerman Ch 66.

6 · Developmental Cerebrovascular Physiology

CBF changes with age (key Zimmerman emphasis)
  • Term newborn: ~40 mL/100 g brain/minute.
  • Peak: 4–8 years of age at ~100 mL/100 g brain/minute.
  • Older adolescents: returns toward adult value ~50 mL/100 g brain/minute.
  • Mirrors CMRO₂ peak in childhood — increased metabolic demand during synaptic pruning.
  • Implication: CPP targets must be age-stratified (Rogers Ch 62 BTF guidelines).
Cerebrovascular reactivity
  • CO₂ reactivity: functional from birth in term newborns; relatively unchanged with age.
  • O₂ reactivity: functional from birth.
  • BP-dependent autoregulation: operates over narrower BP range in younger children.
  • This narrower range explains pediatric vulnerability to hypotension after TBI/HIE.
Cerebrovascular development
  • Vascularization begins early in embryogenesis.
  • Germinal matrix vasculature in premature infants is fragile — IVH risk.
  • Pial vascular plexus develops then penetrates the parenchyma.
  • Watershed zones develop relatively later — explains vulnerability in HIE.
  • Myelination requires substantial CBF; oligodendrocytes are vulnerable to hypoxia-ischemia in premature white matter (periventricular leukomalacia).

7 · Synaptogenesis, Pruning, and Critical Periods

Stages of synapse development
  • Synapse formation begins prenatally; peak synaptogenesis in early childhood.
  • Synaptic density peaks around 1–4 years (varies by region).
  • Synaptic pruning continues into adolescence — frontal lobes last.
  • Experience-dependent modification shapes circuits ("use it or lose it").
  • Critical periods: time windows when specific circuits are highly plastic and dependent on input (e.g., visual cortex, language).
Mechanisms of plasticity
  • Long-term potentiation (LTP): NMDA-dependent strengthening of synapses.
  • Long-term depression (LTD): synapse weakening.
  • BDNF and other neurotrophins support plasticity.
  • Critical illness, sedation, immobility, sleep deprivation, malnutrition all impair plasticity — basis for early mobility and rehabilitation (Zimmerman Ch 69).
ICU implications of developmental plasticity
  • Prolonged sedation, especially with anesthetics that affect NMDA/GABA systems, may have lasting neurodevelopmental effects (FDA black box warning for repeated/prolonged anesthesia < 3 years).
  • Early intervention and rehabilitation leverage plasticity for recovery.
  • Sleep promotion and environmental enrichment support development.
  • Parental engagement and skin-to-skin contact support neurodevelopment.

12 · 40 SAQs — Zimmerman Ch 58 (STUDY MODE)

SAQ 1. List 4 components of a neuron and their functions.

  • Soma: cell body with nucleus, ER, mitochondria.
  • Dendrites: receive input from other neurons.
  • Axon: single output process; carries action potentials.
  • Synapse: chemical or electrical junction for cell-cell communication.

SAQ 2. Major neurotransmitter categories.

  • Amino acids (glutamate, GABA, glycine).
  • Acetylcholine.
  • Monoamines (DA, NE, Epi).
  • Serotonin (5-HT).
  • Histamine.
  • Neuropeptides.
  • Gases (NO, CO).

SAQ 3. Glutamate receptor types.

  • Ionotropic: NMDA, AMPA, kainate.
  • Metabotropic: mGluR 1-8 (G-protein coupled).

SAQ 4. Excitotoxicity cascade.

  • Excess glutamate → NMDA/AMPA overactivation.
  • Ca²⁺ overload → mitochondrial collapse.
  • Activation of proteases/lipases/nucleases.
  • ROS/RNS during reperfusion.
  • Apoptosis/necroptosis → cell death.

SAQ 5. GABA developmental switch — mechanism and clinical relevance.

  • NKCC1 dominant in early development → high intracellular Cl⁻ → GABA-A opens Cl⁻ channel → Cl⁻ efflux → depolarization → GABA EXCITATORY.
  • KCC2 expression increases postnatally → low intracellular Cl⁻ → GABA INHIBITORY.
  • Clinical: phenobarbital first-line in neonatal SE (BZDs less reliable).

SAQ 6. Acetylcholine receptors.

  • Nicotinic (ionotropic): NMJ, autonomic ganglia, CNS arousal.
  • Muscarinic (M1-M5, metabotropic): smooth muscle, gland, cardiac, CNS.

SAQ 7. Functions of microglia.

  • Phagocytic; immune surveillance.
  • Activated by infection (LPS), injury (thrombin), DAMPs.
  • Synaptic pruning during development.
  • Can be neurotoxic (M1) or neuroprotective (M2).
  • Therapeutic target in HIE, TBI, CNS infection.

SAQ 8. Functions of astrocytes.

  • Most abundant glia; form BBB via end-feet.
  • K⁺ buffering; glutamate reuptake.
  • Lactate shuttle to neurons.
  • Reactive astrogliosis → glial scar after injury.
  • GFAP biomarker (released in TBI).

SAQ 9. Oligodendrocytes vs Schwann cells.

  • Oligodendrocytes: CNS myelin; one cell myelinates multiple axons.
  • Schwann cells: PNS myelin; one Schwann cell per axon segment.
  • Both enable saltatory conduction at nodes of Ranvier.

SAQ 10. Pediatric myelination timeline.

  • Begins in utero; major spurt first 2 years.
  • Continues into adolescence.
  • Frontal lobes mature last (into early 20s).

SAQ 11. Blood-brain barrier components.

  • Tight junctions between cerebral capillary endothelial cells.
  • Astrocyte end-feet.
  • Pericytes.
  • Selective transport via GLUT1 for glucose, amino acid transporters.
  • Excludes most large/hydrophilic molecules.

SAQ 12. Circumventricular organs (BBB-deficient regions).

  • Area postrema (CTZ — vomiting).
  • Median eminence (hypothalamic-pituitary).
  • Subfornical organ (thirst, Ang II sensing).
  • OVLT (osmoreceptor).
  • Pineal gland (melatonin).
  • Subcommissural organ.
  • Posterior pituitary (ADH, oxytocin).

SAQ 13. Cerebral cortex layers and key cell types.

  • 6 cortical layers (I–VI) defined by cell density and connectivity.
  • Layer III/V pyramidal neurons — selectively vulnerable in HIE (Rogers Ch 66).
  • Layer IV granular — sensory input.
  • Layer V output to subcortical structures.

SAQ 14. Basal ganglia nuclei.

  • Caudate.
  • Putamen.
  • Globus pallidus (internal + external).
  • Subthalamic nucleus.
  • Substantia nigra.
  • Selectively vulnerable in HIE; Parkinson's targets substantia nigra dopaminergic neurons.

SAQ 15. Limbic system components and functions.

  • Amygdala (emotion, fear).
  • Hippocampus (memory consolidation — CA1 selectively vulnerable in HIE).
  • Cingulate gyrus (attention, emotion).
  • Fornix (output of hippocampus).
  • Mammillary bodies.

SAQ 16. Thalamus functions.

  • Sensory relay (all sensory pathways except olfaction).
  • Motor relay.
  • Consciousness gateway (ARAS-thalamocortical).
  • Selectively vulnerable in severe HIE.

SAQ 17. Hypothalamic functions.

  • Autonomic regulation.
  • Endocrine (releasing factors, posterior pituitary).
  • Thermoregulation.
  • Satiety, water balance.
  • Sleep-wake cycle.
  • ADH and oxytocin secretion via posterior pituitary.

SAQ 18. Brainstem components and cranial nerves.

  • Midbrain: CN III (oculomotor), IV (trochlear).
  • Pons: CN V (trigeminal), VI (abducens), VII (facial), VIII (vestibulocochlear).
  • Medulla: CN IX (glossopharyngeal), X (vagus), XI (accessory), XII (hypoglossal).
  • CN I (olfactory) and II (optic) do NOT arise from brainstem.

SAQ 19. Reticular activating system function.

  • Promotes arousal/consciousness.
  • Projects from brainstem → thalamus → cortex.
  • Lesions cause coma (Rogers Ch 58 — Comatose Child).

SAQ 20. Cerebellum lobes and functions.

  • Anterior lobe — motor coordination.
  • Posterior lobe — cognitive/affective contribution (Schmahmann syndrome).
  • Flocculonodular lobe — balance/vestibular.
  • Three peduncles: superior (output), middle (input from pons), inferior (input from spinal cord/brainstem).

SAQ 21. Spinal cord tracts.

  • Dorsal columns — proprioception, vibration, fine touch (decussate in medulla).
  • Spinothalamic — pain, temperature, crude touch (decussate at entry).
  • Corticospinal — voluntary motor (pyramidal decussation in medulla).

SAQ 22. Spinal cord termination by age.

  • Adult: L1–L2.
  • Newborn: L2–L3.
  • Cauda equina below conus.
  • LP performed below conus to avoid cord injury.

SAQ 23. Autonomic nervous system divisions.

  • Sympathetic: thoracolumbar; postganglionic NE; fight-or-flight.
  • Parasympathetic: craniosacral; postganglionic ACh; rest-and-digest.
  • Enteric: gut-specific, semi-autonomous.

SAQ 24. Anterior circulation territory.

  • ACA: medial frontal/parietal; leg motor cortex.
  • MCA: lateral cortex (face/arm motor predominantly), Broca/Wernicke (dominant), neglect (non-dominant).

SAQ 25. Posterior circulation territory.

  • PCA: occipital lobe, medial temporal, splenium of corpus callosum.
  • Basilar: pons, midbrain, cerebellum (via PICA, AICA, SCA).

SAQ 26. Circle of Willis components.

  • Anterior communicating artery (ACoA).
  • Two A1 segments of ACA.
  • Two ICA stumps with M1 and PCoA origins.
  • Two PCoA.
  • Two P1 segments of PCA.
  • Basilar artery terminus.
  • ~50% have incomplete circle.

SAQ 27. Spinal cord vascular supply.

  • Anterior spinal artery (single midline) — anterior 2/3 of cord.
  • Posterior spinal arteries (paired) — posterior 1/3.
  • Artery of Adamkiewicz — T9–T12 reinforces lumbar enlargement.
  • ASA syndrome: motor + spinothalamic loss with preserved proprioception.

SAQ 28. CBF developmental curve.

  • Newborn ~40 mL/100 g/min.
  • Peak 4–8 years ~100 mL/100 g/min.
  • Adolescent/adult ~50 mL/100 g/min.
  • Mirrors CMRO₂ during synaptic pruning years.

SAQ 29. Cerebrovascular reactivity development.

  • CO₂ reactivity functional from birth in term newborns.
  • O₂ reactivity functional from birth.
  • BP-dependent autoregulation operates over narrower range in younger children.
  • Pediatric vulnerability to hypotension post-injury.

SAQ 30. Periventricular leukomalacia mechanism.

  • White matter injury in premature infants.
  • Oligodendrocyte precursors vulnerable to hypoxia-ischemia.
  • Affects motor pathways → cerebral palsy (spastic diplegia).
  • Watershed white matter regions especially vulnerable in premature infants.

SAQ 31. Synaptic density developmental pattern.

  • Rapid synaptogenesis early in development.
  • Peak 1–4 years (varies by region).
  • Use-dependent pruning continues into adolescence.
  • Frontal lobe pruning continues into early 20s.

SAQ 32. Critical periods of development.

  • Time windows when circuits are highly plastic and dependent on input.
  • Visual cortex: birth – ~8 years (amblyopia treatable in this window).
  • Language: early childhood.
  • Cessation defines mature pattern.

SAQ 33. Plasticity mechanisms.

  • LTP (long-term potentiation): NMDA-dependent strengthening.
  • LTD (long-term depression): weakening.
  • BDNF and neurotrophins support plasticity.
  • Impaired by critical illness, prolonged sedation, malnutrition, sleep deprivation.

SAQ 34. FDA anesthesia warning < 3 years.

  • Repeated or prolonged general anesthesia in children < 3 years may affect neurodevelopment.
  • Coordinate multidisciplinary care to minimize cumulative exposure.
  • Discuss with family for elective procedures.
  • Emergency or essential surgery should NOT be delayed.

SAQ 35. Resting and threshold membrane potentials.

  • Resting: ~ −65 mV.
  • Threshold: ~ −55 mV.
  • Peak depolarization: ~ +30 mV.
  • Maintained by Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase; ATP-dependent.

SAQ 36. Selectively vulnerable brain regions in HIE.

  • Hippocampal CA1 (Sommer sector).
  • Cortical layers 3 and 5 pyramidal neurons.
  • Basal ganglia (caudate, putamen).
  • Cerebellar Purkinje cells.
  • Thalamus.
  • Watershed zones.

SAQ 37. Neuroprotective mechanisms.

  • Heat shock proteins (HSP72).
  • Hypoxia-inducible factors (HIF-1α).
  • BDNF, NGF, neurotrophins.
  • Antioxidant systems (glutathione, SOD, catalase).
  • Endogenous adenosine.
  • Basis for ischemic preconditioning research.

SAQ 38. Venous drainage of brain.

  • Superficial veins → superior sagittal, lateral, sigmoid sinuses → IJV.
  • Deep system: internal cerebral veins → vein of Galen → straight sinus.
  • Confluence at torcular Herophili.
  • CSVT involves these sinuses (Zimmerman Ch 66).

SAQ 39. CSF dynamics.

  • Production ~0.3 mL/min (~500 mL/day) primarily by choroid plexus.
  • Total volume ~150 mL adult, ~50–80 mL child, ~50 mL infant.
  • Flow: lateral ventricles → 3rd → aqueduct → 4th → subarachnoid → arachnoid granulations → venous sinuses.
  • Newer "glymphatic" theory: paravascular flow with aquaporin-4 channels.

SAQ 40. Top 5 examiner traps in nervous system development.

  • Forgetting GABA is excitatory in neonates (use phenobarbital, not BZD).
  • Treating pediatric CPP without age-stratified targets.
  • Ignoring premature white matter vulnerability (PVL).
  • Anchoring on "open fontanelle protects from herniation" (it doesn't).
  • Forgetting that ARAS lesions cause coma regardless of cortex.

13 · 15 PICU/Theory-Applied Cases

Case 1. 3-day-old neonate with status epilepticus.

  • Phenobarbital 20 mg/kg IV first-line (NKCC1-dominant GABA-excitatory phenotype).
  • BZDs less reliable in this age.
  • Pyridoxine 100 mg IV trial.
  • Investigate metabolic, infectious, structural causes.

Case 2. 6-year-old severe TBI on multiple sedatives.

  • Age in peak CBF/CMRO₂ phase — high metabolic demand.
  • CPP ≥ 50 mm Hg target.
  • NMDA antagonism (ketamine) rational adjunct as BZD efficacy declines.
  • Aggressive normothermia, normocapnia.

Case 3. 26-week premature infant developing IVH on day 3.

  • Germinal matrix vasculature is fragile.
  • Aggressive prevention of hypotension, hyperoxemia, fluctuations in cerebral perfusion.
  • Indomethacin or ibuprofen for PDA closure if applicable.
  • Watch for hydrocephalus development.

Case 4. Adolescent with new diabetes insipidus after severe TBI.

  • Posterior pituitary dysfunction — ADH deficiency.
  • Treat with DDAVP; cautious free water replacement.
  • Screen anterior axis: cortisol, T4.
  • Monitor Na trajectory (avoid rapid correction).

Case 5. 10-year-old with MELAS stroke-like episode.

  • Mitochondrial dysfunction → energy failure.
  • L-arginine 0.5 g/kg IV bolus + infusion.
  • Avoid valproate (mitochondrial toxicity).
  • Cofactor cocktail (CoQ10, carnitine, riboflavin, thiamine).

Case 6. Infant after cardiac arrest with diffuse cortical and basal ganglia DWI restriction.

  • HIE in selectively vulnerable regions.
  • Cortical layers 3/5, basal ganglia, Purkinje cells.
  • Apply post-arrest bundle.
  • Multimodal prognostication ≥ 72 h.

Case 7. Child with MS-like demyelinating disease.

  • Oligodendrocyte injury affecting CNS myelin.
  • MRI: white matter lesions.
  • ADEM (monophasic) vs MS vs MOGAD distinction.
  • Steroids first-line; IVIG/PLEX if refractory.

Case 8. Patient with Guillain-Barré syndrome.

  • PNS demyelination — Schwann cell-mediated immune injury.
  • Ascending weakness, areflexia.
  • NCS shows demyelination.
  • IVIG or PLEX treatment.
  • Watch for respiratory failure (VC, NIF monitoring).

Case 9. Anterior spinal artery syndrome post-aortic surgery.

  • Motor + spinothalamic loss; dorsal columns preserved.
  • Maintain MAP for spinal cord perfusion.
  • CSF drainage may help in select cases.
  • Rehab planning.

Case 10. 15-year-old with thunderclap headache and ACoA aneurysm.

  • Most common adult aneurysm site (pediatric distribution different).
  • SAH management; nimodipine; vasospasm monitoring TCD.
  • Endovascular coiling vs clipping.

Case 11. Toddler with intractable vomiting from chemotherapy.

  • Area postrema (CTZ — BBB-deficient circumventricular organ) directly sensing chemo.
  • 5-HT3 antagonists (ondansetron), dexamethasone, NK1 antagonists (aprepitant).

Case 12. Patient on prolonged propofol infusion in PICU.

  • Propofol acts on GABA-A receptors.
  • Risk of propofol infusion syndrome (PRIS) at > 4 mg/kg/h or > 48 h.
  • Acidosis, rhabdomyolysis, cardiac failure.
  • Use cautiously in pediatrics.

Case 13. Child needing repeated anesthesia < 3 years for cancer treatment.

  • FDA warning consideration.
  • Coordinate multidisciplinary care to minimize cumulative exposure.
  • Discuss with family — risks vs benefits.
  • Do NOT delay essential treatment.

Case 14. Concussion in adolescent athlete.

  • Diffuse axonal injury at microscale.
  • Myelin and axon integrity disrupted.
  • Frontal lobes (still maturing) particularly vulnerable.
  • Cognitive rest then graduated return to play (CISG Berlin/Amsterdam).

Case 15. PICU patient with prolonged immobilization and sedation.

  • Impairs plasticity and recovery.
  • Early mobility (Zimmerman Ch 69); minimize sedation; sleep promotion.
  • Family engagement; environmental enrichment.
  • PICS-p framework discussion.

14 · One-Page Infographic — Zimmerman Ch 58

Cells

  • Neurons (soma, dendrites, axon, synapse).
  • Glia: microglia, astrocytes (BBB end-feet), oligodendrocytes (CNS myelin), Schwann (PNS myelin), ependyma (CSF).

Receptors and switches

  • NMDA, AMPA, kainate, mGluR — excitatory.
  • GABA-A (ionotropic) and GABA-B; glycine.
  • NKCC1 → KCC2 developmental switch — GABA excitatory → inhibitory.
  • Phenobarbital first-line in neonatal SE.

Anatomy highlights

  • Circle of Willis collateralizes anterior + posterior circulations.
  • ASA: motor + STT; PSA: dorsal columns.
  • Spinal cord ends L1–L2 (adult), L2–L3 (newborn).
  • Cranial nerves III–XII originate in brainstem (I, II do not).

Development

  • CBF: newborn ~40 → peak 4–8 y ~100 → adult ~50.
  • CO₂/O₂ reactivity from birth; BP autoregulation narrower in younger children.
  • Synaptogenesis peaks 1–4 y; pruning to adolescence; frontal lobes mature last.
  • Critical periods (visual, language) — high plasticity.
  • Premature white matter vulnerability → PVL.

ICU pearls

  • Phenobarbital first in neonatal SE.
  • Age-stratified CPP targets.
  • NMDA antagonism rational in late refractory SE (BZD escape).
  • Posterior pituitary injury → central DI.
  • FDA warning: prolonged anesthesia < 3 y.
  • Open fontanelle does NOT prevent herniation.
