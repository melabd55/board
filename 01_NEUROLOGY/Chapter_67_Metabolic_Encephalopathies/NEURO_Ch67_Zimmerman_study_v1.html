1 · Structured Zimmerman Mirror — CNS Infections Topics

PEARLS (Zimmerman Ch 67 highlights)
  • Classic triad (fever + neck stiffness + altered mental status) present in < 50% of pediatric meningitis cases.
  • Streptococcus species (viridans, pneumoniae, milleri) and Staphylococcus aureus are most common pediatric subdural empyema organisms (~30% no growth).
  • Viral encephalitis is common — enteroviruses, herpesviruses, arboviruses lead the burden.
  • Time-to-antibiotic delays directly increase mortality — risk of poor outcome rises ~30% per hour of delay.
  • Postinfectious immune-mediated disease (ADEM, AHL) and autoimmune encephalitis (anti-NMDAR) require high index of suspicion.
ICU layer: Empiric broad-spectrum antibiotics + acyclovir within 1 hour of meningitis suspicion. Do NOT delay for LP if LP must be delayed for safety.
BACTERIAL MENINGITIS — EPIDEMIOLOGY & PATHOGENS BY AGE
  • Incidence highest in first year of life (~65/100,000); decreased dramatically with Hib, conjugate pneumococcal, and meningococcal vaccines.
  • Neonates (< 1 month): Group B Streptococcus (GBS / S. agalactiae), E. coli, Listeria monocytogenes, Klebsiella.
  • 1–2 months: transitional — S. pneumoniae, N. meningitidis, late-onset GBS, E. coli, H. influenzae.
  • 2 months–5 years: S. pneumoniae, N. meningitidis, H. influenzae type b (if unimmunized).
  • > 5 years: S. pneumoniae, N. meningitidis predominate.
  • CSF shunt-related / post-neurosurgical: coagulase-negative staphylococci, S. aureus (MSSA/MRSA), gram-negative bacilli including Pseudomonas, Cutibacterium acnes.
  • Asplenia / complement deficiency: increased risk of encapsulated organisms (S. pneumoniae, N. meningitidis, Salmonella).
PATHOGENESIS
  • Tissue injury via multiple mechanisms: bacterial proliferation in subarachnoid space, bacterial products, host immune response, leukocyte migration.
  • Parenchymal injury mechanisms:
    • Ischemia from impaired CBF autoregulation, arterial/venous thrombosis, vasculitis, microvascular thrombosis, raised ICP.
    • Direct cytotoxicity from bacterial products and host immune mediators.
  • Bacterial virulence factors enable BBB penetration (E. coli type 1 fimbriae, OmpA; GBS pilus protein, fibrinogen-binding protein; S. pneumoniae phosphorylcholine).
  • Host susceptibility: PAI-1 polymorphism, IRAK-4 / MyD88 deficiency, complement factor H polymorphism, mannose-binding lectin variants.
CLINICAL MANIFESTATIONS
  • No combination of signs is 100% predictive — high index of suspicion required.
  • Classic triad in < 50% of pediatric cases (fever, neck stiffness, altered mental status).
  • Most have fever; nuchal rigidity often absent; mental status changes may be subtle (irritability).
  • Kernig and Brudzinski signs have limited utility in pediatric meningitis.
  • Petechiae/purpura — suggests meningococcus (also S. pneumoniae, Hib less commonly).
  • Poorly reactive pupils, bulging fontanel, diplopia, papilledema, vomiting → raised ICP.
  • Focal neurologic signs (cranial nerve involvement) in < 25% on admission.
  • Obtundation/coma in ~15%.
  • Seizures in 20–30% within first 3 days; risk factors include age < 2 years, pneumococcal etiology, altered mental status, CSF WBC < 1000.
  • Seizures associated with ~9× mortality increase.
CSF DIAGNOSIS
  • CSF analysis: WBC count + differential, protein, glucose, Gram stain, culture, lactate.
  • Multiplex PCR panels (FilmArray meningitis/encephalitis) increasingly used for rapid pathogen identification.
  • Bacterial pattern: WBC > 1000/µL with PMN predominance, high protein, low glucose (< 50% serum), Gram stain positive in 50–75%.
  • Viral pattern: lymphocyte/monocyte predominance, normal protein and glucose; enterovirus and mumps may have hypoglycorrhachia.
  • "Bloody tap" — correction approximations (e.g., 1 WBC per 500–1000 RBCs) not well validated.
  • Procalcitonin and CRP — utility uncertain for bacterial vs aseptic distinction.
  • CSF lactate — supports bacterial diagnosis but evidence mixed.
  • LP contraindications: coagulopathy, raised ICP, cardiorespiratory instability, mass effect on imaging.
  • Head CT before LP if focal neurologic signs or signs of raised ICP — but normal CT does NOT rule out herniation risk.
  • Repeat LP: consider if S. pneumoniae nonsusceptible to 3rd-gen cephalosporin, no clinical improvement, dexamethasone given, or neonatal gram-negative meningitis.
Trap: Do NOT delay empiric antibiotics waiting for LP. If LP must be delayed for safety reasons → blood cultures, then antibiotics.
EMPIRIC ANTIBIOTIC THERAPY BY AGE
  • < 1 month: ampicillin + cefotaxime (or cefepime when cefotaxime unavailable) ± aminoglycoside.
  • 1 month–5 years: vancomycin + 3rd-generation cephalosporin (ceftriaxone or cefotaxime).
  • > 5 years: vancomycin + 3rd-generation cephalosporin.
  • Asplenia / immunodeficiency: vancomycin + 3rd-gen cephalosporin.
  • Basilar skull fracture: vancomycin + 3rd-gen cephalosporin.
  • CSF shunt or post-neurosurgical: vancomycin + ceftazidime OR cefepime OR meropenem (cover Pseudomonas).
  • Cochlear implant: vancomycin + 3rd-gen cephalosporin (pneumococcal cover).
  • Add acyclovir if HSV encephalitis suspected (any age with altered mental status).
  • Add rifampin if S. pneumoniae fully resistant to 3rd-gen cephalosporin (susceptibility-dependent).
DEXAMETHASONE
  • Dexamethasone 0.15 mg/kg q6h × 2–4 days as adjunct — strongest evidence in H. influenzae type b meningitis.
  • Pneumococcal meningitis: pediatric evidence less robust; AAP supports use.
  • Bacterial meningitis: ideally administered BEFORE or with first antibiotic dose.
  • Reduces hearing loss in Hib (NEJM 1991 — Lebel); less benefit in non-Hib pediatric meningitis.
  • NOT used in viral meningitis or neonatal meningitis.
VIRAL ENCEPHALITIS
  • Enteroviruses (coxsackievirus, echovirus, enterovirus 71) — most common cause of pediatric viral meningitis/encephalitis worldwide.
  • Herpesviruses:
    • HSV-1 (sporadic encephalitis, classic temporal lobe involvement).
    • HSV-2 (neonatal disseminated and CNS infection).
    • VZV (post-varicella vasculopathy, also direct encephalitis).
    • EBV, CMV, HHV-6 (immunocompromised).
  • Arboviruses: West Nile, La Crosse, Eastern equine, Japanese encephalitis (regional).
  • Rabies — primarily bat exposure in North America; almost universally fatal once symptomatic.
  • HSV encephalitis: acyclovir 60 mg/kg/day IV in divided doses (20 mg/kg q8h) for 21 days; check PCR before stopping.
  • Untreated HSV encephalitis mortality ~70%; survivors with severe sequelae.
BRAIN ABSCESS & SUBDURAL EMPYEMA
  • Brain abscess: hematogenous spread (cyanotic CHD, endocarditis), direct extension (sinusitis, otitis, mastoiditis), trauma, neurosurgical.
  • Common pathogens: Streptococcus (viridans, intermedius/milleri group), Staphylococcus aureus, anaerobes (Bacteroides, Prevotella).
  • Imaging: MRI with contrast — ring-enhancing lesion with restricted diffusion on DWI (distinguishes from tumor/necrosis).
  • Treatment: surgical drainage (CT-guided aspiration or open) + 4–8 weeks IV antibiotics (vancomycin + ceftriaxone + metronidazole).
  • Pediatric subdural empyema: complication of frontal/ethmoidal sinusitis in adolescents; Streptococcus milleri group classic.
  • Pott's puffy tumor: subperiosteal abscess of frontal bone with associated intracranial complications.
POSTINFECTIOUS DEMYELINATION & AUTOIMMUNE ENCEPHALITIS
  • Acute disseminated encephalomyelitis (ADEM): monophasic, post-infectious or post-vaccination demyelinating disease.
    • Presentation: encephalopathy + multifocal neurologic deficits (typically 2–4 weeks post-trigger).
    • MRI: multifocal poorly demarcated lesions in white matter, basal ganglia, thalami, brainstem.
    • CSF: lymphocytic pleocytosis, elevated protein, oligoclonal bands less common than in MS.
    • Treatment: high-dose methylprednisolone 30 mg/kg/day × 5 d, then taper; IVIG or plasma exchange if refractory.
    • Typically monophasic; recurrence raises suspicion for MS or MOG-associated disease.
  • Acute hemorrhagic leukoencephalitis (AHL / Weston Hurst): severe variant of ADEM with hemorrhagic necrosis; high mortality.
  • Anti-NMDAR encephalitis: autoimmune; subacute psychiatric symptoms, dyskinesia, seizures, autonomic instability.
    • CSF anti-NMDAR antibody.
    • Ovarian teratoma search in adolescent females.
    • Treatment: methylprednisolone + IVIG; plasma exchange; second-line rituximab, cyclophosphamide.
  • MOG-associated disease (MOGAD): emerging recognition; optic neuritis, encephalitis, transverse myelitis; relapsing or monophasic.
  • Other autoimmune encephalitis: anti-LGI1, anti-GABA-B, anti-AMPA, anti-GAD65, anti-CASPR2.
TUBERCULOUS MENINGITIS (TBM)
  • Often subacute presentation — fever, headache, vomiting, behavioral change over weeks.
  • Risk factors: TB exposure, HIV, BCG-unvaccinated children, recent travel.
  • CSF: lymphocyte predominance, very high protein, low glucose, AFB stain low yield (10–40%), TB PCR (GeneXpert MTB/RIF), culture.
  • Imaging: basal meningeal enhancement, hydrocephalus, tuberculomas, infarcts (basal ganglia common).
  • Treatment: 4-drug ATT (isoniazid, rifampin, pyrazinamide, ± ethambutol or streptomycin) for 2 months → continuation phase.
  • Adjunctive dexamethasone reduces mortality (Thwaites NEJM 2004 — adult; pediatric extrapolation).
  • Pyridoxine to prevent isoniazid toxicity.
  • Often requires hydrocephalus management (EVD/VP shunt).
FUNGAL CNS INFECTION
  • Cryptococcus neoformans / gattii — immunocompromised (HIV, malignancy, transplant); LP with high opening pressure; india ink, cryptococcal antigen.
  • Aspergillus, Mucor — invasive in immunocompromised; angioinvasion.
  • Coccidioides — endemic SW US/Mexico; can cause meningitis.
  • Treatment: amphotericin B (liposomal preferred) + flucytosine for cryptococcal; voriconazole or isavuconazole for invasive aspergillosis.
  • Repeated LP for ICP management in cryptococcal meningitis.
NEUROIMAGING IN CNS INFECTION
  • MRI with contrast preferred when feasible.
  • Meningitis: meningeal enhancement, ventriculitis (FLAIR ependymal hyperintensity), abscess complications.
  • HSV encephalitis: bilateral asymmetric medial temporal/inferior frontal T2/FLAIR hyperintensity, DWI restriction, gyriform enhancement.
  • ADEM: multifocal poorly demarcated subcortical white matter lesions ± basal ganglia, thalami, brainstem.
  • Abscess: ring-enhancing lesion with central DWI restriction (vs tumor — no DWI restriction).
  • Tuberculous meningitis: basal cistern enhancement, tuberculomas, hydrocephalus, basal ganglia infarcts.
  • Anti-NMDAR encephalitis: often normal MRI; mesial temporal T2 hyperintensity in minority.
  • Subdural empyema: crescent-shaped subdural collection with strong rim enhancement and DWI restriction.
PREVENTION
  • Pneumococcal conjugate vaccine (PCV13/PCV15/PCV20).
  • Hib conjugate vaccine.
  • Meningococcal conjugate (MenACWY) and serogroup B (MenB) vaccines.
  • BCG (in TB-endemic regions).
  • Maternal GBS screening and intrapartum antibiotic prophylaxis.
  • Chemoprophylaxis for close contacts of N. meningitidis (rifampin, ceftriaxone, ciprofloxacin) and Hib (rifampin).
  • Pre-exposure rabies vaccine for high-risk groups; post-exposure rabies immunoglobulin + vaccine.
COMPLICATIONS
  • Acute: seizures, raised ICP, herniation, SIADH, CSW, septic shock, DIC.
  • Subacute: hydrocephalus, subdural effusion/empyema, vasculitis with stroke, cranial nerve palsies (especially CN VIII in pneumococcal).
  • Late: sensorineural hearing loss (10–30% pneumococcal), epilepsy, cognitive impairment, motor deficits, learning disability.
  • All children post-bacterial meningitis: audiology assessment within 4 weeks of discharge.

6 · 25 SAQs — Zimmerman Ch 67 (STUDY MODE)

SAQ 1. Classic triad of bacterial meningitis — and what proportion of pediatric cases present with it?

  • Fever + neck stiffness + altered mental status.
  • Present in < 50% of pediatric cases.
  • High index of suspicion required given absence of full triad.

SAQ 2. Empiric antibiotic regimen for neonate < 1 month with suspected meningitis.

  • Ampicillin + cefotaxime (or cefepime when cefotaxime unavailable) ± aminoglycoside.
  • Covers GBS, E. coli, Listeria, Klebsiella.
  • Acyclovir if HSV suspected.

SAQ 3. Empiric regimen for child > 1 month with bacterial meningitis.

  • Vancomycin + 3rd-generation cephalosporin (ceftriaxone or cefotaxime).
  • Covers S. pneumoniae (including resistant), N. meningitidis, H. influenzae.

SAQ 4. Empiric regimen for CSF shunt infection.

  • Vancomycin + ceftazidime OR cefepime OR meropenem.
  • Covers CoNS, S. aureus (MRSA), gram-negative including Pseudomonas.
  • Neurosurgery for shunt tap and externalization.

SAQ 5. When and how to administer dexamethasone in bacterial meningitis?

  • 0.15 mg/kg IV q6h × 2–4 days (max 10 mg/dose).
  • Strongest evidence in Hib meningitis (Lebel 1988 — reduced hearing loss).
  • AAP supports use in pneumococcal meningitis.
  • Give BEFORE or WITH first antibiotic dose.
  • Not used in viral or neonatal meningitis.

SAQ 6. Time-to-antibiotic significance in meningitis.

  • ~30% increase in poor outcome per hour of delay.
  • Goal: empiric antibiotics within 1 hour of suspicion.
  • Do NOT delay for LP if LP must be deferred.

SAQ 7. When should head CT precede LP?

  • Focal neurologic signs.
  • Signs of raised ICP (papilledema, bulging fontanelle, poorly reactive pupils).
  • GCS < 11 or significantly altered mental status.
  • Suspected mass-occupying lesion.
  • Coagulopathy.
  • Note: normal CT does NOT eliminate herniation risk.

SAQ 8. CSF pattern of bacterial meningitis.

  • WBC > 1000/µL with PMN predominance.
  • Elevated protein (> 100 mg/dL).
  • Low glucose (< 50% serum value).
  • Gram stain positive in 50–75%.
  • Elevated CSF lactate.

SAQ 9. CSF pattern of viral encephalitis.

  • Lymphocyte/monocyte predominance.
  • Normal protein and glucose typically.
  • Enterovirus and mumps may cause hypoglycorrhachia.
  • Multiplex PCR for HSV, EV, EBV, etc.

SAQ 10. HSV encephalitis — treatment.

  • Acyclovir 60 mg/kg/day IV divided q8h × 21 days.
  • Start empirically with suspicion; do not wait for PCR confirmation.
  • Monitor renal function; ensure hydration.
  • Repeat CSF HSV PCR before stopping.
  • Untreated mortality ~70%; treated < 20%.

SAQ 11. HSV encephalitis MRI signature.

  • Bilateral asymmetric medial temporal + inferior frontal lobe T2/FLAIR hyperintensity.
  • DWI restriction.
  • Gyriform enhancement.
  • Petechial hemorrhage on GRE/SWI.

SAQ 12. ADEM — clinical features and treatment.

  • Monophasic post-infectious or post-vaccination demyelination (2–4 weeks post-trigger).
  • Encephalopathy + multifocal neurologic deficits.
  • MRI: multifocal poorly demarcated white matter, basal ganglia, thalami, brainstem lesions.
  • Treatment: methylprednisolone 30 mg/kg/day × 5 days then taper; IVIG or PLEX if refractory.
  • Recurrence raises suspicion for MS or MOG-associated disease.

SAQ 13. Anti-NMDAR encephalitis — presentation.

  • Subacute behavioral / psychiatric changes (4–6 wks).
  • Movement disorder (orofacial dyskinesia, chorea, dystonia).
  • Seizures.
  • Autonomic instability (HR, BP swings, hypoventilation).
  • CSF anti-NMDAR antibody; lymphocytic pleocytosis.
  • Search ovarian teratoma in adolescent females.

SAQ 14. Anti-NMDAR encephalitis — treatment.

  • First-line: methylprednisolone 30 mg/kg/day + IVIG 2 g/kg total.
  • Plasma exchange as alternative or addition.
  • Second-line: rituximab 375 mg/m²/week × 4; cyclophosphamide.
  • Teratoma resection if found.
  • Long ICU course expected (months); good recovery with persistent treatment.

SAQ 15. Brain abscess MRI features.

  • Ring-enhancing lesion with central DWI restriction.
  • Surrounding vasogenic edema.
  • Distinguishes from tumor (no DWI restriction in tumor necrosis).

SAQ 16. Brain abscess — treatment principles.

  • Surgical drainage (CT-guided aspiration or open) when possible.
  • Empiric: vancomycin + ceftriaxone + metronidazole.
  • 4–8 weeks IV antibiotics.
  • Source identification (sinusitis, otitis, dental, endocarditis).
  • Serial imaging.

SAQ 17. Pediatric subdural empyema — typical organism and source.

  • Streptococcus milleri/intermedius group most common.
  • Source: frontal/ethmoidal sinusitis in adolescents.
  • Pott's puffy tumor — subperiosteal frontal abscess with intracranial extension.
  • Treatment: surgical drainage + IV antibiotics (vancomycin + ceftriaxone + metronidazole).

SAQ 18. TB meningitis CSF pattern.

  • Lymphocytic pleocytosis (100–500).
  • Very high protein (often > 200 mg/dL).
  • Low glucose.
  • AFB stain low yield (10–40%).
  • GeneXpert MTB/RIF — faster, more sensitive.
  • TB culture (gold standard, slow).

SAQ 19. TB meningitis treatment.

  • 4-drug ATT × 2 months: isoniazid + rifampin + pyrazinamide + ethambutol (or streptomycin).
  • Continuation phase: INH + RIF for additional 7–10 months.
  • Adjunctive dexamethasone (Thwaites 2004) reduces mortality.
  • Pyridoxine to prevent INH neuropathy.
  • Manage hydrocephalus (EVD/VP shunt).

SAQ 20. Late complications of bacterial meningitis.

  • Sensorineural hearing loss (10–30% pneumococcal).
  • Hydrocephalus.
  • Epilepsy.
  • Cognitive impairment, learning disability.
  • Motor deficits.
  • Vasculitis-related stroke (acute), encephalomalacia.
  • Audiology assessment at 4 weeks post-discharge for all.

SAQ 21. Pneumococcal meningitis — repeat LP indications.

  • Isolate nonsusceptible to 3rd-gen cephalosporin.
  • No clinical improvement after 48 h.
  • Dexamethasone given (mask fever response).
  • Neonates with gram-negative meningitis until CSF sterile.

SAQ 22. Vaccine-preventable bacterial meningitis pathogens.

  • Streptococcus pneumoniae (PCV13/15/20).
  • Haemophilus influenzae type b (Hib).
  • Neisseria meningitidis serogroups A, C, W, Y (MenACWY) and B (MenB).
  • Maternal GBS prophylaxis (intrapartum penicillin).

SAQ 23. Meningococcal close-contact chemoprophylaxis.

  • Rifampin 600 mg q12h × 2 days (adult dose; weight-based in children).
  • Ceftriaxone 250 mg IM single dose (alternative; safe in pregnancy).
  • Ciprofloxacin 500 mg single dose (adults; not used in children due to fluoroquinolone caution).
  • Close contacts: household, daycare, intimate contacts within 7 days before symptom onset.

SAQ 24. Encephalitis with negative initial workup — what next?

  • Repeat CSF studies (HSV PCR may be falsely negative in first 72 h).
  • Expanded autoimmune encephalitis panel (anti-NMDAR, LGI1, GABA-B, GAD65, MOG).
  • Paraneoplastic panel.
  • Whole-body imaging for occult tumor (teratoma, neuroblastoma).
  • Consider broader infectious workup (rickettsial, fungal, parasitic).
  • Empiric immunotherapy if autoimmune suspected (Graus 2016).

SAQ 25. Top 5 pitfalls in pediatric CNS infection.

  • Delaying antibiotics for LP — never delay for > 1 hour.
  • Not starting acyclovir empirically when HSV encephalitis suspected.
  • Missing meningococcal sepsis with petechiae — fatal delay.
  • Failing to obtain audiology at 4 weeks post-bacterial meningitis.
  • Anchoring on infection diagnosis and missing autoimmune encephalitis (anti-NMDAR).

7 · 20 PICU Cases

Case 1. 3-week-old neonate with fever, poor feeding, full fontanelle.

  • Empiric: ampicillin + cefotaxime + acyclovir.
  • LP after stable; HSV PCR essential.
  • Septic workup; admit PICU for close monitoring.
  • Maternal history for GBS, HSV, STI screening.

Case 2. 5-year-old with rapidly progressive purpuric rash, fever, hypotension.

  • Meningococcemia / meningococcal meningitis.
  • Ceftriaxone immediately; vasopressors; fluid resuscitation.
  • Activate sepsis bundle; ICU admission.
  • Contact tracing; chemoprophylaxis for household and close contacts.
  • Public health notification.

Case 3. 8-year-old with fever, headache, focal seizures involving the right face, MRI shows L temporal lobe T2/FLAIR hyperintensity.

  • HSV encephalitis.
  • Acyclovir 60 mg/kg/day IV ÷ q8h × 21 days.
  • Treat seizures (lev 40–60 mg/kg).
  • cEEG; ICU monitoring.
  • Repeat CSF HSV PCR before stopping.

Case 4. 12-year-old with sinusitis develops new headache, vomiting, neuro deficit. MRI: crescent-shaped subdural collection with rim enhancement and DWI restriction.

  • Subdural empyema from sinusitis.
  • Surgical drainage by neurosurgery + ENT for sinus drainage.
  • Vancomycin + ceftriaxone + metronidazole (S. milleri group + anaerobes).
  • Anti-epileptics; ICU monitoring.
  • 4–6 weeks IV antibiotics.

Case 5. 14-year-old with subacute psychiatric symptoms, dyskinesias, autonomic instability.

  • Anti-NMDAR encephalitis.
  • CSF anti-NMDAR Ab, lymphocytic pleocytosis.
  • Pelvic US for teratoma (adolescent female).
  • Methylprednisolone + IVIG; rituximab if refractory.
  • Long ICU course; supportive care for autonomic instability.

Case 6. 6-year-old VP shunt patient with fever, vomiting, irritability.

  • Shunt infection / malfunction.
  • Vancomycin + ceftazidime / cefepime / meropenem.
  • Neurosurgery for shunt tap; externalization typically required.
  • Treat 10–21 d; replace shunt when CSF sterile.

Case 7. 3-year-old with 2-week history of fever, vomiting, behavioral change, then cranial nerve palsies.

  • TB meningitis working diagnosis.
  • Lymphocytic CSF with high protein, low glucose.
  • GeneXpert MTB/RIF; AFB; TB culture.
  • MRI: basal enhancement, hydrocephalus, possible infarcts.
  • 4-drug ATT + adjunctive dexamethasone + pyridoxine.
  • EVD/VP shunt for hydrocephalus.

Case 8. 10-year-old immunocompromised (post-HSCT) with fever and headache. CSF cryptococcal antigen positive.

  • Cryptococcal meningitis.
  • Liposomal amphotericin B + flucytosine for induction (2 weeks).
  • Then fluconazole consolidation (8 weeks) + maintenance.
  • Manage raised ICP — therapeutic LPs to drop OP to < 50% baseline.
  • Treat underlying immunosuppression cautiously.

Case 9. 11-year-old with sudden encephalopathy and multifocal weakness 3 weeks after viral illness. MRI: multifocal subcortical WM + thalamic lesions.

  • ADEM.
  • Methylprednisolone 30 mg/kg/day × 5 d then taper.
  • IVIG if refractory or PLEX.
  • cEEG if seizures; supportive care.
  • Rehab consult; long-term neurology follow-up.

Case 10. 7-year-old with severe pneumococcal meningitis with right hemiparesis on day 3.

  • Vasculitis-related stroke (cerebritis evolving to infarct).
  • MRI/MRA to characterize.
  • Continue antibiotics; consider continuing dexamethasone.
  • Antiplatelet therapy controversial — discuss with stroke neurology.
  • cEEG; ICU monitoring.

Case 11. 2-month-old with fever and lethargy. LP shows WBC 50, lymphocyte predominance, normal glucose, protein 80.

  • Viral meningitis possible (enterovirus most common in this age).
  • CSF multiplex PCR (FilmArray ME).
  • Empiric antibiotics (vancomycin + cefotaxime) until cultures negative.
  • Add acyclovir if HSV suspected.
  • Discharge if cultures negative and clinical improvement.

Case 12. 9-year-old with cyanotic CHD (post-Fontan) presents with new headache and focal seizure.

  • Brain abscess from right-to-left shunt physiology.
  • MRI with contrast + DWI.
  • Empiric vancomycin + ceftriaxone + metronidazole.
  • Surgical drainage with neurosurgery.
  • 4–8 weeks IV antibiotics.
  • Endocarditis workup; cardiology involved.

Case 13. 15-year-old with severe pneumococcal meningitis day 1 — develops cerebral edema with bradycardia and hypertension.

  • Raised ICP from severe meningitis.
  • HOB 30°; hyperosmolar therapy (3% NaCl 3–5 mL/kg).
  • Sedation; consider ICP monitor.
  • Continue dexamethasone; antibiotics.
  • Treat seizures; cEEG.

Case 14. 5-year-old post-bacterial meningitis discharge planning.

  • Audiology assessment at 4 weeks post-discharge.
  • Neurodevelopmental assessment at 6 and 12 months.
  • Vaccine status review and catch-up.
  • Family education on long-term sequelae.
  • Outpatient infectious disease and neurology follow-up.

Case 15. 17-year-old with severe encephalitis, autoimmune panel pending. Continued seizures, severe agitation.

  • Treat empirically: methylprednisolone + IVIG.
  • Continue antiviral coverage (acyclovir).
  • cEEG, sedation.
  • Search for occult tumor (CT chest/abdomen/pelvis).
  • Family meeting; long ICU course anticipated.

Case 16. 8-year-old with West Nile virus encephalitis (positive serology).

  • Supportive care — no specific antiviral.
  • Treat seizures; cEEG.
  • Watch for flaccid paralysis (poliomyelitis-like syndrome).
  • Public health notification (notifiable).
  • Long-term rehab.

Case 17. 3-year-old with rabies exposure (bat bite). Discuss management.

  • Post-exposure prophylaxis: rabies immunoglobulin (20 IU/kg infiltrated around wound + IM) + rabies vaccine series (day 0, 3, 7, 14).
  • Wound care; tetanus prophylaxis.
  • If symptomatic rabies develops — almost universally fatal; Milwaukee protocol experimental.
  • Public health involvement.

Case 18. 5-year-old with meningococcemia, septic shock, DIC.

  • Aggressive resuscitation; vasopressors.
  • Ceftriaxone; isolate.
  • DIC management (cryoprecipitate, FFP, platelets).
  • Monitor for Waterhouse-Friderichsen syndrome (adrenal hemorrhage) — empiric hydrocortisone if refractory shock.
  • Skin necrosis may require limb amputation.
  • Family chemoprophylaxis; public health.

Case 19. 13-year-old with refractory anti-NMDAR encephalitis on first-line therapy. Ovarian teratoma identified.

  • Teratoma resection essential.
  • Second-line immunotherapy: rituximab 375 mg/m²/week × 4 + cyclophosphamide.
  • Continue methylprednisolone, IVIG, plasma exchange.
  • Long ICU course; supportive care.
  • Maintenance immunosuppression for 12–24 months.

Case 20. 2-year-old after dog bite, develops mood/behavior change 8 weeks later.

  • Possible rabies — review immunization status post-exposure.
  • Symptomatic rabies almost universally fatal.
  • Hospital infection control with airborne and contact precautions.
  • Palliative care if symptomatic; Milwaukee protocol case-by-case in specialized centers.
  • Family preparation; public health notification.

Recognition

  • Classic triad in < 50% of pediatric cases.
  • Petechiae/purpura → meningococcemia.
  • Subacute behavioral + dyskinesia → anti-NMDAR.
  • Temporal lobe + fever + seizures → HSV encephalitis.

Empiric Antibiotics (within 1 hour)

  • < 1 mo: ampicillin + cefotaxime ± aminoglycoside.
  • > 1 mo: vancomycin + ceftriaxone.
  • VP shunt: vancomycin + ceftazidime/cefepime/meropenem.
  • Suspect HSV: add acyclovir 60 mg/kg/day.
  • Dexamethasone 0.15 mg/kg q6h (Hib strongest evidence).

CSF Patterns

  • Bacterial: PMN ↑, ↑protein, ↓glucose, Gram + 50–75%.
  • Viral: lymph predominance, normal glc/protein.
  • TB: lymph, very ↑protein, ↓glucose, GeneXpert.
  • Anti-NMDAR: lymph, normal glc, Ab in CSF.

Imaging

  • HSV: bilateral asymmetric temporal/frontal T2/FLAIR + DWI ↑.
  • ADEM: multifocal subcortical WM + BG + thalami.
  • Abscess: ring enhancement + central DWI ↑.
  • TB: basal cisterns enhancement, infarcts, hydrocephalus.
  • Subdural empyema: crescentic rim-enhancing collection.

Red Flags

  • Time-to-antibiotic delay → 30% increase poor outcome per hour.
  • Hearing loss 10–30% post-pneumococcal — audiology at 4 weeks.
  • Anti-NMDAR — search teratoma in adolescent ♀.
  • Subdural empyema — sinusitis source; surgical emergency.
  • TB meningitis — adjunctive dexamethasone reduces mortality.

Exam Pearls

  • Empiric Abx + acyclovir < 1 h.
  • Dexamethasone with or before first antibiotic.
  • Brain abscess: surgical drainage + 4–8 weeks IV antibiotics.
  • ADEM: methylprednisolone 30 mg/kg/day × 5 d.
  • Anti-NMDAR: methylpred + IVIG; rituximab second-line.
  • Audiology 4 weeks post-meningitis.
