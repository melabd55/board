Mock RCPSC Block A — 10 SAQs (timed)

⏱ 30 min total · 3 min per SAQ
SAQ A-1
Distinguish SIADH from cerebral salt wasting (CSW).
Show Answer
  • SIADH — euvolemic / hypervolemic; UOP normal or low; urine Na >40; treat with fluid restriction.
  • CSW — hypovolemic; UOP elevated (polyuria); urine Na >100; BUN ↑, Hct ↑; treat with sodium AND volume replacement; fludrocortisone for refractory.
  • Volume status is the key discriminator.
  • Restricting fluid in CSW is dangerous (cerebral hypoperfusion).
SAQ A-2
Triple-phase response after pituitary / craniopharyngioma surgery.
Show Answer
  • Phase 1 (0–24 h) — central DI from posterior pituitary stalk injury.
  • Phase 2 (days 2–7) — release of stored ADH → transient SIADH.
  • Phase 3 (after day 7) — permanent DI as stored ADH depleted.
  • Adjust DDAVP and fluids at each transition.
  • Anterior pituitary hormones also need replacement evaluation.
SAQ A-3
Diagnose diabetes insipidus postoperatively.
Show Answer
  • Polyuria (UOP >4 mL/kg/h sustained).
  • Urine specific gravity <1.005 (dilute).
  • Urine osmolality <300 mOsm/kg.
  • Serum sodium and osmolality rising.
  • DDAVP 0.05–0.4 mcg IV/SC/IN per dose; titrate.
  • Match urine output losses with hypotonic fluid (D5W).
SAQ A-4
Posterior fossa syndrome (cerebellar mutism).
Show Answer
  • Mutism / reduced speech + cerebellar / brainstem signs after midline posterior fossa surgery.
  • Incidence 8–31 %; onset 1–4 days postoperatively.
  • Features — mutism, hypotonia, ataxia, behavioral changes, dysphagia.
  • Mostly transient (weeks–months); residual deficits in some.
  • Supportive care, speech therapy.
SAQ A-5
VP shunt malfunction recognition and workup.
Show Answer
  • Symptoms — headache, vomiting, irritability, altered mental status, papilledema, Cushing's triad.
  • Emergent CT — assess ventricle size.
  • Shunt series X-ray — disconnection / fracture.
  • Shunt tap for ICP and CSF.
  • Neurosurgery consult.
SAQ A-6
Moyamoya perioperative care.
Show Answer
  • Avoid hypotension, hyperventilation, dehydration, hyperthermia, sympathetic activation.
  • Liberal IV fluids.
  • Aspirin postoperatively.
  • Treat pain aggressively.
  • Monitor for ischemic stroke — frequent neuro exams.
  • Revascularization — direct (STA-MCA) or indirect (EDAS, pial synangiosis).
SAQ A-7
Venous air embolism management.
Show Answer
  • Sudden ↓ ETCO₂, hypotension, arrhythmia, mill-wheel murmur.
  • Risk — sitting posterior fossa surgery.
  • Flood surgical field with saline.
  • Trendelenburg + left lateral decubitus.
  • Aspirate air from central venous catheter.
  • FiO₂ 100 %; support hemodynamics.
SAQ A-8
Tiered ICP management cascade.
Show Answer
  • Baseline — HOB 30°, midline head, sedation, normothermia, normocapnia, normoglycemia.
  • Tier 1 — CSF drainage (EVD); hypertonic saline; mannitol; deepen sedation; treat seizures.
  • Tier 2 — NMBA; brief hyperventilation; mild hypothermia 35 °C; pentobarbital coma; decompressive craniectomy.
  • Throughout — re-image to rule out evolving lesion.
SAQ A-9
Craniosynostosis types and surgery options.
Show Answer
  • Sagittal — scaphocephaly (most common).
  • Coronal — anterior plagiocephaly.
  • Metopic — trigonocephaly.
  • Lambdoid — posterior plagiocephaly (rare).
  • Procedures — strip craniectomy + helmet (<6 mo), open vault remodel, endoscopic, spring-assisted.
  • Major concerns — blood loss, air embolism, hypothermia.
SAQ A-10
Sodium correction safety limits.
Show Answer
  • ≤8–10 mEq/L per 24 h.
  • Faster correction risks osmotic demyelination (central pontine myelinolysis).
  • 3 % HTS for symptomatic hyponatremia — 3–5 mL/kg over 10–15 min.
  • If over-corrected, can re-lower with D5W ± DDAVP.
  • Long-term ODS — quadriparesis, dysphagia, locked-in syndrome.

Mock RCPSC Block B — 5 Sequential ICU Cases

CASE B-1
12-year-old POD 1 after craniopharyngioma resection; UOP 8 mL/kg/h, Na 152, urine SG 1.002.
Q. Management.
Show Answer
  • Central DI — Phase 1 of triple-phase response.
  • DDAVP 0.05–0.1 mcg IV.
  • Match UOP losses with D5W.
  • Monitor Na q2–4 h.
  • Anticipate Phase 2 (SIADH) on days 2–7 — reduce or hold DDAVP as urine output drops.
CASE B-2
14-year-old POD 3 after midline posterior fossa tumor resection; Na 124, UOP 4 mL/kg/h, urine Na 130, BUN 22, Hct 41 %.
Q. Diagnosis and management.
Show Answer
  • Cerebral salt wasting — hypovolemic hyponatremia.
  • Replace sodium AND volume — isotonic or 3 % saline.
  • Fludrocortisone for refractory.
  • Do NOT fluid restrict — worsens cerebral hypoperfusion.
CASE B-3
8-year-old POD 2 after medulloblastoma resection; previously alert, now mute, hypotonic, ataxic.
Q. Diagnosis and management.
Show Answer
  • Posterior fossa syndrome (cerebellar mutism).
  • Onset 1–4 days postop, mostly transient.
  • Supportive — speech therapy, PT/OT.
  • Family counseling.
  • Consider fluoxetine / bromocriptine / dextroamphetamine if prolonged.
CASE B-4
10-year-old VP shunt patient with new headache, vomiting, diplopia.
Q. Workup.
Show Answer
  • Emergent CT — assess ventricle size.
  • Shunt series X-ray — disconnection / fracture.
  • Shunt tap for ICP and CSF.
  • Neurosurgery consult.
  • Manage raised ICP (HOB, hyperosmolar).
CASE B-5
9-year-old in sitting position for pineal tumor resection; sudden ↓ ETCO₂ from 35 to 18, SBP drops, arrhythmia.
Q. Diagnosis and management.
Show Answer
  • Venous air embolism.
  • Flood surgical field with saline.
  • Trendelenburg + left lateral decubitus.
  • Aspirate air from central venous catheter.

  • Support hemodynamics.

Block C — Remaining SAQs (randomized)

Q C-1
Structured handoff bundle from OR to PICU.
Show Answer
  • Procedure, intraoperative findings, extent of resection.
  • Anesthesia agents and reversal status.
  • Hemodynamics — blood loss, transfusions, vasoactives.
  • Fluid balance, lines, drains, dressings.
  • Anticipated complications.
  • Postoperative goals (sedation, CPP, ventilation, EVD).
  • Pain plan.
  • Family communication and follow-up imaging.
Q C-2
Common shunt complications.
Show Answer
  • Infection (5–15 %).
  • Obstruction — proximal / distal / valve.
  • Disconnection / fracture.
  • Overdrainage — slit ventricle, subdural hematoma.
  • Migration, abdominal pseudocyst.
  • Catheter-related thrombosis (VA shunt).
Q C-3
Anesthetic considerations in neurosurgery.
Show Answer
  • Volatile anesthetics — vasodilation; autoregulation impaired >1 MAC.
  • Propofol — cerebral vasoconstrictor; reduces CMR.
  • Opioids — short-acting (remifentanil) preferred.
  • Avoid succinylcholine in chronic immobilization / denervation.
  • Avoid prolonged anesthesia <3 y (FDA warning).
  • Avoid nitrous oxide in posterior fossa.
Q C-4
Routine postoperative care after neurosurgery.
Show Answer
  • HOB 30°; midline head.
  • Normothermia, normoglycemia, normocapnia.
  • Avoid hypotension and hyperoxia.
  • Seizure prophylaxis case-by-case.
  • VTE prophylaxis — mechanical first.
  • Antibiotic prophylaxis 24–48 h.
  • Pain management.
Q C-5
Differential for delayed awakening after neurosurgery.
Show Answer
  • Residual anesthetic / sedative / opioid effect.
  • Residual NMBA.
  • Hypothermia.
  • Metabolic — glucose, sodium, ammonia.
  • Postop hemorrhage / edema / pneumocephalus / hydrocephalus.
  • Cerebral ischemia.
  • Seizure / NCSE.
  • Posterior fossa syndrome.
  • Pre-existing encephalopathy.
Q C-6
Indications for ETV / CPC.
Show Answer
  • Obstructive hydrocephalus (aqueductal stenosis, tumor).
  • ETV creates CSF outflow via floor of third ventricle.
  • CPC reduces CSF production.
  • Reduces lifelong shunt dependence.
  • Selected in infants based on imaging and prognostic score.
Q C-7
Hemispherectomy postoperative concerns.
Show Answer
  • Fluid balance challenges.
  • Infection risk; may need EVD.
  • Hemiparesis, hemianopia — expected.
  • Continuous EEG.
  • AED continuation (don't taper).
Q C-8
Intraoperative neurophysiologic monitoring in spinal surgery.
Show Answer
  • SSEP — posterior columns.
  • MEP — corticospinal tract.
  • EMG — nerve root monitoring.
  • Wake-up test largely replaced.
  • Changes trigger immediate intervention.
Q C-9
Pediatric aneurysm postoperative care.
Show Answer
  • Monitor for vasospasm — TCD daily.
  • Nimodipine prophylaxis (adult extrapolation).
  • Maintain euvolemia and adequate CPP.
  • Watch for rebleeding (first 24 h) and hydrocephalus.
  • Continuous EEG.
Q C-10
Why is hyponatremia common after neurosurgery?
Show Answer
  • SIADH from surgical stress, opioids, pain.
  • CSW from BNP release.
  • Hypotonic IV fluids contribute.
  • Pituitary surgery — Phase 2 SIADH.
  • Recognition + correct diagnosis crucial.
Q C-11
Symptomatic hyponatremia treatment.
Show Answer
  • 3 % hypertonic saline 3–5 mL/kg over 10–15 min.
  • Repeat to raise Na 3–5 mEq/L per dose.
  • Target Na rise ≤8–10 mEq/L per 24 h.
  • Treat seizures with benzodiazepines.
  • Identify cause.
Q C-12
Hyperosmolar therapy comparison (HTS vs. mannitol).
Show Answer
  • HTS (3 % continuous or 23.4 % bolus) — first-line in pediatric TBI.
  • Less rebound; maintains intravascular volume.
  • Mannitol — osmotic diuresis; may cause hypovolemia.
  • Both need intact BBB; monitor osmolality and renal function.
Q C-13
Emergence considerations after neurosurgery.
Show Answer
  • Avoid coughing / bucking — hypertension and bleeding risk.
  • Lidocaine, remifentanil titration, dexmedetomidine.
  • Allow rapid neuro exam.
  • Adequate analgesia.
Q C-14
Indications for intentional delayed extubation.
Show Answer
  • Anticipated airway swelling.
  • Severe hemodynamic instability.
  • Intentional therapeutic coma.
  • Prolonged complex surgery.
  • Continuous EEG and ICP monitoring during sedation.
Q C-15
Posterior fossa surgery — anesthetic concerns.
Show Answer
  • Position — prone or sitting.
  • Venous air embolism risk (sitting).
  • Avoid nitrous oxide.
  • Brainstem manipulation — bradycardia, hypotension.
  • Posterior fossa syndrome risk.
Q C-16
Postoperative seizure prophylaxis after neurosurgery.
Show Answer
  • Case-by-case — supratentorial tumor, cortical AVM, epilepsy surgery, severe TBI.
  • Levetiracetam first-line.
  • Phenytoin/fosphenytoin if urgent.
  • Duration usually 7 days unless seizures.
  • Continuous EEG for high-risk.
Q C-17
CSF leak postoperative management.
Show Answer
  • Recognize — clear / pink drainage; rhinorrhea / otorrhea.
  • β2-transferrin confirms.
  • Bed rest, head elevation, lumbar drain.
  • Surgical repair if persistent.
  • Watch for meningitis.
Q C-18
Endocrine evaluation after pituitary / craniopharyngioma surgery.
Show Answer
  • Anterior — cortisol, thyroid, GH, gonadotropins, prolactin.
  • Posterior — ADH function.
  • Stress-dose hydrocortisone perioperatively.
  • Replace deficient hormones.
  • Endocrinology consult.
Q C-19
Postoperative imaging timing.
Show Answer
  • Immediate CT — hemorrhage, hydrocephalus, mass effect.
  • MRI within 48 h — residual tumor (before postsurgical enhancement at 72 h).
  • Follow-up MRI per oncology protocol.
  • Stat imaging for clinical deterioration.
Q C-20
Why avoid nitrous oxide in posterior fossa surgery?
Show Answer
  • N₂O is 30× more soluble than nitrogen.
  • Diffuses into air-filled spaces and expands them.
  • After dural closure, residual air causes tension pneumocephalus.
  • Substitute with air-oxygen or volatile anesthetic.
Q C-21
Pediatric epilepsy surgery options.
Show Answer
  • Temporal lobectomy — mesial temporal sclerosis.
  • Lesionectomy — FCD, tumor.
  • Hemispherectomy — Rasmussen, hemimegalencephaly.
  • Corpus callosotomy — drop attacks.
  • VNS, RNS, DBS — palliative.
Q C-22
Tranexamic acid in pediatric craniosynostosis.
Show Answer
  • Antifibrinolytic agent.
  • Reduces blood loss and transfusion requirement.
  • 10–15 mg/kg load + 1 mg/kg/h infusion.
  • Routine in many programs.
  • Adjuncts — cell saver, preop anemia management.
Q C-23
EVD care principles.
Show Answer
  • Sterile insertion with chlorhexidine bundle, antibiotic-impregnated catheter.
  • Zero at level of tragus.
  • Drain to specified level above tragus.
  • Daily CSF sampling for monitoring.
  • Infection risk increases after day 5.
Q C-24
AVM treatment options.
Show Answer
  • Endovascular embolization.
  • Microsurgical resection.
  • Stereotactic radiosurgery.
  • Staged combination often used.
  • Postoperative — strict BP control, EEG, vasospasm watch.
Q C-25
Postoperative pain management after neurosurgery.
Show Answer
  • Acetaminophen scheduled.
  • Avoid NSAIDs in immediate postop (bleeding).
  • Opioids — short-acting, titrated.
  • Local anesthetic / scalp blocks.
  • Dexmedetomidine adjunct.
Q C-26
Why is hyperthermia harmful postoperatively?
Show Answer
  • Increases CMR, glutamate, oxidative stress.
  • Disrupts BBB.
  • Each 1 °C worsens secondary injury.
  • Target 36.0–37.5 °C; treat aggressively.
Q C-27
VTE prophylaxis after pediatric neurosurgery.
Show Answer
  • Mechanical first — SCDs.
  • Pharmacologic — LMWH when bleeding risk acceptable (24–48 h post-op).
  • Risk-stratify adolescent, immobile, paretic, cancer.
  • DVT screening in high-risk.
  • Mobilization as safe.
Q C-28
Future directions in pediatric neurosurgical critical care.
Show Answer
  • Multimodal monitoring integration (CPPopt, PbtO₂, NIRS, cEEG).
  • Robotic / minimally invasive.
  • Real-time intraop imaging.
  • Endoscopic / awake / less-invasive approaches.
  • Standardized handoff bundles.
  • ML for complication prediction.
Q C-29
Intraoperative hemodynamic management priorities.
Show Answer
  • Maintain age-appropriate MAP and CPP.
  • Avoid hypotension during induction.
  • Vasopressors — phenylephrine, norepinephrine first-line.
  • Large IV access for posterior fossa, scoliosis, craniosynostosis.
  • Hyperoxia avoided; titrate to PaO₂ 80–100 mmHg.
Q C-30
Surgical positioning concerns.
Show Answer
  • Prone — posterior fossa, spine; eye protection, pressure points.
  • Sitting — posterior fossa in older; VAE risk.
  • Supine — most supratentorial.
  • Mayfield pins <2 y — caution given thin skull; use horseshoe.
