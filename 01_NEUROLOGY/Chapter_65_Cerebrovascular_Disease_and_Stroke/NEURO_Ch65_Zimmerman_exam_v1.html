ZIMMERMAN Ch 66 — Pediatric Stroke & ICH — EXAM MODE

SOURCE: ZIMMERMAN   Companion to: ZIMMERMAN_66 STUDY MODE

Block A — Mock RCPSC SAQ (10 questions, 12 min)

A1. VIPS study key findings on cerebral arteriopathy.

Show answer
  • Vascular Effects of Infection in Pediatric Stroke — multicenter prospective cohort.
  • Arteriopathy detected in 46% of all pediatric AIS patients.
  • 55% of previously healthy children (no other risk factor) had arteriopathy.
  • Recent minor infection (URI within 1 week) tripled stroke risk.
  • Reinforces that vascular imaging is essential in every pediatric AIS workup.

A2. List six categories of cerebral arteriopathy in pediatric AIS.

Show answer
  • Focal cerebral arteriopathy (FCA) — ~25%; VZV-associated common.
  • Cervicocephalic arterial dissection (CCAD) — trauma or spontaneous.
  • Moyamoya disease/syndrome — bilateral distal ICA stenosis.
  • Primary CNS angiitis (cPACNS) — large or small vessel.
  • Sickle cell vasculopathy.
  • Post-infectious vasculopathy (bacterial meningitis, TB, VZV).
  • Secondary vasculitis (SLE, Takayasu, PAN, Susac).
  • Post-radiation, genetic (NF1, ACTA2, COL4A1, FMD).

A3. Explain the penumbra concept.

Show answer
  • Core: CBF below critical threshold → cell death; "no-flow" region; not salvageable.
  • Penumbra: surrounding area with reduced CBF but viable tissue; salvageable with prompt reperfusion.
  • Oligemia: mildly reduced CBF; functional but vulnerable to further insult.
  • Goal of tPA/thrombectomy: rescue penumbra before it becomes core.
  • Modifiers: ischemia duration, CPP, collateral circulation, fever.

A4. A 10-year-old with HbSS presents with acute right hemiparesis. Outline immediate management.

Show answer
  • Emergent exchange transfusion to HbS < 30%.
  • Simple transfusion to Hb 10 g/dL if exchange unavailable (avoid > 10 — hyperviscosity).
  • Sickle-negative, antigen-matched (E, C, Kell), leuko-reduced PRBC.
  • Maintain SpO₂ > 93% through transfusion and first night.
  • Avoid IV tPA (no pediatric SCD data, increased bleeding risk).
  • NO cooling blankets — precipitate sickling.
  • Cardiology consult if cardiac dysfunction.
  • Plan chronic transfusion + HSCT discussion for secondary prevention.

A5. IV tPA considerations in pediatric AIS.

Show answer
  • No high-quality pediatric RCT data (TIPS feasibility closed early).
  • AHA 2019: "hyperacute therapies for AIS remain controversial."
  • Use with caution age > 2; avoid < 2 years and neonates; avoid in SCD.
  • Case-by-case in older children and adolescents with confirmed large-vessel AIS and PedNIHSS ≥ 4.
  • Within 4.5 hours of last seen well.
  • Dose 0.9 mg/kg (max 90 mg); 10% bolus, remainder over 60 min.
  • Multidisciplinary review essential before administration.
  • Risk of symptomatic ICH appears low in selected patients.

A6. 11-year-old with malignant left MCA infarct, midline shift 7 mm, GCS dropping. Manage.

Show answer
  • Surgical emergency — emergent decompressive hemicraniectomy.
  • Per Ojemann's perspective: do NOT delay surgery for ICP monitor placement.
  • Wide craniectomy ≥ 12 cm with duraplasty.
  • Intubate; HOB 30°; hyperosmolar therapy as bridge.
  • Multidisciplinary discussion with family.
  • Cranioplasty at 6–12 weeks post-craniectomy.
  • Pediatric outcomes generally better than adults due to neuroplasticity.
  • Aggressive rehabilitation.

A7. Discuss antithrombotic choice by AIS etiology.

Show answer
  • Cardioembolic (mechanical valve, mural thrombus): anticoagulation.
  • Extracranial dissection: anticoagulation 3–6 months (Chest); AHA considers controversial.
  • Intracranial dissection: antiplatelet preferred (rupture risk with anticoagulation).
  • Antiphospholipid syndrome with recurrent events: anticoagulation.
  • FCA / post-varicella: ASA ≥ 12 months.
  • Moyamoya: ASA until surgical revascularization.
  • CSVT (with or without hemorrhagic infarct): anticoagulation 3–6 months.
  • SCD: exchange transfusion, NOT antithrombotic.

A8. CSVT — common predisposing factors in pediatrics.

Show answer
  • Local: mastoiditis, sinusitis, otitis, meningitis, trauma, dehydration.
  • Systemic hypercoagulable state.
  • Nephrotic syndrome (loss of antithrombin III).
  • IBD, malignancy.
  • Estrogen-containing OCPs in adolescents.
  • Polycythemia.
  • Neonatal: birth-related, dehydration, perinatal asphyxia.
  • Recent intracranial surgery.

A9. Spetzler-Martin AVM grading and its application.

Show answer
  • Size: 1 pt < 3 cm; 2 pts 3–6 cm; 3 pts > 6 cm.
  • Eloquence of brain: 0 non-eloquent; 1 eloquent.
  • Deep venous drainage: 0 no; 1 yes.
  • Total grade 1–5; higher = higher operative risk.
  • Grade 1–3: surgical resection often feasible.
  • Grade 4–5: combination embolization + radiosurgery + selective surgery.
  • ARUBA trial (adult): unruptured AVM medical management vs intervention controversial; pediatric data limited.

A10. List 5 acute care principles for pediatric AIS in the PICU.

Show answer
  • PICU admission ≥ 48 h for monitoring.
  • Hourly pediatric GCS + pupils by RN; serial PedNIHSS by medical staff.
  • Permissive hypertension — do not lower BP routinely.
  • Aggressive normothermia — fever increases infarct size.
  • cEEG if seizures suspected; no prophylactic ASM.
  • Urgent imaging for any neurologic deterioration.
  • Maintain MAP, normocapnia, normoglycemia.
  • NO cooling blankets in SCD.

Block B — Oral Cases (5 sequential, 35 min)

B1. 9-year-old previously healthy with sudden onset right hemiparesis 2 hours ago. Recent varicella 6 weeks earlier.

B1a — Initial workup.
  • Activate stroke pathway; PedNIHSS scoring.
  • Rapid MRI (DWI/FLAIR/GRE/MRA head + neck).
  • Labs per Box 66.2 (CBC, coags, ESR, glucose, type & screen).
  • Echo with bubble study.
  • Hypercoagulability panel.
  • Investigate VZV exposure history.
B1b — MRI shows L basal ganglia infarct + M1 narrowing.
  • FCA — likely post-varicella vasculopathy.
  • ASA 3–5 mg/kg/day for ≥ 12 months.
  • Acyclovir if active VZV (check serology, CSF VZV PCR if suspicious).
  • Repeat MRA at 3–6 months — TCA may show improvement; progressive form requires extended ASA.
  • Steroids generally NOT used in FCA.
B1c — Develops focal seizure on day 2.
  • Treat acute seizure: lorazepam 0.1 mg/kg IV.
  • Load levetiracetam 40–60 mg/kg.
  • cEEG monitoring.
  • Repeat imaging if persistent neurologic decline.
  • No prophylactic ASM for stroke-related seizure prevention.
B1d — Discharge plan and follow-up.
  • Rehab: PT, OT, SLP.
  • Continue ASA ≥ 12 months.
  • Repeat MRA at 3–6 months.
  • Annual stroke clinic follow-up.
  • School integration plan; neuropsychology at 6–12 months.
  • Family education on recurrence warning signs.

B2. 11-year-old HbSS with new aphasia and right hemiparesis.

B2a — Immediate management.
  • Activate stroke pathway.
  • Emergent exchange transfusion to HbS < 30%.
  • Sickle-negative, antigen-matched, leuko-reduced PRBC.
  • Avoid IV tPA — increased bleeding risk in SCD.
  • NO cooling blankets — precipitate sickling.
  • SpO₂ > 93% through transfusion and first night.
  • Cardiology consult if cardiac dysfunction.
B2b — Imaging confirms M2 MCA infarct.
  • Continue exchange transfusion.
  • cEEG; supportive PICU care.
  • Investigate stroke triggers: BP, fever, acute chest syndrome, recent surgery, transfusion, priapism.
  • Optimize Hb, hydration.
B2c — Secondary prevention plan.
  • Chronic transfusion regimen indefinitely — target HbS < 30%.
  • SWiTCH 2012 — hydroxyurea alone inferior to transfusion for secondary prevention.
  • Monitor iron overload; chelation (deferasirox, deferoxamine).
  • HSCT discussion — curative option.
B2d — Family asks about prevention for sibling with HbSS.
  • Annual TCD screening ages 2–16 in HbSS / HbSβ⁰.
  • If TCD velocity ≥ 200 cm/s → chronic transfusion (STOP, NEJM 1998).
  • After stabilization with normal TCD: can transition to hydroxyurea (TWiTCH, Lancet 2016).
  • Silent infarct on MRI → chronic transfusion (SIT, NEJM 2014).
  • Adherence to hydroxyurea, hydration, infection prevention.

B3. 14-year-old after football tackle develops sudden ataxia, vomiting, and vertigo.

B3a — Differential and imaging.
  • Posterior circulation stroke — vertebral artery dissection most likely.
  • Other: cerebellar hemorrhage, basilar occlusion, cerebellitis.
  • MRI brain + MRA neck + T1 fat-sat (crescent sign of intramural hematoma).
  • Cervical CT to rule out fracture.
  • Connective tissue disease workup (Ehlers-Danlos vascular type, Marfan).
B3b — Confirmed extracranial vertebral dissection.
  • Anticoagulation 3–6 months (Chest guidelines).
  • UFH initially; transition to LMWH (1 mg/kg q12h) or warfarin.
  • Hard collar precautions; activity restriction.
  • Imaging follow-up at 3–6 months for healing.
  • Multidisciplinary stroke team management.
B3c — Develops worsening posterior fossa edema on day 2.
  • Cerebellar stroke with mass effect.
  • Watch for 4th ventricle compression → hydrocephalus.
  • Neurosurgery consult.
  • Suboccipital craniectomy + dural expansion if mass effect with brainstem compression.
  • EVD if hydrocephalus develops.
B3d — Long-term plan.
  • Anticoagulation 3–6 months; repeat MRA at end.
  • Activity restriction during anticoagulation.
  • Connective tissue genetic counseling if positive workup.
  • Sports clearance after full healing and stroke team review.
  • Family education on dissection recurrence risk.

B4. 9-year-old with sudden severe headache, vomiting, then unresponsive. CT shows large lobar ICH.

B4a — Initial management.
  • ABCs; intubate if GCS ≤ 8.
  • BP control: target SBP < 160; avoid hypotension.
  • Reverse any coagulopathy (vitamin K, PCC, FFP, platelets).
  • HOB 30°; hyperosmolar if signs of herniation.
  • Type and crossmatch.
  • Urgent CTA for vascular cause (AVM, aneurysm).
  • Neurosurgery consult.
B4b — CTA shows AVM Spetzler-Martin grade 3.
  • Multidisciplinary AVM management plan.
  • Acute surgical evacuation of hematoma if mass effect.
  • AVM definitive treatment: combination of surgery, embolization, stereotactic radiosurgery based on grade and location.
  • Cerebral angiography for full vascular delineation.
  • Long-term follow-up.
B4c — Day 3 develops IVH and hydrocephalus.
  • EVD placement by neurosurgery.
  • Manage hydrocephalus; antibiotics for EVD.
  • Daily neurologic exams; consider intrathecal tPA in some protocols for IVH clearance (controversial in pediatrics).
  • Watch for vasospasm if associated SAH.
B4d — Family asks about long-term outcome.
  • Depends on hematoma location, size, neurologic status at presentation.
  • Pediatric ICH outcomes generally better than adults.
  • Aggressive rehab; PT/OT/SLP.
  • Neuropsychology assessment.
  • Long-term AVM management plan with annual angiography until obliteration confirmed.
  • Family education on rebleeding warning signs.

B5. 7-year-old with new aphasia and right facial droop. Time last seen well 3 hours ago. CTA shows left M1 occlusion.

B5a — Discuss treatment options.
  • Multidisciplinary stroke team discussion.
  • IV tPA — case-by-case < 4.5 h; 0.9 mg/kg.
  • Mechanical thrombectomy — increasing pediatric experience; LVO with disabling deficit at experienced center.
  • Both can be considered; bridging tPA + thrombectomy used in adults.
  • Family informed of off-label/limited pediatric data.
B5b — Decision for thrombectomy at tertiary pediatric center.
  • Transfer urgently if needed.
  • Maintain MAP, permissive HTN.
  • Cerebral perfusion imaging if available (penumbra/core mismatch).
  • Pediatric interventional neuroradiologist.
  • Post-procedure: ASA 24 h after intervention.
B5c — Post-thrombectomy ICH on follow-up CT.
  • Hold ASA temporarily.
  • BP control SBP < 160.
  • Repeat imaging in 24 h.
  • Resume ASA once stable per neurology/neurosurgery agreement.
  • Monitor closely in PICU.
B5d — Etiology workup.
  • Echo with bubble study for cardioembolic source.
  • Vessel imaging for arteriopathy.
  • Hypercoagulability panel.
  • Investigate recent infection (VIPS).
  • Long-term secondary prevention based on etiology.
  • Rehab; school integration plan.

Block C — Randomized SAQ Drill (20)

C1. VIPS arteriopathy rate.

Show
  • 46% of all AIS; 55% of previously healthy children.

C2. FCA classic post-infectious trigger.

Show
  • Varicella zoster virus (VZV) — post-varicella vasculopathy.

C3. SCD acute AIS therapy.

Show
  • Exchange transfusion to HbS < 30%; avoid tPA; avoid cooling blanket.

C4. tPA pediatric AIS — when reasonable.

Show
  • Case-by-case > 2 years; < 4.5 h LSW; confirmed LVO; PedNIHSS ≥ 4; multidisciplinary review.

C5. Thrombectomy pediatric AIS.

Show
  • LVO with disabling deficit; experienced pediatric center; basilar artery occlusion may have extended window.

C6. SCD primary prevention TCD threshold.

Show
  • ≥ 200 cm/s → chronic transfusion (STOP 1998).

C7. Decompressive hemicraniectomy indication.

Show
  • Malignant MCA infarct with mass effect; do not delay for ICP monitor; within 48 h.

C8. Cerebellar infarct surgical approach.

Show
  • Suboccipital craniectomy + dural expansion; watch for 4th ventricle compression → EVD.

C9. CSVT treatment with hemorrhagic infarct.

Show
  • Anticoagulation still indicated (deVeber NEJM 2001); 3–6 months.

C10. Aspirin dose AIS.

Show
  • 3–5 mg/kg/day PO; doses below Reye syndrome risk.

C11. LMWH dose pediatric.

Show
  • Enoxaparin 1 mg/kg q12h SC (1.5 in < 2 mo); anti-Xa 0.5–1.0 IU/mL.

C12. Extracranial dissection treatment.

Show
  • Anticoagulation 3–6 months (Chest); follow-up imaging.

C13. Intracranial dissection treatment.

Show
  • Antiplatelet preferred (rupture risk with anticoagulation).

C14. Spetzler-Martin components.

Show
  • Size + Eloquence + Deep venous drainage = 1–5 grade.

C15. Cavernous malformation MRI.

Show
  • "Popcorn" T2/SWI/GRE with surrounding hemosiderin; familial form has CCM1/2/3 mutations.

C16. Pediatric aneurysm features.

Show
  • Rare; more posterior circulation; more giant; mycotic (endocarditis), traumatic, familial, CTD.

C17. HIT recognition.

Show
  • Thrombocytopenia + thrombosis on heparin; 4T score; switch to argatroban/bivalirudin; avoid warfarin acutely.

C18. CSVT predisposing factors.

Show
  • Local infection, dehydration, hypercoagulable, nephrotic, IBD, malignancy, OCP, polycythemia.

C19. Mycotic aneurysm management.

Show
  • Antibiotics for underlying endocarditis; endovascular treatment when accessible; anticoagulation generally AVOIDED.

C20. Pediatric ICH most common cause.

Show
  • Arteriovenous malformation (AVM).

Block D — Randomized PICU Case Drill (15)

D1. 7-yo previously healthy + sudden hemiparesis + MRA distal ICA narrowing.

Show
  • FCA; ASA ≥ 12 mo; repeat MRA 3–6 mo; acyclovir if active VZV.

D2. 10-yo HbSS + aphasia.

Show
  • Exchange transfusion HbS < 30%; avoid tPA + cooling; chronic transfusion + HSCT discussion.

D3. 14-yo post-tackle + ataxia.

Show
  • Vertebral dissection; MRA neck + T1 fat-sat; anticoagulation 3–6 mo if extracranial.

D4. 11-yo post-Fontan + seizure + hemiparesis.

Show
  • Cardioembolic AIS; anticoagulation per cardiology; echo bubble; treat seizures.

D5. 8-yo M1 occlusion at 5 h, NIHSS 16.

Show
  • LVO; thrombectomy at experienced center; tPA window closed.

D6. 12-yo malignant L MCA infarct + 7 mm shift.

Show
  • Emergent decompressive hemicraniectomy; don't wait for ICP monitor.

D7. 15-yo SAH + ant-comm aneurysm.

Show
  • Neurosurgery; BP < 160; nimodipine; coiling vs clipping; EVD if hydrocephalus.

D8. 6-yo cerebellar infarct + 4th vent compression.

Show
  • Surgical emergency — posterior fossa decompression; EVD; ASA per etiology.

D9. 10-yo nephrotic + headache + papilledema.

Show
  • CSVT; MRV; anticoagulation 3–6 mo even with hemorrhagic infarct; treat nephrotic syndrome.

D10. 9-yo ICH + AVM grade 3.

Show
  • Stabilize; BP < 160; reverse coag; surgical evacuation if mass effect; multidisciplinary AVM plan.

D11. 13-yo endocarditis + mycotic aneurysm.

Show
  • Antibiotics; endovascular treatment; AVOID anticoagulation; cardiology + neurosurgery + ID.

D12. 15-yo OCP + thunderclap HA.

Show
  • CT + CTA/MRA; MRV for CSVT; stop OCP; if CSVT anticoagulate; if RCVS nimodipine.

D13. 14-yo APS + second AIS.

Show
  • Switch from ASA to anticoagulation; confirm Ab; hematology + rheumatology.

D14. 3-yo multifocal AIS + ↑inflammatory + lymph CSF.

Show
  • cPACNS; high-dose steroids + IVIG; biopsy if small-vessel; cyclo/rituximab; long-term rheum f/u.

D15. 7-yo multiple cavernomas + new seizure.

Show
  • Familial cavernous malformation; genetic counseling; annual MRI; surgical resection for symptomatic; ASM.

Top 5 traps

  • Failing to obtain vascular imaging (missing arteriopathy).
  • Lowering BP in acute AIS (worsens penumbra).
