Pages 2924–2925 · Chapter opening + Key Points

CHAPTER 56 ■ Developmental Neurobiology, Neurophysiology, and the PICU — Key Points

Key points distilled
  • Biochemical, molecular, physiologic, structural differences distinguish newborn, infant, child, adolescent, and adult brain — these differences shape therapy of brain injury at each age.
  • Programmed cell death (PCD) in the developing brain is uniquely sensitive to imbalances in trophic and neurotransmitter input — anesthetics and sedatives may exacerbate developmental neuroapoptosis.
  • Synaptogenesis is highly active until puberty.
  • Myelination outlasts most other CNS developmental processes — continues through adolescence.
  • GABA switches from excitatory to inhibitory around the time of birth; before the switch, the developing brain has enhanced excitotoxicity risk.
  • Pressure autoregulation operates similarly across ages, but the developing brain is at greater ischemic risk because age-appropriate MAP is lower.
  • CBF and CMR increase from birth and plateau between 3 and 9 years of age — mirroring synapse formation.
  • Blood-brain barrier integrity — tight junctions + low transcytosis — restricts hydrophilic substances and osmotic agents.
RCPSC relevance 🔥🔥
  • Theory chapter — examiners use as a "physiology layer" question on neuroprotection and age-specific brain vulnerability.
Pages 2926–2928 · INTRODUCTION + Brain Development Timeline

INTRODUCTION

Setting
  • Pediatric neurointensive care must tailor therapy for TBI, hypoxic-ischemic injury, stroke, refractory status epilepticus, and CNS infections across the developmental spectrum.
  • Structural, biochemical, physiologic, and behavioral differences require age-specific approaches.
  • For many topics extrapolation from newborn or adult data is necessary — pure pediatric evidence is incomplete.

Brain Development Timeline

Embryogenesis and beyond
  • Neurulation — formation of neural tube from neuroectoderm at gestational weeks 3–4.
  • Neurogenesis — peak in the first half of gestation; mostly complete by term except in dentate gyrus and subventricular zone.
  • Neuronal migration — peaks gestational weeks 12–24.
  • Synaptogenesis — accelerates after birth and continues actively until puberty.
  • Myelination — begins late gestation, continues through adolescence and into early adulthood.
  • Brain volume — increases dramatically from gestation, with region-specific growth windows.
ICU layer
  • Insults at different developmental windows produce different deficit patterns — e.g., periventricular leukomalacia in preterm infants reflects vulnerability of pre-oligodendrocytes.
  • Imaging interpretation must account for incomplete myelination.
Pages 2929–2931 · Programmed Cell Death

Programmed Cell Death (PCD) in Development

Pathways
  • During brain development, many more neurons are generated than survive — surplus is pruned by PCD (apoptosis).
  • Intrinsic (mitochondrial) pathway — Bax/Bak release cytochrome-c → caspase-9 → caspase-3.
  • Extrinsic (death receptor) pathway — Fas/TNF → caspase-8 → caspase-3.
  • Pro-death factors — Bax, Bak, BAD, caspases 3, 8, 9.
  • Pro-survival factors — Bcl-2, Bcl-xL, neurotrophic factors (BDNF, NGF).
  • Balance shifts in developing brain — loss of trophic or NT input tilts toward apoptosis.
ICU implication 🔥🔥🔥
  • Developmental neuroapoptosis is triggered by NMDA antagonists (ketamine), GABA agonists (volatile anesthetics, propofol, benzodiazepines, barbiturates), and ethanol in animal models.
  • Prolonged or repeated anesthesia in infants and toddlers may have developmental consequences — drives FDA warnings on cumulative anesthesia exposure >3 hours in <3 year-olds.
  • Minimize sedation duration; reach for non-pharmacologic strategies.
Exam trap
  • Many sedatives that are "safe" in adults trigger developmental neuroapoptosis in the immature brain.
Pages 2932–2934 · Neuronal differentiation + Glia

Stem Cell Commitment + Neuronal Differentiation

  • Neuroepithelial cells generate radial glia → neural progenitors → mature neurons and glia.
  • Sequence — neurogenesis → gliogenesis (astrocytes, oligodendrocytes).
  • Transcription factors (Pax6, Ngn2, Neurod1) and signaling cascades (Notch, Wnt, BMP) direct fate.
  • Endogenous stem cells persist in adulthood in subventricular zone and dentate gyrus — limited contribution to repair.

Astrocytes

  • Most numerous CNS cell type.
  • Functions — synaptic support, NT recycling (glutamate uptake via EAAT1/2), ion homeostasis (K⁺ buffering), water transport (AQP4), BBB maintenance, scar formation.
  • Reactive astrogliosis after injury — protective and inhibitory contributions.

Microglia

  • Resident immune cells of CNS, derived from yolk-sac primitive macrophages.
  • Resting (ramified) state in normal brain.
  • Activated (amoeboid) after injury — phagocytosis, cytokine release.
  • M1 (pro-inflammatory) vs. M2 (resolution / repair) polarization.
Pages 2935–2940 · Synaptogenesis + Neurotransmitters

Synaptogenesis

  • Synapse density peaks 1–2 years postnatally in different cortical regions, then is pruned.
  • Activity-dependent — visual cortex shaped by visual input, language areas by language exposure.
  • Critical periods — windows of heightened plasticity for specific functions.

Amino Acid Neurotransmitters

Glutamate (excitatory)
  • Most abundant excitatory NT in CNS.
  • ~50 % of forebrain synapses are glutamatergic.
  • Receptors — ionotropic (AMPA, NMDA, kainate) and metabotropic.
  • NMDA receptor is voltage-dependent (Mg²⁺ block) and Ca²⁺-permeable — drives excitotoxicity via Ca²⁺ overload.
  • Developing brain has higher NMDA receptor density and altered subunit composition (NR2B-rich early) — increased excitotoxic vulnerability.
GABA (developmental switch)
  • Major inhibitory NT in mature CNS.
  • Early in development — high intracellular Cl⁻ via NKCC1 → GABA depolarizing (excitatory).
  • After birth, KCC2 expression increases — Cl⁻ extruded — GABA becomes hyperpolarizing (inhibitory).
  • Delayed GABA switch in disease (e.g., temporal lobe epilepsy, hypoxic-ischemic injury).
  • Has trophic role beyond signaling.
Pearl 🔥🔥🔥
  • The GABA excitatory-to-inhibitory switch around birth is a high-yield exam concept.
  • Pre-switch — benzodiazepines / phenobarbital may behave atypically (sometimes paradoxical effects in neonates).
  • NKCC1 antagonists (bumetanide) under investigation for neonatal seizures.
Pages 2941–2944 · Other neurotransmitters

Adenosine

  • Neuronal, glial, and cerebrovascular transmitter.
  • Inhibitory via A1 receptors; vasodilator via A2 receptors.
  • Accumulates during hypoxia and inhibits glutamate release — endogenous neuroprotection.
  • Methylxanthines (caffeine) antagonize adenosine — relevant to apnea of prematurity treatment.

Acetylcholine

  • Major excitatory transmitter system.
  • Cortical innervation from basal forebrain (nucleus of Meynert) appears prenatally; matures postnatally.
  • Receptors — nicotinic (ionotropic) and muscarinic (G-protein coupled).
  • Roles in attention, learning, memory; cholinergic deficit in Alzheimer disease.

Monoaminergic Systems

  • Dopamine, norepinephrine, epinephrine, serotonin, histamine.
  • Form during telencephalic vesicle formation; project widely.
  • Dopamine — striatal (motor), mesocortical (executive), tuberoinfundibular (prolactin inhibition).
  • Norepinephrine — from locus coeruleus; arousal, attention, stress response.
  • Serotonin — from raphe nuclei; mood, sleep, appetite.
ICU implication 🔥🔥
  • Catecholamines (epinephrine, norepinephrine, dopamine) used for hemodynamic support in PICU cross the BBB poorly but may have CNS effects in neonates with immature BBB.
  • α2-agonists (dexmedetomidine, clonidine) reduce sympathetic outflow at locus coeruleus — increasingly preferred sedative in pediatric NICU/PICU.
Pages 2945–2948 · Growth factors and neurotrophins

Growth Factors

  • BDNF (brain-derived neurotrophic factor) — most studied; supports neuronal survival, synaptic plasticity, LTP.
  • NGF (nerve growth factor) — cholinergic neuron survival.
  • NT-3, NT-4/5 — various subpopulations.
  • GDNF (glial-derived neurotrophic factor) — dopaminergic neurons.
  • IGF-1 — important in oligodendrocyte development and myelination.
  • VEGF — vascular and neuronal effects.
ICU implication
  • Loss of trophic support drives developmental apoptosis after injury.
  • BDNF gene polymorphisms may modify recovery from pediatric TBI.
  • Erythropoietin (EPO) studied as neuroprotectant in neonatal HIE and TBI.
Pages 2949–2952 · CNS-immune interactions and vascular development

CNS and Immune System Interactions

  • Microglia and astrocytes mediate CNS innate immunity.
  • Cytokines (IL-1β, TNF-α, IL-6) modulate both injury and repair.
  • Adaptive immune system — meningeal lymphatics drain CNS antigens to deep cervical lymph nodes.
  • BBB largely keeps lymphocytes out under normal conditions; selective trafficking in disease.

Vascular Development

  • Vasculogenesis (de novo) — angioblasts form primitive vascular plexus.
  • Angiogenesis — new vessels from existing ones.
  • CNS vasculature follows neural development; pericyte recruitment and astrocyte end-foot apposition form mature BBB.
  • Germinal matrix in preterm brain — vulnerable to hemorrhage owing to thin-walled, immature vessels.
Pages 2953–2957 · Cerebrovascular Reactivity + Pressure Autoregulation

Cerebrovascular Reactivity

Three major reactivity systems
  • Pressure autoregulation — maintains constant CBF across MAP range via myogenic, neurogenic, metabolic mechanisms.
  • CO₂ reactivity — hypercapnia → vasodilation, ↑ CBF; hypocapnia → vasoconstriction, ↓ CBF.
  • O₂ reactivity — hypoxia → vasodilation, ↑ CBF (less potent than CO₂).

Pressure Autoregulation

  • Maintains CBF constant between a lower (LLA) and upper (ULA) limit of cerebral perfusion pressure (CPP).
  • Adult LLA ~50–60 mmHg; ULA ~150 mmHg.
  • Pediatric autoregulation operates over a similar relative range but absolute thresholds are lower owing to lower baseline MAP.
  • Disrupted by — TBI, hypoxic-ischemic injury, sepsis, prematurity, anesthetics, vasopressors.
  • Pressure-passive CBF after injury → infarction at low CPP, hemorrhage at high CPP.
ICU implication 🔥🔥🔥
  • Maintain age-appropriate MAP for the patient — "normal" adult MAP is too low for a neonate's tissue and may be too high for sustaining autoregulation in injury.
  • Continuous CPP monitoring during TBI; consider autoregulation-guided thresholds (PRx — pressure reactivity index).
  • Avoid sustained hypocapnia — vasoconstriction risks secondary ischemia.
Exam trap
  • Hyperventilation as a bridge for acute ICP crisis is acceptable, but routine prophylactic hyperventilation is harmful.
Pages 2958–2962 · CBF and Metabolism

CBF and Brain Metabolism

  • Adult — tight coupling of CBF to brain metabolism (glucose, oxygen consumption).
  • Pediatric — CBF and cerebral metabolic rate increase from birth, peak between 3 and 9 years (often higher than adult values), then decline to adult levels.
  • Mirrors synaptogenesis and pruning timeline.
  • Coupling preserved in normal pediatric brain; uncoupling after TBI or seizures.

Substrate Utilization

  • Glucose — primary CNS fuel via glycolysis and oxidative decarboxylation.
  • LCMRg (local cerebral metabolic rate for glucose) increases with developmental maturation.
  • Monocarboxylate transporters (MCT1, MCT4) for lactate / pyruvate develop over the perinatal period — neonate can use lactate as alternative substrate during stress.
  • Ketone bodies (β-hydroxybutyrate, acetoacetate) — alternative substrate during fasting, ketogenic diet (refractory epilepsy treatment).
Pearl 🔥🔥
  • Neonatal brain can use lactate as fuel — explains relative tolerance of mild hyperlactatemia in healthy neonates.
  • Ketogenic diet works partly through alternative substrate provision plus altered NT balance.
Pages 2963–2968 · BBB + Conclusion

Blood-Brain Barrier (BBB)

  • Anatomic basis — endothelial tight junctions (claudin-5, occludin, ZO-1), low rate of transcytosis, paucity of fenestrations.
  • Supporting cells — pericytes, astrocyte end-feet, basement membrane.
  • Transport — passive diffusion of small lipophilic molecules; transporters for glucose (GLUT1), amino acids, monocarboxylates; efflux pumps (P-glycoprotein).
  • Restricts hydrophilic agents, osmotic agents, large proteins, most antibiotics.
  • Disrupted by — injury, inflammation, hyperosmolarity, severe hypertension.
ICU implication 🔥🔥🔥
  • Antibiotic CNS penetration depends on BBB integrity, lipophilicity, molecular size.
  • Mannitol's effect requires intact BBB — established osmotic gradient draws water out of brain parenchyma.
  • BBB disruption after TBI / encephalitis — vasogenic edema, contrast enhancement on MRI.

CONCLUSION

  • Fundamental developmental differences in neurobiology and neurophysiology shape disease, recovery, and therapy across the pediatric age spectrum.
  • Pediatric PICU clinicians must integrate developmental neuroscience with bedside care.
  • Areas of active investigation — developmental neuroapoptosis from anesthetics, BBB-targeted drug delivery, neuroprotection in HIE / TBI.

  • Newborn — CBF ~40 mL/100 g/min; rises through infancy.
  • Children 3–9 y — peak CBF ~70–100 mL/100 g/min; CMR similarly high.
  • Adult — CBF ~50 mL/100 g/min.

Equations & thresholds

Cerebral Perfusion Pressure. CPP = MAP − ICP. Maintain age-appropriate CPP — infant ≥40 mmHg; toddler ≥50 mmHg; older child ≥60 mmHg; adolescent ≥70 mmHg.
Cerebral Blood Flow (Fick). CBF = (MAP − ICP) ÷ cerebrovascular resistance. CMRO₂ = CBF × (CaO₂ − CvO₂).
Pressure Reactivity Index (PRx). Moving Pearson correlation of MAP and ICP. PRx <0 = preserved autoregulation; PRx >0.3 = impaired.
CO₂ reactivity. ~3 % change in CBF per 1 mmHg PaCO₂. Hypocapnia <25 mmHg can drop CBF by 30–50 %.

3. Global Evidence Layer (2020–2025) — Full Citations

① FDA Drug Safety Communication — Anesthesia and Brain Development

  • Authoring body: US Food and Drug Administration
  • Source: FDA Drug Safety Communication · December 2016 (revised 2017, ongoing surveillance); supporting research — SmartTots Consortium, GAS trial (McCann ME et al. Lancet. 2019;393:664–677), PANDA trial (Sun LS et al. JAMA. 2016;315:2312–2320), MASK study (Warner DO et al. Anesthesiology. 2018;129:89–105)
  • Links: FDA: FDA anesthesia/sedation warning · GAS DOI: 10.1016/S0140-6736(18)32485-1 · PANDA DOI: 10.1001/jama.2016.6967
  • Key findings:
    • Single short anesthesia exposure (<3 hours) in children <3 years not clearly associated with measurable neurocognitive deficit (GAS, PANDA, MASK).
    • Cumulative or repeated exposure may carry risk — FDA warning persists for procedures lasting >3 hours or repeated exposures in <3 y.
    • Animal studies show developmental neuroapoptosis from NMDA antagonists and GABA agonists.
  • ICU impact: Minimize sedation duration; reach for non-pharmacologic strategies; consider dexmedetomidine which has less neuroapoptotic signal.

② Adolescent Brain Cognitive Development Study (ABCD)

  • Authoring body: NIH-funded multi-site consortium
  • Source: Volkow ND et al. Dev Cogn Neurosci. 2018;32:4–7; ongoing longitudinal data 2018–2030
  • Link: ABCD: abcdstudy.org · Foundational DOI: 10.1016/j.dcn.2017.10.002
  • Key findings:
    • Largest longitudinal pediatric neuroimaging cohort tracking 11 000+ children from age 9–10 through adolescence.
    • Maps normal developmental trajectories of brain structure, function, and connectivity.
    • Quantifies effects of substance use, screen time, stress, sleep, and physical activity on the developing brain.
  • ICU impact: Provides normative reference for assessing post-injury developmental trajectories.

③ Cerebrovascular Autoregulation Monitoring — Pediatric Consensus

  • Authoring body: Approaches and Considerations of Multimodal Neuromonitoring — Pediatric Neurocritical Care Research Group
  • Source: Appavu B, Foreman B, Hu X et al. Neurocrit Care. 2022;37(Suppl 2):S105–S128; Lewis PM et al. Pediatr Crit Care Med. 2020;21:e164–e173
  • Link: DOI: 10.1007/s12028-022-01586-0 · Neurocritical Care Society: neurocriticalcare.org
  • Key findings:
    • PRx-guided CPP titration feasible in pediatric TBI.
    • "Optimal CPP" (CPPopt) — individualized CPP corresponding to lowest PRx — better outcomes when actual CPP near CPPopt.
    • Bedside continuous monitoring via standard ICP/MAP signals.
  • ICU impact: Replacing "one size fits all" CPP targets with individualized autoregulation-guided thresholds.

④ Pediatric Brain Injury Neuroprotection — Erythropoietin Trials

  • Key trials:
    • HEAL (High-dose Erythropoietin for Asphyxia and encephaLopathy) — Wu YW et al. N Engl J Med. 2022;387:148–159 — neonatal HIE with hypothermia
    • PENUT (Preterm Erythropoietin Neuroprotection Trial) — Juul SE et al. N Engl J Med. 2020;382:233–243
  • Link: HEAL DOI: 10.1056/NEJMoa2119660 · PENUT DOI: 10.1056/NEJMoa1907423
  • Key findings:
    • HEAL — adding erythropoietin to hypothermia did NOT improve death or neurodevelopmental impairment at 22–36 months in neonatal HIE.
    • PENUT — high-dose EPO did NOT reduce death or neurodevelopmental impairment in extremely preterm infants.
    • Negative trials despite promising animal data — translation challenge.
  • ICU impact: Reinforces hypothermia as standard for neonatal HIE; tempers enthusiasm for adjunctive EPO.

⑤ Bumetanide for Neonatal Seizures (NEMO Trial)

  • Authoring body: NEMO consortium (European)
  • Source: Pressler RM et al. Lancet Neurol. 2015;14:469–477; subsequent studies through 2023
  • Link: DOI: 10.1016/S1474-4422(14)70303-5 · PMID 25765333
  • Key findings:
    • Bumetanide (NKCC1 antagonist) — rationale based on developmental GABA switch.
    • Trial stopped early for safety (ototoxicity); modest seizure reduction.
    • Pre-clinical promise has not translated to clear pediatric benefit.
  • ICU impact: Phenobarbital remains first-line for neonatal seizures; bumetanide not in routine practice.

⑥ Dexmedetomidine vs. Other Sedatives in Pediatric ICU

  • Source: Hayden JC et al. Pediatr Crit Care Med. 2020;21:e655–e662; Plambech MZ et al. Acta Anaesthesiol Scand. 2022
  • Link: Representative DOI: 10.1097/PCC.0000000000002358
  • Key findings:
    • Dexmedetomidine — α2-agonist with no GABAergic action; minimal developmental neuroapoptosis signal.
    • Provides sedation without respiratory depression.
    • Useful for ventilator weaning, NIV tolerance, post-extubation.
    • Side effects — bradycardia, hypotension, withdrawal after prolonged use.
  • ICU impact: Increasingly preferred when prolonged sedation needed in young children.

⑦ Pediatric Brain Tumor / Developing Brain Radiation Effects

  • Source: Children's Oncology Group + St. Jude Lifetime Cohort · ongoing publications; representative — Mulhern RK et al. Lancet Oncol. 2004;5:399–408 (foundational); recent updates 2020–2024
  • Link: COG: childrensoncologygroup.org · St. Jude Lifetime: St. Jude Lifetime Cohort
  • Key findings:
    • Cranial radiation in young children (especially <3 y) causes substantial cognitive impairment.
    • Proton therapy reduces volume of normal brain irradiated and may preserve cognition.
    • Drives delay or omission of cranial RT in young children (e.g., infants with medulloblastoma).
  • ICU impact: Frames the developmental cost-benefit conversation for CNS oncology decisions.

5. ICU Decision Engine

IF Infant or toddler needs prolonged sedation
THEN
  • Minimize cumulative anesthesia/sedation duration.
  • Prefer dexmedetomidine over benzodiazepines / propofol / ketamine when feasible.
  • Daily sedation interruption + sedation scoring.
  • Reach for non-pharmacologic strategies — environmental, parental presence.
IF Pediatric TBI with altered consciousness
THEN
  • Maintain age-appropriate CPP — infant ≥40, toddler ≥50, older child ≥60.
  • Consider PRx-based individualized CPP target (CPPopt).
  • Avoid sustained hypocapnia (target PaCO₂ 35–40 mmHg).
  • Avoid hypotension and hyperthermia.
IF Acute ICP crisis (impending herniation)
THEN
  • Brief hyperventilation acceptable as bridge.
  • Hyperosmolar therapy (mannitol or hypertonic saline).
  • Sedation + analgesia + neuromuscular blockade if needed.
  • Surgical consultation for decompression.
IF Neonatal HIE
THEN
  • Therapeutic hypothermia (33.5 °C × 72 h) within 6 h of birth.
  • Adjunctive EPO not supported by HEAL trial.
  • Continuous aEEG / EEG monitoring.
  • Avoid hyperthermia, hyperoxia, hypoglycemia, hypocapnia.
IF Neonatal seizures
THEN
  • Phenobarbital first-line.
  • Levetiracetam emerging as alternative.
  • Treat reversible causes (hypoglycemia, hypocalcemia, hyponatremia).
  • EEG monitoring to detect electrographic-only seizures.

6. SAQ Bank — 40 Questions

SAQ 1. Describe the GABA developmental switch and its ICU implication.
Answer.
  • Early in development NKCC1 dominates; intracellular Cl⁻ is high; GABA opening Cl⁻ channels depolarizes neurons (excitatory).
  • Around birth, KCC2 expression rises; Cl⁻ is extruded; GABA becomes hyperpolarizing (inhibitory).
  • Implication — typical GABAergic anticonvulsants (phenobarbital, benzodiazepines) may be less effective or paradoxical in neonatal seizures.
  • NKCC1 antagonist (bumetanide) was studied (NEMO trial) but limited by ototoxicity.
SAQ 2. Outline age-appropriate CPP targets.
Answer.
  • Infant ≥40 mmHg.
  • Toddler ≥50 mmHg.
  • Older child ≥60 mmHg.
  • Adolescent ≥70 mmHg.
  • Individualized via PRx-derived CPPopt is increasingly used.
SAQ 3. Why does CBF peak in childhood?
Answer.
  • CBF and cerebral metabolic rate increase from birth.
  • Peak between age 3 and 9 years.
  • Mirrors synaptogenesis and high metabolic demand of an actively pruning brain.
  • Declines to adult levels by adolescence.
SAQ 4. Describe two ICU-relevant features of pediatric BBB.
Answer.
  • Tight junctions plus low transcytosis restrict hydrophilic agents and osmotic agents.
  • Mannitol works by establishing osmotic gradient across intact BBB.
  • BBB disruption by injury / inflammation produces vasogenic edema and contrast enhancement.
  • Antibiotic CNS penetration depends on lipophilicity, molecular size, BBB integrity — relevant to bacterial meningitis treatment.
SAQ 5. Describe developmental neuroapoptosis from anesthetics.
Answer.
  • NMDA antagonists (ketamine) and GABA agonists (volatile anesthetics, propofol, benzodiazepines, barbiturates) trigger developmental neuroapoptosis in animal models.
  • Mechanism — loss of trophic / NT input tips developing neurons toward apoptosis.
  • FDA warning — repeated or prolonged anesthesia >3 h in children <3 y.
  • Single short exposures appear safe (GAS, PANDA, MASK trials).
  • Dexmedetomidine has less neuroapoptotic signal.
SAQ 6. Outline the pressure autoregulation curve.
Answer.
  • CBF plateau between lower (LLA) and upper (ULA) limits of CPP.
  • Below LLA — CBF falls passively → ischemia.
  • Above ULA — CBF rises passively → hyperperfusion / hemorrhage.
  • Curve shifts right in chronic hypertension.
  • Curve widens or is lost after acute injury — pressure-passive CBF.
SAQ 7. Define PRx and its clinical use.
Answer.
  • Pressure Reactivity Index — moving Pearson correlation of MAP and ICP.
  • PRx <0 — preserved autoregulation.
  • PRx >0.3 — impaired autoregulation.
  • Used to derive CPPopt — the CPP at which PRx is lowest (best autoregulation).
  • Maintaining CPP near CPPopt may improve outcomes in pediatric TBI.
SAQ 8. Why is hypocapnia dangerous in pediatric TBI?
Answer.
  • CO₂ reactivity — ~3 % drop in CBF per 1 mmHg drop in PaCO₂.
  • Hypocapnia <25 mmHg can drop CBF 30–50 %.
  • In an already-injured brain with disrupted autoregulation, this can precipitate secondary ischemia.
  • Brief hyperventilation for impending herniation is acceptable; routine prophylactic hyperventilation is harmful.
SAQ 9. Describe programmed cell death pathways relevant to pediatric brain injury.
Answer.
  • Intrinsic (mitochondrial) — Bax/Bak release cytochrome-c → caspase-9 → caspase-3.
  • Extrinsic (death receptor) — Fas/TNF → caspase-8 → caspase-3.
  • Pro-death — Bax, Bak, BAD, caspases.
  • Pro-survival — Bcl-2, Bcl-xL, neurotrophins (BDNF, NGF).
  • Developing brain has heightened apoptotic machinery — vulnerable to NT/trophic loss.
SAQ 10. Why is the neonatal brain more excitotoxicity-sensitive?
Answer.
  • Higher NMDA receptor density.
  • NR2B-rich subunit composition early in development (longer Ca²⁺ flux).
  • Reduced inhibitory tone before GABA switch.
  • Higher metabolic demand of developing synapses.
  • Drives vulnerability to hypoxic-ischemic injury.
SAQ 11. What is the role of microglia in CNS injury?
Answer.
  • Resident immune cells; derived from yolk-sac primitive macrophages.
  • Resting (ramified) state in normal brain.
  • Activated (amoeboid) after injury — phagocytosis, cytokine release.
  • M1 (pro-inflammatory) vs. M2 (resolution / repair) polarization.
  • Both protective and harmful contributions; target of investigational neuroprotection.
SAQ 12. Outline astrocyte functions.
Answer.
  • Synaptic support and NT recycling (EAAT1/2 for glutamate).
  • K⁺ buffering.
  • Water transport via AQP4.
  • BBB maintenance via end-foot apposition.
  • Glial scar formation after injury.
  • Energy substrate delivery (astrocyte-neuron lactate shuttle).
SAQ 13. Why is the developing brain a "glucose-and-lactate" user?
Answer.
  • Glucose is primary fuel; LCMRg increases with maturation.
  • Monocarboxylate transporters (MCT1, MCT4) develop perinatally allowing lactate uptake.
  • Neonate can use lactate as alternative substrate during stress.
  • Explains relative tolerance of mild hyperlactatemia in healthy neonates.
  • Ketogenic diet works partly through alternative substrate (ketone bodies).
SAQ 14. What is therapeutic hypothermia for neonatal HIE?
Answer.
  • Core temperature 33.5 °C for 72 hours.
  • Initiated within 6 hours of birth.
  • For moderate or severe HIE (modified Sarnat staging).
  • Reduces death + neurodevelopmental impairment.
  • Mechanism — reduces metabolic rate, glutamate release, apoptotic signaling.
  • Adjunctive EPO did not add benefit (HEAL trial).
SAQ 15. When is bumetanide used in neonatal seizures?
Answer.
  • Investigational based on GABA developmental switch.
  • NKCC1 antagonist — lowers intracellular Cl⁻ → restores GABA inhibition.
  • NEMO trial stopped early for ototoxicity; modest seizure reduction.
  • Not in routine clinical practice.
  • Phenobarbital remains first-line.
SAQ 16. Why is dexmedetomidine increasingly preferred?
Answer.
  • α2-agonist with no GABAergic action.
  • Minimal developmental neuroapoptosis signal.
  • Sedation without respiratory depression.
  • Useful for ventilator weaning, NIV tolerance, post-extubation.
  • Side effects — bradycardia, hypotension, withdrawal after prolonged use.
SAQ 17. Describe synaptogenesis pattern across regions.
Answer.
  • Active until puberty.
  • Region-specific timing — primary sensory areas mature first; prefrontal cortex last.
  • Synapse density peaks 1–2 years postnatally in visual cortex; later in prefrontal cortex.
  • Pruning follows formation — "use it or lose it" activity-dependent refinement.
  • Critical periods — windows of heightened plasticity for specific functions.
SAQ 18. What is the role of adenosine in brain physiology?
Answer.
  • Neuronal, glial, and cerebrovascular transmitter.
  • A1 receptors — inhibitory.
  • A2 receptors — vasodilator.
  • Accumulates during hypoxia and inhibits glutamate release — endogenous neuroprotection.
  • Methylxanthines (caffeine) antagonize adenosine — used for apnea of prematurity.
SAQ 19. List five neurotrophic factors and their roles.
Answer.
  • BDNF — neuronal survival, synaptic plasticity, LTP.
  • NGF — cholinergic neuron survival.
  • NT-3 / NT-4-5 — subpopulations of neurons.
  • GDNF — dopaminergic neurons.
  • IGF-1 — oligodendrocyte development and myelination.
  • VEGF — vascular and neuronal effects.
SAQ 20. Why are catecholamines used for hemodynamic support in pediatric brain injury?
Answer.
  • Maintain age-appropriate MAP and CPP.
  • Cross BBB poorly but may have CNS effects in neonates with immature BBB.
  • Norepinephrine common in TBI to maintain CPP.
  • Avoid excessive vasoconstriction that may reduce regional CBF in injured brain.
SAQ 21. Describe germinal matrix hemorrhage vulnerability.
Answer.
  • Preterm brain has a germinal matrix near caudate / thalamus / lateral ventricle wall.
  • Thin-walled, immature vessels supply this region.
  • Fluctuating CBF + pressure-passive circulation → vulnerable to hemorrhage.
  • Grades 1–4 of IVH (Papile classification).
  • Hemorrhage involutes by 32–34 weeks corrected age.
SAQ 22. Outline CO₂ reactivity in the developing brain.
Answer.
  • ~3 % change in CBF per 1 mmHg PaCO₂.
  • Hypercapnia → cerebral vasodilation → ↑ CBF and ICP.
  • Hypocapnia → cerebral vasoconstriction → ↓ CBF, potential ischemia.
  • Functional from very early in development.
  • Target PaCO₂ 35–40 mmHg in TBI; avoid prophylactic hyperventilation.
SAQ 23. Describe BDNF role in pediatric brain.
Answer.
  • Most-studied neurotrophin.
  • Supports neuronal survival, synaptic plasticity, LTP.
  • Critical in critical periods.
  • BDNF gene polymorphisms (Val66Met) may modify recovery from pediatric TBI.
  • Exercise increases BDNF — basis for rehabilitation benefit.
SAQ 24. Why is the BBB important for antibiotic selection in meningitis?
Answer.
  • BBB restricts hydrophilic antibiotics under normal conditions.
  • Inflammation increases BBB permeability — some antibiotics achieve CNS levels only in inflamed meninges.
  • Lipophilic drugs (e.g., chloramphenicol, fluoroquinolones) penetrate well.
  • β-lactams require higher doses; meropenem and ceftriaxone reach therapeutic CNS levels in meningitis.
  • Vancomycin requires high doses; monitor CSF levels.
SAQ 25. Explain how mannitol works.
Answer.
  • Osmotic agent that does not cross intact BBB.
  • Establishes osmotic gradient — water moves from brain parenchyma into vasculature.
  • Reduces brain water content and ICP.
  • Dose 0.25–1 g/kg IV bolus.
  • Requires intact BBB; in disrupted BBB, mannitol can leak into brain and worsen edema.
  • Monitor serum osmolality and renal function.
SAQ 26. When is hyperventilation acceptable in pediatric TBI?
Answer.
  • Acute herniation crisis — brief hyperventilation as bridge to definitive intervention.
  • Avoid prophylactic / sustained hyperventilation.
  • Target PaCO₂ 35–40 mmHg normally.
  • If hyperventilating, monitor cerebral oxygenation (PbtO₂, SjvO₂) for ischemia.
SAQ 27. Describe the role of EAAT1/2 in synaptic glutamate.
Answer.
  • Excitatory amino acid transporters on astrocytes.
  • Remove glutamate from the synaptic cleft after release.
  • Prevents excitotoxicity by limiting NMDA receptor activation.
  • Loss of function (genetic or injury-induced) increases seizure and excitotoxic risk.
  • Astrocyte dysfunction after injury impairs glutamate clearance.
SAQ 28. Outline brain growth factors relevant to recovery.
Answer.
  • BDNF, NGF, NT-3, NT-4/5 — neuronal survival.
  • GDNF — dopaminergic neurons.
  • IGF-1 — myelination.
  • VEGF — neurovascular.
  • Erythropoietin — investigational neuroprotectant (HEAL, PENUT negative).
SAQ 29. Why must antibiotic doses be higher for CNS infection?
Answer.
  • BBB limits drug entry even when inflamed.
  • Higher serum concentrations are needed for adequate CSF levels.
  • Some drugs require continuous infusion or higher peak dosing.
  • CSF assays guide therapy in difficult cases (e.g., vancomycin in MRSA meningitis).
SAQ 30. Describe ABCD study contribution.
Answer.
  • Largest longitudinal pediatric neuroimaging cohort.
  • 11 000+ children tracked from age 9–10 onwards.
  • Maps normal developmental trajectories of brain structure, function, connectivity.
  • Quantifies effects of substance use, screen time, stress, sleep, physical activity.
  • Provides normative reference for post-injury developmental trajectories.
SAQ 31. What is the difference between the M1 and M2 microglial phenotype?
Answer.
  • M1 — pro-inflammatory; TNF, IL-1β, IL-6 release; phagocytosis.
  • M2 — resolution / repair; IL-10, TGF-β; tissue remodelling.
  • Balance shifts in time after injury — early M1, later M2.
  • Persistent M1 phenotype may contribute to secondary injury.
  • Therapeutic targets — promote M2 polarization for neuroprotection.
SAQ 32. Describe the role of AQP4 in pediatric brain.
Answer.
  • Aquaporin-4 — water channel on astrocyte end-feet at BBB.
  • Mediates water flux between blood, CSF, and parenchyma.
  • Contributes to cytotoxic edema (water moves into cells).
  • Target of anti-AQP4 antibody in NMOSD (neuromyelitis optica spectrum disorder).
  • Genetic loss-of-function causes severe brain edema in animal models.
SAQ 33. What is the role of GLUT1 in pediatric brain?
Answer.
  • Glucose transporter at BBB.
  • Mediates glucose entry into brain.
  • GLUT1 deficiency syndrome — autosomal dominant SLC2A1 mutation.
  • Presents with infantile-onset seizures, developmental delay, complex movement disorder.
  • Low CSF glucose (<2.2 mmol/L) with normal serum glucose.
  • Treated with ketogenic diet.
SAQ 34. Why does prolonged anesthesia worry the developing brain?
Answer.
  • GABA agonists and NMDA antagonists trigger developmental apoptosis in animal models.
  • FDA warning — cumulative anesthesia >3 h or repeated exposure in <3 y.
  • Single short exposures generally safe (GAS, PANDA, MASK).
  • Minimize duration; consider non-pharmacologic strategies and dexmedetomidine.
SAQ 35. What is the ketogenic diet and its mechanism?
Answer.
  • High-fat, very-low-carbohydrate diet producing ketosis.
  • Used for refractory pediatric epilepsy (especially infantile spasms, Dravet, Doose).
  • Mechanisms — alternative substrate (ketone bodies), altered GABA/glutamate balance, mitochondrial effects.
  • ~50 % of children have >50 % seizure reduction.
  • Side effects — acidosis, hyperlipidemia, growth restriction, renal stones.
SAQ 36. Outline pediatric cerebral oximetry use.
Answer.
  • Near-infrared spectroscopy (NIRS) measures regional cerebral oxygen saturation (rSO₂).
  • Reflects venous-weighted oxygen saturation in 1–2 cm of frontal cortex.
  • Use cases — cardiac surgery, ECMO, TBI, cerebral palsy with bypass.
  • rSO₂ <40 % or >25 % drop from baseline triggers intervention.
  • Limited by regional sampling and patient-specific baseline variability.
SAQ 37. What is intracranial compliance in pediatrics?
Answer.
  • Compliance = change in volume / change in pressure.
  • Initial ICP rise with added volume small — compensation by CSF / venous displacement.
  • Once compensation exhausted, small volume changes produce large ICP rise.
  • Infants — open fontanelles and sutures provide extra compliance, but mask true intracranial pressure changes.
  • Monro-Kellie doctrine — fixed cranial volume = brain + blood + CSF in adults; partially relaxed in infants.
SAQ 38. Describe the basis of cerebral perfusion-pressure-directed therapy.
Answer.
  • CPP = MAP − ICP.
  • Maintain age-appropriate CPP to ensure adequate cerebral perfusion.
  • Lower CPP risks ischemia; higher CPP risks hyperperfusion / hemorrhage.
  • Modern approach — individualized CPPopt via PRx monitoring.
  • Adjuncts — sedation, analgesia, hyperosmolar therapy, head-of-bed elevation, normothermia.
SAQ 39. What is the role of erythropoietin (EPO) in neuroprotection?
Answer.
  • EPO receptors expressed on neurons; protective in animal models of HIE and TBI.
  • HEAL trial — adding EPO to hypothermia did NOT improve outcomes in neonatal HIE.
  • PENUT trial — high-dose EPO did NOT improve outcomes in extremely preterm infants.
  • Translational disappointment despite animal promise.
  • Hypothermia remains the standard for neonatal HIE.
SAQ 40. Describe the developmental trajectory of myelination.
Answer.
  • Begins late gestation in brainstem and cerebellum.
  • Internal capsule by term.
  • Sensory pathways before motor pathways.
  • Cortical myelination active through age ~2 y.
  • Frontal / association cortex myelination continues through adolescence and into early adulthood.
  • Disruptions — preterm periventricular leukomalacia, leukodystrophies, demyelinating diseases.

7. Case Bank — 18 Clinical Cases

Case 1 — Neonatal HIE.
Term infant with low Apgars, cord pH 6.9. At 4 hours of life — encephalopathic, seizures.
Q. Immediate management.
Answer.
  • Confirm criteria for therapeutic hypothermia — pH ≤7.0 or BE ≤−16; Sarnat stage 2 or 3 encephalopathy.
  • Initiate hypothermia (33.5 °C × 72 h) within 6 h.
  • Continuous aEEG / EEG monitoring.
  • Treat seizures with phenobarbital first-line.
  • Avoid hyperthermia, hyperoxia, hypoglycemia, hypocapnia.
  • Adjunctive EPO not supported by HEAL.
Case 2 — Pediatric TBI with refractory ICP.
8-year-old after MVC has ICP 28 mmHg despite sedation, head-of-bed elevation, normocapnia, normothermia.
Q. Escalation.
Answer.
  • Hyperosmolar therapy — hypertonic saline 3 % or mannitol 0.5–1 g/kg.
  • Deepen sedation; consider neuromuscular blockade.
  • Brief hyperventilation if herniation suspected; monitor cerebral oxygenation.
  • Consider CPPopt-guided therapy via PRx.
  • Surgical decompressive craniectomy if refractory.
Case 3 — Infant with prolonged anesthesia exposure.
2-month-old undergoing multiple cardiac procedures with cumulative anesthesia >10 h.
Q. Implications.
Answer.
  • Cumulative anesthesia >3 h in <3 y triggers FDA warning.
  • Discuss with family — benefit of necessary surgery outweighs theoretical neurodevelopmental risk.
  • Minimize subsequent exposures; consider dexmedetomidine for sedation.
  • Plan developmental surveillance.
Case 4 — Status epilepticus in a neonate.
Term newborn at 24 h of life develops focal clonic seizures and continuous electrographic seizures on aEEG.
Q. Management.
Answer.
  • Phenobarbital first-line (20 mg/kg loading).
  • Investigate cause — glucose, electrolytes (Na, Ca, Mg), pyridoxine trial, head ultrasound, MRI, metabolic screen.
  • If refractory — phenobarbital second load, levetiracetam, midazolam infusion.
  • Bumetanide not in routine practice (NEMO trial).
  • Continue EEG monitoring.
Case 5 — Preterm IVH.
27-week preterm with grade III IVH and progressive ventricular dilation.
Q. Mechanism and management.
Answer.
  • Germinal matrix vulnerability — thin-walled vessels, pressure-passive CBF, fluctuating CBF.
  • Avoid hyperosmolar boluses, rapid volume changes, hypercapnia.
  • Serial head US for ventricle size.
  • Neurosurgical drainage (VSGS, ventriculo-subgaleal shunt or reservoir) if progressive hydrocephalus.
  • Long-term — VP shunt or ETV if persistent.
Case 6 — GLUT1 deficiency syndrome.
2-year-old with developmental delay, complex movement disorder, infantile-onset seizures. CSF glucose 1.8 mmol/L with normal serum glucose.
Q. Diagnosis and management.
Answer.
  • GLUT1 deficiency syndrome (SLC2A1 mutation).
  • CSF/serum glucose ratio <0.5 supports diagnosis.
  • Genetic confirmation.
  • Ketogenic diet — provides ketone bodies as alternative brain fuel.
  • Avoid drugs that further inhibit GLUT1 (e.g., caffeine, valproate).
Case 7 — Refractory pediatric status epilepticus.
5-year-old with multifocal refractory status epilepticus despite phenobarbital, fosphenytoin, levetiracetam, midazolam infusion.
Q. Options including ketogenic diet.
Answer.
  • Continuous EEG monitoring.
  • Pentobarbital coma or thiopental coma — burst suppression target.
  • Ketogenic diet initiation (PICU formulation — ketogenic enteral feeds or IV emulsion).
  • Trial of immunotherapy (steroids, IVIG, plasma exchange) if autoimmune etiology suspected.
  • Consider anakinra for FIRES (febrile infection-related epilepsy syndrome).
Case 8 — Hypocapnic ischemia.
Toddler with severe TBI inadvertently hyperventilated to PaCO₂ 22 mmHg for 24 h. Develops new infarction on CT.
Q. Mechanism, lesson.
Answer.
  • Hypocapnia → cerebral vasoconstriction → ↓ CBF 30–50 % at PaCO₂ <25 mmHg.
  • In injured brain with disrupted autoregulation, this precipitates ischemia.
  • Target PaCO₂ 35–40 mmHg.
  • Brief hyperventilation only for herniation crisis.
  • Monitor cerebral oxygenation (PbtO₂ or NIRS) when hyperventilating.
Case 9 — Mannitol overuse.
Adolescent with TBI receives repeated mannitol boluses; serum osmolality 340 mOsm, develops AKI.
Q. Management and alternatives.
Answer.
  • Hold further mannitol; switch to hypertonic saline.
  • Volume resuscitation cautiously; avoid further hyperosmolarity.
  • Monitor renal function and electrolytes.
  • Target serum osmolality <320 mOsm with mannitol.
  • With disrupted BBB, mannitol can leak into brain — reduced efficacy.
Case 10 — Anti-NMDA receptor encephalitis.
12-year-old develops psychiatric symptoms, dyskinesias, seizures, autonomic instability. CSF anti-NMDA receptor antibody positive.
Q. Mechanism and treatment.
Answer.
  • Auto-antibodies internalize NMDA receptors → glutamate hypofunction.
  • Treatment — high-dose steroids + IVIG or PLEX (first-line).
  • Refractory — rituximab + cyclophosphamide.
  • Search for tumor (ovarian teratoma, neuroblastoma).
  • ICU support for autonomic / respiratory failure; status epilepticus.
Case 11 — Sedation in young infant on ECMO.
2-month-old on VA-ECMO requiring sedation. Concern about cumulative midazolam exposure.
Q. Sedation strategy.
Answer.
  • Minimize benzodiazepine + opioid duration.
  • Add dexmedetomidine for opioid/benzodiazepine sparing.
  • Daily sedation interruption when safe.
  • Use validated pediatric sedation scoring (SBS).
  • Develop wean plan and watch for withdrawal.
Case 12 — Hyperammonemia.
Neonate at 36 h with refusal to feed, hypotonia, hyperventilation, ammonia 800.
Q. Mechanism and emergent management.
Answer.
  • Urea cycle defect (e.g., OTC deficiency) likely.
  • Hyperammonemia crosses BBB and causes cerebral edema via astrocyte swelling (glutamine accumulation).
  • Treat — stop protein, IV dextrose, sodium phenylacetate-benzoate (Ammonul) or sodium benzoate.
  • L-arginine.
  • Hemodialysis if NH3 >500 or rising despite scavengers.
  • Investigate underlying enzyme defect.
Case 13 — Refractory pediatric TBI with PRx 0.4.
10-year-old TBI patient — autoregulation monitoring shows PRx 0.4 at current CPP of 65 mmHg.
Q. Action.
Answer.
  • Impaired autoregulation (PRx >0.3).
  • Plot PRx vs. CPP — find CPP at which PRx is lowest (CPPopt).
  • Titrate MAP / vasoactive agents to bring CPP near CPPopt.
  • Monitor cerebral oxygenation in parallel.
  • Re-evaluate periodically — CPPopt may shift with disease evolution.
Case 14 — Caffeine for apnea of prematurity.
28-week preterm with recurrent apnea events.
Q. Mechanism and dosing.
Answer.
  • Caffeine antagonizes adenosine A1 receptors → stimulates respiratory drive.
  • Load 20 mg/kg, maintenance 5–10 mg/kg/day.
  • Reduces apnea, BPD, cerebral palsy (CAP trial).
  • Long-acting; once-daily dosing.
  • Continue until 34–36 weeks corrected age or apnea-free for ~7 days.
Case 15 — Meningitis antibiotic dosing.
8-year-old with bacterial meningitis. Treatment plan.
Q. Antibiotic and dosing considerations.
Answer.
  • Empiric vancomycin + ceftriaxone (high dose for CNS) until culture/sensitivities.
  • Add ampicillin if <3 mo (Listeria).
  • Dexamethasone with or before first antibiotic dose (reduces hearing loss in Hib).
  • Higher doses needed for adequate CSF levels.
  • BBB permeability increases with inflammation but drops as inflammation resolves — full-course dosing important.
Case 16 — NMOSD presentation.
14-year-old with bilateral optic neuritis and longitudinally extensive transverse myelitis. Anti-AQP4 positive.
Q. Pathophysiology, treatment.
Answer.
  • Antibody-mediated attack on AQP4 (astrocyte end-foot water channel at BBB).
  • Complement-mediated damage.
  • Acute — high-dose IV methylprednisolone, PLEX.
  • Maintenance — rituximab, eculizumab, satralizumab, inebilizumab.
  • Avoid β-interferons (worsen NMOSD vs. MS).
Case 17 — Pediatric stroke.
6-year-old with sickle cell disease has acute right hemiparesis. MRI shows left MCA infarct.
Q. Acute management.
Answer.
  • Exchange transfusion to reduce HbS <30 %.
  • Hydration, oxygenation.
  • Pediatric stroke neurology consult — consider tPA / thrombectomy in selected cases.
  • Long-term — chronic transfusion or hydroxyurea; TCD monitoring.
  • Stroke prevention — primary prevention if TCD >200 cm/s on chronic transfusion.
Case 18 — Adolescent TBI with second-impact concern.
15-year-old football player with mild concussion 2 weeks ago has new concussion event.
Q. Risk and management.
Answer.
  • Second-impact syndrome — repeat injury during recovery from first → catastrophic edema (rare but described).
  • Mechanism may involve impaired autoregulation in still-recovering brain.
  • Acute — emergent CT, observation, ICU monitoring.
  • Plan return-to-play only after symptom-free, normal neurocognitive testing, graduated exertion protocol.
  • Counseling on long-term CTE risk.

10. Final One-Page Infographic

🧠 Pediatric Developmental Neurobiology — Master Map

① Developmental flow

  • Neurulation → neurogenesis → migration → synaptogenesis (peaks 1–2 y) → myelination (continues through adolescence) → pruning (puberty).

② Key developmental switches

  • GABA — excitatory (NKCC1 dominant) → inhibitory (KCC2 dominant) around birth.
  • NMDA — high NR2B subunit early, increasing NR2A later — affects excitotoxicity profile.
  • CBF/CMR — increase from birth, peak 3–9 y, then decline.

③ Critical ICU thresholds

  • CPP — infant ≥40, toddler ≥50, child ≥60, adolescent ≥70.
  • PaCO₂ — 35–40 in TBI; brief hyperventilation only for herniation.
  • Therapeutic hypothermia — 33.5 °C × 72 h within 6 h of birth.
  • Phenobarbital — first-line neonatal seizures.
  • Mannitol — 0.5–1 g/kg; serum osm cap ~320.

④ Red flags

  • Cumulative anesthesia >3 h in <3 y — minimize duration.
  • PaCO₂ <25 mmHg in injured brain — risk of secondary ischemia.
  • Pressure-passive CBF after TBI — vulnerable to hypotension and hypertension.
  • Open fontanelles mask early ICP rises in infants — clinical assessment remains crucial.

⑤ Exam pearls

🔥🔥🔥 GABA switch around birth — paradoxical neonatal seizure response.
🔥🔥🔥 CBF/CMR peaks 3–9 y — mirrors synaptogenesis.
🔥🔥🔥 Age-appropriate CPP — never apply adult thresholds to a neonate.
🔥🔥 Anesthesia + developmental neuroapoptosis — FDA warning <3 y / >3 h.
🔥🔥 Hypothermia for neonatal HIE; EPO doesn't add benefit.
🔥 Dexmedetomidine — α2-agonist, no GABAergic action, less neuroapoptotic signal.
