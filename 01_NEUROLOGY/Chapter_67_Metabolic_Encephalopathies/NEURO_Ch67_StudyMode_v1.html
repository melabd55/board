Chapter 67 — Metabolic Encephalopathies in Children — STUDY MODE

Rogers' Textbook of Pediatric Intensive Care, Chapter 67 (Ducharme-Crevier, Jouvet, Du Pont-Thibodeau). Pages 3526–3582. Classification: DISEASE / ICU. Adaptive distribution: 40 SAQs + 40 PICU cases. Cross-links to Chapters 58 (Coma), 60 (Imaging), 64 (Status Epilepticus), 66 (HIE).

Classification & Yield

  • Type: DISEASE / ICU.
  • Yield: 🔥🔥🔥 — RCPSC core: hyperammonemia recognition and management, DKA cerebral edema, dysnatremia correction, inborn errors of metabolism emergency workup, hepatic encephalopathy.
  • Mortality: Highly variable by etiology — can be excellent with prompt recognition (electrolyte) or guarded (acute liver failure, mitochondrial crisis).

1 · Structured Rogers Mirror

KEY POINTS (page 3526)
  • Many causes — diagnosis requires systematic workup: history, exam, labs, imaging.
  • Intracranial hypertension is a frequent complication; strict monitoring and neuroprotection are essential.
  • Specific treatments for endogenous intoxications must be started promptly, in parallel with diagnostic workup.
ICU layer: Empiric treatment (D10, ammonia scavengers, hypertonic saline, thiamine, pyridoxine, fluid restriction) commonly begun before definitive diagnosis confirmed.
RCPSC angle: Pediatric ICU candidates are expected to recognize that "metabolic encephalopathy" is a working diagnosis with parallel treatment and diagnostic workflow.
CLASSIFICATION BY PATHOPHYSIOLOGY (Table 67.1, page 3527)

Three categories

  • Endogenous intoxication: accumulation of neurotoxic metabolite(s) due to enzyme deficit, organ failure, or compromised clearance.
    • Hyperammonemia and liver disease — urea cycle disorders, Reye syndrome, hepatic encephalopathy.
    • Organic acid disorders — MSUD, PA (propionic), MMA (methylmalonic), IVA (isovaleric), 3HiB-uria, GA type I.
    • Ketoacidosis.
    • Wilson disease (copper).
  • Energy failure: insufficient substrate or impaired energy production.
    • Hypoglycemia — hyperinsulinism, glycogen storage disorders.
    • Mitochondrial defects — PDH (pyruvate dehydrogenase) deficiency, pyruvate carboxylase deficiency, respiratory chain disease.
    • Fatty acid oxidation disorders (FAODs).
    • Thiamine deficiency (Wernicke encephalopathy).
    • Biotin-responsive basal ganglia disease (BBGD).
  • Water, electrolyte, endocrine disturbances:
    • Disorders of osmolality: DKA, nonketotic hyperosmolar coma, hyponatremia, hypernatremia.
    • Hypocalcemia, hypercalcemia, hypomagnesemia, hypophosphatemia.
    • Thyroid disorders.
    • Intestinal disease–induced encephalopathy (D-lactic acidosis in short bowel).
    • Burn encephalopathy.
EPIDEMIOLOGY AND MECHANISMS OF DISEASE (page 3527)
  • Mechanisms: endogenous intoxication, energy failure, or osmotic/endocrine disturbance.
  • Encephalopathy is the clinical phenotype — global cerebral dysfunction in the absence of structural lesion.
  • Cerebral edema is a frequent complication and accelerates clinical decline.
  • Outcome depends on early recognition, immediate institution of specific therapy, and prevention of secondary brain injury.
ENCEPHALOPATHIES WITH ENDOGENOUS INTOXICATION (pages 3528–3548)

Hyperammonemia and the glutamate-glutamine cycle

  • Urea cycle disorders — six enzymes; OTC deficiency (X-linked) is most common.
  • Secondary inhibition of urea cycle by organic acidurias (propionyl-CoA accumulation inhibits N-acetylglutamate synthase).
  • Hepatic failure — loss of detoxification capacity.
  • Mechanism of injury: ammonia crosses BBB, glutamate-glutamine cycle in astrocytes traps NH₃ as glutamine → osmotic astrocyte swelling → cerebral edema.
  • Glutamine elevation in CSF correlates with severity.
  • Symptoms by age: vomiting, lethargy, refusal to feed in neonates; behavioral change, ataxia, seizures in older children.

Hyperammonemia management

  • Stop protein intake immediately.
  • Provide caloric support — D10 + intralipid + glucose-insulin to reverse catabolism.
  • Ammonia scavengers: sodium benzoate + sodium phenylacetate (Ammonul) IV.
  • L-arginine for urea cycle substrate (omit in arginase deficiency).
  • L-carnitine.
  • Emergent CRRT/dialysis if ammonia > 500 µmol/L OR rapidly rising despite scavengers.
  • Treat seizures.
  • Avoid sedation that masks neuro exam where possible.
  • Long-term: protein-restricted diet, scavenger therapy, possibly liver transplant.

Urea cycle disorders — specifics

  • OTC deficiency — high ammonia, low citrulline, elevated urine orotic acid.
  • Citrullinemia (ASS1) — high citrulline.
  • Argininosuccinic aciduria (ASL) — high argininosuccinate.
  • Hyperornithinemia-hyperammonemia-homocitrullinuria (HHH).
  • Carbamoyl phosphate synthetase 1 (CPS1) deficiency.
  • N-acetylglutamate synthase (NAGS) deficiency — carbamylglutamate is specific therapy.

Hepatic encephalopathy

  • Pediatric acute liver failure (PALF) registry has informed management standards.
  • Mechanism: ammonia + inflammation + osmotic + cerebral hyperperfusion.
  • West Haven staging: I (mild confusion) → II (lethargy) → III (stupor) → IV (coma).
  • Treatment: lactulose, rifaximin, ammonia scavengers in selected cases, fluid management, treat infection/triggers.
  • ICP monitoring controversial — bleeding risk from coagulopathy.
  • Hyperosmolar therapy for cerebral edema with herniation signs.
  • Liver transplant — definitive treatment for ALF when transplant criteria met.

Reye syndrome

  • Acute non-inflammatory encephalopathy with fatty hepatic infiltration following viral illness.
  • Historically associated with aspirin in children — now rare due to avoidance of salicylates.
  • Hyperammonemia, hypoglycemia, elevated LFTs, prolonged PT.
  • Differential: rule out fatty acid oxidation disorders.
  • Supportive ICU care; mannitol/hypertonic saline for cerebral edema.

Organic acid disorders

  • Maple syrup urine disease (MSUD) — branched-chain α-keto acid dehydrogenase deficiency; leucine, isoleucine, valine and their ketoacids accumulate.
  • Propionic aciduria, methylmalonic aciduria, isovaleric aciduria, glutaric aciduria type I.
  • Triggered by catabolic state (illness, fasting, infection).
  • Workup: plasma amino acids, urine organic acids, acylcarnitine profile.
  • Treatment: stop protein, D10 + intralipid, L-carnitine, B12 in MMA, biotin in MCC/PA, specific cofactors.
  • CRRT if severe and unresponsive.

Wilson disease

  • ATP7B mutation — defective copper export.
  • Presents in older children/adolescents with hepatic failure or neuropsychiatric symptoms.
  • Kayser-Fleischer rings; low ceruloplasmin; elevated 24-h urine copper.
  • Treatment: D-penicillamine, trientine, zinc; liver transplant for fulminant ALF.
ENCEPHALOPATHIES WITH ENERGY FAILURE (pages 3548–3565)

Hypoglycemia

  • Neonatal: hyperinsulinism, glycogen storage disorders, FAOD, hypopituitarism.
  • Older child: insulin overdose, hyperinsulinism, hepatic dysfunction, sepsis, alcohol.
  • Brain regions most vulnerable: parieto-occipital cortex, hippocampus.
  • Treatment: D10 2–5 mL/kg IV bolus; continue infusion to maintain euglycemia.
  • Investigate: insulin, C-peptide, cortisol, growth hormone, ammonia, lactate, ketones, acylcarnitine, urine organic acids during hypoglycemia ("critical sample").
  • Diazoxide for hyperinsulinism (after critical sample obtained).

Mitochondrial defects

  • Respiratory chain complex deficiencies, PDH deficiency, pyruvate carboxylase deficiency.
  • Symptoms: lactic acidosis, exercise intolerance, stroke-like episodes (MELAS), Leigh syndrome, encephalopathy with catabolic stress.
  • MRI patterns: bilateral basal ganglia, brainstem (Leigh), or cortical/subcortical not respecting vascular territories (MELAS).
  • Treatment: avoid valproate (mitochondrial toxin), provide cofactor cocktail (CoQ10, carnitine, riboflavin, thiamine, vitamin C).
  • L-arginine for MELAS stroke-like episodes (0.5 g/kg IV bolus + infusion).
  • Ketogenic diet for some PDH disease (bypasses defect).

Fatty acid oxidation disorders (FAODs)

  • MCAD (medium-chain acyl-CoA dehydrogenase) deficiency most common.
  • Hypoketotic hypoglycemia, hyperammonemia, sudden death during fasting/illness.
  • Workup: acylcarnitine profile.
  • Treatment: avoid fasting; D10 emergency dose, carnitine in selected types.
  • Newborn screening detects MCAD in most jurisdictions.

Thiamine deficiency / Wernicke encephalopathy

  • Classic triad: ophthalmoplegia, ataxia, confusion (full triad in <20%).
  • Risk: chronic vomiting, malnutrition, prolonged TPN without supplementation, oncology patients, post-bariatric surgery (adolescent).
  • Treatment: thiamine 100 mg IV (pediatric weight-based 5–10 mg/kg) BEFORE glucose; glucose alone may precipitate Wernicke.
  • Continue daily thiamine until resolution.

Biotin-responsive basal ganglia disease (BBGD)

  • SLC19A3 thiamine transporter mutation.
  • Childhood subacute encephalopathy with bilateral caudate/putamen necrosis.
  • Biotin 5–10 mg/kg/day + thiamine 20–40 mg/kg/day — dramatic improvement.
  • Empiric trial reasonable in any unexplained pediatric encephalopathy with bilateral basal ganglia lesions.
WATER, ELECTROLYTE, AND ENDOCRINE DISTURBANCES (pages 3565–3580)

Diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA) cerebral edema

  • Incidence ~0.5–1% of pediatric DKA episodes.
  • Onset 4–12 h after starting treatment.
  • Mortality 20–25% in those who develop overt cerebral edema.
  • Risk factors: younger age, new diagnosis, severe acidosis, low PCO₂, high BUN, treatment with bicarbonate, rapid Na drop, insulin bolus.
  • Symptoms: headache, deteriorating mental status, bradycardia, hypertension, papilledema, posturing.
  • Treatment: hypertonic saline 3% 5–10 mL/kg over 30 min OR mannitol 0.5–1 g/kg IV; slow IV fluids; head of bed 30°; reduce insulin if needed; intubate and hyperventilate as rescue.
  • Per ISPAD 2022 guidelines: prevention via gradual fluid resuscitation (10 mL/kg over 30 min if shock, then maintenance + deficit over 24–48 h); avoid bicarbonate unless severe acidosis with circulatory compromise.

Dysnatremia

  • Hyponatremia: Na < 135 mmol/L.
    • Acute symptomatic (seizure, coma): 3% NaCl 2–4 mL/kg bolus.
    • Chronic correction: max 8–10 mmol/L per 24 h (avoid osmotic demyelination).
    • Etiologies: SIADH, CSW, water intoxication, hypotonic IV fluids, adrenal insufficiency.
  • Hypernatremia: Na > 145 mmol/L.
    • Etiologies: diabetes insipidus, free water loss, salt poisoning.
    • Correction max 10–12 mmol/L per 24 h (avoid cerebral edema from rapid correction).
    • Calculate free water deficit; replace over 48 h.

Calcium, magnesium, phosphate

  • Hypocalcemia: causes seizures, tetany, prolonged QT; treat with IV calcium gluconate.
  • Hypercalcemia: lethargy, weakness, polyuria, arrhythmia; IV fluids, bisphosphonates, calcitonin.
  • Hypomagnesemia: refractory seizures, hypokalemia, hypocalcemia.
  • Hypophosphatemia: weakness, respiratory failure (severe), encephalopathy with refeeding syndrome.

Thyroid storm and myxedema coma

  • Storm: agitation, tachycardia, hyperthermia, encephalopathy; propylthiouracil + beta-blocker + steroids + iodine.
  • Myxedema coma: bradycardia, hypothermia, lethargy; IV levothyroxine + hydrocortisone.

Burn encephalopathy

  • Multifactorial: sepsis, hyponatremia, hypoxia, anesthesia, infection, inflammatory mediators.
  • Management: meticulous resuscitation, infection control, nutrition, neuroprotection.

Intestinal disease–induced encephalopathy

  • D-lactic acidosis: short bowel syndrome with bacterial overgrowth producing D-lactate.
  • Symptoms: episodic ataxia, slurred speech, confusion.
  • Treatment: bowel rest, low-carbohydrate diet, antibiotics (metronidazole, vancomycin).

7 · 40 SAQs — Chapter 67 (STUDY MODE — answers visible)

SAQ 1. Define metabolic encephalopathy.

  • Global cerebral dysfunction in the absence of a structural brain lesion.
  • Caused by endogenous intoxication, energy failure, or electrolyte/endocrine disturbance.

SAQ 2. List the three pathophysiologic categories.

  • Endogenous intoxication (accumulation of neurotoxic metabolites).
  • Energy failure (insufficient substrate or impaired ATP production).
  • Water/electrolyte/endocrine disturbance.

SAQ 3. Outline acute hyperammonemia management.

  • Stop protein; D10 + intralipid for caloric support.
  • Ammonul (Na benzoate + Na phenylacetate) IV.
  • L-arginine (omit in arginase deficiency).
  • L-carnitine.
  • Specific cofactors based on suspected disorder (B12, biotin).
  • Emergent CRRT if NH₃ > 500 or rising.

SAQ 4. When to initiate CRRT in hyperammonemia?

  • NH₃ > 500 µmol/L.
  • Rapidly rising despite scavengers.
  • Persistent > 200 µmol/L after appropriate medical therapy.
  • Severe encephalopathy.

SAQ 5. Why is sodium phenylacetate effective?

  • Conjugates with glutamine → phenylacetylglutamine excreted in urine.
  • Provides alternative nitrogen disposal pathway, bypassing the urea cycle.
  • Sodium benzoate similarly conjugates with glycine → hippurate.

SAQ 6. List 4 urea cycle disorders.

  • Ornithine transcarbamylase (OTC) deficiency — X-linked, most common.
  • Carbamoyl phosphate synthetase (CPS1) deficiency.
  • Argininosuccinate synthetase (ASS1) — citrullinemia.
  • Argininosuccinate lyase (ASL).
  • Arginase deficiency.
  • N-acetylglutamate synthase (NAGS).

SAQ 7. Distinguishing labs for urea cycle disorders.

  • OTC: elevated NH₃, low citrulline, elevated urine orotic acid.
  • CPS1: elevated NH₃, low citrulline, low/normal urine orotic acid.
  • Citrullinemia (ASS1): elevated citrulline.
  • ASL: elevated argininosuccinate.
  • NAGS: improves with carbamylglutamate (specific therapy).

SAQ 8. Why does propionic aciduria cause hyperammonemia?

  • Propionyl-CoA accumulation inhibits N-acetylglutamate synthase (NAGS).
  • NAGS produces N-acetylglutamate, the activator of CPS1.
  • Decreased CPS1 activity → impaired urea cycle → secondary hyperammonemia.

SAQ 9. Reye syndrome — features and avoid which medication.

  • Acute non-inflammatory encephalopathy with fatty hepatic infiltration.
  • Follows viral illness (varicella, influenza).
  • Hyperammonemia, hypoglycemia, elevated LFTs, coagulopathy.
  • Avoid aspirin in pediatric viral illnesses to prevent.
  • Treatment: supportive ICU care; rule out FAODs.

SAQ 10. Maple syrup urine disease — pathophysiology and management.

  • Branched-chain α-keto acid dehydrogenase deficiency.
  • Leucine, isoleucine, valine and their keto-acids accumulate.
  • Sweet-smelling urine.
  • Treatment: BCAA-restricted formula; D10 + intralipid for catabolic crisis; CRRT if severe.
  • Thiamine responsive in select forms.

SAQ 11. DKA cerebral edema risk factors.

  • Younger age (<5 y).
  • New diagnosis.
  • Severe acidosis with low PCO₂.
  • Elevated BUN.
  • Treatment with bicarbonate.
  • Rapid sodium drop, insulin bolus.

SAQ 12. DKA cerebral edema treatment.

  • 3% NaCl 5–10 mL/kg over 30 min OR mannitol 0.5–1 g/kg IV.
  • Reduce IV fluid rate by ~⅓.
  • Head of bed 30°.
  • Reduce insulin if hypoglycemia developing.
  • Intubate + brief hyperventilation if herniation imminent.
  • Urgent imaging.

SAQ 13. PECARN FLUID trial conclusion.

  • Kuppermann NEJM 2018 — fluid rate and sodium content did not affect neurologic outcome in pediatric DKA.
  • Reassures gradual replacement remains reasonable.
  • Informed ISPAD 2022.

SAQ 14. Why avoid bicarbonate in DKA?

  • Associated with worse outcomes including cerebral edema.
  • Paradoxical CNS acidosis.
  • Use only if severe acidosis with circulatory compromise unresponsive to fluids.

SAQ 15. Acute symptomatic hyponatremia treatment.

  • 3% NaCl 2–4 mL/kg IV bolus over 10–20 min.
  • Repeat once if persistent symptoms.
  • Target Na rise of 4–6 mmol/L to relieve symptoms.
  • Continue gradual correction not exceeding 8–10 mmol/L in 24 h.

SAQ 16. Osmotic demyelination syndrome — risk factors.

  • Chronic hyponatremia (> 48 h).
  • Rapid correction (> 10–12 mmol/L per 24 h).
  • Hypokalemia, alcoholism, malnutrition, liver disease.
  • Presents 2–6 days after rapid correction with dysarthria, dysphagia, paraparesis, locked-in syndrome.

SAQ 17. Hypernatremia correction strategy.

  • Calculate free water deficit.
  • Replace over 48 h (longer if chronic).
  • Correction limit ≤ 10–12 mmol/L per 24 h to avoid cerebral edema.
  • Treat underlying cause (DI: desmopressin; salt poisoning: dialysis; free water loss: fluid replacement).

SAQ 18. Critical sample workup for hypoglycemia.

  • Send during hypoglycemic episode, before glucose if at all possible.
  • Glucose, insulin, C-peptide, cortisol, growth hormone.
  • Ammonia, lactate, beta-hydroxybutyrate, free fatty acids.
  • Acylcarnitine profile.
  • Urine organic acids, ketones, reducing substances.

SAQ 19. Differential by hypoglycemia pattern.

  • High insulin, low ketones: hyperinsulinism.
  • High ammonia + low ketones: FAOD.
  • Hyperinsulinism + hyperammonemia: HI/HA syndrome (GLUD1 mutation).
  • High lactate: glycogen storage or mitochondrial disease.
  • Low cortisol/GH: hypopituitarism, Addison's.
  • Hypoketotic + acidosis: organic aciduria, MCAD.

SAQ 20. Wernicke encephalopathy — triad and treatment.

  • Triad: ophthalmoplegia (CN VI palsy, nystagmus), ataxia, confusion (full triad in <20%).
  • Pediatric risk: oncology, chronic vomiting, TPN without supplementation, post-bariatric, refeeding syndrome.
  • Treatment: thiamine 5–10 mg/kg IV BEFORE glucose; continue daily.

SAQ 21. Biotin-responsive basal ganglia disease.

  • SLC19A3 thiamine transporter mutation.
  • Subacute encephalopathy with bilateral caudate/putamen necrosis.
  • Empiric biotin 5–10 mg/kg/day + thiamine 20–40 mg/kg/day.
  • Dramatic recovery if treated early; high suspicion in unexplained encephalopathy with BG lesions.

SAQ 22. MELAS — recognition and acute therapy.

  • Mitochondrial Encephalomyopathy with Lactic Acidosis and Stroke-like episodes.
  • mtDNA mutation, commonly m.3243A>G in MT-TL1.
  • Imaging: cortical/subcortical lesions not respecting vascular territories.
  • Acute treatment: L-arginine 0.5 g/kg IV bolus + 0.5 g/kg/day infusion x 3–5 days.
  • Avoid valproate; mitochondrial cocktail (CoQ10, carnitine, riboflavin, thiamine).

SAQ 23. Why is valproate contraindicated in mitochondrial disease?

  • Valproate inhibits mitochondrial β-oxidation and may precipitate acute liver failure.
  • POLG mutations particularly at risk.
  • Use levetiracetam or alternatives for seizure management.

SAQ 24. Leigh syndrome — pattern.

  • Mitochondrial respiratory chain or PDH deficiency.
  • MRI: symmetric bilateral basal ganglia and brainstem T2 hyperintensities.
  • Lactic acidosis, developmental regression, brainstem dysfunction.
  • Treatment: cofactors (thiamine, riboflavin, CoQ10), avoid metabolic stress, supportive.

SAQ 25. Hepatic encephalopathy West Haven staging.

  • Grade I: mild confusion, anxiety, sleep changes, asterixis absent.
  • Grade II: lethargy, disorientation, asterixis present.
  • Grade III: stupor, marked confusion, arouses to noxious stimuli only.
  • Grade IV: coma, with or without posturing.

SAQ 26. PALF (pediatric acute liver failure) — when to refer for transplant.

  • Grade III/IV encephalopathy + INR > 2 without correction OR INR > 1.5 with encephalopathy.
  • Worsening trajectory despite supportive care.
  • Wilson disease, acetaminophen toxicity, hepatitis B with fulminant course.
  • Refer EARLY to transplant center — outcomes better with early listing.

SAQ 27. PALF management essentials.

  • ICU admission; airway protection if encephalopathy.
  • Lactulose; rifaximin.
  • Vitamin K for coagulopathy.
  • Hyperosmolar therapy for cerebral edema.
  • Avoid invasive procedures unless critical (coagulopathy).
  • Transplant evaluation.
  • Avoid sedatives that mask neurologic exam.

SAQ 28. Wilson disease — pediatric presentation and treatment.

  • ATP7B mutation; defective copper export.
  • Older children/adolescents with hepatic, neuropsychiatric, or hematologic presentation.
  • Kayser-Fleischer rings on slit lamp; low ceruloplasmin; elevated 24-h urine copper.
  • Treatment: D-penicillamine, trientine, zinc.
  • Liver transplant for fulminant ALF.

SAQ 29. Reye syndrome — why is it now rare?

  • Association with aspirin in viral illness.
  • Public health campaigns to avoid salicylates in pediatric viral illness in 1980s.
  • Differential expanded to include previously undiagnosed FAODs and IEMs.

SAQ 30. Thyroid storm management.

  • Beta-blocker (propranolol) for symptoms.
  • Propylthiouracil (PTU) or methimazole.
  • Iodine (after PTU) to block release.
  • Steroids (hydrocortisone) for adrenal protection and conversion inhibition.
  • Treat hyperthermia, dehydration; ICU monitoring.

SAQ 31. Myxedema coma management.

  • IV levothyroxine 4 µg/kg loading dose (or weight-based pediatric).
  • Hydrocortisone first (rule out adrenal insufficiency before thyroid).
  • Warming, BP support, ventilatory support as needed.
  • Treat hyponatremia carefully.

SAQ 32. Hypocalcemia presentation.

  • Seizures, tetany, perioral numbness, carpopedal spasm.
  • Prolonged QT interval; arrhythmia risk.
  • Treatment: IV calcium gluconate 100 mg/kg over 10 min for acute symptomatic; maintenance infusion.
  • Check Mg — hypomagnesemia precipitates refractory hypocalcemia.

SAQ 33. D-lactic acidosis in short bowel syndrome.

  • Bacterial overgrowth in colonized short bowel produces D-lactate.
  • Standard lactate assay measures L-lactate; specifically request D-lactate.
  • Symptoms: episodic ataxia, slurred speech, confusion, after carbohydrate load.
  • Treatment: bowel rest, low-carbohydrate diet, metronidazole or vancomycin.

SAQ 34. Burn encephalopathy contributors.

  • Sepsis with systemic inflammation.
  • Hyponatremia from fluid management.
  • Hypoxia from inhalation injury.
  • Anesthesia, sedation, infection.
  • Inflammatory mediators.
  • Treatment: meticulous resuscitation, infection control, nutrition, neuroprotection.

SAQ 35. Inborn error of metabolism — neonatal presentation pattern.

  • Honeymoon period (24–72 h after birth).
  • Lethargy, poor feeding, vomiting, hypotonia.
  • Then progressive encephalopathy with hyperammonemia, acidosis, or hypoglycemia.
  • Sepsis-like picture commonly mimics — workup must include IEM screening.

SAQ 36. Hypotonic vs isotonic maintenance fluids.

  • Isotonic (0.9% NaCl or LR) preferred (Moritz NEJM 2015).
  • Hypotonic (D5 ½ NS) associated with hospital-acquired hyponatremia and encephalopathy.
  • Modern AAP / CHOP standard: use isotonic maintenance.

SAQ 37. Refeeding syndrome encephalopathy.

  • Severe malnutrition + reintroduction of nutrition → intracellular phosphate, magnesium, potassium shift.
  • Hypophosphatemia → muscle weakness, respiratory failure, encephalopathy.
  • Wernicke encephalopathy risk.
  • Prevention: slow reintroduction of calories, thiamine prophylaxis, electrolyte monitoring.

SAQ 38. When is empiric pyridoxine indicated in neonatal seizures?

  • Refractory neonatal seizures.
  • Empiric trial 50–100 mg IV pyridoxine.
  • If cessation: pyridoxine-dependent epilepsy (ALDH7A1 mutation); continue long-term.
  • Also consider folinic acid 5 mg in pyridoxine-resistant cases.

SAQ 39. Neonatal sepsis vs IEM differentiation.

  • Both can present similarly with lethargy, hypotonia, poor feeding.
  • IEM clues: family history, consanguinity, prior sibling deaths, metabolic acidosis with anion gap, ketosis, hyperammonemia.
  • Workup: blood gas, lactate, ammonia, plasma amino acids, urine organic acids alongside sepsis workup.
  • Treat both empirically until one excluded.

SAQ 40. Top 5 examiner traps in pediatric metabolic encephalopathy.

  • Giving glucose before thiamine in suspected thiamine deficiency.
  • Using valproate in mitochondrial or organic acid disorders.
  • Rapid sodium correction in chronic hyponatremia → osmotic demyelination.
  • Bicarbonate use in DKA → cerebral edema risk.
  • Failing to initiate empiric BBGD therapy (biotin + thiamine) in unexplained encephalopathy with bilateral basal ganglia lesions.

Pathophysiology Categories

  • Endogenous intoxication: hyperammonemia, organic acidurias, hepatic, Reye, Wilson.
  • Energy failure: hypoglycemia, mitochondrial, FAODs, thiamine, BBGD.
  • Water/electrolyte/endocrine: DKA, dysnatremia, Ca/Mg/P, thyroid, burn, intestinal.

Critical Workup

  • Bedside glucose immediately.
  • Ammonia, lactate, anion gap, blood gas.
  • Plasma amino acids, urine organic acids, acylcarnitine profile.
  • LFTs, coagulation, electrolytes including Ca/Mg/P.
  • Critical sample for hypoglycemia.

Empiric Treatments

  • D10 + intralipid (reverse catabolism).
  • Stop protein in suspected IEM.
  • Ammonul + arginine + carnitine if NH₃ elevated.
  • Thiamine BEFORE glucose if Wernicke risk.
  • Biotin + thiamine empiric if BG lesions.
  • Pyridoxine 100 mg IV in refractory neonatal seizures.
  • L-arginine 0.5 g/kg in MELAS-like episodes.
  • Hypertonic saline 3% for acute symptomatic hyponatremia or cerebral edema.

Correction Limits

  • Na: ≤ 8–10 mmol/L per 24 h for both hyper- and hyponatremia (12 mmol/L acceptable for hypernatremia).
  • NH₃: dialysis if > 500 or rising.
  • Glucose: 3.3–10 mmol/L.
  • Acidosis in DKA: gradual; avoid bicarbonate.

Red Flags

  • Sepsis-like neonate with normal cultures — think IEM.
  • DKA at 4–12 h with bradycardia/HTN — cerebral edema.
  • Bilateral basal ganglia lesions — BBGD, Leigh, glutaric aciduria.
  • Stroke crossing vascular territories — MELAS.
  • Encephalopathy on valproate — hyperammonemia from urea cycle defect.
  • Post-bariatric or chronic vomiting with confusion — Wernicke.

Exam Pearls

  • Empiric treatment in parallel with diagnosis.
  • Thiamine BEFORE glucose in Wernicke.
  • Avoid valproate in mitochondrial / organic acid disease.
  • Avoid bicarbonate in DKA.
  • Avoid rapid Na correction.
  • Empiric biotin + thiamine for BG lesions.
  • L-arginine for MELAS.
  • Ammonul + dialysis at NH₃ > 500.
  • Isotonic maintenance IVF (not hypotonic).
