Block A — Mock RCPSC SAQ (10 questions, 12 minutes)

Start a timer. Answer all 10 in writing before revealing answers. Target: 80% to pass.

A1. A 6-year-old with isolated TBI has ICP 26 mm Hg sustained for 6 minutes despite head-of-bed elevation, normocapnia, sedation, and analgesia. What are the next FOUR escalation steps you should consider in BTF tier-1 order, and what is the threshold for moving to tier 2?

Show answer
  • Confirm adequate sedation/analgesia depth; bolus if patient stimulated or coughing.
  • CSF drainage if external ventricular drain in situ (intermittent or continuous per protocol).
  • Hyperosmolar therapy: 3% saline bolus 2–5 mL/kg over 10–20 min OR mannitol 0.25–1 g/kg if euvolemic and serum osmolality safe.
  • Neuromuscular blockade trial after deepening sedation if ICP remains elevated.
  • Tier 2 trigger: refractory ICP >20 mm Hg despite all tier-1 measures, or rapid neurologic deterioration — move to barbiturate coma, controlled mild hyperventilation with multimodal monitoring, or decompressive craniectomy.

A2. List the BTF 2019 pediatric severe TBI thresholds for ICP and CPP by age band.

Show answer
  • ICP treatment threshold: >20 mm Hg sustained >5 min (all pediatric ages).
  • CPP minimum: 40 mm Hg infants/toddlers; ≥50 mm Hg school age; ≥60 mm Hg adolescents.
  • Avoid CPP <40 mm Hg at any age — associated with worse outcome.
  • Hyperosmolar Na target 150–160 mmol/L with osmolality ≤360 mOsm/kg.

A3. A 14-year-old MVC patient has GCS 6, isolated severe TBI, and arrives at 45 minutes post-injury. ICU asks whether to give tranexamic acid. What is your recommendation and the supporting evidence?

Show answer
  • Give TXA if within 3 hours of injury — CRASH-3 trial (Lancet 2019) showed 9% relative risk reduction in head-injury related death in mild–moderate TBI; signal weaker in severe TBI but benefit if given early.
  • Dose: 1 g IV (or weight-based ~15 mg/kg in pediatrics) over 10 min, then 1 g over 8 h.
  • Do NOT give if >3 h post-injury — signal of harm in CRASH-3 subgroup.
  • Adjunct only — not a substitute for surgical hematoma control or BTF tier therapy.

A4. A 3-year-old with severe TBI is hyperventilated to PaCO₂ 28 mm Hg by the transport team. Why is this potentially harmful, and what is the appropriate target?

Show answer
  • Prophylactic hyperventilation causes cerebral vasoconstriction → reduced CBF → secondary ischemia, especially in the first 24 h when CBF is already depressed.
  • BTF target PaCO₂ 35–40 mm Hg (normocapnia).
  • Brief mild hyperventilation to PaCO₂ 30–35 mm Hg only as rescue bridge for impending herniation, with multimodal monitoring (PbtO₂ or jugular venous oxygen saturation) when possible.
  • Avoid PaCO₂ <30 mm Hg unless documented hyperemia.

A5. List five clinical or imaging findings that should raise suspicion for abusive head trauma in an infant <1 year old presenting with depressed consciousness.

Show answer
  • Bilateral, interhemispheric, or convexity subdural hemorrhages of differing ages on MRI.
  • Multilayered retinal hemorrhages extending to the periphery on dilated fundoscopy.
  • Diffuse hypoxic-ischemic injury or "big black brain" pattern out of proportion to history.
  • History inconsistent with developmental stage (e.g., "rolled off couch" causing extensive injury) or changing story.
  • Associated injuries: posterior rib fractures, metaphyseal corner fractures, bruising in non-mobile infant, frenulum tear.

A6. A 10-year-old with C5 complete SCI from a diving injury has BP 78/40, HR 52. What is the diagnosis, target MAP, and why is the MAP target important?

Show answer
  • Diagnosis: neurogenic shock (loss of sympathetic outflow above T6) on background of high cervical SCI.
  • Target MAP: 85–90 mm Hg sustained for 7 days per AANS/CNS guidelines (extrapolated to pediatrics).
  • Rationale: spinal cord perfusion is pressure-passive after injury; hypotension worsens secondary cord ischemia and final neurologic outcome.
  • First-line: volume resuscitation, then norepinephrine or phenylephrine for vasopressor support; atropine or pacing for bradycardia.

A7. Define SCIWORA and explain how its prevalence differs between young children and adolescents.

Show answer
  • SCIWORA = Spinal Cord Injury Without Radiographic Abnormality on plain films and CT, but MRI may show cord edema or hemorrhage.
  • Younger children (<8 years) have disproportionately large heads, ligamentous laxity, and horizontally oriented facets → greater risk of cord injury without bony failure.
  • Adolescents have adult-pattern spines; SCIWORA much less common.
  • Management: hard collar, MRI within 72 h, neuro-protective MAP targets, neurosurgery consult.

A8. Describe the PECARN low-risk decision rule for the <2-year-old age band and explain its role in CT decisions.

Show answer
  • For children <2 years with GCS 14 or 15: CT recommended if any of — altered mental status, loss of consciousness ≥5 sec, severe mechanism, palpable skull fracture, scalp hematoma (non-frontal), or not acting normally per parent.
  • If none present → very low risk of clinically important TBI (<0.02%) → observation rather than CT is appropriate.
  • Reduces unnecessary CT exposure in this radiosensitive age band.
  • Validation: Kuppermann et al., Lancet 2009; widely adopted in North American PEDs.

A9. A child with severe TBI develops sodium 158 mmol/L on day 3 with urine output 6 mL/kg/h and urine specific gravity 1.003. What is the diagnosis, and how do you distinguish it from cerebral salt wasting?

Show answer
  • Diagnosis: central diabetes insipidus — high serum Na, dilute polyuria, low urine osmolality (<300 mOsm/kg).
  • CSW: low or normal Na, polyuria with HIGH urine Na (>40 mmol/L) and volume DEPLETION; treat with fluid + sodium replacement.
  • DI: euvolemic to volume DEPLETED, low urine osmolality, low ADH; treat with desmopressin and free water replacement.
  • Distinguish using fluid balance, urine osmolality, and urine sodium concentration.
  • Implication: bifrontal or hypothalamic injury — also screen pituitary axis (cortisol, T4).

A10. List the four cardinal incomplete spinal cord syndromes and one neurologic hallmark of each.

Show answer
  • Central cord syndrome — upper extremity weakness > lower; common in elderly hyperextension and in pediatric hyperextension; bladder involvement variable.
  • Anterior cord syndrome — loss of motor, pain, temperature below the lesion with preservation of dorsal column function (proprioception/vibration); worst prognosis.
  • Brown-Séquard syndrome — ipsilateral motor and proprioception loss, contralateral pain/temperature loss; best prognosis.
  • Posterior cord syndrome — loss of proprioception, vibration, and fine touch; rare in trauma.

Block B — Mock RCPSC Oral Cases (5 sequential, 40 minutes)

Each case has 4–5 sequential prompts. Verbalize your answer, then reveal. Use Block E rubric to grade yourself.

B1. A 4-year-old struck by car. GCS 6, intubated in field, hypotensive on arrival (BP 75/45). CT shows diffuse cerebral edema with effaced basal cisterns and no surgical hematoma.

B1a — Initial 30 minutes — your priorities?
  • ABC and ATLS: confirm tube placement, secure two large IV access, type-and-screen.
  • Restore MAP with isotonic crystalloid then norepinephrine — target age-appropriate CPP (≥50 mm Hg here, MAP ~60–65 + ICP).
  • Head of bed 30°, midline neck, sedation/analgesia (fentanyl + midazolam) running.
  • Normocapnia (PaCO₂ 35–40), SpO₂ >94%, avoid hyperthermia.
  • FAST exam + secondary survey for extracranial injuries; activate trauma team and neurosurgery.
B1b — ICP probe placed at 60 min; ICP 28, CPP 42. Next steps?
  • Verify adequate sedation depth — bolus and uptitrate.
  • 3% NaCl 3 mL/kg bolus over 15 min; check Na, osmolality after.
  • Drain CSF if EVD placed; if no EVD, neurosurgery consult to consider placement.
  • Raise MAP further to bring CPP to ≥50 with norepinephrine.
  • Trial neuromuscular blockade if cough/shiver-driven ICP spikes despite optimal sedation.
B1c — Despite tier-1, ICP 32 sustained. Discuss tier-2 options.
  • Controlled mild hyperventilation to PaCO₂ 30–35 mm Hg as a bridge, with multimodal monitoring (PbtO₂, jugular bulb O₂ if available).
  • Decompressive craniectomy — pediatric data (Taylor et al.) supports use in refractory pediatric ICP; weigh early vs late.
  • Barbiturate coma — pentobarbital infusion with continuous EEG (burst suppression target), needs vasopressor support.
  • Therapeutic hypothermia — Cool Kids RCT was negative; not recommended as routine, but mild controlled normothermia (35–36°C) is reasonable adjunct.
  • Discuss prognosis and family communication early — refractory ICP carries poor outcome.
B1d — On day 3 Na 159, UOP 6 mL/kg/h, urine sg 1.002. Manage.
  • Diagnose central DI; differentiate from CSW (urine Na, volume status).
  • Replace ongoing free water deficit cautiously — calculate deficit, correct over 48 h to avoid rapid drops in Na (cerebral edema risk).
  • Desmopressin (DDAVP) IV/intranasal; start low dose, titrate.
  • Screen anterior pituitary axes — cortisol, free T4 — replace if deficient.
  • This finding also raises concern for evolving herniation or hypothalamic injury — urgent CT and discuss with neurosurgery.

B2. A 9-month-old presents with apnea, GCS 4, bulging fontanelle. Father says baby "fell from couch." CT shows acute and subacute subdural collections with diffuse cerebral edema.

B2a — Differential and immediate concerns?
  • Abusive head trauma top of list — history inconsistent with injury severity, mixed-age subdurals, infant age.
  • Immediate priorities: airway, normoxia, normocapnia, MAP support to age-appropriate target.
  • Activate child protection team and notify police/CPS per jurisdiction.
  • Skeletal survey, dilated fundoscopy, MRI brain/spine, social work consult.
  • Document objectively; preserve evidence; do not interrogate caregivers — let safeguarding team lead.
B2b — Eye exam shows multilayered retinal hemorrhages. Significance?
  • Multilayered retinal hemorrhages extending to periphery are strongly associated with AHT, especially when bilateral.
  • Not pathognomonic — must integrate with imaging, history, skeletal survey, and clinical exam.
  • Document by ophthalmology with retinal photography.
  • Continue protective custody assessment with safeguarding team.
B2c — ICP 24. Tier-1 ICP management for an infant?
  • HOB 30°, midline neck, normothermia, normocapnia.
  • Sedation (fentanyl + midazolam), analgesia.
  • 3% NaCl 3 mL/kg bolus — infant tolerates well; monitor Na trajectory.
  • CSF drainage if EVD placed.
  • Target CPP ≥40 mm Hg in this age band.
B2d — MRI shows extensive HIE on DWI. Family asks about prognosis.
  • Diffuse DWI restriction with bilateral parenchymal injury portends very poor neurologic outcome.
  • Avoid early prognostication on day 1; reassess at 72 h, with serial exam off sedation, EEG, and repeat imaging.
  • Communicate with palliative care, social work, and safeguarding present.
  • Discuss goals of care once data converges; do not withdraw based on imaging alone.
  • Mandatory reporting and ongoing documentation continue regardless of medical trajectory.

B3. A 15-year-old after football tackle. C-spine immobilized. GCS 15. Complaining of mid-cervical pain and bilateral upper extremity tingling. Strength 4/5 in arms, 5/5 in legs.

B3a — Working diagnosis, initial workup?
  • Possible central cord syndrome — upper > lower extremity weakness, often hyperextension mechanism.
  • Maintain hard collar, log-roll precautions, neurosurgery consult.
  • Cervical CT to rule out fracture; if negative and neurologic deficit persists → MRI cervical spine to look for cord edema, ligamentous injury, hematoma.
  • Full neurologic exam with ASIA grading documented.
B3b — MRI shows cord edema at C4–C5 with no bony injury. Plan?
  • SCIWORA versus central cord syndrome with imaging-only abnormality.
  • Hard collar continued; admit to PICU for hemodynamic and respiratory monitoring.
  • MAP target 85–90 mm Hg for 7 days — vasopressor support if needed.
  • DVT prophylaxis (mechanical first; pharmacologic per protocol if stable bleeding risk).
  • Rehabilitation consult; serial neuro exams; surgical decompression debated — discuss STASCIS data and individualize.
B3c — Develops bradycardia (HR 45) and BP 80/50 the next day. Cause and management?
  • Neurogenic shock from disrupted sympathetic outflow above T6.
  • Volume status assessment; isotonic fluid if hypovolemic.
  • Norepinephrine or phenylephrine for vasoconstriction; atropine or transcutaneous pacing for symptomatic bradycardia.
  • Maintain MAP target; check for triggers (suctioning, distended bladder, bowel) for autonomic dysreflexia in subacute phase.
B3d — What is the prognosis discussion with family?
  • Incomplete cord injuries generally have better recovery than complete; central cord pattern often improves with conservative management — legs > arms recovery.
  • Document ASIA grade serially; avoid premature prognostication.
  • Early rehab integration improves outcomes.
  • Anticipate respiratory complications, urinary retention, autonomic dysreflexia, pressure ulcers — set up MDT.

B4. A 7-year-old fell from a tree, GCS 13 on arrival, drops to 7 within 30 minutes. CT shows a 12 mm left temporal epidural hematoma with midline shift 8 mm.

B4a — Immediate steps?
  • Intubate using neuro-protective induction (etomidate or ketamine, rocuronium); avoid hypotension.
  • Activate neurosurgery for emergent craniotomy and evacuation — talk-and-deteriorate pattern with mass effect = surgical emergency.
  • Hyperosmolar bolus (3% NaCl 3–5 mL/kg) as bridge while moving to OR.
  • Brief controlled hyperventilation only if signs of herniation.
  • Type and cross, correct coagulopathy if present, antibiotics if open wound.
B4b — Why is rapid surgical evacuation key?
  • Epidural hematoma is typically arterial (middle meningeal artery) → rapid expansion, herniation, brainstem compression.
  • Mortality if treated promptly is low (<10%); delay → near-100% mortality with herniation.
  • Classic biconvex, lens-shaped, hyperdense CT collection that does not cross suture lines.
  • Best outcome of any acute intracranial hematoma if treated early.
B4c — Post-op ICP 22, CPP 55. Plan?
  • BTF tier-1 measures: optimize sedation, normocapnia, normothermia, HOB 30°.
  • EVD or parenchymal ICP monitor placed intra- or post-op.
  • 3% saline infusion to target Na 145–155 mmol/L.
  • Maintain MAP for CPP ≥50–60 mm Hg in school-aged child.
  • Avoid hyperventilation unless herniation; continuous EEG if concern for seizures.
B4d — Family asks about long-term outcome.
  • If evacuated early and pre-op GCS >5 with reactive pupils, the prognosis after pure epidural is often excellent.
  • Outcome depends on duration of cerebral compression, secondary injury, and presence of additional lesions.
  • Neuropsychology and rehab follow-up at 3–6 months recommended.
  • Watch for post-traumatic seizures, growing skull fracture, behavioral changes.

B5. A 13-year-old after high-speed MVC: GCS 5, blown right pupil, bradycardic, BP 180/95. CT shows right uncal herniation from large acute subdural.

B5a — Recognize the syndrome and respond.
  • Cushing's triad (hypertension, bradycardia, irregular respirations) + unilateral blown pupil = transtentorial (uncal) herniation.
  • Brief hyperventilation to PaCO₂ 30 mm Hg as rescue.
  • Hyperosmolar bolus: 3% NaCl 5 mL/kg or mannitol 1 g/kg.
  • HOB 30°, midline neck, paralysis if not already.
  • Emergent neurosurgery for craniotomy ± decompressive craniectomy.
B5b — In the OR a large craniectomy is performed. Postop ICP 30. Tier 2 plan?
  • Recheck for retained mass effect — repeat CT.
  • Continue hyperosmolar therapy with Na target 150–160 mmol/L.
  • Consider barbiturate coma with continuous EEG burst suppression; vasopressor support to maintain CPP.
  • Controlled mild hyperventilation with PbtO₂ monitoring if available.
  • Maintain Na/glucose/temperature tightly.
B5c — Day 2 develops fever 39.5°C, no obvious source. Approach?
  • Septic workup: cultures (blood, urine, tracheal aspirate), CXR.
  • Consider central fever/hypothalamic origin once infection excluded.
  • Aggressive fever control to normothermia — APAP, cooling devices; fever worsens secondary injury.
  • Re-image if neurologic change to rule out post-traumatic infarct or new collection.
  • VTE prophylaxis review.
B5d — On day 5 patient has flaccid limbs, no brainstem reflexes, no spontaneous respirations. Approach?
  • Death by neurologic criteria (brain death) clinical exam and ancillary testing per regional protocol and pediatric guidelines (Nakagawa 2011 updated 2023).
  • Two independent exams separated by minimum interval; apnea test if hemodynamically tolerated.
  • Ancillary tests if exam confounded (sedation, hypothermia, hemodynamic instability): cerebral perfusion scan, CT angiography, or EEG per protocol.
  • Communicate with family with palliative care and organ donation organization at appropriate time.
  • Document carefully; involve all required signatories per provincial pediatric brain death policy.

Block C — Randomized SAQ Drill (30 questions)

Mixed Rogers Ch 62 content. Open answers only after committing.

C1. Define primary vs secondary brain injury.

Show answer
  • Primary: mechanical damage at moment of impact — shear, contusion, axonal disruption, vascular tear; not preventable in ICU.
  • Secondary: downstream insults — hypoxia, hypotension, hypercapnia, hyperglycemia, fever, seizures, mass effect, oxidative stress, excitotoxicity — preventable/modifiable.
  • ICU mission: minimize secondary injury.

C2. Three CT findings that predict worse pediatric TBI outcome.

Show answer
  • Effacement or compression of basal cisterns.
  • Midline shift >5 mm.
  • Traumatic subarachnoid hemorrhage in basal cisterns; also diffuse axonal injury pattern; bilateral hemispheric edema.

C3. List the components of the modified pediatric Glasgow Coma Scale (verbal).

Show answer
  • 5 — coos/babbles (age appropriate).
  • 4 — irritable cry, consolable.
  • 3 — inappropriate cry, inconsistent.
  • 2 — moans/grunts to pain.
  • 1 — no response.

C4. Why is mannitol osmolarity ceiling around 320 mOsm/kg important?

Show answer
  • Beyond ~320 mOsm/kg risk of acute kidney injury and rebound intracranial hypertension.
  • Hypertonic saline preferred in modern protocols, especially if osmolality climbing.
  • BTF lists Na up to 160 mmol/L and osmolality ≤360 mOsm/kg for 3% saline.

C5. When is decompressive craniectomy considered in pediatric TBI?

Show answer
  • Refractory intracranial hypertension despite tier-1 and tier-2 measures.
  • Especially diffuse cerebral swelling without surgical hematoma.
  • Pediatric early trial (Taylor 2001) suggested benefit; adult RESCUEicp shows decreased mortality but more disability.
  • Family discussion essential; reconstruction (cranioplasty) typically at 6–12 weeks.

C6. Indications for continuous EEG monitoring after severe pediatric TBI.

Show answer
  • Unexplained altered mental status disproportionate to imaging.
  • Paralyzed/sedated patient where clinical seizure detection is impossible.
  • Refractory ICP or refractory metabolic derangements.
  • Status epilepticus or post-status to ensure no nonconvulsive seizures.
  • Barbiturate coma titration to burst suppression.

C7. List four common post-traumatic seizure risk factors.

Show answer
  • Severe TBI (GCS <9).
  • Penetrating head injury.
  • Depressed skull fracture.
  • Intracranial hemorrhage (epidural, subdural, contusion).
  • Age <2 years and abusive head trauma.

C8. First-line antiseizure prophylaxis duration after severe TBI.

Show answer
  • Levetiracetam (preferred over phenytoin in pediatric practice) for 7 days post-injury.
  • Goal: prevent early post-traumatic seizures (<7 d) — not proven to reduce late epilepsy.
  • Continue if seizures occur; otherwise stop at 7 days.

C9. Define ICP plateau wave and significance.

Show answer
  • Lundberg A wave — sustained rise in ICP >50 mm Hg lasting 5–20 minutes.
  • Indicates exhausted intracranial compliance and impending herniation.
  • Immediate action required — assess CPP, sedation, osmolar therapy, neurosurgery review.

C10. PRx interpretation: PRx 0.5 vs PRx −0.3.

Show answer
  • PRx (pressure reactivity index) = correlation of slow waves of MAP and ICP.
  • +0.5 (positive) = pressure-passive cerebrovasculature → autoregulation impaired → worse outcomes.
  • −0.3 (negative) = intact autoregulation.
  • CPPopt = CPP at which PRx is most negative (best autoregulation).

C11. Three causes of refractory pediatric ICP that an EVD will NOT solve.

Show answer
  • Diffuse cerebral edema with collapsed ventricles (no CSF to drain).
  • Mass effect from expanding hematoma — needs surgical evacuation.
  • Hyperemia from autoregulatory failure — needs blood-flow modulation (hyperventilation, blood pressure tuning).

C12. What is "talk and deteriorate" pattern?

Show answer
  • Patient initially conscious post-injury, then deteriorates → classic for epidural hematoma with lucid interval.
  • Highly time-critical — emergent neurosurgery.
  • Implies expanding intracranial mass.

C13. Differentiate epidural and subdural hematoma on CT.

Show answer
  • Epidural — biconvex/lens-shaped, hyperdense, does NOT cross suture lines, often arterial bleed (middle meningeal artery).
  • Subdural — crescentic/concave-out, follows brain surface, CROSSES suture lines, often venous bleed (bridging veins).
  • Different clinical courses: epidural rapid; subdural may evolve.

C14. Pediatric cervical spine anatomical differences from adults (4).

Show answer
  • Disproportionately large head size → flexion fulcrum at C2–C3.
  • Horizontal facet joint orientation → more translational injury.
  • Ligamentous laxity → more SCIWORA.
  • Incomplete ossification → pseudosubluxation of C2 on C3 (normal up to 8 years).

C15. What is the role of steroids in acute SCI?

Show answer
  • NASCIS protocol high-dose methylprednisolone no longer recommended in pediatric SCI — risk > benefit (infection, GI bleed, hyperglycemia).
  • AANS/CNS 2013 guideline: not recommended.
  • Pediatric guidance largely extrapolates from adult: avoid.

C16. Indications for cervical CT in pediatric trauma.

Show answer
  • Altered mental status / GCS <14.
  • Focal neurologic deficit.
  • Neck pain or midline tenderness in cooperative child.
  • High-risk mechanism (high-speed MVC, fall from height, axial loading).
  • Distracting injury or intoxication.

C17. Define autonomic dysreflexia and one trigger.

Show answer
  • Uncontrolled sympathetic surge below SCI level T6 or above causing severe hypertension, headache, sweating, bradycardia.
  • Triggers: bladder distention (most common), bowel impaction, pressure sore, ingrown nail.
  • Management: sit patient up, loosen restrictive clothing, identify and remove trigger; short-acting antihypertensive (nifedipine, GTN) if severe.

C18. Markers for impending cerebral herniation on physical exam.

Show answer
  • Pupillary asymmetry or fixed dilated pupil.
  • Cushing's triad: HTN, bradycardia, irregular respirations.
  • Posturing (decorticate, decerebrate).
  • Sudden decline in GCS >2 points.
  • Loss of brainstem reflexes (corneal, cough, gag).

C19. Goals for normoglycemia in pediatric TBI.

Show answer
  • Avoid hypoglycemia (<3.3 mmol/L) and severe hyperglycemia (>10 mmol/L).
  • Both worsen secondary injury.
  • Modest insulin infusion if persistently >10; avoid tight glycemic control given hypoglycemia risk.

C20. Cool Kids trial — design and result.

Show answer
  • Hutchison et al. NEJM 2008 — randomized controlled trial of moderate hypothermia (32–33°C) vs normothermia in pediatric severe TBI.
  • No benefit; signal of harm in hypothermia group → therapeutic hypothermia not recommended as routine treatment.
  • Fever still treated aggressively to normothermia.

C21. Three early complications of severe TBI in PICU.

Show answer
  • Refractory intracranial hypertension and herniation.
  • Post-traumatic seizures.
  • Cerebral salt wasting, SIADH, diabetes insipidus.
  • Coagulopathy and DVT.
  • Ventilator-associated pneumonia.

C22. Why is glucose-containing IV fluid avoided in early TBI resuscitation?

Show answer
  • Hyperglycemia worsens lactate production, oxidative stress, and final neurologic outcome.
  • Isotonic crystalloid (0.9% NaCl, lactated Ringer's) preferred for resuscitation.
  • Add maintenance dextrose only after acute resuscitation phase and per blood glucose.

C23. Pediatric Trauma Quality Improvement Program (TQIP) — purpose.

Show answer
  • National benchmarking platform for trauma centers to compare outcomes and process measures.
  • Drives best practices including pediatric TBI guideline adherence.
  • Reference for institutional QI projects on TBI care.

C24. Indications for tracheostomy in pediatric severe TBI.

Show answer
  • Expected prolonged ventilation (>14 d) and inability to protect airway long-term.
  • Failure to wean despite optimal medical management.
  • Discuss timing with rehab and family; balance against possibility of recovery.

C25. Imaging modality of choice for diffuse axonal injury.

Show answer
  • MRI with susceptibility-weighted imaging (SWI) and diffusion tensor imaging — sensitive for microhemorrhages and white-matter shear lesions.
  • CT often underestimates DAI; consider MRI within 72 h if clinical-imaging mismatch.

C26. Markers of poor prognosis in pediatric severe TBI.

Show answer
  • Persistent ICP >25 mm Hg.
  • CPP <40 mm Hg.
  • Bilateral fixed dilated pupils.
  • Effaced basal cisterns and midline shift >5 mm.
  • Age <4 years and abusive mechanism.

C27. Spinal shock vs neurogenic shock.

Show answer
  • Spinal shock — temporary loss of all reflexes/motor/sensory function below SCI level; days–weeks.
  • Neurogenic shock — hypotension + bradycardia from disrupted sympathetic outflow above T6; hemodynamic state.
  • Both can co-exist; treat differently — neurogenic shock needs MAP support.

C28. Define growing skull fracture.

Show answer
  • Diastatic skull fracture with underlying dural tear; pulsatile brain herniation widens fracture margins over weeks–months.
  • Almost exclusive to infants <3 years.
  • Requires surgical repair (dural reconstruction).

C29. Concussion vs mild TBI — when to discharge from ED.

Show answer
  • Mild TBI (GCS 13–15) with normal CT/PECARN low-risk can usually go home with detailed return precautions to reliable caregivers.
  • Return for vomiting >2 times, worsening headache, focal weakness, seizure, behavior change.
  • Concussion is a clinical diagnosis; CISG Berlin/Amsterdam consensus statement guides return-to-school then return-to-play protocols.

C30. Components of brain death determination in children (clinical exam).

Show answer
  • Coma with known irreversible cause.
  • Absent brainstem reflexes (pupillary, corneal, oculocephalic/oculovestibular, gag, cough).
  • Apnea test positive.
  • Two independent exams separated by required interval per age; ancillary studies if exam confounded.
  • Documented per local pediatric brain death protocol.

Block D — Randomized PICU Case Drill (35 cases)

Each case has a single best-answer summary. Treat as oral-style mini-cases.

D1. 5-year-old struck by car, GCS 5, fixed dilated right pupil. CT: right uncal herniation from acute SDH.

Show plan
  • Brief hyperventilation (PaCO₂ ~30) + hyperosmolar bolus + emergent decompressive craniotomy.
  • Pre-OR ICP control: HOB 30°, midline neck, full sedation/paralysis.

D2. 2-month-old infant, irritable, full fontanelle, retinal hemorrhages, mixed-density subdurals.

Show plan
  • Abusive head trauma workup; safeguarding; skeletal survey; ophthalmology; CT/MRI brain & spine.
  • Stabilize airway, normalize CO₂/O₂, treat ICP per BTF infant target.

D3. 8-year-old with severe TBI on day 2 develops hyponatremia 124 mmol/L, weight gain, low urine output.

Show plan
  • SIADH — fluid restriction, isotonic maintenance, recheck Na q4–6h.
  • Avoid rapid correction; hypertonic saline only if seizing or symptomatic.

D4. 10-year-old severe TBI day 4: Na 122, polyuria, urine Na 70, BUN rising.

Show plan
  • CSW — replace volume with isotonic saline and sodium; consider hydrocortisone or fludrocortisone.
  • Document urine output and fluid balance hourly.

D5. 14-year-old MVC, GCS 7, hypotensive 80/50 on arrival despite 2 L NS.

Show plan
  • Search for extracranial hemorrhage (FAST, pelvis, thigh, scalp); transfuse RBCs and activate massive transfusion protocol if needed.
  • Maintain MAP for CPP ≥60; TXA within 3 h.

D6. 7-year-old fall from bicycle, GCS 14 in ED. Mild headache. CT shows linear non-displaced parietal fracture, no intracranial blood.

Show plan
  • Observe 4–6 h, return precautions; not for ICU admission unless deteriorates.
  • No need for prophylactic antiseizures or ICP monitoring.

D7. 13-year-old diving accident, C4 SCI, ASIA A. Day 1 — BP 75/45, HR 50.

Show plan
  • Neurogenic shock: fluid then norepinephrine; MAP 85–90 for 7 days; atropine/pacing if symptomatic bradycardia.
  • Hard collar, log-roll, neurosurgery for spinal stabilization; DVT prophylaxis.

D8. 6-year-old severe TBI day 3, on pentobarbital coma, fever 39°C.

Show plan
  • Septic workup; cooling to normothermia; reassess barbiturate level.
  • Re-image if neuro change.

D9. 11-year-old severe TBI — ICP 30 despite tier 1. Family asks about decompressive craniectomy.

Show plan
  • Discuss benefits (ICP reduction, mortality reduction) vs risks (severe disability survival).
  • Pediatric Taylor RCT supports use in selected refractory cases; shared decision making.

D10. 9-year-old penetrating head injury from gunshot, GCS 4.

Show plan
  • ATC airway, hyperosmolar bolus, emergent CT and neurosurgery; broad-spectrum antibiotics; antiseizure prophylaxis 7 d.
  • Image documentation for forensic chain of custody.

D11. 3-year-old fall down stairs, GCS 13, vomited twice. PECARN risk?

Show plan
  • Age >2: vomiting alone not high-risk; observe 4–6 h; CT if symptoms progress.
  • If <2 y, vomiting + altered behavior = strong CT indication.

D12. 15-year-old football tackle, transient quadriparesis, now resolved on field.

Show plan
  • Possible cervical cord neurapraxia / transient quadriparesis; collar, MRI to rule out canal stenosis or cord edema.
  • Return to play only after specialist evaluation per CISG consensus.

D13. 2-year-old with closed head injury — develops focal motor seizure 8 h post-injury.

Show plan
  • Treat acute seizure (lorazepam), load levetiracetam, repeat imaging to exclude expanding hematoma.
  • Continuous EEG if persistent altered mental status.

D14. 9-year-old severe TBI on day 2 develops INR 1.8, platelets 70, fibrinogen 1.0.

Show plan
  • Trauma-induced coagulopathy: cryoprecipitate for low fibrinogen, FFP and platelets per TEG/ROTEM.
  • Avoid further bleeding; reverse coagulopathy before invasive procedures.

D15. 12-year-old MVC with isolated severe TBI, GCS 6, arrives 90 min post-injury.

Show plan
  • TXA per CRASH-3 (within 3 h) at 1 g load + 1 g over 8 h (or weight-based ~15 mg/kg).
  • Initiate BTF tier-1, place ICP monitor in PICU.

D16. 4-year-old TBI day 5 — sudden new R hemiparesis.

Show plan
  • Urgent CT/CTA to rule out post-traumatic arterial dissection or new infarct or hematoma.
  • Consider antiplatelet/anticoagulation if dissection — vascular neurosurgery input.

D17. Infant 6-month-old presents with seizures, lethargy. CT: bilateral SDH of differing densities. No history of trauma.

Show plan
  • AHT highly suspected; activate safeguarding; ophthalmology; skeletal survey; MRI.
  • Treat seizures, normalize physiology, social work, mandatory reporting.

D18. 10-year-old severe TBI requires barbiturate coma. Discuss monitoring.

Show plan
  • Continuous EEG to titrate to burst suppression; arterial line; vasopressor support; daily echo; cooling/feeding modifications.
  • Pentobarbital level monitoring; watch for ileus and infections.

D19. 8-year-old SCI level T6, day 10: severe headache, BP 180/120, HR 50, sweating.

Show plan
  • Autonomic dysreflexia — sit up, remove triggers (catheterize bladder, disimpact bowel).
  • Short-acting antihypertensive (nifedipine, GTN) if persistent.

D20. 14-year-old severe TBI with refractory ICP. PRx 0.7, CPPopt graph suggests CPP 70 mm Hg.

Show plan
  • Target CPP near CPPopt (≈70) using vasopressors and careful volume status.
  • If autoregulation worsens (PRx more positive), reduce CPP; monitor PbtO₂.

D21. 11-year-old TBI day 2 develops fever 40°C, no infectious source identified.

Show plan
  • Cooling to normothermia, antipyretics, surface cooling devices.
  • Septic workup mandatory; consider central fever once excluded.

D22. 5-year-old severe TBI — neurosurgery places EVD. Initial ICP 35, draining 20 mL/h CSF.

Show plan
  • Confirm EVD level (usually 10–15 cm H₂O above tragus); avoid overdrainage which collapses ventricle.
  • Sterile technique; antibiotic-impregnated catheters; daily CSF for infection screen.

D23. 7-year-old severe TBI on day 1 has PaO₂ 60 mm Hg on FiO₂ 0.6.

Show plan
  • Optimize PEEP cautiously (avoid >10 cm H₂O if ICP issues); recruitment maneuvers as appropriate.
  • Treat underlying pulmonary contusion or aspiration; maintain SpO₂ >94% and PaCO₂ 35–40.

D24. 9-year-old severe TBI — Na 142 day 1, plan for hyperosmolar?

Show plan
  • Bolus 3% NaCl 3 mL/kg for ICP spikes; titrate continuous 3% saline to Na 150–155 if recurrent spikes.
  • Monitor osmolality (<360) and serum Na q4–6h; avoid Na drops >10 in 24 h.

D25. 12-year-old SCI C5 — respiratory distress on day 2.

Show plan
  • Diaphragmatic and intercostal paresis; assist with NIV or intubation; vital capacity <15 mL/kg high-risk.
  • Active chest physio, suctioning, early consideration of tracheostomy if no diaphragmatic function.

D26. 10-year-old TBI day 7 — preparing for trach discussion; family ambivalent.

Show plan
  • Multi-disciplinary family meeting; share prognosis and goals of care; rehab and palliative care input.
  • Trach decisions individualized; bridge with cuffed ETT if uncertain.

D27. 3-year-old fall from height, ED course unremarkable, but 6 h later vomits, lethargic. CT: posterior fossa epidural hematoma.

Show plan
  • Posterior fossa hematomas can rapidly compress brainstem and obstruct CSF — urgent neurosurgery.
  • Avoid bag-valve hyperventilation that worsens venous obstruction; gentle ventilation; rapid OR.

D28. 8-year-old MVC, isolated SCI thoracic T10, no head injury. Vitals stable.

Show plan
  • MAP 85–90 mm Hg for 7 days, no steroids, neurosurgery for stabilization timing.
  • VTE prophylaxis (mechanical first; pharmacologic per protocol), bladder care, skin care.

D29. 2-year-old severe TBI — family meeting about prognosis on day 3.

Show plan
  • Integrate clinical exam (off sedation), imaging (MRI), EEG, ICP trajectory.
  • Avoid early definitive prognostication; coordinate palliative care; honest uncertainty communication.

D30. 14-year-old severe TBI — EVD blocked, ICP rising rapidly.

Show plan
  • Flush EVD per protocol; if persistent failure, neurosurgery to reposition or replace.
  • Bridge with hyperosmolar therapy and tier-2 measures while securing functional CSF drainage.

D31. 11-year-old severe TBI with bilateral pupillary dilation, age-appropriate sedation. Plan?

Show plan
  • Treat as impending herniation: hyperosmolar bolus, hyperventilation as bridge, urgent CT, surgical decompression considered.
  • Reassess pupils after sedation hold if possible to differentiate medication-induced changes.

D32. 9-year-old TBI day 4 — preparing for extubation. What criteria?

Show plan
  • Adequate GCS-M ≥5 (localizes or follows commands), gag/cough, off vasopressors, normocapnia.
  • Spontaneous breathing trial, secretion management, no upper airway issue from prolonged intubation.

D33. 5-year-old with traumatic SAH — late deterioration on day 5.

Show plan
  • Consider post-traumatic vasospasm; TCD, CTA; nimodipine controversial in pediatrics.
  • Maintain euvolemia, MAP, exclude hydrocephalus.

D34. 10-year-old AHT with bilateral retinal hemorrhages and acute subdurals — day 2 deteriorates with seizures.

Show plan
  • Treat seizures (benzodiazepine load → levetiracetam infusion); cEEG monitoring.
  • MRI brain and spine; safeguarding continues; CT to exclude evolving collection.

D35. 15-year-old severe TBI, on day 6, no brainstem reflexes, no spontaneous respirations off paralysis and sedation cleared.

Show plan
  • Initiate pediatric brain death determination protocol — two exams by qualified physicians, apnea test, ancillary if confounded.
  • Discuss with family, palliative care, and organ donation coordinator at appropriate time.
