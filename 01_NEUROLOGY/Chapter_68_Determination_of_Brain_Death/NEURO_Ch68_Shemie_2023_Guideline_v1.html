GUIDELINE SUPPLEMENT — Shemie 2023 Canadian Brain-Based Definition of Death

Primary link: Chapter 68 (The Determination of Brain Death)

Secondary link: Chapter 66 (Hypoxic-Ischemic Encephalopathy) — for 48-h post-arrest delay rule

Document type: Clinical Practice Guideline — Shemie et al., Can J Anesth/J Can Anesth, 2023

DOI: 10.1007/s12630-023-02431-4 · Endorsed by: CCCS, CMA, CACCN, CAS, Canadian Neurological Sciences Federation, Canadian Blood Services, CDTRP, CAEP, NPAC, CCCS

This supplement discusses the 2023 Canadian Clinical Practice Guideline on the brain-based definition of death and criteria for its determination after circulatory arrest or arrest of neurologic function. It is linked to the Rogers Pediatric Neurocritical Care Masterclass as a companion to Chapter 68 (Brain Death Determination). All content below is original commentary and discussion; the original guideline can be accessed via the DOI above. Pediatric age definitions per the guideline: newborns (37 wk corrected gestational age to < 2 months), infants (2 months to < 1 year), children (1 year to < 18 years).

🍁 Canadian context: This is the current Canadian standard for death determination, replacing the 2006 Canadian Forum recommendations (Shemie 2006 CMAJ) and several subsequent updates. It unifies adult and pediatric criteria under a single brain-based definition. RCPSC SEAP candidates working in Canada should follow this guideline.

Classification & Yield

  • Type: CLINICAL PRACTICE GUIDELINE — supplements DISEASE / ICU Chapter 68.
  • Yield: 🔥🔥🔥 — Highly examinable for RCPSC candidates working in Canadian centers; key distinctions vs Nakagawa 2011 (US pediatric) and Greer 2023 (unified pediatric/adult AAN-led consensus).

1 · Guideline Structure & Development

Development methodology
  • Funded by Health Canada; partnership between Canadian Critical Care Society, Canadian Blood Services, and Canadian Medical Association.
  • Reported per RIGHT Checklist (Reporting Items for Practice Guidelines in Healthcare).
  • Multidisciplinary panel: critical care nurses and physicians (adult + pediatric), radiologists, neurologists, neurointensivists, anesthesiologists, ethicists, lawyers, patient family, public partners, methodologists.
  • GRADE methodology (Grading of Recommendations, Assessment, Development, Evaluation).
  • Adapted Nominal Group Technique for consensus.
  • Seven working groups: definition of death, DNC, ancillary investigations, DCC, whole brain vs brainstem death, legal/ethical considerations, stakeholder engagement.
ICU layer: The Canadian guideline integrates ethics and legal input from the outset, reflecting medico-legal challenges that brain death determination raises in clinical practice.
Recommendation strength terminology
  • "Recommend" = strong recommendation.
  • "Suggest" = weak recommendation.
  • Foundational medical principles (FMPs) — reflect collective understanding of underpinnings for medical, legal, ethical framework; not based on formal evidence assessment but on historical and evolving concepts.
  • Good practice statements — actionable but not GRADE-rated.

2 · The Unified Brain-Based Definition of Death

Core definition
  • Death is the permanent cessation of brain function.
  • "Permanent" means brain function is lost, will not resume spontaneously, and will not be restored by intervention.
  • Characterized by:
    • Complete absence of any form of consciousness (wakefulness AND awareness).
    • Absence of brainstem reflexes, including the ability to breathe independently.
  • May result from cessation of blood flow to the brain (post-circulatory arrest) OR devastating brain injury.
  • One unified definition; two determination pathways: DCC (circulatory criteria) and DNC (neurologic criteria).
ICU layer: The unified definition reduces ambiguity. Both pathways arrive at the same destination — permanent cessation of brain function — through different routes.
What does NOT preclude death determination
  • Residual brain cell activity that is not associated with consciousness or brainstem function:
    • Posterior pituitary antidiuretic hormone (ADH) release.
    • Temperature regulation function.
    • Cellular-level neuronal activity (microscopic).
  • Spinal reflexes (triple flexion, plantar response, isolated jerks, Lazarus sign).
Trap: Preserved posterior pituitary function (no DI) does NOT exclude brain death. The brainstem function that is required to be absent is consciousness arising from the reticular activating system and the capacity to breathe — not every neural function in the cranium.
What DOES preclude death determination
  • Any level of consciousness remaining.
  • Any residual brainstem function regardless of how diminished.
RCPSC angle: If a patient demonstrates ANY brainstem reflex or any responsive consciousness, death cannot be declared. Expect examiners to test this with subtle cases (e.g., mild facial movement to stimulus, partial pupillary response).

3 · Death Determination by Circulatory Criteria (DCC)

Foundational principle
  • DCC is based on absence of extracranial circulation leading to permanent absence of intracranial (brain) circulation.
  • The biological logic: no circulation → no oxygen to brain → permanent brain death.
  • Applies primarily to potential organ donors undergoing controlled or uncontrolled donation after circulatory determination of death.
Monitoring requirements (strong recommendations)
  • Continuous invasive arterial blood pressure monitoring is the preferred method to confirm permanent cessation of circulation in potential organ donors.
  • If invasive arterial line not possible: continuous ECG monitoring acceptable (weak recommendation, moderate certainty).
  • Threshold: arterial pulse pressure ≤ 5 mm Hg (within measurement error of monitoring equipment).
  • Other noninvasive monitoring devices should NOT be used as primary confirmation.
ICU layer: The invasive arterial line distinguishes Canadian guidance — physiologic certainty is preferred over noninvasive measures that may be confounded by artifact.
Observation periods (strong recommendations)
  • Controlled DCC: 5 minutes minimum observation after circulation ceases.
  • Uncontrolled DCC: 10 minutes minimum observation.
  • Once observation period has passed without autoresuscitation, death is confirmed.
  • Variably called "hands-off" or "no-touch" period.
Maastricht Classification reference
  • Category II: Uncontrolled DCC — sudden unexpected cardiac arrest with unsuccessful resuscitation (in or out of hospital).
  • Category III: Controlled DCC — following planned withdrawal of life-sustaining measures with anticipated cardiac arrest.
  • Category V: Controlled DCC — following medical assistance in dying (MAID) with anticipated cardiac arrest.
  • Reference: Thuong et al., Transpl Int 2016.

4 · Death Determination by Neurologic Criteria (DNC)

Prerequisites (foundational medical principle)
  • Established cause of devastating brain injury severe enough to cause death.
  • Supported by neuroimaging evidence consistent with the established cause.
  • Potential confounders considered and excluded:
    • Sedatives, analgesics, neuromuscular blockers.
    • Severe metabolic / electrolyte / endocrine derangements.
    • Hypothermia.
    • Hemodynamic instability.
  • Core body temperature ≥ 36°C (weak recommendation; updated from previous ≥ 34°C threshold).
  • 48-hour delay after return of spontaneous circulation in post-cardiac-arrest patients with hypoxic-ischemic injury who do NOT have imaging evidence consistent with devastating brain injury.
Canadian update: The 48-hour post-arrest delay (without imaging evidence of devastating injury) is one of the most clinically impactful changes — links directly to Chapter 66 (HIE post-resuscitation care).
Three clinical components (foundational medical principle)
  • DNC is primarily a clinical assessment requiring ALL three of:
    1. Absence of consciousness (no wakefulness or awareness in response to stimuli).
    2. Absence of brainstem function (cranial nerve testing).
    3. Absence of capacity to breathe (formal apnea testing).
  • All components must be performed to the fullest extent possible.
  • Bilateral motor responses, bilateral brainstem reflexes, and apnea testing required.
  • Inability to perform a component only acceptable when pre-existing condition (e.g., anophthalmia), acquired condition (e.g., cervical spine injury), or safety (e.g., apnea test instability) prevents.
Number of clinical assessments (Canadian 2023)
  • Age 1 year and older (children and adults): ONE complete clinical assessment is sufficient.
  • Less than 1 year corrected gestational age: TWO complete clinical assessments separated in time.
  • Infants less than 2 months corrected gestational age: minimum 24-hour interval between two assessments advised (extendable per physician judgment).
Major Canadian departure from Nakagawa 2011 (US pediatric): Most pediatric patients ≥ 1 year now require only ONE complete clinical assessment, not two. This aligns more closely with adult practice and Greer 2023 unified guideline framework.
Pupillary assessment
  • Either quantitative pupillometry OR clinical pupil assessment is acceptable (weak recommendation).
  • Quantitative pupillometry emerging as an objective adjunct.
Oculocephalic reflex (Canadian 2023 change)
  • Strong recommendation AGAINST the addition of oculocephalic reflex testing (doll's eyes) to vestibulo-ocular (cold caloric) reflex testing.
  • VOR (cold caloric) is sufficient.
  • Rationale: avoids cervical manipulation in patients with potential c-spine injury; eliminates redundant testing.
Canadian change: Use vestibulo-ocular (cold caloric) alone — drop the doll's eyes test. This is a strong recommendation with moderate certainty in evidence.
Infratentorial brain injury
  • Clinical assessment is necessary but NOT sufficient for DNC in patients with isolated infratentorial brain injury without supratentorial involvement.
  • Ancillary investigation REQUIRED to determine death in this scenario.
  • Examples: pontine hemorrhage, brainstem stroke, isolated brainstem trauma.
RCPSC angle: Examiners often test this — a patient with brainstem stroke meeting clinical exam criteria still requires ancillary study because cerebral function above the tentorium may be preserved despite absent brainstem reflexes.

5 · Apnea Testing (Shemie 2023)

PaCO₂ threshold (weak recommendation)
  • Use arterial CO₂ partial pressure threshold ≥ 60 mm Hg AND ≥ 20 mm Hg above baseline.
  • Same as US guidance.
  • Confirms maximal stimulus to respiratory centers.
Oxygenation during test (weak recommendation)
  • Either positive pressure (CPAP) OR passive oxygenation acceptable.
  • CPAP option allows easier maintenance of oxygenation during prolonged test.
Exogenous CO₂ administration (Canadian 2023 addition)
  • Suggest using exogenously administered CO₂ for patients with high pre-test probability for cardiorespiratory instability that could prevent successful completion of the apnea test.
  • Also for patients who fail to complete the apnea test because of cardiorespiratory instability.
  • Allows controlled CO₂ rise without disconnection from ventilator.
ICU layer: Exogenous CO₂ administration is a practical innovation in this guideline — useful in cyanotic CHD, severe pulmonary disease, or ECMO patients where traditional apnea testing is difficult or unsafe.

6 · Ancillary Investigations

Foundational principle
  • Ancillary investigation alone is NOT sufficient to determine death.
  • Will NOT override a clinical assessment that is inconsistent with death.
  • Indicated when clinical exam or apnea test cannot be fully performed, or when confounders cannot be resolved.
Adult ancillary recommendations
  • Suggested (weak recommendation):
    • CT perfusion.
    • CT angiography (CTA).
    • Transcranial Doppler (TCD).
    • Radionuclide brain perfusion study with lipophilic radiopharmaceutical (with or without tomographic imaging).
  • Suggested AGAINST (weak recommendation):
    • EEG.
    • Brainstem auditory evoked potentials (BAEP).
    • Somatosensory evoked potentials (SSEP).
    • Radionuclide brain flow-only study (lipophobic radiopharmaceutical).
    • Four-vessel cerebral angiography.
    • Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI).
Canadian change: Four-vessel angiography is NO LONGER recommended as an ancillary investigation. Previously considered gold standard. EEG no longer recommended for adult ancillary either.
Pediatric ancillary recommendations
  • Suggested (weak recommendation):
    • Radionuclide brain perfusion study with lipophilic radiopharmaceutical (preferred): ⁹⁹ᵐTc-HMPAO or equivalent — incorporates both flow and parenchymal phase.
    • If lipophilic study unavailable: radionuclide brain flow study with lipophobic radiopharmaceutical (⁹⁹ᵐTc-DTPA, ⁹⁹ᵐTc-glucoheptonate, ⁹⁹ᵐTc-pertechnetate) — flow phase only.
  • Suggested AGAINST (weak recommendation):
    • EEG.
    • Transcranial Doppler (TCD).
    • BAEP, SSEP.
    • CT angiography (CTA).
    • Four-vessel cerebral angiography.
  • Infants < 2 months corrected gestational age: Suggest AGAINST performing ancillary investigation (weak recommendation, very low certainty).
Major pediatric departure: Pediatric ancillary toolkit narrows considerably. Lipophilic radionuclide perfusion is the primary modality. CTA — which IS suggested for adults — is suggested AGAINST in pediatrics. EEG also not recommended in pediatric ancillary.

7 · Good Practice Statements (DCC and DNC)

Team and process
  • Multidisciplinary support team (nurses, social workers, psychologists, spiritual care, religious officials, donor coordinators) involved as early as possible when patient may progress to death.
  • Any clinician participating in death determination must have requisite skills, training, knowledge.
  • Clinicians determining death must NOT have association or active involvement in transplant procedures, organ allocation, or care of intended recipients (firewall principle).
  • Assessment for DNC should be made in all persons with devastating brain injuries meeting criteria, regardless of organ donation potential.
Investigator qualifications
  • Physicians supervising and interpreting radiologic ancillary investigation must be trained in neuroradiology.
  • Physicians supervising and interpreting radionuclide ancillary studies must be residency-trained in nuclear medicine.
  • Physicians supervising and interpreting TCD must be trained in neurosonography with relevant experience.
  • Cause of devastating brain injury must be supported by neuroimaging consistent with the established cause.
Documentation
  • Standardized checklist should be used for death determination and documentation.
  • All phases of death determination must be clearly documented by the most responsible physician.

8 · Communication with Families / Substitute Decision Makers

Core principles
  • Consent for DNC testing should NEITHER be required NOR requested.
  • Death determination is a medical decision; family understanding and acceptance are critical for bereavement, not consent.
  • Use clear, consistent language for DNC.
  • Prepare families early for what typically happens during and after DNC.
  • Acknowledge that what families see (chest rising/falling) and feel (warm body) may make DNC challenging to understand.
  • Anticipate and explain spinal-mediated motor responses (Lazarus sign, triple flexion).
  • Repeat information over several encounters.
  • Use multimodal communication (images, written aids, family-focused materials).
  • Invite families to witness the clinical assessment.
  • Provide opportunities for family rituals at transition from life to death.
Family resistance to DNC
  • Legitimate reasons for reasonable accommodation: religious freedom, cultural beliefs, shock, grief, misunderstanding, mistrust.
  • It is generally NOT advisable to exempt patients from death determination given clinician duty to assess vital status.
  • In exceptional circumstances, brief delay (matter of days) in removing ventilation and somatic supports after DNC may be considered.
  • Engage ethics, palliative care, hospital legal where appropriate.

10 · Key Changes from Prior Canadian Practice (per Guideline)

Definition
  • Single unified definition of death = permanent cessation of brain function (whether via circulatory or neurologic pathway).
DCC changes
  • Invasive arterial BP monitoring with arterial pulse pressure ≤ 5 mm Hg is the standard.
  • ECG may be considered when arterial line not feasible.
  • Other noninvasive monitoring should not be used as primary.
  • 10-minute observation for uncontrolled DCC (previously sometimes shorter).
  • Permanent cessation of intracranial circulation must be maintained should postmortem organ donation interventions be deployed.
DNC changes
  • Cause of devastating brain injury must be supported by neuroimaging consistent with the cause (new explicit requirement).
  • 48-h delay post-ROSC for HIE patients without imaging evidence of devastating injury before performing clinical assessment.
  • Oculocephalic reflex removed from required clinical assessment (use cold caloric alone).
  • Core body temperature ≥ 36°C (upgraded from prior ≥ 34°C).
  • Apnea testing options: positive pressure (CPAP), passive oxygenation, OR exogenous CO₂ administration.
  • Infratentorial-only injury requires ancillary investigation (clarified).
  • Single clinical assessment now sufficient for adults and children ≥ 1 year.
Ancillary changes
  • Adult ancillary toolkit updated: CTA, CT perfusion, TCD, lipophilic radionuclide perfusion.
  • Four-vessel angiography no longer recommended.
  • Pediatric ancillary preferentially uses lipophilic radionuclide perfusion; lipophobic flow if unavailable.
  • No ancillary recommended for infants < 2 months corrected gestational age.
Pediatric interval
  • Minimum 24 h between clinical assessments for infants < 2 months corrected gestational age (extendable).
  • Two assessments for < 1 year corrected gestational age.
  • One assessment sufficient for ≥ 1 year.

14 · SAQs — Shemie 2023 Specific (STUDY MODE — answers visible)

SAQ 1. State the unified definition of death per Shemie 2023.

  • Permanent cessation of brain function.
  • "Permanent" = function is lost, will not resume spontaneously, will not be restored by intervention.
  • Characterized by absence of consciousness AND brainstem reflexes AND capacity to breathe.
  • Applies to all persons in all circumstances.
  • Two determination pathways: DCC (circulatory) and DNC (neurologic).

SAQ 2. List the three clinical components required for DNC.

  • Absence of consciousness (no wakefulness or awareness).
  • Absence of brainstem function (cranial nerve testing).
  • Absence of capacity to breathe (formal apnea testing).
  • All three required; performed to fullest extent possible.

SAQ 3. How many clinical assessments are required for DNC per Shemie 2023 by age?

  • ≥ 1 year (children and adults): ONE complete assessment (strong recommendation).
  • < 1 year corrected gestational age (infants): TWO complete assessments separated in time (weak recommendation).
  • < 2 months corrected gestational age (newborns): TWO assessments minimum 24-h interval (extendable per physician judgment).

SAQ 4. What is the Canadian minimum core temperature for DNC and how does this differ from previous Canadian practice?

  • ≥ 36°C (Shemie 2023, weak recommendation).
  • Updated from previous ≥ 34°C threshold.
  • Reflects need for normothermic conditions to avoid confounding clinical exam.

SAQ 5. When is a 48-hour observation period required post-ROSC before DNC assessment per Shemie 2023?

  • In patients with hypoxic-ischemic injury who do NOT have imaging evidence consistent with devastating brain injury.
  • Allows time for confounders to resolve and prevents premature determination.
  • Important link to Chapter 66 (HIE) post-resuscitation care.

SAQ 6. Why is the oculocephalic reflex removed from required clinical assessment per Shemie 2023?

  • Strong recommendation AGAINST adding oculocephalic (doll's eyes) to vestibulo-ocular reflex (cold caloric) testing.
  • VOR (cold caloric) alone is sufficient.
  • Avoids cervical manipulation in patients with potential c-spine injury.
  • Eliminates redundant testing.

SAQ 7. Apnea testing options per Shemie 2023.

  • Passive oxygenation via T-piece or catheter.
  • Positive pressure (CPAP).
  • Exogenously administered CO₂ (for unstable patients or those who fail standard test).
  • Threshold: PaCO₂ ≥ 60 AND ≥ 20 above baseline.

SAQ 8. When does Shemie 2023 require ancillary investigation for DNC?

  • Clinical assessment cannot be fully completed (anophthalmia, cervical injury, severe facial trauma, apnea test instability).
  • Confounders cannot be resolved.
  • Concern about validity of clinical assessment.
  • Isolated infratentorial brain injury without supratentorial involvement (REQUIRED).

SAQ 9. Why is ancillary REQUIRED for isolated infratentorial brain injury?

  • Supratentorial / cerebral function may be preserved despite absent brainstem reflexes.
  • Clinical exam alone tests brainstem; cannot confirm absence of cortical function.
  • "Isolated brainstem death" with preserved supratentorial perfusion does NOT meet DNC criteria.
  • Ancillary investigation must show absent supratentorial blood flow.

SAQ 10. Adult ancillary investigations recommended by Shemie 2023.

  • CT perfusion.
  • CT angiography.
  • Transcranial Doppler.
  • Radionuclide brain perfusion with lipophilic radiopharmaceutical (⁹⁹ᵐTc-HMPAO or equivalent).
  • All weak recommendations with varying evidence certainty.

SAQ 11. Adult ancillary investigations Shemie 2023 suggests against.

  • EEG.
  • Brainstem auditory evoked potentials (BAEP).
  • Somatosensory evoked potentials (SSEP).
  • Radionuclide brain flow-only study with lipophobic radiopharmaceutical.
  • Four-vessel cerebral angiography.
  • MRI.

SAQ 12. Pediatric ancillary investigations recommended by Shemie 2023.

  • Radionuclide brain perfusion with lipophilic radiopharmaceutical (preferred) — ⁹⁹ᵐTc-HMPAO or equivalent.
  • If lipophilic unavailable: lipophobic radionuclide flow study (⁹⁹ᵐTc-DTPA, ⁹⁹ᵐTc-glucoheptonate).
  • Suggested AGAINST: EEG, TCD, BAEP, SSEP, CTA, four-vessel angiography.

SAQ 13. Ancillary investigation for infants < 2 months corrected GA per Shemie 2023.

  • NOT recommended (weak recommendation, very low certainty).
  • Rely on full clinical assessment performed twice with minimum 24-h interval.
  • Reflects limited reliability of ancillary studies in this age.

SAQ 14. DCC monitoring requirements per Shemie 2023.

  • Continuous invasive arterial blood pressure monitoring (strong recommendation, moderate certainty).
  • Arterial pulse pressure ≤ 5 mm Hg (within error of measurement).
  • Continuous ECG monitoring if invasive arterial line not feasible (weak recommendation).
  • Other noninvasive monitoring should NOT be used as primary.

SAQ 15. DCC observation times per Shemie 2023.

  • Controlled DCC (Maastricht Cat III or V): minimum 5 minutes observation.
  • Uncontrolled DCC (Maastricht Cat II): minimum 10 minutes observation.
  • Both strong recommendations.
  • Confirms permanent cessation of circulation without autoresuscitation.

SAQ 16. Define autoresuscitation.

  • Unassisted resumption of spontaneous circulation after cessation.
  • Observation period in DCC is designed to detect this.
  • Absence of autoresuscitation through observation period = permanent cessation confirmed.

SAQ 17. Substitute decision maker/family consent for DNC testing per Shemie 2023.

  • Consent should NEITHER be required NOR requested.
  • Death determination is a medical decision.
  • Family understanding and acceptance are facilitated for bereavement and donation pathway, not for consent.

SAQ 18. When may removal of somatic supports after DNC be delayed for family accommodation?

  • In exceptional circumstances (e.g., religious objection).
  • Brief delay (matter of days) may be considered.
  • Engage ethics, palliative care, hospital legal.
  • Generally not advisable to exempt patients from death determination given clinician duty.

SAQ 19. Firewall between determination and donation per Shemie 2023.

  • Clinicians determining death must NOT have association or active involvement in transplant procedures, organ allocation, or care of intended recipients.
  • Reduces conflict of interest.
  • Donation discussion led by Organ Donation Organization, separate from determination team.

SAQ 20. Communication strategies for families per Shemie 2023.

  • Clear, consistent language for DNC.
  • Prepare families early for what happens during/after DNC.
  • Acknowledge that patient appearance (chest rising, warm body) may make DNC challenging to understand.
  • Anticipate and clarify spinal-mediated motor responses.
  • Repeat information over multiple encounters.
  • Use multimodal methods (images, written aids).
  • Invite families to witness clinical assessment.
  • Allow time and space for rituals.

SAQ 21. Does preserved posterior pituitary function preclude DNC per Shemie 2023?

  • No — residual cellular activity (ADH release, temperature regulation, cellular-level activity) does NOT preclude DNC.
  • The brain function that must be absent is consciousness arising from reticular activating system + brainstem reflexes + capacity to breathe.
  • Posterior pituitary hormone release is not a measure of consciousness or brainstem function.

SAQ 22. Spinal motor reflexes — significance per Shemie 2023.

  • Spinal reflexes (triple flexion, plantar response, abdominal reflex, isolated jerks, Lazarus sign, undulating toe flexion, myoclonus, "respiratory-like" movements) do NOT preclude DNC.
  • Mediated by spinal cord without transmission to/from brainstem or cerebrum.
  • Family education critical to prevent distress at these movements.

SAQ 23. Maastricht classification categories addressed by Shemie 2023.

  • Category II: Uncontrolled DCC — sudden cardiac arrest with failed resuscitation.
  • Category III: Controlled DCC — following planned withdrawal of life-sustaining measures.
  • Category V: Controlled DCC — following medical assistance in dying (MAID).

SAQ 24. What is required if any postmortem organ donation interventions are deployed?

  • Permanent cessation of intracranial (brain) circulation must be maintained.
  • Any intervention that restores brain circulation invalidates the death determination.
  • Critical for emerging organ recovery techniques (e.g., normothermic regional perfusion).

SAQ 25. Specialist requirements for ancillary investigations.

  • Radiologic ancillary: trained neuroradiologist.
  • Radionuclide ancillary: residency-trained nuclear medicine.
  • TCD: trained in neurosonography with relevant experience.
  • CT angiography: experience with correct protocol and scale.
  • CT perfusion: requisite training in performance and interpretation.

SAQ 26. What distinguishes Shemie 2023 from Nakagawa 2011 in pediatric patients ≥ 1 year?

  • Shemie 2023: ONE clinical assessment sufficient (strong recommendation).
  • Nakagawa 2011: TWO examinations required at any pediatric age.
  • Major Canadian departure aligning pediatric practice closer to adult.

SAQ 27. Define devastating brain injury per Shemie 2023.

  • Brain injury (trauma, SAH, stroke, hypoxic injury, etc.) assessed as immediate threat to life OR incompatible with good functional recovery.
  • No effective treatments remain.
  • Early limitation or withdrawal of therapy is being considered with emphasis on end-of-life care.

SAQ 28. What is "isolated brainstem death" per Shemie 2023?

  • Infratentorial brain injury for which DNC criteria are fulfilled on clinical exam.
  • Ancillary evidence may show preserved supratentorial/cerebral blood flow, perfusion, or neurophysiologic function.
  • Does NOT meet DNC criteria — requires absent supratentorial function.
  • Hence ancillary investigation is required for isolated infratentorial injury.

SAQ 29. Whole brain death definition.

  • Determination of death based on cessation of brainstem function requiring demonstration of brainstem AND cerebral involvement.
  • Standard adopted in Shemie 2023.
  • Distinguishes from "brainstem-only" death concept used in some jurisdictions (UK historically).

SAQ 30. Top 5 changes Shemie 2023 makes to Canadian practice.

  • Single unified definition of death (permanent cessation of brain function).
  • One clinical assessment sufficient for patients ≥ 1 year (down from two).
  • Oculocephalic reflex removed; cold caloric (VOR) is sufficient.
  • Core temperature ≥ 36°C (from ≥ 34°C).
  • 48-h post-arrest delay for HIE without imaging evidence of devastating injury.
  • Pediatric ancillary preference: lipophilic radionuclide perfusion.
  • No ancillary for infants < 2 months CGA.
  • Four-vessel cerebral angiography no longer recommended.
  • EEG not recommended as ancillary in adults or pediatrics.
  • Apnea test options: passive O₂, CPAP, exogenous CO₂.

15 · PICU Cases — Applying Shemie 2023 (STUDY MODE)

Case 1. 14-year-old after severe TBI from MVC, day 5. Imaging shows extensive bilateral cortical injury with herniation. Prerequisites met. In Toronto pediatric ICU.

  • Per Shemie 2023: ONE complete clinical assessment is sufficient for children ≥ 1 year.
  • Three components: absence of consciousness, brainstem function, capacity to breathe.
  • Use cold caloric (VOR) alone — oculocephalic NOT required.
  • Apnea test: passive O₂, CPAP, or exogenous CO₂; PaCO₂ ≥ 60 AND ≥ 20 above baseline.
  • If complete and consistent → declare DNC; document on standardized checklist.
  • ODO separately if donation considered.

Case 2. 3-week-old after severe HIE, in Calgary NICU. Family preparing for goodbye.

  • Per Shemie 2023: newborn < 2 months CGA requires TWO complete assessments minimum 24-h interval.
  • Ancillary investigation NOT recommended in this age.
  • Rely on full clinical exam performed twice.
  • Engage multidisciplinary team for family support; offer to witness assessment.
  • Document checklist carefully.

Case 3. 10-year-old post-cardiac arrest, day 3. CT shows diffuse cerebral edema but no clear devastating injury pattern on imaging. ROSC achieved 36 hours ago.

  • Per Shemie 2023: 48-hour delay after ROSC required when imaging does NOT show devastating injury evidence.
  • Wait until 48 hours post-ROSC before initiating clinical assessment.
  • Allows confounders (sedatives, temperature, hemodynamics) to resolve.
  • Repeat imaging may help; if devastating injury evident, 48-h delay may not apply (case-by-case).
  • Documentation includes time of ROSC and rationale for timing.

Case 4. 16-year-old with massive brainstem stroke. Clinical exam consistent with brain death; supratentorial imaging not clearly devastating.

  • Isolated infratentorial brain injury.
  • Per Shemie 2023: clinical assessment NECESSARY but NOT sufficient.
  • Ancillary investigation REQUIRED.
  • For adult: CT perfusion, CTA, TCD, or lipophilic radionuclide perfusion.
  • Confirms absence of supratentorial blood flow before DNC declaration.

Case 5. 8-year-old post-AHT in Vancouver. Clinical assessment consistent with brain death. Family from cultural background where brain death not traditionally accepted.

  • Per Shemie 2023: consent for DNC testing not required nor requested.
  • One complete clinical assessment sufficient for age ≥ 1 year.
  • Engage multidisciplinary team early (cultural liaison, chaplaincy, social work, palliative care).
  • Reasonable accommodation: brief delay (days) for family acceptance before removing somatic supports.
  • Document determination on checklist; explain process clearly to family.
  • Coordinate with safeguarding team given AHT (forensic implications).

Case 6. 12-year-old on VA-ECMO post-arrest. Suspected to meet brain death criteria. Apnea testing challenging on ECMO.

  • Reduce sweep gas flow to allow CO₂ accumulation.
  • Per Shemie 2023: exogenous CO₂ administration is an explicit option for patients who cannot tolerate standard apnea testing.
  • Threshold remains PaCO₂ ≥ 60 AND ≥ 20 above baseline.
  • If apnea test cannot be completed → ancillary investigation (lipophilic radionuclide perfusion or CTA).
  • Coordinate with ECMO team and ODO.

Case 7. Potential organ donor undergoing controlled DCC in Edmonton ICU after planned WLST.

  • Establish invasive arterial blood pressure monitoring (strong recommendation).
  • If invasive line not feasible: continuous ECG (weak recommendation).
  • Confirm arterial pulse pressure ≤ 5 mm Hg.
  • Observe 5 minutes (controlled DCC, Maastricht Category III).
  • If no autoresuscitation → confirm permanent cessation of circulation; death declared.
  • Proceed with organ recovery per donation protocol.

Case 8. 7-year-old after near-drowning, day 4 post-ROSC. Imaging shows diffuse hypoxic-ischemic injury (extensive cortical DWI restriction).

  • Imaging evidence consistent with devastating brain injury — 48-h post-ROSC delay rule does NOT mandate further waiting.
  • Verify other prerequisites: ≥ 36°C, MAP age-appropriate, sedatives metabolized.
  • Single clinical assessment (≥ 1 year) per Shemie 2023.
  • Apnea test per options (passive O₂, CPAP, or exogenous CO₂).
  • If complete and consistent → declare DNC.

Case 9. 11-year-old with cervical instability after MVC. Brain death suspected.

  • Per Shemie 2023: oculocephalic reflex (doll's eyes) NOT required.
  • Use cold caloric (VOR) alone for vestibulo-ocular testing — does not require neck movement.
  • Continue with all other brainstem reflexes and apnea test.
  • If c-spine precludes other elements: ancillary investigation indicated.

Case 10. 9-year-old with cyanotic CHD, baseline SpO₂ 75%, suspected brain death.

  • Per Shemie 2023: high pre-test probability of cardiorespiratory instability — exogenous CO₂ administration is a recommended option.
  • Allows controlled CO₂ rise without disconnection.
  • Same threshold (PaCO₂ ≥ 60 AND ≥ 20 above baseline).
  • If unable, ancillary investigation (lipophilic radionuclide perfusion).

Case 11. 15-year-old day 5 post-TBI in Halifax PICU. Core temperature 35.4°C, otherwise prerequisites met.

  • Per Shemie 2023: core temperature must be ≥ 36°C.
  • Rewarm patient gradually to ≥ 36°C before clinical assessment.
  • Once normothermic and stable, proceed with single complete assessment.
  • Document all prerequisites on checklist.

Case 12. 3-month-old in PICU after severe meningitis. Suspected brain death.

  • Age < 1 year CGA: two complete clinical assessments separated in time required.
  • Per Shemie 2023 (infants 2 mo to < 1 y): physician judgment on interval (typically > 12-24 h).
  • Ancillary investigation available in this age band (lipophilic radionuclide perfusion preferred).
  • Note: ancillary NOT recommended only for < 2 months CGA.

Case 13. Adult ICU patient with severe pulmonary disease, suspected brain death. Apnea test risk of hypoxemia high.

  • Per Shemie 2023: CPAP-supported apnea test maintains FRC and oxygenation.
  • Alternative: exogenous CO₂ administration.
  • If neither feasible → ancillary investigation (CT perfusion, CTA, TCD, or lipophilic radionuclide perfusion).

Case 14. 10-year-old day 7 post-arrest in Winnipeg PICU. Family asking why ancillary EEG is not being ordered.

  • Per Shemie 2023: EEG NOT recommended as ancillary investigation for adult OR pediatric DNC.
  • Explain to family: clinical assessment is primary; recommended pediatric ancillary is radionuclide lipophilic perfusion if needed.
  • If clinical exam is complete and consistent, no ancillary needed.

Case 15. Adult heart-failure patient in Toronto undergoing MAID. Plan for controlled DCC organ donation.

  • Maastricht Category V — controlled DCC after MAID.
  • Per Shemie 2023: invasive arterial line; pulse pressure ≤ 5 mm Hg; 5 min observation.
  • Firewall: clinicians determining death not involved in transplant team.
  • Family and ODO coordination; ethical approval per provincial regulations.

Case 16. 5-year-old with sustained DI from severe TBI, otherwise meeting DNC criteria. Does preserved posterior pituitary function preclude declaration?

  • No — per Shemie 2023, residual cellular activity (ADH release, temperature regulation) does NOT preclude DNC.
  • Required absent functions: consciousness (RAS), brainstem reflexes, capacity to breathe.
  • Posterior pituitary ADH release does not reflect these.
  • Continue with determination if other criteria met.

Case 17. Adult patient in Ottawa, suspected brain death. Documentation discussion with team.

  • Per Shemie 2023: standardized checklist should be used.
  • Document each prerequisite, clinical component, apnea test result.
  • Most responsible physician documents fulfillment.
  • Use Canadian provincial templates where available (Trillium Gift of Life Network, Canadian Blood Services).

Case 18. 12-year-old with persistent triple flexion during clinical assessment. Family distressed.

  • Per Shemie 2023: triple flexion is a spinal-mediated reflex that does NOT preclude DNC.
  • Anticipate and clarify these movements to family proactively.
  • Explain mechanism: spinal cord function alone without brainstem/cerebral input.
  • Continue with determination if all other criteria met.

Case 19. Family in Quebec requests time before withdrawal of somatic support after DNC declaration.

  • Per Shemie 2023: reasonable accommodation may be considered in exceptional circumstances.
  • Brief delay (matter of days) is acceptable.
  • Document discussions; engage ethics, palliative care.
  • Communicate ongoing process clearly.
  • Quebec has specific provincial framework — consult institutional policy.

Case 20. Pediatric patient (10 y) declared brain dead. Family agrees to donation. Hospital uses normothermic regional perfusion technique.

  • Per Shemie 2023: permanent cessation of intracranial circulation must be MAINTAINED if any postmortem organ donation interventions are deployed.
  • Any intervention that restores brain circulation invalidates the death determination.
  • Critical consideration for emerging organ recovery techniques.
  • Coordinate with ODO and transplant teams to ensure brain circulation remains absent.

17 · One-Page Infographic — Shemie 2023 At a Glance

Definition

  • Death = permanent cessation of brain function.
  • Two pathways: DCC (circulatory) and DNC (neurologic).
  • Applies to all persons in all circumstances.

DCC Essentials

  • Invasive arterial BP monitoring (preferred); ECG if not feasible.
  • Pulse pressure ≤ 5 mm Hg.
  • Observation: 5 min controlled (Cat III/V), 10 min uncontrolled (Cat II).
  • Intracranial circulation must remain absent if postmortem interventions occur.

DNC Prerequisites

  • Established irreversible cause + supporting neuroimaging.
  • Core temp ≥ 36°C.
  • Confounders excluded (sedatives, NMB, metabolic, hemodynamic).
  • 48-h post-ROSC delay if HIE without devastating imaging evidence.

DNC Clinical Components

  • Absence of consciousness.
  • Absence of brainstem function (cold caloric, NOT oculocephalic; pupillary; corneal; gag; cough; sucking/rooting in neonates).
  • Absence of capacity to breathe (apnea test).

Apnea Test

  • Threshold: PaCO₂ ≥ 60 AND ≥ 20 above baseline.
  • Options: passive O₂, CPAP, OR exogenous CO₂.

Number of Assessments (Pediatric)

  • < 2 months CGA: 2 assessments, ≥ 24 h interval (extendable); NO ancillary.
  • 2 mo – < 1 y: 2 assessments separated in time.
  • ≥ 1 year: 1 complete assessment.

Ancillary Hierarchy

  • Adult: CT perfusion, CTA, TCD, lipophilic radionuclide perfusion.
  • Pediatric: lipophilic radionuclide perfusion preferred; lipophobic flow if unavailable.
  • NOT recommended (adult or pediatric): EEG, BAEP, SSEP, 4-vessel angiography, MRI.
  • NOT recommended in pediatrics: TCD, CTA.

Red Flags

  • Isolated infratentorial injury — clinical exam alone insufficient.
  • Core temp < 36°C — rewarm first.
  • Sedatives on board — wait for clearance.
  • Post-ROSC HIE without devastating imaging — wait 48 h.
  • Family resistance — reasonable accommodation, not exemption.

RCPSC Pearls

  • "Recommend" = strong; "suggest" = weak (GRADE).
  • Foundational medical principles vs good practice statements vs PICO recommendations.
  • Firewall: determination team separate from transplant team.
  • Standardized checklist mandatory.
  • Consent not required for DNC testing.
