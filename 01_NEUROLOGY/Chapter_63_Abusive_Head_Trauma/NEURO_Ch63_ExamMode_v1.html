Chapter 63 — Abusive Head Trauma — EXAM MODE

Rogers' Textbook of Pediatric Intensive Care, Chapter 63 (Simon, Ferguson, Even, Berger). Classification: DISEASE / ICU. Adaptive distribution: 40 SAQs + 40 PICU cases. EXAM MODE: answers hidden. Time yourself in Blocks A and B.

How to use:
  • Block A — 10 timed mock RCPSC SAQs (12 minutes). Write answers first, then reveal.
  • Block B — 5 sequential oral cases (40 minutes). Self-grade using Block E rubric.
  • Block C — 30 randomized SAQs (drill mode).
  • Block D — 35 randomized PICU cases (drill mode).
  • Block E — examiner feedback rubric for self-assessment.

Block A — Mock RCPSC SAQ (10 questions, 12 minutes)

A1. Define abusive head trauma and explain why this term replaced "shaken baby syndrome."

Show answer
  • AHT = inflicted brain injury in an infant or young child from a combination of shaking, blunt impact, and hypoxia-ischemia.
  • Term endorsed by AAP 2009 (Christian & Block).
  • Rationale: integrates multiple mechanisms (not just shaking); medico-legally neutral as to specific act; less prejudicial in court testimony.

A2. List five imaging findings on a head CT or MRI that should raise concern for AHT in an infant.

Show answer
  • Bilateral subdural hematomas.
  • Interhemispheric (parafalcine) subdural hemorrhage.
  • Subdurals of differing densities (mixed-age blood).
  • Cervical canal subdural hemorrhage on spine MRI.
  • Diffuse hypoxic-ischemic injury or "big black brain" pattern out of proportion to history.
  • Loss of grey-white matter differentiation.
  • Non-linear, non-diastatic skull fracture.

A3. An 8-month-old has GCS 6 with bilateral SDH. List the BTF 2019 first-tier ICP measures and the threshold for escalation.

Show answer
  • HOB 30°, head midline.
  • Optimize sedation/analgesia ± neuromuscular blockade.
  • CSF drainage via EVD.
  • Hyperosmolar bolus: 3% NaCl 3–5 mL/kg or mannitol 0.25–1 g/kg.
  • Maintain CPP ≥40 mm Hg in this age band.
  • Escalation trigger: ICP >20 mm Hg sustained >5 min despite tier 1, or rapid neurologic decline.
  • Tier 2: barbiturate coma, controlled hyperventilation with monitoring, decompressive craniectomy.

A4. A 3-month-old presents with vomiting and lethargy. Ophthalmology shows bilateral multilayered retinal hemorrhages extending to the periphery. Explain the significance.

Show answer
  • Highly characteristic of AHT — strongly associated when bilateral, multilayered, peripheral.
  • Mechanism: shaking-induced retinal shear and retinoschisis.
  • Rare in noninflicted TBI and never with this pattern from accidental falls.
  • Document by pediatric ophthalmology with retinal photography within 72 h.
  • Triggers comprehensive AHT workup and mandatory CPS report.

A5. List the PediBIRN 4-variable AHT screening rule.

Show answer
  • Respiratory compromise (apnea, intubation on scene).
  • Bruising of ears, neck, or torso.
  • Bilateral or interhemispheric subdural fluid collection/hemorrhage.
  • Non-diastatic, non-linear skull fracture.
  • Application: in a PICU-admitted child ≤3 y with head injury; ≥1 variable → full AHT workup.
  • Sensitivity ~90%; specificity ~50% (Hymel KP et al., Pediatrics 2014).

A6. A pediatric intensivist suspects AHT but lacks diagnostic certainty. What is the reporting threshold and the legal protection framework?

Show answer
  • "Reasonable suspicion" — not certainty — triggers mandatory report.
  • All US states and Canadian provinces have mandatory reporting laws for physicians.
  • Good-faith reporting confers civil and criminal immunity (per state/provincial statute).
  • If other children at home → report immediately, do not delay.
  • Physician is NOT responsible for identifying the perpetrator — that is CPS/police role.

A7. List five mimics of AHT and one discriminating feature of each.

Show answer
  • Noninflicted TBI — history consistent with injury severity and developmental stage.
  • Glutaric aciduria type I — macrocephaly, metabolic acidosis, urine organic acids abnormal.
  • HLH — cytopenia, hyperferritinemia, splenomegaly.
  • Hemorrhagic disease of newborn — vitamin K deficiency, exclusive breastfeeding without K prophylaxis.
  • Bleeding disorders (hemophilia, vWD) — persistent or severe coagulopathy, family history.
  • Arteriovenous malformation — vascular imaging shows nidus.

A8. An ALT of 95 IU/L is noted on screening labs in a 6-month-old with suspected AHT. What is the next step and why?

Show answer
  • Obtain abdominal CT — ALT/AST >80 IU/L is the validated trigger for occult abdominal injury (Lindberg 2013).
  • Approximately one-third of abused children with abdominal injury have NO bruising or abdominal tenderness.
  • Findings (liver laceration, splenic injury, duodenal hematoma) support AHT diagnosis even if grades 1–2 don't require intervention.

A9. A 9-month-old with severe AHT is paralyzed and sedated. Why is continuous EEG monitoring essential?

Show answer
  • ~70% of severe AHT cases have seizures; 25% have nonconvulsive status epilepticus (Hasbani 2013).
  • Sedation and NMB mask clinical seizure activity.
  • Seizures contribute to secondary brain injury.
  • cEEG allows detection and titration of antiseizure therapy.
  • First-line prophylaxis: levetiracetam 40–60 mg/kg load, then 20–30 mg/kg q12h x 7 days.

A10. Why is outcome after AHT worse than after noninflicted TBI at the same admission GCS?

Show answer
  • Younger age at injury → greater developmental vulnerability.
  • Delay to medical care by perpetrator → prolonged secondary insult (hypoxia, hypoperfusion).
  • Hypoxic-ischemic component frequently superimposed.
  • Prior maltreatment / chronic injury may underlie acute presentation.
  • Recurrent abuse risk if not removed from environment.
  • Shein 2014: GCS 3 mortality 60%; GCS 9–11 mortality 9.5% (similar to severe noninflicted TBI).

Block B — Mock RCPSC Oral Cases (5 sequential, 40 minutes)

B1. A 4-month-old male is brought in by his father, who reports the baby "fell off the changing table." On arrival the infant is lethargic with GCS 9. Head circumference is at the 95th centile (was 50th at 2-month visit). Bruising is noted on the left ear pinna.

B1a — Initial 30 minutes: priorities?
  • ABCs — assess airway protection; intubate if GCS drops to 8 or signs of ICH.
  • IV access, IO if needed; isotonic resuscitation only.
  • Glucose, electrolytes, CBC, coags, ALT/AST, type and screen.
  • Non-contrast head CT immediately.
  • Recognize red flags: history of low-energy mechanism + GCS 9 + macrocephaly with rapid HC growth + ear bruising in non-mobile infant — all highly concerning for AHT.
  • Notify child protection team and social work; do not interrogate caregivers — preserve evidence.
B1b — CT shows bilateral SDH with interhemispheric extension and mild midline shift. Next steps?
  • Admit PICU; ICP monitoring discussion with neurosurgery.
  • Apply BTF tier 1: HOB 30°, sedation, normocapnia, normothermia, normoxia.
  • Hyperosmolar therapy if ICP >20 (3% NaCl 3 mL/kg bolus).
  • Initiate AHT workup: skeletal survey, MRI brain+spine, ophthalmology, full labs including factor VIII/IX, urine organic acids.
  • Mandatory CPS report — do not delay regardless of caregiver consent.
  • Notify parents of report in respectful, brief, fact-based language with social work present.
B1c — Ophthalmology reports bilateral multilayered retinal hemorrhages extending to periphery and macular retinoschisis. How does this affect the case?
  • Pattern highly characteristic of AHT (Levin, Morad).
  • Document with retinal photography.
  • Strongly supports AHT diagnosis combined with bilateral SDH.
  • Inform CPS of new findings — strengthens report.
  • Continue ICU care; this finding does not change management algorithm but informs prognosis and forensic narrative.
B1d — Skeletal survey shows posterior rib fractures and metaphyseal corner fractures of the proximal tibia. Significance and management?
  • Posterior rib fractures in pre-mobile infant = highly specific for inflicted injury (chest squeezing during shaking).
  • Metaphyseal "bucket-handle" fractures classic for inflicted twisting injury.
  • Combination diagnostic for child abuse.
  • Pediatric orthopedics consult.
  • Document with photographs; repeat skeletal survey at 10–14 days to detect occult healing fractures.
  • CPS investigation likely will place child in protective custody.
B1e — Day 5: infant is stable for discharge planning. Outline the discharge plan.
  • Multidisciplinary meeting: PICU, neurology, ophthalmology, social work, CPS, foster placement worker, primary care.
  • Medications: levetiracetam 7-day course (extend if seizures occurred).
  • Follow-up: ophthalmology in 2 weeks, neurology in 4 weeks, repeat skeletal survey at 14 days.
  • Developmental pediatric follow-up; rehab consult (>50% AHT survivors have abnormal development at 1 y).
  • Discharge destination per CPS — likely foster care or kinship placement; document safe environment.
  • GOS-E Peds outcome at 6 months and 1 year.

B2. An 8-week-old female is brought by ambulance after an apneic episode. Mother says baby "stopped breathing in her crib." On arrival, GCS 4, fixed dilated right pupil. Head CT shows large right SDH with herniation.

B2a — Immediate management?
  • Recognize impending herniation: pupillary asymmetry, GCS 4.
  • Intubate emergently using neuro-protective induction (etomidate or ketamine + rocuronium); avoid hypotension.
  • Hyperosmolar bolus: 3% NaCl 5 mL/kg over 10 min.
  • Brief controlled hyperventilation (PaCO₂ ~30) as rescue bridge.
  • HOB 30°, midline neck, paralysis.
  • Activate neurosurgery for emergent decompressive surgery.
  • Notify child protection team immediately.
B2b — Post-op, ICP 28. Tier-1 ICP management in an 8-week-old?
  • Sedation/analgesia (fentanyl + midazolam) at adequate depth.
  • NMB if persistent stimulation-driven spikes.
  • 3% NaCl infusion targeting Na 150–155 mmol/L.
  • CSF drainage if EVD placed.
  • Maintain MAP for CPP ≥40 mm Hg.
  • Normothermia, normocapnia, normoglycemia.
  • cEEG continuously.
B2c — Day 2: MRI shows extensive "big black brain" pattern with global DWI restriction. Family pleads "what is the prognosis?"
  • Multifocal leukoencephalomalacia portends very poor neurologic outcome.
  • Avoid early definitive prognostication — reassess at 72 h–7 d with serial exam off sedation, EEG, repeat imaging.
  • Communicate honestly with uncertainty; engage palliative care, social work, chaplaincy.
  • CPS investigation continues regardless of trajectory.
  • Discuss goals of care once data converges; family decision-making remains with mother unless CPS intervenes.
B2d — On day 6, infant has no spontaneous respirations off paralysis, no brainstem reflexes, no cortical activity on EEG. Plan?
  • Initiate pediatric brain death determination protocol (Nakagawa et al., updated guidelines).
  • Two independent clinical exams by qualified physicians, separated by required interval for age.
  • Apnea test if hemodynamically tolerated; ancillary testing (cerebral perfusion scan, CTA, or EEG) if exam is confounded.
  • Coordinate with forensic medicine — death of child in suspected AHT requires medical examiner notification.
  • Discuss organ donation with family at appropriate time, through Organ Donation Organization.
  • Document carefully; engage hospital legal and CPS.

B3. A 5-month-old presents with seizures. Mother and father are both present; mother reports no trauma. Initial head CT is normal. Hb 9.8 g/dL, HC at 90th centile (previously 50th).

B3a — Apply the PIBIS scoring tool.
  • Abnormal dermatologic exam: depends on physical exam findings (+2 if present).
  • Age ≥3 months: yes (+1).
  • HC >85th percentile: yes (+1).
  • Hb <11.2 g/dL: yes (+1).
  • Score ≥2 → screening head CT (already done).
  • Since CT is normal but multiple PIBIS variables positive — proceed to rapid MRI brain + spine.
B3b — Rapid MRI shows bilateral chronic SDH with small acute components. Differential and plan?
  • Top differential: AHT (bilateral SDH of different ages).
  • Differential includes glutaric aciduria type I (macrocephaly + bilateral SDH).
  • Send urine organic acids, plasma amino acids, glutarylcarnitine.
  • Skeletal survey now and at 10–14 d.
  • Ophthalmology dilated exam.
  • Coagulation workup including factor levels.
  • Mandatory CPS report — siblings status to be investigated.
B3c — Organic acid screen returns normal. Skeletal survey shows healing rib fractures. How does this resolve the diagnosis?
  • Glutaric aciduria essentially ruled out.
  • Healing rib fractures of different ages = repeated inflicted injury.
  • AHT is the diagnosis.
  • Strengthen CPS report; protective custody likely.
  • Continue medical management of seizures (lev), monitor neuro status, plan rehab follow-up.
B3d — Father requests to take baby home and refuses further workup. Manage this challenging interaction.
  • Maintain professional, respectful tone.
  • Explain medical necessity calmly; do not interrogate.
  • Involve hospital social work and child protection team.
  • CPS report already filed; emergency safeguarding orders may be in place.
  • Do not allow discharge against medical advice in suspected AHT.
  • If caregivers become threatening, security and hospital legal counsel.
  • Document all interactions verbatim.

B4. A 14-month-old in PICU with severe AHT day 3. ICP has been 25 despite tier-1 measures for 2 hours.

B4a — Discuss tier-2 escalation options.
  • Mild controlled hyperventilation (PaCO₂ 30–35) with multimodal monitoring (PbtO₂ if probe placed; if <2 y with unossified skull, PbtO₂ technically difficult).
  • Barbiturate coma — pentobarbital infusion titrated to burst suppression on cEEG; vasopressor support anticipated.
  • Decompressive craniectomy — pediatric data (Taylor 2001) supports use in refractory cases; balance ICP reduction against survival with severe disability.
  • Therapeutic hypothermia NOT recommended as routine (Cool Kids RCT negative); maintain normothermia.
  • Family meeting to discuss prognosis and goals of care.
B4b — Family asks about decompressive craniectomy outcomes.
  • Reduces ICP and may reduce mortality.
  • RESCUEicp (adult) showed reduced mortality but more disability.
  • Pediatric Taylor RCT — early benefit in selected refractory cases.
  • Cranioplasty reconstruction typically at 6–12 weeks.
  • Discuss honestly; engage palliative care and neurosurgery in shared decision.
B4c — Day 4: Na 158, polyuria 5 mL/kg/h, urine sg 1.003. Diagnosis and management?
  • Central diabetes insipidus — implies hypothalamic injury or evolving herniation.
  • Differentiate from CSW (urine Na, volume status).
  • Treatment: DDAVP IV or intranasal; replace free water deficit cautiously over 48 h.
  • Avoid rapid Na drop >10 mmol/L per 24 h (cerebral edema risk).
  • Screen anterior pituitary axes (cortisol, T4); replace if deficient.
  • Urgent reassessment of intracranial status; repeat imaging.
B4d — Day 7: ICP controlled; preparing for extubation. Criteria?
  • Adequate GCS-M ≥5 (localizes or follows commands).
  • Gag and cough reflexes present.
  • Off vasopressors; normocapnia maintained.
  • Spontaneous breathing trial passed.
  • Secretion management adequate.
  • Plan tracheostomy if no progress by 14 d (individualize).

B5. A 6-month-old admitted with AHT 2 weeks ago is now stable. CPS has placed the infant in foster care. Outline the discharge process.

B5a — Multidisciplinary discharge planning.
  • Members: PICU, neurology, ophthalmology, neurosurgery, social work, CPS caseworker, foster placement, primary care.
  • Establish safe discharge destination — foster family briefed on medical needs.
  • Document custody arrangement and emergency contact information.
B5b — Medications and follow-up.
  • Levetiracetam — continue if seizures occurred; otherwise discontinue after 7 days of prophylaxis.
  • Follow-up: ophthalmology 2 weeks, neurology 4 weeks, neurosurgery 6 weeks, repeat MRI at 3 months.
  • Developmental pediatric and rehab — early intervention referral (developmental delay expected in >50%).
  • Repeat skeletal survey at 10–14 d after first.
  • Audiology and vision baseline.
B5c — Long-term outcome assessment and counseling.
  • GOS-E Peds at 6 months and 1 year.
  • Neuropsychological assessment at 12 months.
  • School readiness assessment at age-appropriate time.
  • Long-term issues: post-traumatic epilepsy, cerebral palsy, intellectual disability, vision loss, behavioral disorders.
  • Foster family education on these expected sequelae.
B5d — Forensic and legal coordination.
  • Provide CPS and police with timely medical records when legally requested.
  • Physician may be called as a fact witness — describe medical facts, not legal conclusions.
  • Physician's role is NOT to identify perpetrator.
  • Avoid speculation in records; use objective language.
  • Engage hospital legal counsel if subpoenaed.

Block C — Randomized SAQ Drill (30 questions)

C1. Why was the term "shaken baby syndrome" replaced with "abusive head trauma"?

Show answer
  • "Shaken baby syndrome" implied a single mechanism — actual injury usually involves shaking + blunt impact + hypoxia.
  • AHT is medico-legally neutral as to specific act.
  • AAP 2009 consensus (Christian & Block) recommended the change.

C2. Cite three risk factors for AHT.

Show answer
  • Infant age <2 y, male sex, prematurity, colic.
  • Young/single parents, substance abuse, mental illness.
  • Community stress: deployment, natural disaster, economic crisis, pandemic.

C3. List the recommended evaluation components for suspected AHT.

Show answer
  • Skeletal survey + repeat at 10–14 days.
  • Brain and spine MRI (DWI/SWI).
  • Dilated ophthalmologic exam with retinal photography.
  • ALT/AST screening for abdominal injury.
  • Bleeding workup: CBC, platelets, PT/PTT, INR, fibrinogen, factor VIII/IX.
  • Urine toxicology, urine organic acids if appropriate.

C4. What is the typical retinal hemorrhage pattern in AHT?

Show answer
  • Multilayered (preretinal, intraretinal, subretinal).
  • Extends to periphery.
  • Bilateral typically.
  • Macular retinoschisis highly specific.

C5. Name three mimics of AHT.

Show answer
  • Noninflicted TBI.
  • Glutaric aciduria type I.
  • Hemorrhagic disease of newborn.
  • Hemophilia A or B, von Willebrand disease.
  • HLH, AVM.

C6. What ALT/AST cut-off triggers abdominal CT in suspected AHT?

Show answer
  • >80 IU/L (Lindberg 2013).
  • Approximately 1/3 of abused children with abdominal injury have no bruising or tenderness.

C7. CPP target by age band in pediatric severe TBI per BTF 2019.

Show answer
  • Infant/toddler: ≥40 mm Hg.
  • School-age: ≥50 mm Hg.
  • Adolescent: ≥60 mm Hg.
  • Avoid CPP <40 at any age.

C8. First-line antiseizure prophylaxis after severe AHT.

Show answer
  • Levetiracetam 40–60 mg/kg load → 20–30 mg/kg q12h.
  • Continue 7 days unless seizures occur.
  • Preferred over phenytoin in pediatrics (tolerability).

C9. Proportion of severe AHT cases presenting with seizures.

Show answer
  • ~70%; 25% have nonconvulsive status (Hasbani 2013).
  • Justifies cEEG.

C10. Why is open fontanelle no guarantee against intracranial hypertension?

Show answer
  • Once compensatory mechanisms exhaust, ICP rises.
  • Pressure-volume curve still steep beyond compensation.
  • Open fontanelle delays — does not prevent — herniation.

C11. Mortality in AHT at initial GCS 3 vs 9–11.

Show answer
  • GCS 3: ~60%.
  • GCS 9–11: ~9.5% (similar to severe noninflicted TBI).
  • Source: Shein 2014.

C12. Cervical SDH prevalence in AHT vs noninflicted TBI.

Show answer
  • AHT: >60%.
  • Noninflicted TBI: <1%.
  • Source: Choudhary et al.

C13. Define "big black brain" pattern.

Show answer
  • Diffuse multifocal leukoencephalomalacia with extensive low attenuation on CT.
  • Postulated mechanism: trigeminal vasodilation, hyperemia, BBB breakdown, neuroinflammation.
  • Very poor prognosis.

C14. PediBIRN 4-variable rule components.

Show answer
  • Respiratory compromise.
  • Bruising of ears, neck, or torso.
  • Bilateral or interhemispheric subdural collection.
  • Non-diastatic, non-linear skull fracture.

C15. PIBIS scoring variables.

Show answer
  • Abnormal dermatologic exam (+2).
  • Age ≥3 months (+1).
  • HC >85th percentile (+1).
  • Hb <11.2 g/dL (+1).
  • Cut-off: score ≥2 → CT.

C16. Why is mild PT/PTT prolongation common after AHT and not diagnostic of bleeding disorder?

Show answer
  • TBI causes consumptive coagulopathy from tissue factor release.
  • Dilutional from resuscitation fluids.
  • Bleeding disorder suggested by persistent, severe, or escalating coagulopathy.

C17. What is GOS-E Peds?

Show answer
  • Glasgow Outcome Scale Extended for Pediatrics.
  • Validated pediatric TBI outcome measure.
  • Core outcome for TBI common data elements.

C18. Hyperosmolar therapy options and ceilings in pediatric severe AHT.

Show answer
  • 3% NaCl bolus 3–5 mL/kg over 10–20 min; continuous to Na 150–160, osmolality ≤360.
  • Mannitol 0.25–1 g/kg; osmolality ceiling ~320 mOsm/kg.
  • 3% NaCl is first-line in pediatric AHT.

C19. Cool Kids RCT result.

Show answer
  • Hutchison NEJM 2008 — RCT of moderate hypothermia (32–33°C) vs normothermia in pediatric severe TBI.
  • No benefit; signal of harm in hypothermia group.
  • Therefore: maintain normothermia, not hypothermia.

C20. Should steroids be given in pediatric TBI or AHT?

Show answer
  • No — CRASH steroid trial (Edwards Lancet 2005) showed increased mortality with steroids in TBI.
  • No role in pediatric AHT.

C21. Define mandatory reporting threshold for child abuse.

Show answer
  • Reasonable suspicion — not certainty.
  • All US states and Canadian provinces require reporting by physicians.
  • Good-faith reporting confers legal immunity.

C22. Whose responsibility is it to identify the perpetrator?

Show answer
  • NOT the physician's.
  • CPS and police investigate and identify perpetrators.
  • Physician documents medical findings objectively.

C23. What language should be used in CPS reports?

Show answer
  • Plain, non-medical language ("bruise" not "ecchymosis").
  • State severity (life-threatening or not).
  • State confidence ("concern for abuse" vs "diagnostic of abuse").
  • Reports are dynamic — update as new data emerge.

C24. When should reporting be made if other children at home?

Show answer
  • Immediately — do not delay.
  • Protect siblings as priority.

C25. Indications for tracheostomy after severe AHT.

Show answer
  • Expected prolonged ventilation (>14 d) and inability to protect airway.
  • Failure to wean despite optimal medical care.
  • Discuss with rehab and family; consider neurologic recovery prospects.

C26. Why is PbtO₂ monitoring often impractical in <2 y AHT patients?

Show answer
  • Bolt fixation requires ossified skull.
  • Infants <2 y have unossified or incompletely ossified skulls — bolt may cause fracture.
  • Alternatives: NIRS-derived measures (investigational).

C27. Period of PURPLE Crying program goal.

Show answer
  • Universal parental education program from National Center on Shaken Baby Syndrome.
  • Normalizes infant crying and teaches coping strategies.
  • Cluster RCTs show reduction in AHT hospitalizations.
  • Primary prevention strategy.

C28. Why did Jenny 1999 JAMA paper become landmark in AHT literature?

Show answer
  • Documented that 31% of AHT cases were missed at initial physician contact.
  • 28% had re-injury before correct diagnosis.
  • Missed cases more likely to have neurologic deficit and death.
  • Justifies low threshold for imaging and AHT workup in vague infant presentations.

C29. Components of pediatric brain death determination per Nakagawa 2011.

Show answer
  • Coma with known irreversible cause.
  • Absent brainstem reflexes (pupillary, corneal, oculocephalic/oculovestibular, gag, cough).
  • Positive apnea test.
  • Two independent exams separated by required interval for age.
  • Ancillary studies if exam is confounded.

C30. Multidisciplinary team members essential to AHT case management.

Show answer
  • Pediatric intensivist (case lead).
  • Pediatric neurosurgery.
  • Pediatric ophthalmology.
  • Pediatric radiology.
  • Child protection / safeguarding team.
  • Social work, forensic pediatrics, hospital legal counsel.
  • Rehab, palliative care as appropriate.

  • Toxic exposure part of injury picture.

D20. 3-month-old AHT, brain death exam considered.

Show plan
  • Two independent exams + apnea test per pediatric protocol.
  • Ancillary studies if confounded.
  • Engage forensic medicine; medical examiner notification.
  • Discuss organ donation with family at appropriate time.

D21. 4-month-old AHT — preparing for extubation day 6.

Show plan
  • Confirm GCS-M ≥5, gag/cough present, off vasopressors.
  • SBT passed.
  • Plan discharge destination per CPS.

D22. 10-month-old AHT, VAP day 5.

Show plan
  • Empiric antibiotics per local antibiogram.
  • Optimize VAP bundle.
  • Continue extubation planning.

D23. 6-week-old AHT, mother sole caregiver, supportive; father in jail.

Show plan
  • Mother retains decision-making unless CPS removes custody.
  • Engage mother in care plan.
  • Provide emotional support; social work involvement.
  • CPS coordinates discharge to safe environment.

D24. 5-month-old AHT day 7, repeat MRI shows progression of leukoencephalomalacia.

Show plan
  • Worsening imaging consistent with evolving secondary injury.
  • Goals of care meeting with family.
  • Palliative care integration.

D25. 9-month-old AHT, fever 39°C day 2.

Show plan
  • Septic workup.
  • Cool aggressively to normothermia.
  • Consider central fever once infection excluded.

D26. 8-week-old AHT, family declines workup, wants to take baby home.

Show plan
  • Explain medical necessity calmly.
  • Involve social work, child protection team.
  • CPS report filed; emergency safeguarding orders.
  • Do not allow AMA discharge.

D27. 14-month-old AHT, on pentobarbital coma, hypotension.

Show plan
  • Vasopressor support (norepinephrine).
  • Maintain CPP target.
  • Daily cEEG to titrate to burst suppression.
  • Watch for ileus, infection.

D28. 3-month-old AHT, Na 126 with weight gain, low UOP.

Show plan
  • SIADH.
  • Fluid restriction with isotonic maintenance.
  • Slow correction.
  • Hypertonic saline only if symptomatic.

D29. 11-month-old AHT day 5, persistent thrombocytopenia.

Show plan
  • Rule out HLH (ferritin, fibrinogen, triglycerides, marrow).
  • Rule out ITP, sepsis, marrow suppression.
  • Continue AHT diagnostic pathway.

D30. 5-month-old AHT, organ donation discussion.

Show plan
  • Confirm brain death per pediatric protocol.
  • Engage Organ Donation Organization.
  • Family consent with cultural/religious considerations.
  • Coordinate with forensic medicine.

D31. 9-month-old AHT, foster placement; pre-discharge multidisciplinary meeting.

Show plan
  • Members: PICU, neurology, ophthalmology, social work, CPS, foster placement.
  • Establish medication and follow-up plan.
  • Document safe destination.

D32. 7-month-old AHT, family medical history of hemophilia.

Show plan
  • Send factor VIII, IX levels.
  • Hemophilia doesn't typically produce bilateral SDH and retinal hemorrhages with multilayered pattern.
  • Don't exclude AHT based on family history alone.

D33. 4-month-old AHT subpoena for medical records.

Show plan
  • Engage hospital legal counsel.
  • Provide records when legally required.
  • Physician role: fact witness — describe medical facts not legal conclusions.
  • Avoid speculation in records.

D34. 3-month-old AHT, child protection team initially concludes "accidental"; intensivist disagrees.

Show plan
  • Continue documenting findings.
  • Discuss with team — multidisciplinary review.
  • Intensivist can independently file CPS report regardless of team's conclusion.
  • Reports are dynamic — update as new findings emerge.

D35. 6-month-old AHT — long-term follow-up plan.

Show plan
  • Ophthalmology, neurology, neurosurgery, rehab.
  • Developmental pediatric follow-up.
  • GOS-E Peds at 6 m and 1 y.
  • Neuropsych at 12 m, school readiness at age-appropriate time.
  • Long-term: post-traumatic epilepsy, CP, intellectual disability, vision loss.

Top 5 examiner traps for Chapter 63
