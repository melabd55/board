CHAPTER 62 ■ Head and Spinal Cord Trauma — Key Points

Key points distilled
  • Hypotension and hypoxemia are strongly associated with poor TBI outcome — the focus of resuscitation.
  • Hypoventilation (lung or neurologic origin) is common after TBI / spinal cord injury.
  • Mechanism and pattern of injury anticipate complications — meningitis, carotid dissection, hypopituitarism.
  • Optimal trauma care is multidisciplinary — emergency, neurosurgery, intensive care, rehabilitation, orthopedic, social work.
Pages 3217–3220 · Epidemiology

Epidemiology of Pediatric Neurotrauma

TBI
  • Leading cause of death and disability in children >1 year old worldwide.
  • US — ~2.87 million TBI-related ED visits, hospitalizations, deaths annually; over 837 000 in children.
  • ~2 500 pediatric deaths per year in US.
  • ~75 % of pediatric TBIs are mild (concussion).
  • Long-term cognitive, psychological, behavioral impairments common after moderate / severe TBI.
  • Bimodal — toddlers (falls, AHT) and adolescents (MVC, sports).
Spinal cord injury
  • ~1 200 new pediatric cases per year in US.
  • Bimodal — MVC and sports / diving / trampoline.
  • Higher cervical spine vulnerability in young children (large head, weak neck musculature, elastic spine).
  • SCIWORA more common in <8 y due to elastic spine.
Pages 3221–3225 · Mechanism + Severity Classification

Mechanism of Injury

  • Direct / contact force — depressed skull fracture, focal contusion.
  • Acceleration / deceleration — coup-contrecoup contusions.
  • Rotational / torsional — diffuse axonal injury (DAI), subdural hemorrhage from bridging vein tear.
  • Blast — primary (overpressure), secondary (projectile), tertiary (displacement).
  • Penetrating — gunshot, sharp objects.
  • Inflicted (abusive head trauma) — shaking ± impact; subdurals of different ages, retinal hemorrhages, posterior rib fractures.

Pages 3226–3232 · Primary Injury Patterns

Primary Injury Patterns on Imaging

Skull fractures
  • Linear — most common; may overlie hematoma.
  • Depressed — bone fragment inward >1 cm; surgical elevation often needed.
  • Basilar — petrous, sphenoid, occipital base; CSF rhinorrhea / otorrhea; Battle / raccoon eyes; hemotympanum.
  • Diastatic — suture widening (especially in <3 y); growing fractures with leptomeningeal cyst risk.
  • Compound — overlying skin laceration; infection risk.

Diffuse Axonal Injury (DAI)

  • Mechanism — rotational / shearing forces tear axons at gray-white junction.
  • Locations — gray-white junction, corpus callosum, brainstem (worse with deeper location).
  • CT — often subtle (small petechial hemorrhages).
  • MRI SWI — sensitive (2–6× more lesions than GRE).
  • Clinical — disproportionate coma to CT findings.
  • Outcome — correlates with depth and burden of lesions.
Pages 3233–3240 · Resuscitation and Acute Management

Initial Resuscitation

ABCDE priorities
  • Airway with cervical spine protection — manual in-line stabilization; rigid collar.
  • Breathing — supplemental O₂ to SpO₂ ≥94 %; intubate if GCS ≤8, airway compromise, or impending respiratory failure.
  • Circulation — avoid hypotension at all costs (doubles mortality with single episode).
  • Disability — pupils, GCS, motor response, neurological exam.
  • Exposure — full examination for associated injuries; warm to normothermia.

Avoiding Secondary Insults

The big "Hs and Ds"
  • Hypotension — single episode doubles mortality.
  • Hypoxemia — sustained SpO₂ <90 %.
  • Hyperthermia — each 1 °C above normal worsens outcome.
  • Hyperglycemia — >180 mg/dL.
  • Hypoglycemia — <80 mg/dL.
  • Hypocapnia — sustained PaCO₂ <35.
  • Hypercapnia — PaCO₂ >45 in injured brain.
  • Hyperoxia — sustained PaO₂ >300 mmHg.
  • Seizures — convulsive and nonconvulsive.
  • Anemia — Hb <7 g/dL in severe TBI.

Intubation in TBI

  • Rapid sequence intubation with cervical spine protection.
  • Pretreatment — atropine 20 mcg/kg if <1 y (prevent reflex bradycardia); lidocaine 1 mg/kg may blunt ICP rise.
  • Induction — etomidate (hemodynamically stable) or ketamine (avoid in raised ICP traditionally, but recent data support use).
  • Paralytic — rocuronium 1 mg/kg (sugammadex reversal available); avoid succinylcholine in chronic NMD / late immobilization.
  • Confirm ETT placement; secure; head of bed 30°.
Pages 3241–3250 · ICP Management Tiered Approach

ICP / CPP-Directed Therapy

ICP monitoring indications (BTF 3rd ed)
  • Severe TBI (GCS ≤8) with abnormal admission CT.
  • Severe TBI with normal CT plus 2 of — age, motor posturing, hypotension.
  • Choose EVD (preferred when CSF drainage needed) or parenchymal probe.
Age-adjusted CPP targets
  • Infant ≥40 mmHg.
  • Toddler ≥50 mmHg.
  • Older child ≥60 mmHg.
  • Adolescent ≥70 mmHg.
  • Individualized via PRx-derived CPPopt is emerging standard.

Tier 1 Therapy

  • HOB 30°; midline head position; loosen cervical collar (allow venous return).
  • Sedation + analgesia (fentanyl, midazolam, dexmedetomidine).
  • Normothermia (36–37.5 °C); aggressive fever control.
  • Normocapnia (PaCO₂ 35–40 mmHg).
  • Normoglycemia (target 100–180 mg/dL).
  • CSF drainage via EVD if available.
  • Hyperosmolar therapy — 3 % saline 3–5 mL/kg bolus or continuous; mannitol 0.5–1 g/kg.
  • Treat seizures aggressively.

Tier 2 Therapy (refractory ICP)

  • Neuromuscular blockade — vecuronium / rocuronium with continuous EEG monitoring.
  • Brief hyperventilation — PaCO₂ 30–35 mmHg; monitor PbtO₂ for ischemia.
  • 23.4 % saline boluses.
  • Mild hypothermia 35 °C — controversial in pediatric TBI (Cool Kids trial neutral).
  • Pentobarbital coma — burst suppression target; watch hemodynamics.
  • Decompressive craniectomy — early or late (DECRA, RESCUEicp informs; specific pediatric data limited).
  • Lumbar drain rarely.

Pages 3251–3258 · Temperature, Seizure, Coagulation, TXA

Temperature Management

  • Aggressive normothermia (36–37.5 °C) for all severe TBI.
  • Each 1 °C above normal worsens outcome.
  • Therapeutic hypothermia — Cool Kids and Hypotherm pediatric trials neutral / negative.
  • Mild hypothermia (35 °C) considered for refractory ICP in tier 2; not for prophylaxis.
  • Hyperthermia management — acetaminophen, ibuprofen if no bleeding risk, external cooling.

Seizure Prophylaxis

  • Early post-traumatic seizures (within 7 days) — prophylactic AED reduces incidence (levetiracetam or phenytoin).
  • Late post-traumatic seizures — AED prophylaxis does NOT reduce incidence.
  • Duration — 7 days post-injury; longer only if seizures occur.
  • Continuous EEG for severe TBI — up to 30 % have nonconvulsive seizures.
  • Levetiracetam first-line — fewer interactions, well tolerated.

Trauma Coagulopathy

  • Acute traumatic coagulopathy — early consumptive coagulopathy after severe TBI.
  • Recognized by INR, fibrinogen, PT/PTT, platelets.
  • Goal-directed correction — PRBC : FFP : platelet ratio 1:1:1 in massive transfusion.
  • Fibrinogen replacement (cryoprecipitate) for <200 mg/dL.
  • Avoid hypothermia (worsens coagulopathy) and acidosis.

Tranexamic Acid (TXA) — CRASH-3

  • CRASH-3 trial — TXA within 3 h of TBI reduces death in mild-to-moderate cases (GCS 9–15 or 3–8 with reactive pupils).
  • Mechanism — antifibrinolytic; reduces hematoma expansion.
  • No benefit beyond 3 h or in severe injury (GCS 3 + bilateral fixed dilated pupils).
  • Pediatric extrapolation supported; specific pediatric trials emerging.
  • Dose — 1 g loading over 10 min, then 1 g over 8 h infusion (adult); pediatric ~15 mg/kg load + 2 mg/kg/h.
Pages 3259–3268 · Pituitary, Vascular, Infectious Complications

Pituitary Dysfunction After TBI

  • Up to 30 % of moderate-severe TBI develop pituitary dysfunction.
  • Acute — diabetes insipidus, adrenal insufficiency, syndrome of inappropriate ADH.
  • Chronic — GH deficiency, hypogonadism, hypothyroidism.
  • Screen with cortisol, free T4/TSH, IGF-1, gonadotropins at 3 and 12 months.
  • Basilar skull fracture, hypothalamic injury increase risk.

Carotid / Vertebral Artery Injury

  • Modified Denver / Memphis criteria — basilar skull fracture, GCS <8, focal neurologic deficit, cervical hyperextension mechanism, seat belt sign, complex midface fracture, cervical spine fracture.
  • CT angiography is screening modality.
  • Treatment — antiplatelet therapy (aspirin) for Grades I-III, endovascular intervention for high-grade.
  • Watch for delayed ischemic stroke.

Post-Traumatic Hydrocephalus

  • Communicating — blood in subarachnoid space impairs CSF reabsorption.
  • Obstructive — intraventricular hemorrhage, parenchymal hematoma compression.
  • Presentation — neurological deterioration, papilledema, deteriorating neurodevelopment.
  • Treatment — EVD acutely; VP shunt or ETV definitively.

Infection Risk

  • CSF leak after basilar skull fracture — risk of meningitis.
  • Conservative management — bed rest, head elevation, lumbar drain.
  • Empiric antibiotics if meningitis develops.
  • Open / compound fractures — empiric prophylactic antibiotics 24 h.
  • Indwelling devices (EVD, ICP monitor) — infection rate increases after 5 days.
Pages 3269–3278 · Abusive Head Trauma

Abusive Head Trauma (AHT)

Recognition
  • Most common cause of severe TBI in infants <1 year.
  • Mechanism — shaking ± impact.
  • History inconsistencies — mechanism doesn't match injury.
Findings
  • Subdural hematomas — often interhemispheric, multiple ages.
  • Retinal hemorrhages — multilayered, peripheral, in immature retina.
  • Diffuse cerebral edema, anoxic injury pattern.
  • Posterior rib fractures, metaphyseal corner ("bucket-handle") fractures on skeletal survey.
  • Bruising in non-mobile infant.
  • Old occult fractures of various ages.
Workup
  • Non-contrast head CT immediately.
  • MRI with SWI 3–5 days after CT — DAI, microhemorrhages of different ages.
  • Skeletal survey (initial + 2 weeks repeat).
  • Fundoscopy by ophthalmology.
  • Coagulation studies (rule out bleeding diathesis).
  • Report to child protection — mandatory.
  • Social work, multidisciplinary investigation, photography.
Trap
  • Differential — glutaric aciduria type 1, bleeding diathesis, accidental trauma, birth-related subdural.
  • Multidisciplinary team essential — neurosurgery, pediatrics, child abuse pediatrics, ophthalmology, radiology, social work.
Pages 3279–3290 · Spinal Cord Injury

Pediatric Spinal Cord Injury

Anatomic differences
  • Large head relative to body, weak neck musculature, elastic ligaments, wedge-shaped vertebrae, horizontal facet joints — <8 y prone to upper cervical injury.
  • SCIWORA (spinal cord injury without radiographic abnormality) — disproportionately in <8 y.
  • Pseudosubluxation of C2 on C3 normal up to age 8.

Cervical Spine Clearance

High-risk mechanisms
  • High-speed MVC.
  • Fall >3 m (or >child's height).
  • Axial load injury.
  • Focal neurological deficit.
  • Distracting injury preventing reliable examination.
  • Altered mental status.
Clearance algorithm
  • Awake, alert, no neck pain, no neurological deficit, no distracting injury — examine actively; clear clinically if no tenderness.
  • If any high-risk feature → CT cervical spine.
  • Persistent neuro deficit with normal CT → MRI cervical spine (SCIWORA).
  • Immobilize until clearance complete.

ASIA Classification

GradeDescription
AComplete — no motor or sensory function below the level
BIncomplete — sensory but no motor function below the level
CIncomplete — motor function preserved, majority of key muscles <3/5
DIncomplete — motor preserved, majority of key muscles ≥3/5
ENormal motor and sensory function

Incomplete SCI Patterns

  • Central cord syndrome — upper extremity weakness > lower; older patients with cervical spondylosis; hyperextension injury.
  • Anterior cord syndrome — motor + pain/temperature loss; vibration/proprioception preserved; anterior spinal artery infarct.
  • Brown-Séquard — ipsilateral motor + vibration loss, contralateral pain/temperature loss; hemicord injury.
  • Posterior cord syndrome — proprioception/vibration loss; rare.
  • Conus medullaris syndrome — saddle anesthesia, bowel/bladder dysfunction, mixed UMN/LMN.
  • Cauda equina syndrome — bowel/bladder, saddle anesthesia, LMN signs in legs.

Spinal Shock

  • Areflexia + flaccidity below level of injury immediately post-injury.
  • Resolves over hours to weeks as spinal reflexes return.
  • Bulbocavernosus reflex returning is a marker of spinal shock resolving.

Neurogenic Shock

  • Hypotension + bradycardia from loss of sympathetic tone (cervical / upper thoracic SCI).
  • Bradycardia distinguishes from hemorrhagic shock (tachycardic).
  • Treatment — IV fluids, vasopressors (norepinephrine, dopamine), atropine for bradycardia.
  • Goal MAP ≥85 mmHg in cervical SCI to optimize cord perfusion.
Pages 3291–3302 · SCI Management and Complications

Spinal Cord Injury Management

Acute priorities
  • Spinal immobilization until clearance.
  • Maintain MAP ≥85 mmHg for first 5–7 days (optimize cord perfusion).
  • Surgical decompression / stabilization — early (within 24 h) supported by STASCIS trial in adults.
  • Methylprednisolone — NASCIS trials controversial; current AANS/CNS guidelines say "may be considered" but no longer routinely recommended.
  • Pulmonary care — C3–C5 cord injury affects diaphragm; high cervical → permanent ventilator.

Acute SCI Complications

  • Neurogenic shock — hypotension + bradycardia.
  • Respiratory failure — diaphragm + intercostal paralysis.
  • Autonomic dysreflexia (chronic, T6 and above) — hypertension, bradycardia, headache, sweating above the level after noxious stimulus below; treat by removing trigger (catheter blockage, fecal impaction), elevate head, antihypertensives.
  • VTE — high risk; LMWH prophylaxis when bleeding risk acceptable, mechanical first.
  • Pressure injuries — strict positioning, q2h turns.
  • Heterotopic ossification — peri-articular bone formation.
  • Bladder / bowel dysfunction.
  • Spasticity — emerges over weeks–months.
Pages 3303–3313 · Long-term Outcomes + Conclusion

Long-term Outcomes

TBI
  • Pediatric Glasgow Outcome Scale Extended — Good Recovery, Moderate Disability, Severe Disability, Vegetative State, Death.
  • Cognitive deficits — executive function, attention, memory, processing speed.
  • Behavioral / psychiatric — ADHD, depression, anxiety, irritability.
  • School re-integration — academic accommodations, IEP.
  • Pediatric brain remarkably plastic — long-term improvement possible.
SCI
  • Functional outcome by ASIA grade.
  • Rehabilitation — PT, OT, speech, neuropsychology, vocational.
  • Pediatric SCI has additional considerations — growth, scoliosis (40 % develop after pediatric SCI), school re-integration, transition to adult care.
  • Long-term complications — bladder dysfunction, pressure injuries, autonomic dysreflexia, depression.

Conclusion

  • Neurotrauma care is multidisciplinary and time-sensitive.
  • Avoid secondary insults — hypotension, hypoxemia, hyperthermia, glucose dysregulation, hypocapnia, seizures.
  • Apply BTF pediatric severe TBI guidelines.
  • Recognize abusive head trauma and report.
  • Maintain MAP ≥85 mmHg in acute cervical SCI.
  • Integrate rehabilitation early.

① BTF Pediatric Severe TBI Guidelines (3rd Edition)

  • Source: CRASH-3 collaborators. Lancet. 2019;394:1713–1723
  • Link: DOI 10.1016/S0140-6736(19)32233-0
  • Key findings:
    • TXA within 3 h reduces TBI death in mild-moderate cases.
    • No benefit beyond 3 h or severe with fixed pupils.

④ Cool Kids — Pediatric Hypothermia TBI

  • Source: Adelson PD et al. Lancet Neurol. 2013;12:546–553
  • Link: DOI 10.1016/S1474-4422(13)70077-2
  • Key findings:
    • Hypothermia (32–33 °C × 48 h) did NOT improve outcomes in pediatric severe TBI.
    • Aggressive normothermia is current standard.

⑤ ATLAS / ADAPT-IT — Pediatric TBI Approaches

  • Source: Bell MJ et al. Pediatr Crit Care Med. 2017;18:S1–S98
  • Link: DOI 10.1097/PCC.0000000000001070

⑥ STASCIS — Early Decompression in Cervical SCI

  • Source: Fehlings MG et al. PLoS One. 2012;7:e32037
  • Link: DOI 10.1371/journal.pone.0032037
  • Key findings:

⑦ AANS/CNS Spinal Cord Injury Guidelines

  • Source: AANS/CNS Joint Section on Disorders of the Spine and Peripheral Nerves · 2013
  • Link: CNS Guidelines
  • Key findings:

⑧ AHA/ASA Pediatric Stroke + Vascular Trauma

⑨ Abusive Head Trauma — AAP Clinical Report

⑩ Sport-Related Concussion — CISG Berlin Consensus

5. ICU Decision Engine

IF Severe pediatric TBI (GCS ≤8)
THEN
  • Intubate, secure airway, manual cervical spine stabilization.
  • Avoid hypotension and hypoxia.
  • Non-contrast CT head + cervical spine.
  • ICP monitor (EVD preferred for CSF drainage).
  • Apply BTF tiered protocol.
  • Continuous EEG.
  • Multimodal monitoring if available (PbtO₂, NIRS, PRx).
IF Suspected acute hematoma with impending herniation
THEN
  • Hypertonic saline 3 % bolus 3–5 mL/kg or 23.4 % 0.5 mL/kg.
  • Brief hyperventilation as bridge.
  • Emergent neurosurgical consultation.
  • OR for evacuation if mass effect / herniation.
IF Mild-moderate TBI within 3 h of injury
THEN
  • Consider TXA (extrapolated from CRASH-3).
  • Pediatric dose ~15 mg/kg load + 2 mg/kg/h.
  • Not for severe injury (GCS 3 with bilateral fixed pupils).
IF Cervical spine high-risk mechanism or deficit
THEN
  • Maintain immobilization (rigid collar).
  • CT cervical spine.
  • If normal CT + persistent neuro deficit → MRI (SCIWORA).
  • Maintain MAP ≥85 mmHg if cervical SCI.
  • Early surgical decompression (within 24 h).
IF Suspected abusive head trauma
THEN
  • Stabilize patient first.
  • Head CT, then MRI 3–5 days for DAI.
  • Skeletal survey (initial + 2 weeks).
  • Fundoscopy by ophthalmology.
  • Coagulation studies.
  • Report to child protection.
  • Multidisciplinary team — child abuse pediatrics, social work, ophthalmology.
IF Neurogenic shock (hypotension + bradycardia in SCI)
THEN
  • IV fluids cautiously.
  • Vasopressors — norepinephrine or dopamine.
  • Atropine for bradycardia.
  • MAP target ≥85 mmHg.
  • Beware concomitant hemorrhagic shock — investigate other injuries.
IF Autonomic dysreflexia (chronic SCI ≥T6)
THEN
  • Sit patient up (lower head, drain blood pool).
  • Loosen tight clothing.
  • Identify and remove trigger — bladder distension (catheterize), fecal impaction (manual disimpaction with lidocaine gel), pressure injury.
  • Short-acting antihypertensive — nitrate, hydralazine, captopril.
  • Educate patient/family.

6. SAQ Bank — 40 Questions

SAQ 1. TBI severity classification by GCS.
Answer.
  • Mild — GCS 13–15 (~75 % of pediatric TBI).
  • Moderate — GCS 9–12.
  • Severe — GCS ≤8 (intubate threshold).
  • CT mandatory for moderate and severe; PECARN for mild.

Answer.
  • Neuromuscular blockade.
  • Brief hyperventilation (PaCO₂ 30–35) with PbtO₂ monitoring.
  • Mild hypothermia 35 °C.
  • Pentobarbital coma — burst suppression.
  • Decompressive craniectomy.
SAQ 6. CRASH-3 takeaway.
Answer.
  • TXA within 3 h of TBI reduces death in mild-to-moderate cases.
  • Mechanism — antifibrinolytic; reduces hematoma expansion.
  • No benefit beyond 3 h or in severe injury with bilateral fixed pupils.
  • Pediatric dose ~15 mg/kg load + 2 mg/kg/h infusion (extrapolated).
SAQ 7. Hypothermia in pediatric TBI.
Answer.
  • Cool Kids trial — 32–33 °C × 48 h did NOT improve outcomes.
  • Aggressive normothermia (36–37.5 °C) is current standard.
  • Mild hypothermia (35 °C) only for refractory ICP in Tier 2.
SAQ 8. Distinguish epidural vs. subdural hematoma on CT.
Answer.
  • Epidural — biconvex / lens-shaped; does not cross sutures; arterial (middle meningeal); often associated skull fracture; lucid interval.
  • Subdural — crescent-shaped; crosses sutures; venous (bridging vein); common in abusive head trauma.
SAQ 9. Diffuse axonal injury (DAI).
Answer.
  • Mechanism — rotational / shearing forces.
  • Locations — gray-white junction, corpus callosum, brainstem.
  • CT often subtle; MRI SWI sensitive (2–6× more lesions than GRE).
  • Clinical — disproportionate coma vs. CT findings.
  • Outcome correlates with depth and burden of lesions.
SAQ 10. Abusive head trauma findings cluster.
Answer.
  • Subdural hematomas (interhemispheric, multiple ages).
  • Retinal hemorrhages (multilayered, peripheral).
  • Posterior rib / metaphyseal corner fractures.
  • Diffuse cerebral edema / anoxic injury.
  • Inconsistent history.
  • Bruising in non-mobile infant.
SAQ 11. AHT differential.
Answer.
  • Accidental trauma.
  • Birth-related subdural.
  • Bleeding diathesis.
  • Glutaric aciduria type 1.
  • Connective tissue disorder.
  • Multidisciplinary investigation essential.
SAQ 12. SCIWORA features.
Answer.
  • Spinal cord injury without radiographic abnormality.
  • Common in <8 y — elastic spine, weak neck musculature.
  • CT / X-ray normal; MRI shows cord edema, hemorrhage, contusion.
  • Mechanism — sudden flexion / extension.
  • Spinal immobilization, MRI, neurosurgical consultation.
SAQ 13. ASIA classification of SCI.
Answer.
  • A — complete (no motor or sensory below).
  • B — incomplete (sensory but no motor).
  • C — motor preserved, majority <3/5.
  • D — motor preserved, majority ≥3/5.
  • E — normal.
SAQ 14. Incomplete SCI patterns.
Answer.
  • Central cord — UE > LE weakness; hyperextension.
  • Anterior cord — motor + pain/temperature; vibration spared.
  • Brown-Séquard — ipsilateral motor/vibration; contralateral pain/temp.
  • Posterior cord — vibration/proprioception loss; rare.
  • Conus medullaris — saddle anesthesia, bowel/bladder, mixed UMN/LMN.
  • Cauda equina — bowel/bladder, saddle anesthesia, LMN.
SAQ 15. Distinguish neurogenic vs. spinal shock.
Answer.
  • Spinal shock — areflexia + flaccidity below the level immediately post-injury; resolves hours-weeks.
  • Bulbocavernosus reflex returning marks resolution.
  • Neurogenic shock — hypotension + bradycardia from sympathetic loss (cervical / upper thoracic SCI).
  • Bradycardia distinguishes neurogenic from hemorrhagic shock.
  • Treatment — fluids, vasopressors (NE, dopamine), atropine for bradycardia.
SAQ 16. MAP target in cervical SCI.
Answer.
  • ≥85 mmHg for first 5–7 days.
  • Optimizes spinal cord perfusion.
  • Vasopressors as needed.
  • AANS/CNS guideline-endorsed.
SAQ 17. Methylprednisolone in SCI — current status.
Answer.
  • NASCIS II/III trials controversial.
  • AANS/CNS — "may be considered" but no longer routinely recommended.
  • Risk of infection, GI bleed, hyperglycemia, myopathy.
  • Discuss individually with neurosurgery.
SAQ 18. Autonomic dysreflexia.
Answer.
  • Chronic SCI ≥T6 — sympathetic outflow uninhibited.
  • Trigger — noxious stimulus below the level (catheter blockage, fecal impaction, pressure injury).
  • Features — hypertension, bradycardia, headache, sweating above the level, piloerection.
  • Treatment — sit up, loosen clothing, remove trigger, short-acting antihypertensive.
  • Untreated — stroke risk.
SAQ 19. Pediatric cervical spine anatomic differences.
Answer.
  • Large head relative to body.
  • Weak neck musculature.
  • Elastic ligaments.
  • Wedge-shaped vertebrae.
  • Horizontal facet joints.
  • Pseudosubluxation of C2 on C3 normal up to age 8.
  • SCIWORA more common in <8 y.
  • Upper cervical injuries predominate.
SAQ 20. Pituitary dysfunction after TBI.
Answer.
  • Up to 30 % of moderate-severe TBI.
  • Acute — DI, adrenal insufficiency, SIADH.
  • Chronic — GH deficiency, hypogonadism, hypothyroidism.
  • Screen at 3 and 12 months with cortisol, free T4/TSH, IGF-1, gonadotropins.
  • Basilar skull fracture and hypothalamic injury increase risk.
SAQ 21. Carotid artery injury screening criteria.
Answer.
  • Modified Denver / Memphis criteria.
  • Basilar skull fracture.
  • GCS <8 with cervical hyperextension.
  • Focal neurologic deficit.
  • Seat belt sign on neck.
  • Complex midface fracture.
  • Cervical spine fracture.
  • CTA is screening modality.
SAQ 22. Carotid artery injury treatment.
Answer.
  • Grade I-III — antiplatelet therapy (aspirin).
  • High-grade — endovascular intervention (stenting, coiling, occlusion).
  • Watch for delayed ischemic stroke (peak 7–10 days).
  • Follow-up imaging.
SAQ 23. Post-traumatic seizure prophylaxis.
Answer.
  • Early seizures (within 7 days) — prophylactic AED reduces incidence (levetiracetam or phenytoin).
  • Late seizures — prophylaxis does NOT reduce incidence.
  • Duration — 7 days post-injury.
  • Continuous EEG for severe TBI — up to 30 % nonconvulsive.
  • Levetiracetam first-line.
SAQ 24. Decompressive craniectomy.
Answer.
  • For refractory ICP >20 mmHg despite tier 1 + tier 2.
  • Pediatric Taylor et al. trial — improved outcomes.
  • Adult RESCUEicp — improved survival but disability.
  • Family discussion about quality of life outcomes.
  • Subsequent cranioplasty 6 weeks to 3 months.
SAQ 25. Concussion / mild TBI management.
Answer.
  • SCAT6 assessment.
  • Physical and cognitive rest initially (24–48 h).
  • Graduated return-to-school and return-to-play protocols.
  • Avoid second-impact syndrome — no return until full recovery.
  • Persistent symptoms >4 weeks — multidisciplinary concussion clinic.
SAQ 26. Second-impact syndrome.
Answer.
  • Repeat concussion during recovery from a prior one.
  • Catastrophic cerebral edema with high mortality.
  • Mechanism — impaired autoregulation in recovering brain.
  • Prevention — strict return-to-play protocols.
  • Counseling on long-term CTE risk.
SAQ 27. Post-traumatic hydrocephalus.
Answer.
  • Communicating — blood in subarachnoid impairs CSF reabsorption.
  • Obstructive — IVH / IPH compression.
  • Presentation — deteriorating exam, papilledema, neurodevelopmental decline.
  • Treatment — EVD acutely; VP shunt or ETV definitively.
SAQ 28. Pediatric trauma coagulopathy.
Answer.
  • Acute traumatic coagulopathy — early consumptive coagulopathy after severe TBI.
  • Monitor INR, fibrinogen, PT/PTT, platelets.
  • Goal-directed correction — PRBC : FFP : platelet 1:1:1 in massive transfusion.
  • Cryoprecipitate for fibrinogen <200 mg/dL.
  • Avoid hypothermia (worsens coagulopathy) and acidosis.
SAQ 29. Spinal immobilization in pediatric trauma.
Answer.
  • Rigid collar appropriate for age.
  • Towel rolls / pediatric-specific board (occiput offset to accommodate large head).
  • Maintain until clinical or radiographic clearance.
  • Reassess regularly.
  • Limit duration to reduce pressure injury risk.
SAQ 30. Sport concussion return-to-play protocol.
Answer.
  • Graduated stages (Berlin / Amsterdam consensus).
  • 1 — symptom-limited activity.
  • 2 — light aerobic.
  • 3 — sport-specific exercise (no head impact).
  • 4 — non-contact training drills.
  • 5 — full-contact practice.
  • 6 — return to play.
  • Each stage min 24 h; revert if symptoms recur.
SAQ 31. Glasgow Outcome Scale Extended (pediatric).
Answer.
  • Used for long-term outcome categorization.
  • Good Recovery (upper / lower).
  • Moderate Disability (upper / lower).
  • Severe Disability (upper / lower).
  • Vegetative State.
  • Death.
  • Better than original 5-point GOS for stratification.
SAQ 32. Pediatric brain plasticity in TBI recovery.
Answer.
  • Children generally recover better than adults from TBI.
  • Cortical reorganization and synaptic plasticity contribute.
  • Younger age associated with better motor recovery but variable cognitive/behavioral outcomes.
  • Multidisciplinary rehabilitation essential.
  • Long-term follow-up — academic, behavioral, psychiatric.
SAQ 33. Pediatric SCI long-term considerations.
Answer.
  • Growth — scoliosis (40 % develop after pediatric SCI).
  • Bladder / bowel program.
  • Autonomic dysreflexia (T6 and above).
  • Pressure injury prevention.
  • Spasticity management.
  • School re-integration; vocational planning.
  • Transition to adult care.
  • Psychosocial support.
SAQ 34. CSF leak after basilar skull fracture.
Answer.
  • Clinical — CSF rhinorrhea, otorrhea.
  • Confirm with β2-transferrin.
  • Conservative — bed rest, head elevation, lumbar drain.
  • Surgical repair if persistent.
  • Watch for meningitis — empiric antibiotics if signs.
  • Vaccination against encapsulated organisms (pneumococcus, Hib).
SAQ 35. Pediatric trauma resuscitation priorities.
Answer.
  • ABCDE with cervical spine protection.
  • Avoid hypotension and hypoxia at all costs.
  • Damage control resuscitation in major trauma.
  • Massive transfusion protocol if needed.
  • Identify and treat life-threatening injuries.
  • Trauma team activation.
SAQ 36. Atropine pretreatment in pediatric RSI.
Answer.
  • 20 mcg/kg IV pretreatment in <1 y.
  • Prevents reflex bradycardia during laryngoscopy.
  • Some advocate for all infants <5 y or 0.1 mg minimum dose.
  • Avoid in TBI with HR <60 from raised ICP (atropine may not help; address ICP).
SAQ 37. Ketamine in pediatric TBI RSI.
Answer.
  • Traditionally avoided due to theoretical ICP rise concerns.
  • Recent data support use — does not worsen ICP and maintains hemodynamics.
  • Useful in hemodynamically unstable TBI.
  • Dissociative anesthetic; potent analgesic.
  • Avoid in suspected raised ICP from obstructive hydrocephalus traditionally.
SAQ 38. Children with disabilities sustained from neurotrauma — transition planning.
Answer.
  • Multidisciplinary rehabilitation from acute care through outpatient.
  • Family education and support.
  • School / IEP integration.
  • Vocational planning in adolescence.
  • Transition to adult neurology / rehab / urology.
  • Long-term follow-up for surveillance complications.
SAQ 39. Glasgow Outcome Scale Extended timing.
Answer.
  • Best assessed at 6 months minimum post-injury.
  • 12 months for definitive prognosis after pediatric TBI.
  • Continue to assess long-term — recovery may extend beyond 1 year in children.
SAQ 40. Future directions in pediatric neurotrauma.
Answer.
  • Multimodal monitoring + CPPopt-guided therapy.
  • Biomarkers (S100B, GFAP, neurofilament) for severity and prognosis.
  • Pediatric-specific TXA trials.
  • Adaptive trial designs.
  • Machine learning for outcome prediction.
  • Cellular therapies (stem cells, exosomes) for SCI.
  • Targeted neuroprotection (cyclosporine, NMDA modulators).

Answer.
  • Prerequisites — known irreversible cause, correction of confounders (sedation, NMBA, hypothermia, metabolic).
  • Two separate exams by different physicians.
  • Observation period — 12 h (older), 24 h (younger).
  • Apnea test — PaCO₂ rise ≥20 mmHg above baseline and to ≥60 mmHg without respiratory effort.
  • Ancillary test (EEG, CBF scan) if apnea test not feasible.
  • Family communication and organ donation discussion.
Case 39 — Sports concussion with prolonged symptoms.
15-year-old soccer player 6 weeks post-concussion still with persistent headaches, difficulty concentrating, sleep disturbance.
Q. Approach.
Answer.
  • Post-concussion syndrome.
  • Multidisciplinary concussion clinic referral.
  • Targeted therapy — vestibular rehab, vision therapy, cognitive behavioral therapy.
  • Sleep hygiene.
  • Pharmacotherapy for specific symptoms (amitriptyline for headache, melatonin for sleep).
  • Gradual return to school.
  • Avoid sports until cleared.
Case 40 — Family meeting after devastating TBI.
Adolescent with severe TBI now 1 week into ICU; persistent vegetative state imminent; family struggling with decisions.
Q. Approach.
Answer.
  • Multimodal prognostication (clinical, EEG, MRI, SSEP, biomarkers).
  • Withhold definitive prognosis until full data and at least 72 h off sedation.
  • Family meeting with full team — intensivist, neurosurgery, neurology, palliative care, social work, chaplaincy.
  • Explore values and goals of care.
  • Honest discussion of prognosis without bias.
  • Time-limited trials for ambivalent decisions.
  • Bereavement support if withdrawal of life-sustaining therapy.

10. Final One-Page Infographic

🧠 Pediatric Head & Spinal Cord Trauma — Master Map

① Severity stratification (GCS)

  • Mild 13–15 (PECARN rule for CT).
  • Moderate 9–12 (CT mandatory; admit).
  • Severe ≤8 (intubate; ICU; BTF protocol).

② Primary vs. Secondary injury

  • Primary — at impact; not modifiable.
  • Secondary — hours to days; ICU target.
  • The Hs and Ds — hypotension, hypoxia, hyperthermia, hyper/hypoglycemia, hypocapnia, seizures.

③ Critical thresholds

  • Age-adjusted CPP — infant ≥40, toddler ≥50, child ≥60, adolescent ≥70.
  • SCI MAP target ≥85 mmHg × 5–7 days.
  • HTS 3 % 3–5 mL/kg or 23.4 % 0.5 mL/kg; mannitol 0.5–1 g/kg.
  • TXA within 3 h of TBI (CRASH-3).
  • Glucose 100–180 mg/dL.
  • Normothermia 36.0–37.5 °C.

④ Red flags

  • Pupillary asymmetry → impending herniation.
  • Cushing's triad → late raised ICP.
  • Bradycardia + hypotension after SCI → neurogenic shock.
  • Subdurals of multiple ages + retinal hemorrhages → abusive head trauma.
  • SCIWORA — normal imaging in young child does not exclude cord injury.

⑤ Exam pearls

🔥🔥🔥 Single episode of hypotension doubles mortality.
🔥🔥🔥 Aggressive normothermia, not hypothermia, in pediatric severe TBI.
🔥🔥🔥 TXA within 3 h reduces TBI mortality in mild-moderate.
🔥🔥 SCIWORA — get MRI even if CT normal.
🔥🔥 MAP ≥85 mmHg in acute cervical SCI for 5–7 days.
🔥🔥 Multidisciplinary recognition of AHT is mandatory.
🔥 Pseudosubluxation C2-C3 normal until age 8.
