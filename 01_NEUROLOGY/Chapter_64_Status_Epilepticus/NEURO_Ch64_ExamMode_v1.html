Chapter 64 — Status Epilepticus — EXAM MODE

Rogers' Textbook of Pediatric Intensive Care, Chapter 64 (Press, Wainwright, Topjian). Classification: DISEASE / ICU. Adaptive distribution: 40 SAQs + 40 PICU cases. EXAM MODE: answers hidden. Time yourself in Blocks A and B.

How to use:
  • Block A — 10 timed mock RCPSC SAQs (12 minutes).
  • Block B — 5 sequential oral cases (40 minutes). Self-grade with Block E rubric.
  • Block C — 30 randomized SAQs (drill mode).
  • Block D — 35 randomized PICU cases (drill mode).

Block A — Mock RCPSC SAQ (10 questions, 12 minutes)

A1. State the operational definition of status epilepticus and explain the ILAE 2015 t1/t2 framework.

Show answer
  • SE = continuous or intermittent seizure activity ≥5 minutes without recovery of consciousness (operational definition for treatment initiation).
  • t1 = 5 minutes — point at which a seizure is unlikely to terminate spontaneously and treatment must begin.
  • t2 = 30 minutes — point beyond which neuronal injury is likely.
  • Practical: treat any seizure of unknown duration as already in SE territory.
  • Reference: Trinka E et al. Epilepsia 2015.

A2. A 4-year-old is seizing on arrival. Outline the first 5 minutes of management.

Show answer
  • ABCs — open airway, suction, position; assess oxygenation/ventilation; supplemental O₂.
  • Bedside glucose immediately; treat hypoglycemia with D10 2–5 mL/kg if <3.3 mmol/L.
  • IV access; if no IV after 2 attempts, IO or non-IV BZD (IN/IM/buccal/rectal).
  • Lorazepam 0.1 mg/kg IV (max 4 mg) OR midazolam 0.2 mg/kg IN/IM (max 10 mg) OR buccal midazolam 0.5 mg/kg.
  • Send labs: glucose, Na, Ca, Mg, ammonia, CBC, blood gas, toxicology, ASM levels.
  • Start clock — note exact time of first BZD dose.

A3. A 5-year-old's seizure continues after two doses of lorazepam. What is the next step and what are the equipotent options?

Show answer
  • Move to second-line ASM at 15 minutes.
  • Per ESETT (Kapur NEJM 2019;381:2103): levetiracetam, fosphenytoin, and valproate are equipotent at ~45–47% efficacy.
  • Levetiracetam 40–60 mg/kg IV (max 4500 mg) over 5 min — well-tolerated, no cardiac monitoring.
  • Fosphenytoin 20 mg PE/kg IV (max 1500 mg) over 7–10 min — requires ECG monitoring.
  • Valproate 20–40 mg/kg IV (max 3000 mg) — avoid in <2 y or mitochondrial concern.
  • Choice driven by patient factors.

A4. Define refractory status epilepticus and explain the rationale for moving to anesthetic infusion.

Show answer
  • RSE = SE failing to terminate after one adequately-dosed benzodiazepine + one second-line ASM.
  • Typically reached at 40–60 minutes from seizure onset.
  • Rationale: pharmacoresistance from GABA-A receptor internalization makes further conventional ASMs less effective.
  • Anesthetic options: midazolam 0.05–2 mg/kg/h, pentobarbital 1–5 mg/kg/h, ketamine 1–10 mg/kg/h.
  • Intubate; continuous EEG; titrate to seizure cessation or burst suppression.

A5. Explain GABA-A receptor internalization in prolonged SE and its therapeutic implication.

Show answer
  • Prolonged seizure activity causes GABA-A receptors to be endocytosed (removed from the synaptic membrane).
  • Membrane density of receptors decreases → fewer benzodiazepine binding sites → progressive BZD resistance.
  • Concurrently NMDA receptors traffic to the membrane → glutamate hypersensitivity.
  • Implication: BZDs lose efficacy with each passing minute; ketamine (NMDA antagonist) becomes rational in RSE.
  • Justifies the time-critical, full-dose approach to first-line BZDs.

A6. A 4-year-old with severe AHT is paralyzed and sedated. The team asks whether continuous EEG is necessary.

Show answer
  • Yes — cEEG is essential.
  • ~70% of severe AHT patients have seizures; 25% have nonconvulsive status (Hasbani 2013).
  • Paralysis and sedation mask clinical seizure activity completely.
  • Seizures contribute to secondary brain injury.
  • Minimum 24 h per ACNS guidelines (Herman 2015); ~88% of NCSE detected by 24 h.

A7. A 7-year-old develops new-onset refractory SE after a febrile illness 5 days earlier. CSF mild pleocytosis. Autoimmune panel pending. Outline FIRES management.

Show answer
  • FIRES = febrile infection-related epilepsy syndrome (NORSE subcategory with febrile prodrome 24 h–2 wks prior).
  • Standard tier — BZD, second-line ASM, anesthetic infusion as for any RSE.
  • Ketogenic diet within 7 days of SE onset (Cervenka 2017).
  • Immunotherapy: high-dose methylprednisolone + IVIG; consider plasma exchange.
  • Targeted cytokine therapy: anakinra (IL-1Ra) or tocilizumab (IL-6 antagonist) — emerging (Lai 2020).
  • Continue exhaustive workup for autoimmune encephalitis and infections.
  • Family meeting — high mortality (12–30%) and high rate of drug-resistant epilepsy (>75% of survivors).

A8. A 3-day-old is seizing. What is the first-line ASM and why does it differ from older children?

Show answer
  • Phenobarbital 20 mg/kg IV first-line in neonates.
  • Rationale: in immature brain, NKCC1 chloride transporter predominates over KCC2 → intracellular chloride high → GABA can be depolarizing/excitatory.
  • Benzodiazepines therefore less reliable in neonates.
  • Additional 10 mg/kg may be given for persistent seizures.
  • Empiric pyridoxine 100 mg IV trial for pyridoxine-dependent epilepsy.
  • Investigate: glucose, electrolytes, sepsis (with HSV coverage), inborn errors of metabolism.

A9. A 6-year-old has had a 25-minute generalized tonic-clonic seizure. After ASMs, the child is intubated and paralyzed. GCS off paralytics 7. What is the role of cEEG and the indications for continuing it?

Show answer
  • Persistent altered mental status after CSE → suspect NCSE (18–33% incidence).
  • Initiate cEEG within 1–2 hours.
  • Apply Salzburg Criteria for NCSE diagnosis: epileptiform discharges >2.5 Hz = definite; lower frequency + clinical phenomena or ASM response = possible.
  • Continue cEEG minimum 24 h; extend to 48–72 h if high suspicion (10–12% require >24 h).
  • Treat any electrographic seizures with full-dose ASM.

A10. Outline the main determinants of outcome after pediatric SE.

Show answer
  • Underlying etiology is the primary determinant.
  • Cryptogenic/febrile SE: mortality 0–2%.
  • Acute symptomatic SE: 4–16% depending on cause.
  • FIRES: 12–30%.
  • Age <5 years carries higher mortality.
  • Longer SE duration and higher seizure burden on cEEG → worse functional outcome.
  • Long-term sequelae: drug-resistant epilepsy, cognitive impairment, behavioral disorders, mesial temporal sclerosis (FEBSTAT after febrile SE).

Block B — Mock RCPSC Oral Cases (5 sequential, 40 minutes)

B1. A 4-year-old previously well child arrives at the PED actively seizing. The seizure was witnessed to begin 25 minutes ago and the child has had 5 mg of midazolam IM by EMS.

B1a — Initial assessment and management.
  • ABCs — confirm airway, suction, jaw thrust; supplemental O₂ via NRB; SpO₂, ECG, BP monitoring.
  • IV access; if no IV in 2 attempts, IO.
  • Bedside glucose immediately; D10 2–5 mL/kg if <3.3 mmol/L.
  • Already had midazolam IM 5 mg — count as one BZD dose; give second-line ASM next (do not repeat BZD if already ≥10 mg total midazolam administered).
  • Levetiracetam 40–60 mg/kg IV over 5 min.
  • Send labs: glucose, Na, Ca, Mg, ammonia, CBC, blood gas, toxicology, ASM levels.
  • Head CT after seizure control if focal features or trauma history.
  • Prepare for intubation if respiratory depression or persistent SE.
B1b — At 40 minutes, child remains in convulsive SE despite lev 60 mg/kg. What is the diagnosis and next step?
  • Refractory status epilepticus.
  • Intubate using neuro-protective induction (etomidate or ketamine + rocuronium).
  • Start midazolam infusion 0.2 mg/kg bolus → 0.05–2 mg/kg/h.
  • Initiate continuous EEG immediately.
  • Titrate to seizure cessation or burst suppression with interburst interval 5–10 seconds.
  • Maintain MAP for adequate CPP; vasopressor support if needed.
B1c — At 6 hours, child has been weaned to midazolam 1 mg/kg/h and seizing has stopped, but bursts of electrographic seizures continue. Workup so far reveals normal CT and unrevealing initial labs.
  • Escalate to pentobarbital infusion — bolus 5 mg/kg, infusion 1–5 mg/kg/h — titrate to burst suppression.
  • OR add ketamine infusion 1–3 mg/kg bolus, 1–10 mg/kg/h — NMDA antagonism complements BZD failure.
  • Send broader workup: CSF (after stable) for routine + HSV/enterovirus PCR + autoimmune encephalitis panel; serum autoimmune panel; comprehensive metabolic screen.
  • MRI brain when stable.
  • Maintain cEEG; family meeting regarding course and prognosis.
B1d — Day 5, child remains in super-refractory SE despite midazolam + pentobarbital + ketamine. CSF shows mild pleocytosis. Manage.
  • Super-refractory SE — continuing ≥24 h on anesthetic.
  • Consider FIRES (febrile illness in preceding 2 weeks?).
  • Initiate ketogenic diet within 7 days of SE onset.
  • Empiric immunotherapy: high-dose methylprednisolone (30 mg/kg/day x 5 d) + IVIG 2 g/kg.
  • Consider anakinra (IL-1Ra) or tocilizumab (IL-6 antagonist).
  • Plasma exchange if autoimmune signal.
  • Pyridoxine 100 mg IV trial; biotinidase, ammonia, lactate.
  • Genetic panel for inborn errors and SCN1A/POLG mutations.
B1e — Day 14, repeated wean attempts fail. Family asking about outcomes.
  • SRSE carries mortality 30–50%; survivors typically with drug-resistant epilepsy and cognitive impairment.
  • Outcome is etiology-dependent — emphasize that diagnosis pursuit continues.
  • Multidisciplinary family meeting with neurology, ICU, palliative care, social work, chaplaincy.
  • Discuss goals of care openly with uncertainty acknowledged.
  • If decision made to transition to comfort care: continue ASMs for seizure symptom control; withdraw vasopressors/anesthetics per family wishes; ensure pain and anxiety control; family presence.
  • If continuing aggressive care: maintain ICU support, continue treatment escalation, plan for tracheostomy/PEG if prolonged.

B2. A 3-day-old neonate is brought to PICU with subtle myoclonic and tonic seizures. Born at term via SVD; uncomplicated pregnancy; exclusively breastfed. Today: poor feeding, jittery, then witnessed seizure.

B2a — Initial workup and first-line ASM.
  • ABCs, IV/UVC access.
  • Bedside glucose, Na, Ca, Mg.
  • Phenobarbital 20 mg/kg IV as first-line (NOT benzodiazepines in neonates).
  • Empiric pyridoxine 100 mg IV trial — pyridoxine-dependent epilepsy.
  • Septic workup including blood, urine, CSF cultures; acyclovir + ampicillin + cefotaxime empiric coverage.
  • HSV PCR on CSF.
  • Inborn errors: ammonia, lactate, amino acids, organic acids, acylcarnitine.
  • Brain MRI when stable; cEEG.
B2b — Phenobarbital fails; seizures continue at 25 min. Next steps?
  • Phenobarbital second dose 10 mg/kg.
  • Then levetiracetam 40 mg/kg IV or fosphenytoin 20 mg PE/kg IV.
  • If still refractory: midazolam infusion 0.05–0.4 mg/kg/h.
  • Continue cEEG.
  • Folinic acid trial 5 mg if pyridoxine doesn't work (folinic-acid–responsive seizures).
B2c — Ammonia returns at 280 µmol/L. Manage.
  • Hyperammonemia >200 µmol/L in neonate → suspect urea cycle disorder or organic aciduria.
  • Stop protein intake; provide D10 + intralipid for calories.
  • Sodium benzoate + sodium phenylacetate (Ammonul) IV.
  • L-arginine for urea cycle support; L-carnitine.
  • Emergent dialysis (CRRT) if ammonia >500 µmol/L or rapid rise.
  • Send plasma amino acids, urine orotic acid, organic acids.
  • Continue ASM for seizure control.
B2d — Plasma amino acids show elevated citrulline. Discuss the working diagnosis and long-term plan.
  • Elevated citrulline → citrullinemia (argininosuccinate synthetase deficiency).
  • Confirm with molecular genetic testing.
  • Long-term: protein-restricted diet, nitrogen-scavenging medications, supplementation with arginine.
  • Liver transplant may be curative.
  • Metabolic genetics, genetic counseling for family.
  • Seizure management: maintain phenobarbital; reassess long-term need.

B3. A 10-year-old presents to PED with subacute psychiatric symptoms (4 weeks): emotional lability, hallucinations, then dyskinesias and a generalized tonic-clonic seizure on arrival.

B3a — Working diagnosis and initial management.
  • Suspect anti-NMDA receptor encephalitis.
  • Treat acute seizure: lorazepam 0.1 mg/kg IV.
  • Move quickly through tier — lev or fosphenytoin if persistent.
  • Admit PICU; cEEG.
  • MRI brain with contrast — often normal or mild T2 hyperintensity in mesial temporal lobes.
  • LP after CT clearance: CSF cell count (lymphocytic pleocytosis common), protein, glucose, anti-NMDAR antibody panel.
  • Send serum and CSF autoimmune encephalitis panel (anti-NMDAR, anti-LGI1, anti-GAD65, anti-AMPA, anti-GABA-B).
B3b — CSF confirms anti-NMDAR antibodies. Discuss immunotherapy and tumor workup.
  • First-line: methylprednisolone 30 mg/kg/day IV x 5 days + IVIG 2 g/kg over 5 days OR plasma exchange.
  • Second-line: rituximab 375 mg/m²/week x 4 weeks; cyclophosphamide 750 mg/m².
  • Search for ovarian teratoma in adolescent females — pelvic US and abdominal MRI.
  • Teratoma resection essential — improves outcomes.
  • In males/young children, paraneoplastic association rarer but worth screening.
B3c — Patient progresses to NCSE on cEEG with frequent breakthroughs despite ASMs and immunotherapy. Plan?
  • Escalate to anesthetic infusion (midazolam → pentobarbital or ketamine).
  • Maintain immunotherapy; consider plasma exchange after IVIG.
  • Rituximab early if response inadequate.
  • Long ICU course expected — months in severe cases.
  • Multidisciplinary team: neurology, ICU, neurosurgery (teratoma), psychiatry, rehabilitation, palliative care.
  • Family support and counseling — prognosis can be good with prolonged treatment but requires patience.
B3d — Long-term follow-up and rehabilitation.
  • Anti-NMDAR encephalitis often has good recovery with persistent treatment.
  • Maintenance immunosuppression: rituximab or mycophenolate for 12–24 months to prevent relapse.
  • Cognitive and psychiatric rehabilitation.
  • Annual surveillance for teratoma recurrence.
  • Neuropsychological assessment for school re-entry.
  • Relapse risk ~12% — counsel family.

B4. A 6-year-old known epilepsy on lev monotherapy presents in convulsive SE. She has been intermittently non-adherent. ASM levels low. After two doses of lorazepam, seizing continues.

B4a — Continue ASM tier; how to choose second-line?
  • Per ESETT — lev, fosphenytoin, valproate equipotent.
  • Since on lev already, load to therapeutic level: additional lev 30 mg/kg IV.
  • Alternative: fosphenytoin 20 PE/kg if not on Na channel blocker.
  • Or valproate 30 mg/kg if >2 y and no mitochondrial concern.
  • Treat current crisis; address adherence after stabilization.
B4b — Seizure stops after fosphenytoin. Manage post-ictal period.
  • Observe in PICU minimum 24 h.
  • cEEG if persistent altered mental status (18–33% NCSE).
  • Maintenance ASM: load to therapeutic level; transition to oral when alert.
  • Adherence counseling with psychology/social work.
  • Identify barriers: cost, side effects, mental health, parental factors.
  • Consider long-acting formulations or pill boxes.
B4c — Family asks about adding rescue medication for future episodes.
  • Recommend intranasal midazolam 0.2 mg/kg or buccal midazolam 0.5 mg/kg as home rescue.
  • Use after any seizure ≥5 min duration.
  • Call emergency services if seizure persists ≥10 min or if recovery delayed.
  • Family education: seizure first aid, safe positioning, when to call.
  • Provide written action plan.
B4d — Discuss prevention of future SE in this patient.
  • Optimize maintenance ASM regimen — adherence is the dominant factor.
  • Consider ASM adjustment if breakthrough seizures on therapeutic levels.
  • Address triggers: sleep deprivation, illness, missed doses, alcohol/substance use in older adolescents.
  • Periodic specialist follow-up — pediatric epileptologist.
  • Surgical evaluation if drug-resistant epilepsy emerges.
  • Family adherence support; school education plan.

B5. An 8-year-old after pediatric cardiac arrest is in the PICU on therapeutic hypothermia 33°C, sedated and paralyzed. Twitching of the right face noted.

B5a — Initial action.
  • Initiate continuous EEG immediately.
  • Examine for clinical seizure features off paralytic if safe.
  • Do not wait — start cEEG and reassess.
  • Maintain hypothermia per post-arrest protocol.
B5b — cEEG shows electrographic seizures with lateralized periodic discharges on the left frontotemporal region.
  • Treat with full-dose ASM — lev 40–60 mg/kg IV.
  • If persists: fosphenytoin 20 PE/kg.
  • If still persistent: anesthetic infusion (midazolam).
  • Continue cEEG throughout cooling and rewarming.
  • Post-anoxic seizures occur in ~30% of pediatric arrest survivors.
  • Identify and treat reversible factors (electrolytes, glucose).
B5c — During rewarming, MRI shows extensive HIE with cortical and basal ganglia DWI restriction.
  • Extensive HIE pattern — poor prognosis.
  • Continue full ICU care; do not prognosticate definitively before 72 h.
  • Reassess clinical exam off sedation, EEG, repeat MRI at 5–7 days.
  • Family meeting with palliative care.
  • Continue ASMs for seizure control even if comfort measures discussed.
B5d — Family decides on continued aggressive care. Day 14, patient remains comatose with persistent NCSE on cEEG. Goals of care discussion.
  • Multidisciplinary meeting: PICU, neurology, palliative care, social work, chaplaincy, family.
  • Honest communication: severe global HIE + persistent NCSE on multiple ASMs portends very poor recovery.
  • Discuss tracheostomy/PEG vs transition to comfort care.
  • If continuing aggressive: maintain ASMs, rehab planning, long-term placement.
  • If transitioning to comfort care: continue ASMs for symptom control; withdraw vasopressors, paralytics; ensure pain/anxiety control; family presence.
  • Document discussions carefully; revisit as needed.

Block C — Randomized SAQ Drill (30 questions)

C1. List four causes of acute symptomatic pediatric SE.

Show answer
  • CNS infection (meningitis, encephalitis — HSV, enterovirus).
  • Acute brain injury (TBI, AHT, stroke, HIE).
  • Metabolic (hyponatremia, hypoglycemia, hypocalcemia, hyperammonemia).
  • Toxic (TCA, isoniazid, bupropion, cocaine).

C2. What is the ESETT trial and its main finding?

Show answer
  • Established Status Epilepticus Treatment Trial — Kapur NEJM 2019.
  • RCT comparing levetiracetam, fosphenytoin, and valproate as second-line in BZD-refractory SE.
  • Found all three equipotent (~45–47% seizure cessation at 60 min).
  • Choice now driven by patient factors.

C3. Pediatric lorazepam IV dose for SE.

Show answer
  • 0.1 mg/kg IV (max 4 mg per dose) over 2 min.
  • Repeat once at 5 min if no response.

C4. Pediatric IN/buccal/IM midazolam doses.

Show answer
  • IN: 0.2 mg/kg (max 10 mg).
  • Buccal: 0.5 mg/kg.
  • IM: 0.2 mg/kg.

C5. Neonatal SE first-line ASM.

Show answer
  • Phenobarbital 20 mg/kg IV.
  • Additional 10 mg/kg if seizing persists.
  • Rationale: GABA may be excitatory in neonates (NKCC1 dominance).

C6. When to give empiric pyridoxine in SE?

Show answer
  • Neonate or young infant with refractory SE.
  • 50–100 mg IV trial; if seizure stops, pyridoxine-dependent epilepsy.
  • Long-term oral pyridoxine therapy if positive.

C7. Levetiracetam load dose for pediatric SE.

Show answer
  • 40–60 mg/kg IV (max 4500 mg) over 5 min.
  • Renally adjusted in renal impairment.
  • Well-tolerated; no cardiac monitoring needed.

C8. Fosphenytoin load dose and monitoring requirement.

Show answer
  • 20 mg PE/kg IV (max 1500 mg) over 7–10 min.
  • Continuous ECG and BP monitoring required.
  • Avoid in cardiac conduction disease.

C9. Why avoid valproate in <2 y?

Show answer
  • Hepatotoxicity risk, especially with undiagnosed mitochondrial disease (POLG mutations).
  • Use levetiracetam or fosphenytoin instead.

C10. Define refractory SE.

Show answer
  • SE failing to terminate after one benzodiazepine + one second-line ASM.
  • Typically at 40–60 minutes.
  • Move to anesthetic infusion.

C11. Define super-refractory SE.

Show answer
  • SE continuing or recurring ≥24 h after starting anesthetic infusion.
  • Or recurrence on weaning.
  • Mortality 30–50%.

C12. Midazolam infusion dosing for RSE.

Show answer
  • Bolus 0.2 mg/kg.
  • Infusion 0.05–2 mg/kg/h titrated to seizure cessation or burst suppression.

C13. Pentobarbital infusion dosing.

Show answer
  • Bolus 5 mg/kg.
  • Infusion 1–5 mg/kg/h.
  • Titrate to burst suppression with interburst interval ≥5–10 sec.
  • Vasopressor support anticipated.

C14. Ketamine infusion in RSE — rationale and dose.

Show answer
  • NMDA antagonist — counters glutamate hypersensitivity from NMDA receptor membrane trafficking.
  • Bolus 1–3 mg/kg → infusion 1–10 mg/kg/h.
  • Preserves hemodynamics — useful in hypotensive RSE.

C15. Propofol infusion syndrome — features and risk factors.

Show answer
  • Metabolic acidosis, rhabdomyolysis, hyperkalemia, hypertriglyceridemia, cardiac failure.
  • Risk factors: doses >4 mg/kg/h, duration >48 h, younger age.
  • Limit propofol use in pediatric RSE.

C16. When to initiate ketogenic diet in SRSE?

Show answer
  • Within 7 days of SE onset.
  • Cervenka 2017 — 60% achieved SE resolution.
  • Useful in FIRES, mitochondrial disease, GLUT1 deficiency.
  • Aim for ketosis (β-hydroxybutyrate >2 mmol/L).

C17. Anakinra mechanism and use in FIRES.

Show answer
  • IL-1 receptor antagonist.
  • Counters cytokine storm hypothesized in FIRES.
  • Lai 2020 — earlier initiation may improve outcomes.
  • Off-label use; case series and registry data.

C18. Define NORSE.

Show answer
  • New-Onset Refractory Status Epilepticus in patient without prior epilepsy or active neurologic disease.
  • No clear cause identified within 72 h of presentation.
  • FIRES is a subcategory with febrile illness 24 h–2 wks prior.

C19. NCSE incidence in PICU patients with altered mental status.

Show answer
  • 18–33% of children with unexplained altered consciousness or prolonged postictal state.
  • Higher in younger children and after acute brain injury.

C20. Salzburg Criteria — main components.

Show answer
  • Definite NCSE: epileptiform discharges >2.5 Hz on EEG.
  • Possible NCSE: rhythmic/periodic discharges <2.5 Hz + clinical/EEG improvement after IV ASM.
  • Sensitivity 97.7%, specificity 89.6% (Leitinger 2016).

C21. ACNS recommended cEEG duration.

Show answer
  • Minimum 24 h.
  • ~88% of NCSE detected by 24 h.
  • Extend to 48–72 h if high suspicion (~10–12% require longer).

C22. FEBSTAT — key findings.

Show answer
  • HHV-6 viremia in ~30% of febrile SE cases.
  • Acute hippocampal abnormalities in ~10–20%.
  • Mesial temporal sclerosis on follow-up in ~30%.
  • Justifies follow-up imaging at 6–12 months.

C23. Anti-NMDAR encephalitis — pediatric presentation.

Show answer
  • Subacute behavioral/personality change, psychosis, dyskinesia.
  • Seizures and SE common.
  • Autonomic instability.
  • CSF anti-NMDAR antibodies; lymphocytic pleocytosis.
  • Search for ovarian teratoma (adolescent females).

C24. First-line immunotherapy in autoimmune SE.

Show answer
  • High-dose methylprednisolone 30 mg/kg/day x 5 days.
  • IVIG 2 g/kg total over 2–5 days.
  • Plasma exchange as alternative or addition.
  • Second-line: rituximab, cyclophosphamide.

C25. TCA-induced SE — specific therapy beyond standard ASM.

Show answer
  • Sodium bicarbonate 1–2 mEq/kg IV bolus for QRS widening >100 ms.
  • Avoid phenytoin (Na channel blockade compounds TCA toxicity).
  • Use lev or valproate as second-line.

C26. Isoniazid-induced seizures — specific antidote.

Show answer
  • Pyridoxine 70 mg/kg IV (1 g pyridoxine per gram INH ingested).
  • BZD as adjunct.

C27. Dravet syndrome — ASM to avoid.

Show answer
  • SCN1A mutation → avoid Na channel blockers (phenytoin, fosphenytoin, lamotrigine, carbamazepine, oxcarbazepine).
  • Use levetiracetam, valproate, stiripentol, clobazam, cannabidiol, fenfluramine.

C28. Post-cardiac arrest cEEG recommendation.

Show answer
  • AHA 2019 (Topjian): cEEG ≥24 h after pediatric arrest.
  • Treat clinical and electrographic seizures.
  • ~30% of pediatric arrest survivors have post-anoxic seizures.

C29. Long-term sequelae of pediatric SE.

Show answer
  • Drug-resistant epilepsy.
  • Cognitive impairment.
  • Behavioral and learning disorders.
  • Mesial temporal sclerosis (after febrile SE).
  • Motor deficits if structural injury occurred.

C30. Common precipitants of SE in known epilepsy.

Show answer
  • ASM non-adherence (most common).
  • Recent ASM change.
  • Sleep deprivation.
  • Acute illness/fever.
  • Drug interactions reducing ASM levels.

Show plan

Top 5 examiner traps for Chapter 64

  • Under-dosing first BZD → treatment failure that looks like refractory SE.
  • Using valproate in <2 y or with mitochondrial concern → hepatotoxicity.
  • Forgetting that neonatal SE first-line is phenobarbital (NOT BZD).
