1 · Hydrocephalus — Mechanisms & Classification

CSF physiology refresher
  • CSF produced by choroid plexus and ependymal lining of ventricles (~0.3 mL/min adult).
  • Flow: lateral ventricles → foramina of Monro → 3rd ventricle → aqueduct of Sylvius → 4th ventricle → foramina of Luschka and Magendie → subarachnoid space → arachnoid granulations → venous sinuses.
  • Total volume ~150 mL adult, ~50–80 mL child, ~50 mL infant.
  • Monro-Kellie doctrine — fixed compartment of skull; CSF, blood, brain in equilibrium.
Hydrocephalus types
  • Noncommunicating (obstructive): obstruction within the ventricular system (aqueductal stenosis, intraventricular tumor, colloid cyst, IVH-related).
  • Communicating: obstruction at the level of the subarachnoid space, arachnoid granulations, or venous outflow (post-meningitis, post-SAH, post-IVH).
  • Ex vacuo: compensatory enlargement of ventricles due to brain atrophy (not true hydrocephalus — no pressure issue).
  • Normal pressure hydrocephalus: classical adult triad (gait, cognition, incontinence) — rare in pediatrics.
  • Acute vs chronic: acute requires urgent intervention; chronic may be slowly progressive.
Trap: "Communicating" does NOT mean "less serious." It refers only to the anatomic site of obstruction.
Pediatric hydrocephalus — common etiologies
  • Congenital: aqueductal stenosis (~30%), Chiari II + myelomeningocele, Dandy-Walker malformation, X-linked hydrocephalus (L1CAM mutation), congenital infection (CMV, toxoplasmosis).
  • Acquired: post-IVH (especially premature infants), post-meningitis, post-tumor, post-SAH, post-trauma.
  • Incidence ~1:1000 live births.
  • Improved survival of premature infants with IVH and children with myelomeningocele has increased prevalence.
Clinical presentation
  • Infants (open fontanelle): rapid head circumference growth crossing percentiles, bulging fontanelle, splayed sutures, "setting sun" sign (downward gaze, sclera visible above iris), irritability, vomiting, poor feeding.
  • Older children (closed sutures): headache (worse on waking and lying down), morning vomiting, papilledema, diplopia (CN VI palsy), gait disturbance, cognitive change.
  • Acute decompensation: Cushing's response (hypertension + bradycardia + irregular respirations), depressed consciousness, fixed dilated pupils.
Imaging
  • Ultrasound through fontanelle in neonate.
  • CT for emergency assessment of ventricular size, mass effect, hemorrhage.
  • MRI for structural cause: aqueductal stenosis, Chiari, Dandy-Walker, tumor, congenital infection sequelae.
  • Evans index: largest horizontal width of frontal horns ÷ largest internal skull diameter at same level; >0.30 suggests ventriculomegaly.
  • Fronto-occipital horn ratio (FOR) — pediatric standard.
Treatment
  • Acute decompensation: emergent EVD placement; hyperosmolar therapy (3% NaCl 3–5 mL/kg or mannitol 0.25–1 g/kg) as bridge; HOB 30°; intubation if GCS ≤8.
  • VP shunt: definitive in most cases. Components: ventricular catheter, valve, distal peritoneal catheter.
  • Endoscopic third ventriculostomy (ETV): alternative for obstructive hydrocephalus, especially aqueductal stenosis; creates communication between 3rd ventricle and prepontine cistern.
  • ETV + choroid plexus cauterization (ETV/CPC): emerging in young infants (~<1 year), particularly in low-resource settings (Warf 2005).
  • Shunt alternatives: ventriculoatrial, ventriculopleural (less common).
VP shunt complications (refer to Rogers Ch 61 for detail)
  • Mechanical: obstruction (proximal or distal), disconnection, fracture, migration.
  • Infection (~5–10% within first year); CoNS, S. aureus, gram-negatives.
  • Overdrainage: slit ventricles, subdural hygromas/hematomas, postural headaches.
  • Underdrainage: persistent symptoms.
  • Distal complications: peritoneal pseudocyst, hernia.
  • Shunt failure rate: ~40% by 2 years post-insertion.

2 · Arachnoid Cysts

Background
  • Congenital fluid-filled lesions within the arachnoid layer; CSF-like contents.
  • Most are incidental on imaging.
  • Most common location: middle cranial fossa (Sylvian fissure).
  • Other sites: posterior fossa, suprasellar, quadrigeminal cistern, spinal.
  • Galassi classification (middle fossa): types I, II, III based on size and mass effect.
When concerning
  • Symptomatic mass effect: headache, seizures, focal deficit, developmental delay in children.
  • Acute hemorrhage into cyst — often after trivial trauma; can present as acute SDH or new neurologic deficit.
  • Growth on serial imaging (more likely in young children).
  • Hydrocephalus from CSF flow obstruction.
Surgical management
  • Asymptomatic cysts → observe with serial imaging.
  • Symptomatic or complicated cysts:
    • Endoscopic fenestration (preferred for many).
    • Craniotomy and cyst marsupialization.
    • Cyst-peritoneal shunt (less favored — shunt-dependent).
  • Post-op: monitor for CSF leak, infection, subdural collection, hydrocephalus.

3 · Chiari Malformations

Chiari spectrum overview
  • Defined by cerebellar tissue displacement through foramen magnum.
  • Type I — cerebellar tonsillar descent > 5 mm below foramen magnum.
  • Type II — herniation of cerebellar vermis, brainstem, and 4th ventricle through foramen magnum; associated with myelomeningocele.
  • Type III — encephalocele containing posterior fossa contents (rare, severe).
  • Type 0 — symptoms of Chiari without anatomic descent on imaging.
Chiari I — clinical and imaging
  • Presentation: occipital/cervical headache exacerbated by Valsalva, cough, sneeze; neck pain, vertigo; in older children sometimes asymptomatic incidental finding.
  • Associated syringomyelia (15–80%) — myelopathy, scoliosis, sensory dissociation.
  • Cranial nerve dysfunction (rare).
  • MRI: tonsillar descent below foramen magnum > 5 mm; assess tonsil shape (rounded vs pointed — pointed and crowded suggest pathology).
  • Always obtain spinal MRI to rule out syringomyelia.
  • Phase-contrast cine MRI evaluates CSF flow dynamics.
Chiari I — management
  • Asymptomatic patients with incidental finding — observation.
  • Symptomatic patients: posterior fossa decompression (suboccipital craniectomy + C1 laminectomy ± duraplasty ± tonsillar reduction).
  • Outcomes: pain often improves; syrinx resolution variable; scoliosis may stabilize.
  • Post-op complications: CSF leak, meningitis, hydrocephalus, pseudomeningocele.
Chiari II — associated with myelomeningocele
  • Almost all children with myelomeningocele have Chiari II malformation.
  • Herniation includes cerebellar vermis, brainstem, 4th ventricle.
  • Brainstem dysfunction symptoms in infants: stridor (vocal cord paralysis), apnea, swallowing problems, aspiration, opisthotonos.
  • Hydrocephalus present in most patients with Chiari II + myelomeningocele.
  • Imaging: small posterior fossa, beaked tectum, kinking of medulla, brainstem displacement.
  • Management: treat hydrocephalus first (often resolves brainstem symptoms); if symptoms persist → posterior fossa decompression; outcomes less favorable than Chiari I decompression.
Trap: Stridor or apnea in a myelomeningocele infant should prompt urgent neurosurgical evaluation — Chiari II brainstem decompensation can be fatal.

4 · Dandy-Walker Malformation (DWM)

Anatomy
  • Posterior fossa cyst continuous with 4th ventricle.
  • Cerebellar vermis hypoplasia or agenesis.
  • Elevated tentorium (high torcula).
  • Hydrocephalus in ~80% (often delayed onset).
  • Spectrum includes Dandy-Walker variant (milder) and mega cisterna magna.
Associations
  • Other CNS anomalies: agenesis of corpus callosum, holoprosencephaly, polymicrogyria.
  • Systemic anomalies: congenital heart disease, cleft lip/palate, renal anomalies, genitourinary anomalies.
  • Genetic syndromes: trisomy 13, 18, PHACE, Joubert, Walker-Warburg.
Management
  • Hydrocephalus: VP shunt or cyst-peritoneal shunt or both; ETV may be considered.
  • Developmental delay common; multidisciplinary follow-up.
  • Treat associated systemic anomalies.
  • Outcomes vary widely — depend on extent of brain malformation and associated anomalies.

5 · Encephalocele & Meningocele

Encephalocele
  • Herniation of brain tissue and meninges through a skull defect.
  • Sites: occipital (most common in Western populations), frontoethmoidal (more common in SE Asia), parietal.
  • Associated anomalies: hydrocephalus, microcephaly, agenesis of corpus callosum, brain malformations.
  • Surgical repair: protect sac (avoid rupture before surgery); resect non-functional brain herniation; close dura and skin; manage hydrocephalus.
  • Outcomes depend on volume of herniated brain and associated anomalies.
Cranial meningocele
  • Herniation of meninges through skull defect WITHOUT brain tissue.
  • Better prognosis than encephalocele.
  • Surgical repair: close defect; manage hydrocephalus if present.

6 · Myelomeningocele (Spina Bifida)

Definition & Anatomy
  • Most severe form of spinal dysraphism.
  • Failure of primary neurulation → open neural tube.
  • Herniation of spinal cord through defect in vertebrae, soft tissue, skin.
  • Exposure of neural tissue to amniotic fluid → ongoing neurologic dysfunction.
  • Most commonly lumbosacral.
  • Associated: Chiari II malformation, hydrocephalus, spinal cord tethering, syringomyelia, neurogenic bladder, orthopedic deformities.
Prenatal management
  • Folic acid 400 µg daily before conception reduces neural tube defects ~70%.
  • Prenatal diagnosis: maternal serum AFP, ultrasound, fetal MRI.
  • MOMS trial (Adzick NEJM 2011): fetal repair before 26 weeks reduces need for VP shunt and improves motor function vs postnatal repair.
  • Maternal risks of fetal surgery: preterm labor, uterine dehiscence.
Postnatal management
  • Immediate: protect sac with sterile, moist saline dressing; prone or side-lying position; avoid pressure on sac.
  • Latex precautions (high rate of latex allergy with multiple exposures).
  • Surgical closure within 24–48 hours of birth to prevent infection.
  • Postoperative: monitor for CSF leak, infection, hydrocephalus (50–90% develop hydrocephalus requiring shunt).
  • VP shunt usually placed days to weeks after closure.
  • Monitor for Chiari II decompensation (stridor, apnea, swallowing).
Long-term management
  • Neurogenic bladder — clean intermittent catheterization, anticholinergics, urodynamics; long-term renal protection.
  • Neurogenic bowel — bowel program.
  • Orthopedic — clubfoot, hip dislocation, scoliosis; physical therapy and bracing.
  • Skin care — pressure sore prevention; sensory loss in dermatomes below lesion.
  • Tethered cord — late symptoms (back pain, leg weakness, urinary deterioration) require surgical detethering.
  • Cognitive impairment in some — especially those with hydrocephalus and shunt complications.
  • Latex-free environment lifelong.

11 · SAQs (20)

SAQ 1. Differentiate noncommunicating from communicating hydrocephalus.

  • Noncommunicating: obstruction within the ventricular system (aqueductal stenosis, IVH-related obstruction, tumor, colloid cyst).
  • Communicating: obstruction at subarachnoid space, arachnoid granulations, or venous outflow (post-meningitis, post-SAH).
  • Both can cause raised ICP — terminology refers to anatomic location.

SAQ 2. List signs of hydrocephalus in infants vs older children.

  • Infants: rapidly increasing HC, bulging fontanelle, splayed sutures, sundowning sign, irritability, vomiting, poor feeding.
  • Older children: morning headache, vomiting, papilledema, CN VI palsy, gait disturbance, behavior change.
  • Both: Cushing's triad in acute decompensation.

SAQ 3. Define ETV and when it is preferred over shunt.

  • Endoscopic third ventriculostomy — creates communication between 3rd ventricle and prepontine cistern.
  • Preferred in obstructive (noncommunicating) hydrocephalus, especially aqueductal stenosis in older children.
  • ETV + CPC (choroid plexus cauterization) — alternative in infants < 1 year (Warf 2005).
  • Avoids shunt dependency and shunt complications.

SAQ 4. Chiari I malformation — diagnostic criterion and key associated condition.

  • Cerebellar tonsillar descent > 5 mm below foramen magnum on MRI.
  • Pointed (peg-shaped) tonsils suggest pathology more than rounded.
  • Syringomyelia in 15–80% — always obtain spinal MRI.

SAQ 5. Chiari II malformation — primary association.

  • Myelomeningocele (essentially all cases).
  • Cerebellar vermis + brainstem + 4th ventricle herniate through foramen magnum.
  • Hydrocephalus nearly universal.
  • Brainstem symptoms: stridor (vocal cord paralysis), apnea, swallowing difficulty.
  • Treat hydrocephalus first; decompression if symptoms persist.

SAQ 6. Dandy-Walker malformation — anatomic features.

  • Posterior fossa cyst continuous with 4th ventricle.
  • Cerebellar vermis hypoplasia or agenesis.
  • Elevated tentorium (high torcula).
  • Hydrocephalus in ~80%.

SAQ 7. MOMS trial outcomes.

  • Prenatal repair of myelomeningocele before 26 weeks vs postnatal.
  • Reduced need for VP shunt (40% vs 82%).
  • Improved motor function at 30 months.
  • Maternal risks: preterm labor, uterine dehiscence.

SAQ 8. Initial newborn management of myelomeningocele.

  • Protect sac with sterile, moist saline dressing.
  • Prone or side position; avoid pressure on sac.
  • Latex precautions.
  • IV antibiotics.
  • Surgical closure within 24–48 h.

SAQ 9. Long-term complications of myelomeningocele.

  • Hydrocephalus (50–90% — VP shunt).
  • Chiari II decompensation.
  • Neurogenic bladder.
  • Neurogenic bowel.
  • Orthopedic deformities (clubfoot, scoliosis, hip dislocation).
  • Tethered cord (late).
  • Skin breakdown.
  • Cognitive impairment (subset).

SAQ 10. Folic acid for neural tube defect prevention.

  • 400 µg/day preconception.
  • 4 mg/day if prior NTD pregnancy.
  • ~70% reduction in NTD incidence.

SAQ 11. Distinguish encephalocele from cranial meningocele.

  • Encephalocele: brain tissue + meninges through skull defect.
  • Meningocele: meninges only through skull defect (no brain).
  • Meningocele has better prognosis.

SAQ 12. Arachnoid cyst — when is intervention indicated?

  • Symptomatic mass effect (headache, seizures, focal deficit).
  • Hemorrhage into cyst.
  • Growth on serial imaging.
  • Hydrocephalus from CSF flow obstruction.
  • Endoscopic fenestration is preferred procedure.

SAQ 13. VP shunt failure rate.

  • ~40% within 2 years of insertion.
  • Common failures: obstruction (proximal > distal), disconnection, infection.
  • Lifetime risk of multiple revisions common.

SAQ 14. VP shunt overdrainage syndrome.

  • Slit ventricles, postural (orthostatic) headache.
  • Subdural hygroma or hematoma — from rapid CSF drainage with bridging vein traction.
  • Management: programmable valve adjustment; surgical revision to higher pressure valve.

SAQ 15. Stridor or apnea in myelomeningocele infant — significance.

  • Chiari II decompensation.
  • Brainstem compression in narrow posterior fossa.
  • Treat hydrocephalus first (often resolves symptoms).
  • Posterior fossa decompression if persists.
  • Can be fatal — urgent neurosurgery.

SAQ 16. Why does Chiari II malformation often have hydrocephalus?

  • Small posterior fossa with crowding of brainstem and 4th ventricle.
  • Obstructed CSF outflow at foramina of Luschka and Magendie.
  • Associated aqueductal stenosis in some.
  • Most patients require VP shunt placement.

SAQ 17. Dandy-Walker associated systemic anomalies.

  • Cardiac defects.
  • Cleft lip/palate.
  • Renal anomalies.
  • Genitourinary anomalies.
  • Chromosomal: trisomy 13, 18.
  • Genetic syndromes: PHACE, Joubert, Walker-Warburg.

SAQ 18. ETV Success Score components.

  • Age.
  • Etiology of hydrocephalus.
  • History of prior shunt.
  • Higher scores → higher probability of ETV success vs shunt.

SAQ 19. Tethered cord syndrome — clinical features.

  • Back pain, leg weakness, sensory changes.
  • Urinary incontinence or retention (worsening from baseline).
  • Foot deformity progression.
  • Scoliosis progression.
  • MRI: low-lying conus, thick filum, fatty filum.
  • Treatment: surgical detethering.

SAQ 20. Top 5 pitfalls in pediatric neurosurgical ICU care.

  • Missing stridor/apnea in myelomeningocele infant as Chiari II decompensation.
  • Failing latex precautions in spina bifida patient.
  • Inadequate hyperosmolar therapy during acute hydrocephalus decompensation.
  • Not screening for syringomyelia in Chiari I patients.
  • Premature shunt placement before considering ETV in obstructive hydrocephalus.

12 · PICU Cases (15)

Case 1. 2-month-old with rapidly enlarging head, full fontanelle, sundowning sign.

  • Hydrocephalus — emergency.
  • Urgent imaging (US or CT).
  • Neurosurgery for EVD or shunt.
  • Investigate cause (congenital aqueductal stenosis, post-IVH, infection).

Case 2. Term newborn with lumbosacral sac in delivery room.

  • Protect sac with sterile saline-moist dressing.
  • Prone position.
  • Latex precautions; NICU admission.
  • IV antibiotics.
  • Surgical closure within 24–48 h.
  • Monitor for hydrocephalus; eventually need VP shunt likely.

Case 3. 9-year-old with chronic morning headache and vomiting; MRI shows aqueductal stenosis.

  • Obstructive hydrocephalus.
  • Neurosurgery for ETV — preferred over shunt.
  • Calculate ETV Success Score.
  • Postoperative monitoring.

Case 4. Premature infant 28 weeks gestation, day 14 post-IVH, increasing HC and ventriculomegaly.

  • Post-hemorrhagic hydrocephalus.
  • Serial taps via reservoir or ventricular access device.
  • VP shunt when stable weight (typically > 2 kg) and CSF clears of blood.
  • Neurodevelopmental follow-up.

Case 5. 11-year-old with occipital headache worse with cough, MRI shows tonsils 8 mm below foramen magnum.

  • Chiari I malformation, symptomatic.
  • Spinal MRI for syringomyelia.
  • Posterior fossa decompression with ± duraplasty.
  • Monitor for post-op CSF leak.

Case 6. 6-month-old myelomeningocele post-closure develops new stridor and apnea.

  • Chiari II decompensation.
  • Emergent neurosurgery consult.
  • Treat hydrocephalus first (VP shunt placement if not already done).
  • Posterior fossa decompression if symptoms persist.
  • ICU monitoring.

Case 7. 3-year-old with Dandy-Walker malformation, increasing HC.

  • Hydrocephalus management.
  • VP shunt or cyst-peritoneal shunt or both.
  • Systemic anomaly screen (cardiac, renal, GU).
  • Genetic consult.
  • Developmental follow-up.

Case 8. Infant with occipital encephalocele in delivery room.

  • Protect sac.
  • Imaging to assess herniated content.
  • Neurosurgical repair when stable.
  • Manage hydrocephalus if develops.
  • Long-term neurodevelopmental follow-up.

Case 9. 8-year-old with seizure, MRI shows large left middle fossa arachnoid cyst.

  • Symptomatic cyst.
  • Neurosurgery consult.
  • Endoscopic fenestration preferred procedure.
  • Antiepileptic management.

Case 10. VP shunt patient at age 6 with vomiting, headache, sleepiness.

  • Shunt malfunction.
  • Shunt series (skull X-ray, neck, chest, abdomen) for disconnection.
  • Head CT for ventricular size.
  • Neurosurgery consult.
  • Likely revision.

Case 11. 2-week-old post-myelomeningocele closure with rapid HC growth.

  • Hydrocephalus developing as expected.
  • VP shunt placement.
  • Monitor for shunt complications.
  • Multidisciplinary team setup for long-term care.

Case 12. 12-year-old with Chiari I and known syrinx; develops scoliosis.

  • Chiari I-associated syringomyelia.
  • Posterior fossa decompression often improves syrinx and may stabilize scoliosis.
  • Orthopedic co-management.
  • Serial MRI to assess syrinx response.

Case 13. Adolescent female with prior myelomeningocele develops new urinary symptoms and back pain.

  • Tethered cord syndrome.
  • MRI lumbar spine.
  • Surgical detethering.
  • Urodynamics; urology consult.

Case 14. 10-year-old with myelomeningocele, latex anaphylaxis during dental procedure.

  • Acute anaphylaxis management — epinephrine, IV fluids, antihistamines, steroids.
  • Latex precautions reinforced.
  • Pre-procedure latex-free protocol all settings.
  • Allergy bracelet.
  • EpiPen at home.

Case 15. Counseling pregnant woman after fetal MRI shows myelomeningocele at 22 weeks.

  • Multidisciplinary fetal therapy team referral.
  • MOMS trial criteria assessment.
  • Fetal repair vs postnatal repair discussion.
  • Maternal risks (preterm labor, uterine dehiscence) and benefits (lower shunt rate, better motor function).
  • Folic acid supplementation in future pregnancies.

Hydrocephalus essentials

  • Communicating vs noncommunicating.
  • Acute decompensation: 3% NaCl bolus + emergent EVD.
  • Definitive: VP shunt or ETV (± CPC if <1 y).
  • Shunt failure ~40% in 2 years.

Chiari spectrum

  • I: tonsils >5 mm below foramen magnum; HA + Valsalva; check syrinx.
  • II: with myelomeningocele; stridor/apnea = decompensation.
  • III: rare encephalocele containing posterior fossa contents.

Myelomeningocele

  • Folic acid 400 µg/d preconception → 70% reduction.
  • MOMS: fetal repair < 26 wk reduces shunt + improves motor.
  • Postnatal: protect sac → closure 24–48 h → monitor hydrocephalus.
  • Latex precautions lifelong.

Red Flags

  • Stridor/apnea in MMC = Chiari II decompensation.
  • Chiari I without spinal MRI = miss syrinx.
  • Dandy-Walker = check systemic anomalies and genetics.

Exam pearls

  • ETV preferred for obstructive hydrocephalus.
  • Aqueductal stenosis is most common cause of congenital hydrocephalus.
  • Tethered cord — new symptoms in older child with MMC.
  • Arachnoid cyst hemorrhage after minor trauma — surgical emergency.
