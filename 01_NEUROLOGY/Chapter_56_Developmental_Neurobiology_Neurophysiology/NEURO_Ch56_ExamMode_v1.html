SAQ A-1
Describe the GABA developmental switch and its ICU implication.
Show Answer
  • NKCC1 dominant early → high intracellular Cl⁻ → GABA depolarizing (excitatory).
  • KCC2 rises around birth → Cl⁻ extruded → GABA hyperpolarizing (inhibitory).
  • Implication — typical GABAergic anticonvulsants (phenobarbital, benzodiazepines) may be less effective or paradoxical in neonatal seizures.
  • NKCC1 antagonist bumetanide studied (NEMO trial) but ototoxicity halted enthusiasm.
SAQ A-2
Outline age-appropriate CPP targets.
Show Answer
  • Infant ≥40 mmHg.
  • Toddler ≥50 mmHg.
  • Older child ≥60 mmHg.
  • Adolescent ≥70 mmHg.
  • Individualized via PRx-derived CPPopt is increasingly used.
SAQ A-3
Why does CBF peak in childhood?
Show Answer
  • CBF and cerebral metabolic rate increase from birth.
  • Peak between age 3 and 9 years.
  • Mirrors synaptogenesis and high metabolic demand of actively pruning brain.
  • Declines to adult levels by adolescence.
SAQ A-4
Why is hypocapnia dangerous in pediatric TBI?
Show Answer
  • ~3 % drop in CBF per 1 mmHg PaCO₂.
  • Hypocapnia <25 mmHg can drop CBF 30–50 %.
  • In injured brain with disrupted autoregulation → secondary ischemia.
  • Target PaCO₂ 35–40 mmHg.
  • Brief hyperventilation only for herniation crisis, with cerebral oxygenation monitoring.
SAQ A-5
Define PRx and its clinical use.
Show Answer
  • Pressure Reactivity Index — moving Pearson correlation of MAP and ICP.
  • PRx <0 — preserved autoregulation.
  • PRx >0.3 — impaired autoregulation.
  • CPPopt — the CPP at which PRx is lowest (best autoregulation).
  • Maintaining CPP near CPPopt may improve TBI outcomes.
SAQ A-6
Describe developmental neuroapoptosis from anesthetics.
Show Answer
  • NMDA antagonists (ketamine) and GABA agonists (volatile anesthetics, propofol, benzodiazepines, barbiturates) trigger neuroapoptosis in animal models.
  • Mechanism — loss of trophic / NT input tips developing neurons toward apoptosis.
  • FDA warning — repeated or prolonged anesthesia >3 h in children <3 y.
  • Single short exposures appear safe (GAS, PANDA, MASK trials).
  • Dexmedetomidine has less neuroapoptotic signal.
SAQ A-7
What is therapeutic hypothermia for neonatal HIE?
Show Answer
  • Core temperature 33.5 °C × 72 h.
  • Initiated within 6 h of birth.
  • For moderate / severe HIE (Sarnat 2 or 3) and pH ≤7.0 or BE ≤−16.
  • Reduces death + neurodevelopmental impairment.
  • Adjunctive EPO did NOT add benefit (HEAL trial).
SAQ A-8
Why are pediatric BBB and antibiotic dosing relevant?
Show Answer
  • BBB restricts hydrophilic agents and osmotic agents.
  • Antibiotic CNS penetration depends on lipophilicity, molecular size, and BBB integrity.
  • Inflammation increases BBB permeability — full course dosing important as inflammation resolves.
  • Higher doses needed for adequate CSF levels (ceftriaxone CNS dose; vancomycin trough-guided).
SAQ A-9
Explain how mannitol works.
Show Answer
  • Osmotic agent that does not cross intact BBB.
  • Establishes osmotic gradient drawing water from brain parenchyma into vasculature.
  • Dose 0.25–1 g/kg IV bolus.
  • Requires intact BBB — disrupted BBB allows leak into brain, worsening edema.
  • Monitor serum osmolality (cap ~320 mOsm) and renal function.
SAQ A-10
Why is dexmedetomidine increasingly preferred in pediatric sedation?
Show Answer
  • α2-agonist with no GABAergic action.
  • Minimal developmental neuroapoptosis signal.
  • Provides sedation without respiratory depression.
  • Useful for ventilator weaning, NIV tolerance, post-extubation.
  • Side effects — bradycardia, hypotension, withdrawal after prolonged use.

Mock RCPSC Block B — 5 Sequential ICU Cases

⏱ 30 min total · 6 min per case
CASE B-1
Term infant with low Apgars, cord pH 6.9. At 4 h — encephalopathic, seizures.
Q. Immediate management.
Show Answer
  • Confirm criteria for therapeutic hypothermia — pH ≤7.0 or BE ≤−16; Sarnat stage 2 or 3.
  • Initiate hypothermia (33.5 °C × 72 h) within 6 h.
  • Continuous aEEG / EEG monitoring.
  • Treat seizures with phenobarbital first-line.
  • Avoid hyperthermia, hyperoxia, hypoglycemia, hypocapnia.
CASE B-2
8-year-old after MVC has ICP 28 mmHg despite sedation, head-of-bed elevation, normocapnia, normothermia.
Q. Escalation.
Show Answer
  • Hyperosmolar therapy — hypertonic saline 3 % or mannitol 0.5–1 g/kg.
  • Deepen sedation; consider neuromuscular blockade.
  • Brief hyperventilation if herniation suspected; monitor cerebral oxygenation.
  • Consider CPPopt-guided therapy via PRx.
  • Surgical decompressive craniectomy if refractory.
CASE B-3
Term newborn at 24 h develops focal clonic seizures and continuous electrographic seizures on aEEG.
Q. Management.
Show Answer
  • Phenobarbital first-line (20 mg/kg loading).
  • Investigate — glucose, electrolytes, pyridoxine trial, head US, MRI, metabolic screen.
  • If refractory — phenobarbital second load, levetiracetam, midazolam infusion.
  • Bumetanide not routine practice (NEMO trial).
  • Continue EEG monitoring.
CASE B-4
2-year-old with developmental delay, complex movement disorder, infantile-onset seizures. CSF glucose 1.8 mmol/L with normal serum glucose.
Q. Diagnosis and management.
Show Answer
  • GLUT1 deficiency syndrome (SLC2A1 mutation).
  • CSF/serum glucose ratio <0.5 supports diagnosis.
  • Genetic confirmation.
  • Ketogenic diet — provides ketone bodies as alternative brain fuel.
  • Avoid drugs inhibiting GLUT1 (caffeine, valproate).
CASE B-5
10-year-old TBI patient — autoregulation monitoring shows PRx 0.4 at current CPP of 65 mmHg.
Q. Action.
Show Answer
  • Impaired autoregulation (PRx >0.3).
  • Plot PRx vs. CPP to find CPPopt (CPP at lowest PRx).
  • Titrate MAP / vasoactive agents to bring CPP near CPPopt.
  • Monitor cerebral oxygenation in parallel.
  • Re-evaluate periodically — CPPopt may shift with disease evolution.

Block C — Remaining SAQs (randomized)

Q C-1
Describe two ICU-relevant features of pediatric BBB.
Show Answer
  • Tight junctions plus low transcytosis restrict hydrophilic and osmotic agents.
  • Mannitol depends on intact BBB.
  • BBB disruption by injury / inflammation produces vasogenic edema and contrast enhancement.
  • Antibiotic CNS penetration depends on lipophilicity, size, and BBB state.
Q C-2
Outline the pressure autoregulation curve.
Show Answer
  • CBF plateau between LLA and ULA of CPP.
  • Below LLA — CBF falls passively → ischemia.
  • Above ULA — CBF rises passively → hyperperfusion / hemorrhage.
  • Curve shifts right in chronic hypertension; widens or is lost after acute injury.
Q C-3
Describe PCD pathways relevant to pediatric brain injury.
Show Answer
  • Intrinsic — Bax/Bak release cytochrome-c → caspase-9 → caspase-3.
  • Extrinsic — Fas/TNF → caspase-8 → caspase-3.
  • Pro-death — Bax, Bak, BAD, caspases.
  • Pro-survival — Bcl-2, Bcl-xL, neurotrophins (BDNF, NGF).
Q C-4
Why is the neonatal brain more excitotoxicity-sensitive?
Show Answer
  • Higher NMDA receptor density.
  • NR2B-rich subunit composition early (longer Ca²⁺ flux).
  • Reduced inhibitory tone before GABA switch.
  • Higher metabolic demand of developing synapses.
Q C-5
Outline microglial M1 vs. M2 phenotypes.
Show Answer
  • M1 — pro-inflammatory; TNF, IL-1β, IL-6; phagocytosis.
  • M2 — resolution / repair; IL-10, TGF-β; tissue remodelling.
  • Balance shifts after injury — early M1, later M2.
  • Persistent M1 contributes to secondary injury.
Q C-6
Outline astrocyte functions.
Show Answer
  • Synaptic support; glutamate uptake (EAAT1/2).
  • K⁺ buffering.
  • Water transport via AQP4.
  • BBB maintenance via end-foot apposition.
  • Glial scar formation; energy substrate delivery via lactate shuttle.
Q C-7
Why is the developing brain a "glucose-and-lactate" user?
Show Answer
  • Glucose is primary fuel; LCMRg increases with maturation.
  • MCT1, MCT4 transporters develop perinatally allowing lactate uptake.
  • Neonate can use lactate as alternative substrate during stress.
  • Explains mild hyperlactatemia tolerance.
  • Ketogenic diet works partly via alternative substrate.
Q C-8
What is the role of adenosine in brain physiology?
Show Answer
  • Neuronal, glial, cerebrovascular transmitter.
  • A1 receptors inhibitory; A2 receptors vasodilator.
  • Accumulates during hypoxia and inhibits glutamate release — endogenous neuroprotection.
  • Methylxanthines (caffeine) antagonize adenosine — used for apnea of prematurity.
Q C-9
List five neurotrophic factors and their roles.
Show Answer
  • BDNF — neuronal survival, synaptic plasticity, LTP.
  • NGF — cholinergic neuron survival.
  • NT-3 / NT-4-5 — neuronal subpopulations.
  • GDNF — dopaminergic neurons.
  • IGF-1 — oligodendrocyte development and myelination.
  • VEGF — vascular and neuronal effects.
Q C-10
Describe germinal matrix hemorrhage vulnerability.
Show Answer
  • Preterm brain has germinal matrix near caudate / thalamus / lateral ventricle wall.
  • Thin-walled, immature vessels.
  • Fluctuating CBF + pressure-passive circulation → hemorrhage risk.
  • Grades 1–4 (Papile classification).
  • Involutes by 32–34 weeks corrected age.
Q C-11
Describe BDNF role in pediatric brain.
Show Answer
  • Most-studied neurotrophin.
  • Supports neuronal survival, synaptic plasticity, LTP.
  • Critical during developmental critical periods.
  • Val66Met polymorphism may modify post-TBI recovery.
  • Exercise increases BDNF — basis for rehabilitation benefit.
Q C-12
Why must antibiotic doses be higher for CNS infection?
Show Answer
  • BBB limits drug entry even when inflamed.
  • Higher serum levels needed for adequate CSF concentrations.
  • Some drugs require continuous infusion or higher peak dosing.
  • CSF assays guide therapy in difficult cases.
Q C-13
Describe ABCD study contribution.
Show Answer
  • Largest longitudinal pediatric neuroimaging cohort.
  • 11 000+ children tracked from age 9–10 onwards.
  • Maps normal developmental trajectories.
  • Quantifies effects of substance use, screen time, stress, sleep, physical activity.
  • Provides normative reference for post-injury trajectories.
Q C-14
Describe synaptogenesis pattern across regions.
Show Answer
  • Active until puberty.
  • Primary sensory areas mature first; prefrontal cortex last.
  • Synapse density peaks 1–2 years postnatally in visual cortex; later in prefrontal cortex.
  • Pruning follows — "use it or lose it" activity-dependent refinement.
Q C-15
What is the role of GLUT1 in pediatric brain?
Show Answer
  • Glucose transporter at BBB.
  • Mediates glucose entry into brain.
  • GLUT1 deficiency syndrome — SLC2A1 mutation; autosomal dominant.
  • Infantile-onset seizures, developmental delay, complex movement disorder.
  • Low CSF glucose with normal serum glucose; treat with ketogenic diet.
Q C-16
When is hyperventilation acceptable in pediatric TBI?
Show Answer
  • Acute herniation crisis — brief hyperventilation as bridge to definitive intervention.
  • Avoid prophylactic / sustained hyperventilation.
  • Target PaCO₂ 35–40 mmHg normally.
  • If hyperventilating, monitor cerebral oxygenation (PbtO₂, SjvO₂, NIRS).
Q C-17
Describe role of EAAT1/2 in synaptic glutamate.
Show Answer
  • Astrocyte-located glutamate transporters.
  • Remove glutamate from synaptic cleft after release.
  • Prevents excitotoxicity by limiting NMDA activation.
  • Loss of function (genetic or injury-induced) increases seizure and excitotoxic risk.
Q C-18
What is the role of EPO in neuroprotection?
Show Answer
  • EPO receptors expressed on neurons; protective in animal models.
  • HEAL — EPO + hypothermia did NOT improve outcomes in neonatal HIE.
  • PENUT — high-dose EPO did NOT improve outcomes in extremely preterm infants.
  • Hypothermia remains the standard for neonatal HIE.
Q C-19
Describe the developmental trajectory of myelination.
Show Answer
  • Begins late gestation in brainstem and cerebellum.
  • Internal capsule myelinated by term.
  • Sensory pathways myelinate before motor pathways.
  • Cortical myelination active through age ~2 y.
  • Frontal / association cortex continues through adolescence into early adulthood.
Q C-20
Outline CO₂ reactivity in the developing brain.
Show Answer
  • ~3 % change in CBF per 1 mmHg PaCO₂.
  • Hypercapnia → vasodilation → ↑ CBF and ICP.
  • Hypocapnia → vasoconstriction → ↓ CBF.
  • Functional from early in development.
  • Target PaCO₂ 35–40 mmHg in TBI.
Q C-21
Describe the role of AQP4 in pediatric brain.
Show Answer
  • Aquaporin-4 water channel on astrocyte end-feet.
  • Mediates water flux between blood, CSF, parenchyma.
  • Contributes to cytotoxic edema.
  • Target of anti-AQP4 antibody in NMOSD.
Q C-22
Outline pediatric cerebral oximetry use.
Show Answer
  • NIRS measures regional cerebral oxygen saturation (rSO₂).
  • Venous-weighted oxygen saturation in 1–2 cm of frontal cortex.
  • Use — cardiac surgery, ECMO, TBI.
  • rSO₂ <40 % or >25 % drop from baseline triggers intervention.
Q C-23
What is intracranial compliance in pediatrics?
Show Answer
  • Compliance = ΔV / ΔP.
  • Initial volume changes compensated by CSF / venous displacement.
  • Once compensation exhausted, small volume changes produce large ICP rises.
  • Infants — open fontanelles add compliance but may mask ICP rises.
  • Monro-Kellie doctrine — fixed cranial volume (brain + blood + CSF) in adults; partial in infants.
Q C-24
Describe the basis of CPP-directed therapy.
Show Answer
  • CPP = MAP − ICP.
  • Maintain age-appropriate CPP for cerebral perfusion.
  • Low CPP → ischemia; high CPP → hyperperfusion / hemorrhage.
  • Modern approach — individualized CPPopt via PRx.
  • Adjuncts — sedation, hyperosmolar therapy, head-of-bed elevation, normothermia.
Q C-25
What is the ketogenic diet and its mechanism?
Show Answer
  • High-fat, very-low-carb diet producing ketosis.
  • Used for refractory pediatric epilepsy.
  • Mechanisms — alternative substrate, altered GABA/glutamate balance, mitochondrial effects.
  • ~50 % of children have >50 % seizure reduction.
  • Side effects — acidosis, hyperlipidemia, growth restriction, renal stones.
Q C-26
Why are catecholamines used in pediatric brain injury?
Show Answer
  • Maintain age-appropriate MAP and CPP.
  • Cross BBB poorly but may have CNS effects in neonates.
  • Norepinephrine common in TBI to maintain CPP.
  • Avoid excessive vasoconstriction that may reduce regional CBF in injured brain.
Q C-27
Outline brain growth factors relevant to recovery.
Show Answer
  • BDNF, NGF, NT-3, NT-4/5 — neuronal survival.
  • GDNF — dopaminergic neurons.
  • IGF-1 — myelination.
  • VEGF — neurovascular effects.
  • EPO — neuroprotection (HEAL, PENUT negative).
Q C-28
When is bumetanide used in neonatal seizures?
Show Answer
  • Investigational based on GABA developmental switch.
  • NKCC1 antagonist — lowers intracellular Cl⁻.
  • NEMO trial stopped early for ototoxicity; modest seizure reduction.
  • Not in routine clinical practice.
  • Phenobarbital remains first-line.
Q C-29
Why must antibiotic doses be higher for CNS infection?
Show Answer
  • BBB limits drug entry even when inflamed.
  • Higher serum levels needed for adequate CSF concentrations.
  • Full-course dosing important as inflammation resolves.
  • Therapeutic drug monitoring for vancomycin and aminoglycosides.
Q C-30
What is the difference between M1 and M2 microglia in injury?
Show Answer
  • M1 — pro-inflammatory phenotype; harmful to tissue.
  • M2 — repair / resolution phenotype; tissue-supporting.
  • Therapeutic target — promote M2 polarization for neuroprotection.

Block D — Remaining Case Bank (randomized)

CASE D-1
2-month-old undergoing multiple cardiac procedures with cumulative anesthesia >10 h.
Q. Implications.
Show Answer
  • Cumulative anesthesia >3 h in <3 y triggers FDA warning.
  • Surgery benefit outweighs theoretical neurodevelopmental risk.
  • Minimize subsequent exposures; prefer dexmedetomidine for sedation.
  • Plan developmental surveillance.
CASE D-2
27-week preterm with grade III IVH and progressive ventricular dilation.
Q. Mechanism and management.
Show Answer
  • Germinal matrix vulnerability — thin-walled vessels, pressure-passive CBF.
  • Avoid hyperosmolar boluses, rapid volume changes, hypercapnia.
  • Serial head US for ventricle size.
  • Neurosurgical drainage (subgaleal shunt / reservoir) if progressive.
  • VP shunt or ETV long-term if persistent.
CASE D-3
5-year-old with multifocal refractory status epilepticus despite phenobarbital, fosphenytoin, levetiracetam, midazolam.
Q. Options.
Show Answer
  • Continuous EEG.
  • Pentobarbital coma or thiopental coma — burst suppression target.
  • Ketogenic diet initiation (enteral or IV emulsion).
  • Immunotherapy trial (steroids, IVIG, PLEX) if autoimmune.
  • Consider anakinra for FIRES.
CASE D-4
Toddler with severe TBI hyperventilated to PaCO₂ 22 mmHg for 24 h; new infarction on CT.
Q. Mechanism, lesson.
Show Answer
  • Hypocapnia → vasoconstriction → CBF drop 30–50 %.
  • Injured brain with disrupted autoregulation → ischemia.
  • Target PaCO₂ 35–40 mmHg.
  • Hyperventilate only for herniation, with cerebral oxygenation monitoring.
CASE D-5
Adolescent with TBI receives repeated mannitol; osmolality 340 mOsm; develops AKI.
Q. Management.
Show Answer
  • Hold further mannitol; switch to hypertonic saline.
  • Volume resuscitation cautiously.
  • Monitor renal function and electrolytes.
  • Target serum osmolality <320 mOsm.
  • With disrupted BBB, mannitol can leak into brain — reduced efficacy.
CASE D-6
12-year-old with psychiatric symptoms, dyskinesias, seizures, autonomic instability. CSF anti-NMDA antibody positive.
Q. Mechanism, treatment.
Show Answer
  • Auto-antibodies internalize NMDA receptors → glutamate hypofunction.
  • Treatment — high-dose steroids + IVIG or PLEX (first-line).
  • Refractory — rituximab + cyclophosphamide.
  • Search for tumor (ovarian teratoma, neuroblastoma).
  • ICU for autonomic / respiratory failure; status epilepticus.
CASE D-7
2-month-old on VA-ECMO requiring sedation; concern about cumulative midazolam exposure.
Q. Sedation strategy.
Show Answer
  • Minimize benzodiazepine + opioid duration.
  • Add dexmedetomidine for sparing.
  • Daily sedation interruption when safe.
  • Validated pediatric sedation scoring (SBS).
  • Develop wean plan and watch for withdrawal.
CASE D-8
Neonate at 36 h with refusal to feed, hypotonia, hyperventilation, ammonia 800.
Q. Mechanism and emergent management.
Show Answer
  • Urea cycle defect (e.g., OTC deficiency).
  • Hyperammonemia → cerebral edema via astrocyte swelling (glutamine).
  • Stop protein; IV dextrose.
  • Sodium phenylacetate-benzoate (Ammonul) or sodium benzoate.
  • L-arginine.
  • Hemodialysis if NH3 >500 or rising.
CASE D-9
28-week preterm with recurrent apnea.
Q. Mechanism and dosing.
Show Answer
  • Caffeine antagonizes adenosine A1 receptors → respiratory drive.
  • Load 20 mg/kg, maintenance 5–10 mg/kg/day.
  • Reduces apnea, BPD, cerebral palsy (CAP trial).
  • Once-daily dosing.
  • Continue until 34–36 weeks corrected or apnea-free for ~7 days.
CASE D-10
8-year-old with bacterial meningitis.
Q. Antibiotic and dosing considerations.
Show Answer
  • Empiric vancomycin + ceftriaxone (CNS doses) until culture.
  • Add ampicillin if <3 mo (Listeria).
  • Dexamethasone with or before first antibiotic dose (Hib hearing loss reduction).
  • Higher doses for adequate CSF levels.
  • BBB permeability drops as inflammation resolves — full course important.
CASE D-11
14-year-old with bilateral optic neuritis and longitudinally extensive transverse myelitis. Anti-AQP4 positive.
Q. Pathophysiology, treatment.
Show Answer
  • Antibody-mediated AQP4 attack (astrocyte end-foot).
  • Complement-mediated damage.
  • Acute — high-dose IV methylprednisolone, PLEX.
  • Maintenance — rituximab, eculizumab, satralizumab, inebilizumab.
  • Avoid β-interferons.
CASE D-12
6-year-old with sickle cell disease has acute right hemiparesis. MRI shows left MCA infarct.
Q. Acute management.
Show Answer
  • Exchange transfusion to reduce HbS <30 %.
  • Hydration, oxygenation.
  • Pediatric stroke consult — consider tPA / thrombectomy in selected cases.
  • Long-term — chronic transfusion or hydroxyurea; TCD monitoring.
CASE D-13
15-year-old football player with mild concussion 2 weeks ago has new concussion event.
Q. Risk and management.
Show Answer
  • Second-impact syndrome — catastrophic edema risk.
  • Mechanism — impaired autoregulation in recovering brain.
  • Acute — emergent CT, observation, ICU monitoring.
  • Return-to-play only after symptom-free, normal neurocognitive testing, graduated exertion.
  • Counseling on long-term CTE risk.
