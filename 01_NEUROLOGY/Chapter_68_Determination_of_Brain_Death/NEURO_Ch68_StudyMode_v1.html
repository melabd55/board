1 · Structured Rogers Mirror

KEY POINTS (pages 3566–3568)
  • Prerequisites must be met first — correction of hypotension, hypothermia, severe metabolic derangements; adequate elimination of sedatives/paralytics.
  • Clinical criteria — deep coma, absence of all brainstem reflexes (including apnea), no motor response (spinal reflexes do not count).
  • Pupils midposition or fully dilated and unreactive; no bulbar movement; absent corneal, gag, cough, sucking, rooting; absent oculocephalic / oculovestibular response; no respiratory effort.
  • Two complete examinations are required in pediatric patients.
  • Recommended observation intervals (Nakagawa 2011): 24 h for term newborn–30 days; 12 h for > 30 days to 18 years.
  • Apnea test threshold: PaCO₂ ≥ 60 mm Hg AND ≥ 20 mm Hg above baseline.
  • Abort apnea test if SpO₂ < 85%, hemodynamic instability, or PaCO₂ target cannot be reached safely.
  • Ancillary studies are NOT required if a complete clinical exam and apnea test can be performed.
  • Indications for ancillary studies: when exam or apnea test cannot be completed, confounders present, validity in question, or to shorten observation interval.
  • Recommendations DO NOT apply to preterm (< 37 weeks) infants.
  • Apnea testing is technically challenging on VA-ECMO and with cyanotic heart disease.
  • Use of a structured brain death checklist reduces diagnostic error.
ICU layer: The structured checklist transforms a high-stakes clinical determination into a reproducible procedure. Every step must be documented.
RCPSC angle: Examiners expect verbatim recall of prerequisites, exam steps, apnea test thresholds, and observation intervals.
INTRODUCTION (pages 3568–3570)
  • Mechanical ventilation, ECMO, anesthetic agents enable survival of patients who would otherwise die — but obscure traditional death determination.
  • 1967 Harvard "irreversible coma" criteria — landmark foundation.
  • President's Commission report (1981) refined modern death determination.
  • Uniform Determination of Death Act (UDDA, 1981) provided US legal framework: death = irreversible cessation of (a) circulatory and respiratory function OR (b) all functions of the entire brain including brainstem.
  • Pediatric criteria distinct from adult — Nakagawa 2011 SCCM/AAP/CNS consensus, updated by Greer 2023 unified guidance.
  • Brain death is a clinical diagnosis; ancillary studies confirm or substitute components only when needed.
PREREQUISITES BEFORE DETERMINATION (pages 3570–3580)

Establish known irreversible cause

  • Coma cause must be known and irreversible (e.g., severe TBI, AHT, HIE post-arrest, fulminant hepatic failure, severe meningitis/encephalitis).
  • If cause unclear, brain death determination should be deferred.

Correct confounders

  • Hemodynamics: MAP must be adequate to provide cerebral perfusion; correct shock; vasopressor support if needed. Hypotension can mimic brain death.
  • Temperature: rewarm to core temperature ≥ 36°C; hypothermia profoundly affects neurologic exam and apnea test.
  • Metabolic: correct severe acidosis, electrolyte disturbances (Na, glucose), hepatic and renal failure where possible.
  • Drugs: ensure adequate time for elimination of sedatives, hypnotics, analgesics, neuromuscular blocking agents. Drug levels may help; consider 4–5 half-lives.
  • Neuromuscular blockade: confirm reversal with peripheral nerve stimulator (4/4 train-of-four).
  • Endocrine: consider central diabetes insipidus — may co-exist with brain death but must not be cause of exam findings.

Adequate observation period

  • Minimum 24–48 h post-arrest or after acute injury, depending on cause, before initiating determination.
  • Longer if anoxic injury, sedation history, severe metabolic derangement.
Trap: Initiating determination with uncorrected hypothermia or sedative on board — invalidates exam.
CLINICAL EXAMINATION (pages 3580–3595)

Coma

  • Unresponsive to deep noxious stimuli (nail bed pressure, supraorbital pressure, trapezius pinch).
  • Spinal reflexes (triple flexion, withdrawal at spinal level, Lazarus sign) do NOT exclude brain death.

Absent brainstem reflexes

  • Pupillary response: midposition (4–6 mm) or fully dilated; no constriction to bright light (use bright handheld light, examine in dark room).
  • Corneal reflex: no blink to cotton swab, gauze, or saline applied to cornea.
  • Oculocephalic ("doll's eyes"): no eye movement with rapid head turn (avoid in cervical instability).
  • Oculovestibular (cold caloric): no eye movement after head of bed at 30°, irrigation of each ear with 50 mL ice water; observe ≥1 min; allow ≥5 min between ears.
  • Gag reflex: no response to posterior pharynx stimulation with tongue depressor or suction catheter.
  • Cough reflex: no response to deep endotracheal suctioning.
  • Sucking and rooting reflexes: absent in neonates and young infants.
  • Facial movements: no jaw, grimace, or facial movements to deep stimulation.

Motor exam

  • No purposeful response to noxious stimuli centrally or peripherally.
  • Spinal reflexes (triple flexion, deep tendon reflexes, plantar response) may persist and do NOT exclude brain death.
  • Distinguish complex spinal-origin movements ("Lazarus sign") from cortically-mediated movements — should not be considered evidence of brain function.

APNEA TEST (pages 3595–3610)

Prerequisites

  • Hemodynamic stability: MAP age-appropriate; on minimal or stable vasopressor support.
  • Core temperature ≥ 36°C.
  • Pre-test PaCO₂ at baseline (~35–45 mm Hg) — pre-oxygenate with FiO₂ 1.0 for 10 min to reach SpO₂ ~100%, PaO₂ ≥ 200 mm Hg.
  • Pre-test PaCO₂ measured by ABG.

Performing the test

  • Disconnect from ventilator; deliver passive 100% O₂ via T-piece or catheter at low flow (6 L/min in older child) to prevent hypoxia.
  • Observe for spontaneous respiratory effort for 8–10 min.
  • Repeat ABG at end-test.
  • Threshold: PaCO₂ ≥ 60 mm Hg AND ≥ 20 mm Hg above baseline.
  • Test positive (consistent with brain death): no respiratory effort throughout.

Abort criteria

  • SpO₂ < 85% sustained.
  • MAP drops below age-appropriate target despite vasopressors.
  • Arrhythmia (significant).
  • PaCO₂ target cannot be reached without unacceptable physiologic compromise.
  • Reconnect ventilator; resume support; do not proceed with this examiner's apnea test — request ancillary study.

Special considerations

  • Chronic CO₂ retention (chronic lung disease) — use ≥ 20 mm Hg above baseline as the operative threshold, recognizing PaCO₂ may need to be higher.
  • Patient is recommended to be ventilated at baseline PaCO₂ before the test; pre-test baseline determined by ABG, not assumed.
  • Cyanotic CHD: pre-test SpO₂ targets may be lower; consult pediatric cardiology.
  • ECMO: see special section.
Trap: Skipping the apnea test or substituting ancillary test without justification — invalidates determination.
NUMBER AND TIMING OF EXAMINATIONS (page 3610)
  • Two complete examinations required in pediatric patients (different from updated 2023 adult unified guideline).
  • Each examination must include full clinical exam + apnea test (unless contraindicated).
  • Observation intervals (Nakagawa 2011):
    • Term newborn–30 days: 24 hours.
    • > 30 days–18 years: 12 hours.
  • Greer 2023 unified guideline:
    • Newborn–30 days: minimum 24 hours.
    • > 30 days–18 years: minimum 12 hours.
    • Two examinations remain the pediatric standard.
  • Recommended that the two examinations be performed by different qualified physicians.
  • Documentation requirements vary by jurisdiction; consult institutional and provincial policy.
RCPSC angle: Pediatric requires TWO examinations; adult unified guideline now allows ONE in many circumstances. Examiners often probe this distinction.
ANCILLARY STUDIES (pages 3610–3622)

Indications

  • Component of neurologic exam or apnea test cannot be completed (facial trauma, cervical instability, severe pulmonary disease, hemodynamic instability).
  • Confounders present (drugs, metabolic, hypothermia) and cannot be reversed.
  • Concern about validity of clinical exam.
  • To reduce observation period between examinations (per Greer 2023 framework in select cases).

Types

  • Cerebral blood flow studies:
    • Radionuclide cerebral perfusion scintigraphy (Tc-99m HMPAO or DTPA) — "hollow skull" sign.
    • CT angiography (CTA) — absence of intracranial vascular filling.
    • Conventional cerebral angiography — gold standard for absence of intracranial blood flow.
    • MR angiography — emerging.
    • Transcranial Doppler — operator-dependent; supportive but typically not stand-alone.
  • Electrocerebral silence (EEG):
    • Continuous EEG ≥ 30 minutes meeting ACNS criteria for electrocerebral inactivity.
    • More susceptible to drug and hypothermia confounding.
    • Less reliable in young infants (background may be inherently low voltage).

Limitations

  • Ancillary studies confirm absence of blood flow OR absent cortical electrical activity — they do not replace the clinical exam.
  • False positives (e.g., scalp electrodes after craniectomy distort EEG; preserved blood flow through small collaterals in early phase).
  • Should be obtained AFTER prerequisites met and clinical exam attempted.
SPECIAL POPULATIONS (pages 3622–3630)

Neonates and preterm infants

  • Term newborn (≥ 37 weeks) up to 30 days — Nakagawa 2011 criteria with 24-h interval.
  • Preterm (< 37 weeks) — Nakagawa 2011 does not provide criteria; case-by-case with neurology/neonatology involvement.
  • Sucking and rooting reflexes must be tested in neonates.
  • Apnea test challenges in young infants — surfactant deficiency, lung disease, hemodynamic instability.

ECMO patients

  • VA-ECMO complicates apnea test — ECMO provides oxygenation and CO₂ removal regardless of native respiration.
  • Strategies: reduce sweep gas flow during test to allow CO₂ accumulation; monitor closely.
  • Targeted increase in PaCO₂ by adjusting sweep gas with serial ABGs.
  • Ancillary study may be required if apnea test technically infeasible.
  • Cardiology, ECMO team coordination essential.

Cyanotic heart disease

  • Baseline SpO₂ may be lower than 85%; standard abort threshold may not apply.
  • Pre-oxygenate as feasible; modify thresholds with cardiology input.
  • Ancillary study often needed.

Severe pulmonary disease

  • Inability to safely raise PaCO₂ due to baseline ventilator settings or pulmonary dysfunction.
  • Ancillary study indicated.

Therapeutic hypothermia / TTM

  • Defer brain death determination until rewarmed to ≥ 36°C and sedatives metabolized (typically 24–72 h after rewarming).
  • Sedative half-lives prolonged at low temperature.
DOCUMENTATION & CHECKLIST (pages 3630–3635)
  • Use standardized brain death checklist:
    • Verify cause and irreversibility.
    • Verify prerequisites met.
    • Document clinical exam (each reflex).
    • Document apnea test results.
    • Specify time of brain death (typically time of second apnea test if positive, or completion of ancillary study).
    • Qualified examiner credentials documented.
    • Family communication notes.
  • Reduces diagnostic error and provides legal documentation.
  • Many hospitals use a two-page printed/electronic form.
  • Coordinate with organ donation organization (ODO) separately from determination team — donation discussions occur AFTER determination is complete.
  • Cultural and religious considerations: some families/communities may not accept brain death; engage palliative care and cultural advisors.

SAQ 1. Define brain death.

  • Irreversible cessation of all functions of the entire brain, including the brainstem.
  • Per UDDA 1981 — death = irreversible cessation of (a) circulatory and respiratory function OR (b) all functions of entire brain including brainstem.

SAQ 2. List the prerequisites before initiating brain death determination.

  • Known irreversible cause of coma.
  • Core temperature ≥ 36°C.
  • MAP age-appropriate (≥ 5th centile).
  • Severe metabolic / electrolyte / glucose derangements corrected.
  • Sedatives metabolized (4–5 half-lives; drug levels if available).
  • NMB reversed (TOF 4/4).
  • Adequate observation period (typically ≥ 24 h after acute injury).

SAQ 3. List the brainstem reflexes that must be absent.

  • Pupillary (midposition or fully dilated and unreactive).
  • Corneal.
  • Oculocephalic ("doll's eyes").
  • Oculovestibular (cold caloric).
  • Gag.
  • Cough.
  • Sucking and rooting (neonates and young infants).
  • Facial movements / jaw / grimace.
  • Respiratory effort (apnea test).

SAQ 4. Why do spinal reflexes NOT exclude brain death?

  • Spinal reflexes (triple flexion, deep tendon reflexes, plantar response) are mediated by spinal cord, not brain.
  • Brain death = cessation of brain function only.
  • Patient may exhibit complex movements ("Lazarus sign") that are spinal-mediated.
  • Do not be misled.

SAQ 5. Apnea test threshold.

  • PaCO₂ ≥ 60 mm Hg AND ≥ 20 mm Hg above pre-test baseline.
  • Test positive if no respiratory effort throughout.

SAQ 6. Apnea test abort criteria.

  • SpO₂ < 85% sustained.
  • Significant hemodynamic instability.
  • Arrhythmia.
  • PaCO₂ target cannot be safely achieved.
  • Reconnect ventilator; document; request ancillary study.

SAQ 7. Pre-oxygenation for apnea test.

  • FiO₂ 1.0 for 10 minutes pre-test.
  • Target SpO₂ ~100%, PaO₂ ≥ 200 mm Hg.
  • Baseline ABG to establish pre-test PaCO₂.

SAQ 8. How is oxygen delivered during apnea testing?

  • Disconnect ventilator.
  • Passive 100% O₂ via T-piece or low-flow catheter (~6 L/min).
  • Maintain oxygenation while CO₂ rises.

SAQ 9. Pediatric observation intervals (Nakagawa 2011).

  • Term newborn–30 days: 24 hours.
  • > 30 days–18 years: 12 hours.
  • Two complete examinations required.

SAQ 10. How does Greer 2023 differ from Nakagawa 2011?

  • Greer 2023 unifies pediatric and adult guidance.
  • Pediatric retains 2-exam standard with same intervals as Nakagawa 2011.
  • Adult unified guideline allows ONE exam in many circumstances.
  • Other refinements: ancillary use, ECMO testing, documentation.

SAQ 11. Indications for ancillary studies.

  • Component of clinical exam cannot be completed (facial trauma, c-spine instability).
  • Apnea test contraindicated or aborted.
  • Persistent confounders despite efforts.
  • Concern about validity of clinical exam.
  • To shorten observation interval (per Greer 2023 framework in select cases).

SAQ 12. Types of ancillary studies.

  • Cerebral blood flow:
    • Radionuclide perfusion scintigraphy ("hollow skull").
    • CT angiography (CTA).
    • Conventional cerebral angiography (gold standard).
    • TCD (supportive, not stand-alone).
  • Electrocerebral silence on EEG (≥ 30 min per ACNS).

SAQ 13. Why is EEG less reliable in young infants?

  • Background EEG may be inherently low voltage in neonates.
  • Risk of falsely meeting "electrocerebral silence" criteria.
  • Cerebral perfusion studies generally preferred in this age group.

SAQ 14. Why are ancillary studies NOT substitutes for clinical exam?

  • Brain death is fundamentally a clinical diagnosis.
  • Ancillary studies confirm specific physiologic finding (absent flow OR absent EEG) — not absence of all brain function.
  • False positives possible (post-craniectomy distortion, residual flow).
  • Use only when clinical exam cannot be completed.

SAQ 15. Apnea testing on VA-ECMO.

  • ECMO provides oxygenation and CO₂ removal regardless of native respiration.
  • Modification: reduce sweep gas flow to allow CO₂ accumulation.
  • Serial ABGs to track PaCO₂.
  • Same threshold (≥ 60 AND ≥ 20 above baseline).
  • If unable, use ancillary cerebral perfusion study.
  • Coordinate with ECMO team.

SAQ 16. Preterm (< 37 weeks) brain death determination.

  • Nakagawa 2011 does NOT provide formal criteria.
  • Proceed case-by-case with neurology, neonatology, ethics input.
  • Limited reliability of clinical exam in immature nervous system.
  • Cerebral perfusion studies often used.

SAQ 17. Cyanotic heart disease and apnea test.

  • Baseline SpO₂ may be lower than 85%.
  • Standard abort threshold may not apply — modify with cardiology input.
  • Pre-oxygenate as feasible; document baseline.
  • Ancillary study often required.

SAQ 18. Therapeutic hypothermia and brain death determination.

  • Defer determination until rewarmed to ≥ 36°C.
  • Sedative half-lives prolonged during/after hypothermia.
  • Typically wait 24–72 h after rewarming before exam.
  • Verify sedatives metabolized and TOF 4/4.

SAQ 19. Two qualified examiners requirement.

  • Both examinations should be performed by different qualified physicians.
  • Reduces examiner bias and error.
  • Each must complete full clinical exam + apnea test (or ancillary if indicated).
  • Document credentials.

SAQ 20. Time of death documentation.

  • Typically time of second positive apnea test.
  • If ancillary study used in second exam, time of ancillary study completion.
  • Document precisely on death certificate and medical record.

SAQ 21. Why is checklist documentation essential?

  • Reduces diagnostic error.
  • Provides legal documentation.
  • Ensures all components addressed.
  • Lewis 2020 international survey — wide variation reduced by standardized tools.

SAQ 22. Pupil findings in brain death.

  • Midposition (4–6 mm) or fully dilated.
  • No constriction to bright light.
  • Use bright handheld light; examine in dark room.
  • Both eyes assessed.

SAQ 23. Cold caloric (oculovestibular) test technique.

  • Head of bed elevated 30°.
  • Confirm clear external auditory canal and intact tympanic membrane.
  • Irrigate each ear with 50 mL ice water.
  • Observe for ≥ 1 minute.
  • Allow ≥ 5 minutes between ears.
  • Absence of any eye movement is consistent with absent brainstem function.

SAQ 24. Cough reflex testing.

  • Deep endotracheal suctioning to carina.
  • Observe for absence of cough or motor response.
  • Should not respond to oropharyngeal or tracheal stimulation.

SAQ 25. Sucking and rooting reflexes — when tested.

  • Tested in neonates and young infants.
  • Sucking: stroke palate or insert finger; absent suck.
  • Rooting: stroke perioral skin; absent turning.

SAQ 26. Chronic CO₂ retention and apnea test.

  • Chronic lung disease patients may have baseline PaCO₂ > 45.
  • Threshold of ≥ 20 mm Hg above baseline applies.
  • PaCO₂ may need to reach > 60 for this patient population.
  • Document baseline carefully.

SAQ 27. When is determination of death the appropriate time to discuss organ donation?

  • Donation discussion occurs AFTER brain death determination is complete.
  • By Organ Donation Organization (ODO), separate from determination team.
  • Reduces conflict of interest; maintains family trust.
  • Family's decision; cultural and religious considerations respected.

SAQ 28. DBD vs DCD pathways.

  • DBD = Donation after Brain Death — patient declared brain dead, then donation.
  • DCD = Donation after Circulatory Death — patient does not meet brain death criteria but family elects WLST; donation proceeds after cardiac death.
  • Both require specific protocols; family decision; ODO involvement.

SAQ 29. Family refusal to accept brain death determination.

  • Engage palliative care, ethics consultation, hospital legal counsel.
  • Cultural and religious considerations (some traditions do not accept brain death).
  • Explain determination clearly; provide written materials.
  • Time for family to accept; allow appropriate rituals.
  • If persistent refusal, follow institutional and provincial policy on body management.

SAQ 30. Common causes of pediatric brain death.

  • Severe traumatic brain injury (motor vehicle, fall, AHT).
  • Anoxic-ischemic injury (cardiac arrest, drowning).
  • Intracranial hemorrhage.
  • CNS infection.
  • Tumor with herniation.
  • Status epilepticus refractory.

SAQ 31. Brain death determination on a patient who is hypotensive.

  • Defer determination.
  • Increase MAP support with vasopressors to age-appropriate level.
  • Verify perfusion (urine output, lactate).
  • Only proceed with exam once stable.

SAQ 32. Lazarus sign.

  • Complex spinal-mediated movement seen rarely in brain dead patients.
  • Includes arm flexion, raising of arms.
  • Spinal cord origin — does NOT exclude brain death.
  • Family education important to prevent distress.

SAQ 33. Hormonal management during brain death (for organ donation).

  • Diabetes insipidus from hypothalamic injury — DDAVP.
  • Hypothyroidism — IV T3 or T4.
  • Hydrocortisone for adrenal insufficiency.
  • Maintains organ viability.
  • Per UNOS donor management protocols.

SAQ 34. Common errors in pediatric brain death determination.

  • Premature initiation with sedatives still on board.
  • Inadequate rewarming after TTM.
  • Skipping apnea test without indication.
  • Misinterpreting spinal reflexes as brain function.
  • Inadequate observation interval.
  • Poor documentation.

SAQ 35. Brain death vs persistent vegetative state.

  • Brain death: irreversible cessation of all brain function INCLUDING brainstem; no awareness, no brainstem reflexes, no respiratory drive.
  • PVS: preserved brainstem function (breathing, reflexes) without cortical awareness.
  • Critical distinction — PVS is not death.
  • Different ethical/legal implications.

SAQ 36. Brain death vs locked-in syndrome.

  • Locked-in: preserved consciousness, paralysis (typically pontine lesion preserving vertical eye movements and blinking).
  • Patient is AWAKE despite inability to move or speak.
  • NOT brain death.
  • Recognized by careful exam of vertical eye movements.

SAQ 37. Brain death in pregnancy.

  • Rare situation — ethical and clinical complexities.
  • Maternal brain death may permit continued somatic support for fetal viability.
  • Multidisciplinary team: ICU, OB, neonatology, ethics, hospital legal.
  • Family wishes paramount.

SAQ 38. Continuing care after brain death.

  • Brain death = legal death.
  • Continuing somatic support is only justified for organ donation or family acceptance time.
  • Discontinue mechanical support per protocol after appropriate notification and donation pathway followed.
  • Outside of donation context, prolonged somatic support of a brain dead patient is not standard care.

SAQ 39. Provincial / institutional variation in pediatric brain death.

  • Canadian provinces and US states have specific legal requirements.
  • Number of qualified examiners, documentation requirements vary.
  • Some jurisdictions have specific requirements for neurology/neurosurgery involvement.
  • Consult institutional policy and provincial regulations.

SAQ 40. Top 5 examiner traps in pediatric brain death.

  • Initiating determination with sedatives on board or hypothermic.
  • Substituting ancillary study without justification.
  • Misinterpreting spinal reflexes (Lazarus sign) as brain function.
  • Combining determination team with organ donation discussion.
  • Failing to use structured checklist for documentation.

Three-Step Framework

  • Step 1: Prerequisites (cause, temp, MAP, drugs, NMB, observation).
  • Step 2: Clinical exam (coma + all brainstem reflexes + apnea test).
  • Step 3: Repeat after observation interval (24 h ≤ 30 d; 12 h > 30 d) with different examiner.

Apnea Test Essentials

  • Pre-oxygenate FiO₂ 1.0 × 10 min.
  • Baseline ABG.
  • Disconnect ventilator; passive 100% O₂; observe 8–10 min.
  • Threshold: PaCO₂ ≥ 60 AND ≥ 20 above baseline.
  • Abort: SpO₂ < 85%, hemodynamic instability, arrhythmia.

Ancillary Studies

  • Cerebral blood flow (perfusion scintigraphy, CTA, conventional angiography).
  • Electrocerebral silence (EEG ≥ 30 min per ACNS).
  • NOT a substitute for clinical exam.
  • Indications: exam component impossible, persistent confounders, validity concerns.

Special Populations

  • Preterm < 37 wk: no formal criteria.
  • Neonate term–30 d: 24 h interval; test sucking/rooting.
  • ECMO: reduce sweep gas for apnea test; ancillary if unable.
  • Cyanotic CHD: modify SpO₂ thresholds; cardiology input.
  • TTM: rewarm to ≥ 36°C first; wait sedative metabolization.

Documentation

  • Use standardized checklist.
  • Two qualified examiners.
  • Time of death = time of second positive apnea test or ancillary study.
  • Family notification.
  • ODO involvement SEPARATELY for donation.

Red Flags

  • Hypothermia, sedation, paralytics, hypotension, severe metabolic — defer.
  • Unclear cause — defer until workup complete.
  • Preterm — case-by-case.
  • Spinal reflexes (triple flexion, Lazarus) — NOT brain function.
  • Locked-in syndrome — preserved consciousness, NOT brain death.

Exam Pearls

  • Verbatim intervals: 24 h ≤ 30 d, 12 h > 30 d.
  • Verbatim threshold: PaCO₂ ≥ 60 AND ≥ 20 above baseline.
  • Spinal reflexes do NOT exclude brain death.
  • Ancillary not substitute for clinical exam.
  • ODO separate from determination team.
  • Use checklist always.
