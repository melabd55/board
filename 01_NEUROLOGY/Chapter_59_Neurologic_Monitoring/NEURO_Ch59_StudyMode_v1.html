CHAPTER 59 ■ Neurologic Monitoring — Key Points

Key points distilled
  • Neuromonitoring = continuous surveillance of CNS functional integrity to enable real-time intervention.
  • Aim — detect potentially injurious physiological derangements; modify management to prevent permanent damage.
  • Each modality has unique engineering, physiologic, and interpretive principles that must be understood at the bedside.
  • Digital signal processing uses moving windows — displayed values lag actual events by seconds; alarms fire after a sustained breach.
  • Evidence that any single neuromonitor improves outcome remains limited — multimodal integration is the current direction.
RCPSC pearl 🔥🔥🔥
  • Examiners frame "multimodal neuromonitoring" stems — recognize each modality's signal, limitation, and triggered intervention.
Pages 3082–3084 · Scientific Foundations + Engineering Terms

SCIENTIFIC FOUNDATIONS

Core principles
  • All monitors convert biologic signals to electrical or optical readings via transducers.
  • Signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) determines reliability — minimize noise (electrical, mechanical) to preserve fidelity.
  • Sampling rate — must satisfy Nyquist criterion (≥2× highest frequency of interest).
  • Frequency response — low-pass, high-pass, band-pass filters shape the signal.
  • Calibration and zeroing — essential for absolute pressure measurements (ICP, ABP); transducer position matters (level to tragus / external auditory canal).

Pages 3085–3090 · Intracranial Pressure (ICP) Monitoring

Intracranial Pressure Monitoring

Catheter types
  • External ventricular drain (EVD) — gold standard; intraventricular; calibrated in situ; allows CSF drainage; recalibration possible; infection risk ~5–10 %.
  • Parenchymal fiber-optic / strain-gauge probe (Codman, Camino, Raumedic, Spiegelberg) — easier placement; no drainage; cannot be re-zeroed in situ; drift over days.
  • Subdural / epidural bolts — less accurate; rarely used.
  • Lumbar pressure — only if no obstruction; risks herniation in mass effect.
Indications (pediatric TBI guideline)
  • Severe TBI (GCS ≤8) with abnormal admission CT.
  • Severe TBI with normal CT but two of — age >40, motor posturing, hypotension.
  • Selected post-cardiac-arrest, post-stroke, hepatic encephalopathy, fulminant CNS infection cases.
ICP thresholds (age-adjusted)
  • Infant — >15 mmHg sustained.
  • Older child / adolescent — >20 mmHg sustained >5 min.
  • CPP target (MAP − ICP) — infant ≥40; toddler ≥50; child ≥60; adolescent ≥70.
ICP waveform components
  • P1 (percussion wave) — arterial pulsation, sharp.
  • P2 (tidal wave) — brain compliance; if P2 > P1, compliance impaired.
  • P3 (dicrotic wave) — aortic valve closure.
  • Lundberg A waves (plateau) — ICP >50 mmHg sustained >5 min, ominous.
  • Lundberg B waves — 30 s–2 min oscillations, mild concern.
Pearl 🔥🔥🔥
  • P2 > P1 = decreased intracranial compliance even when mean ICP is "normal" — early warning.
  • Lundberg A wave = impending herniation — emergent action.
Pages 3091–3095 · Continuous EEG and Cerebral Function Monitoring

Continuous EEG (cEEG)

Indications
  • Status epilepticus / suspected NCSE.
  • Unexplained altered mental status.
  • Post-cardiac-arrest care.
  • Severe TBI.
  • Suspected encephalitis / autoimmune encephalitis.
  • Neonatal seizures.
  • Prognostication after anoxic injury.
Patterns to recognize
  • Background — continuous, discontinuous, burst-suppression, isoelectric.
  • Reactivity — change in pattern with stimulation; preserved reactivity = better prognosis.
  • Status patterns — generalized periodic discharges (GPD), lateralized periodic discharges (LPD), rhythmic delta activity (RDA).
  • Stimulus-induced patterns (SIRPIDs) — clinical significance debated.
  • Sleep architecture — preservation predicts recovery.

Amplitude-Integrated EEG (aEEG)

  • Time-compressed, filtered EEG displayed as upper and lower bandwidth.
  • Commonly used in neonates — HIE prognostication, neonatal seizures.
  • Patterns — continuous normal voltage (CNV), discontinuous (DNV), burst-suppression, low voltage, isoelectric.
  • Misses brief or low-amplitude seizures; complement with full cEEG when feasible.
Pearl 🔥🔥🔥
  • Up to 50 % of seizures in critically ill children are nonconvulsive — cEEG is required to detect them.
  • Burst-suppression target in pentobarbital coma — suppression 90–95 % or 6–10 s of background per minute.
Pages 3096–3100 · Transcranial Doppler (TCD)

Transcranial Doppler Ultrasound

Principle
  • Insonates intracranial arteries through bone "windows" (temporal, transorbital, suboccipital).
  • Measures flow velocity (cm/s) — peak systolic, end diastolic, mean.
  • Pulsatility index (PI) = (PSV − EDV) / Mean — reflects downstream vascular resistance.
Pediatric applications
  • Sickle cell stroke screening — STOP trial; primary prevention transfusion when MCA TAMM >200 cm/s.
  • Vasospasm after SAH or meningitis.
  • Cerebral autoregulation monitoring.
  • ECMO neuromonitoring.
  • Brain death ancillary confirmation (oscillating flow, systolic spikes only).
Limitations
  • Operator-dependent.
  • Requires adequate bone windows (~10 % of older adults lack temporal window).
  • Cannot replace direct ICP measurement.
Pages 3101–3105 · Near-Infrared Spectroscopy (NIRS) and Cerebral Oxygenation

NIRS

Principle
  • Near-infrared light (700–900 nm) penetrates scalp / skull; absorbed differentially by oxy- and deoxy-hemoglobin.
  • Reports regional oxygen saturation (rSO₂) — venous-weighted (~75 %); reflects ~1–2 cm of frontal cortex.
  • Cerebral oximetry useful trend monitor.
Use cases
  • Pediatric cardiac surgery and ECMO.
  • TBI — secondary deterioration detection.
  • Neonatal HIE, cardiac arrest, sepsis.
  • Cerebral palsy / bypass risk stratification.
Action thresholds
  • rSO₂ <40 % or >25 % drop from individual baseline — investigate and intervene.
  • Bilateral asymmetry >10 % — suspect focal pathology (catheter malposition, embolism).

Other Cerebral Oxygenation Tools

  • Brain tissue oxygen (PbtO₂) — intraparenchymal probe; threshold <15 mmHg suggests ischemia; trial benefit in adult TBI mixed (BOOST-3 vs. BONANZA), pediatric data emerging.
  • Jugular venous oxygen saturation (SjvO₂) — global measure; <55 % suggests hypoperfusion; rarely used routinely now.
  • Cerebral microdialysis — measures glucose, lactate, pyruvate, glycerol; lactate/pyruvate ratio >25–40 suggests anaerobic metabolism.
Pages 3106–3110 · Autoregulation Monitoring

Cerebral Autoregulation Monitoring

Indices
  • Pressure Reactivity Index (PRx) — Pearson correlation of slow waves of MAP and ICP over a moving 5-min window.
  • Mean Velocity Index (Mx) — correlation of TCD MCA velocity with CPP.
  • Cerebral Oximetry Index (COx) — correlation of NIRS rSO₂ with MAP.
  • PRx <0 = preserved autoregulation; >0.3 = impaired.
CPPopt
  • Plot PRx vs. CPP across moving windows.
  • U-shaped curve — minimum PRx identifies optimal CPP (CPPopt).
  • Maintaining CPP near CPPopt improves outcomes (observational pediatric data).
Pearl 🔥🔥
  • PRx-guided CPPopt is the frontier — replacing one-size-fits-all CPP thresholds in pediatric TBI.
Pages 3111–3121 · Multimodal integration + Conclusion

Multimodal Neuromonitoring

  • Combine ICP, CPP, PRx, PbtO₂, NIRS, cEEG, TCD into integrated dashboard.
  • Trends and patterns outweigh individual values.
  • Trigger interventions when ≥2 modalities suggest deterioration.
  • Documentation and protocol-driven response improve consistency.
ICU layer 🔥🔥🔥
  • Build a routine — rounds review trend curves of all modalities together; document interventions.
  • Distinguish artifact vs. real signal before escalating.
  • Train nurses and trainees to recognize alarm patterns and lag.

Conclusion

  • No single neuromonitor improves pediatric outcome alone.
  • Multimodal integration with CPPopt-guided care is the emerging standard.
  • Future — machine learning, individualized biomarker-based thresholds.

3. Global Evidence Layer — Full Citations

① Pediatric Severe TBI Guidelines — Brain Trauma Foundation 3rd Edition

  • Source: Kochanek PM, Tasker RC et al. Pediatr Crit Care Med. 2019;20(3S):S1–S82
  • Link: DOI 10.1097/PCC.0000000000001735
  • Key findings:
    • ICP monitoring considered in severe TBI with abnormal CT or 2 of (age, posturing, hypotension).
    • Tiered ICP management; hypertonic saline first-line.
    • Age-adjusted CPP targets; PRx-derived CPPopt emerging.

② STOP Trial — Stroke Prevention in Sickle Cell with TCD

  • Source: Adams RJ et al. N Engl J Med. 1998;339:5–11 (foundational); STOP2 — Adams RJ et al. N Engl J Med. 2005;353:2769–2778
  • Link: STOP DOI 10.1056/NEJM199807023390102 · STOP2 DOI 10.1056/NEJMoa050460
  • Key findings:
    • TCD-detected MCA TAMM >200 cm/s identifies children with SCD at high primary stroke risk.
    • Chronic transfusion to HbS <30 % reduces stroke by ~90 %.
    • TWiTCH trial — hydroxyurea can replace transfusion in selected stable patients.

③ ACNS Consensus on cEEG in Critically Ill Patients

  • Source: Herman ST et al. J Clin Neurophysiol. 2015;32:96–108; updated terminology Hirsch LJ et al. J Clin Neurophysiol. 2021;38:1–29
  • Link: DOI 10.1097/WNP.0000000000000165 · ACNS: acns.org/practice/guidelines
  • Key findings:
    • Indications — unexplained altered mental status, suspected NCSE, post-arrest, severe TBI.
    • Up to 50 % of seizures in critically ill children are nonconvulsive.
    • Standardized terminology for periodic and rhythmic patterns (Salzburg, ACNS 2021).

④ BOOST-3 Trial — PbtO₂-Guided TBI Management

  • Source: Bernard F et al. N Engl J Med. 2024 (BOOST-3 main results); BONANZA Australian trial ongoing
  • Key findings:
    • Adding PbtO₂ to ICP-only management did not significantly improve outcomes in severe TBI in adults.
    • Pediatric data limited; emerging interest in CPPopt + PbtO₂ multimodal protocols.

⑤ PRx and CPPopt in Pediatric TBI

  • Source: Smith CA et al. Pediatr Crit Care Med. 2020;21:e672–e679; Lewis PM et al. Pediatr Crit Care Med. 2020;21:e164–e173
  • Key findings:
    • PRx-derived CPPopt feasible in pediatric TBI.
    • Deviation from CPPopt associates with worse outcome.
    • Individualized CPP target replacing one-size thresholds.

⑥ NIRS in Pediatric Cardiac Surgery

  • Source: Hoffman GM et al. (multiple) — pediatric cardiac NIRS protocols; review — Tortoriello TA et al. Pediatr Anesth. 2005;15:495–503
  • Key findings:
    • rSO₂ <40 % or sustained drop >25 % triggers structured response (ABC check, vasoactives, transfusion, decompression).
    • Asymmetry >10 % suggests focal pathology.
    • Routine in pediatric cardiac centers; outcome impact still under study.

⑦ Pediatric Neurocritical Care Research Group — Multimodal Approaches

  • Source: Appavu B, Foreman B, Hu X et al. Neurocrit Care. 2022;37(Suppl 2):S105–S128
  • Link: DOI 10.1007/s12028-022-01586-0
  • Key findings:
    • Multimodal monitoring (ICP, PbtO₂, PRx, cEEG, TCD, NIRS) protocol-driven response improves consistency.
    • Trends outweigh individual values.

5. ICU Decision Engine

IF Severe pediatric TBI (GCS ≤8) with abnormal admission CT
THEN
  • Place ICP monitor (EVD preferred when CSF drainage potentially useful).
  • Maintain age-adjusted CPP.
  • Continuous EEG.
  • Consider PbtO₂ and PRx-derived CPPopt.
IF Persistent altered mental status without clinical seizures
THEN
  • Continuous EEG to detect NCSE (up to 50 % of seizures in critically ill children are nonconvulsive).
  • Treat electrographic seizures per pediatric SE protocol.
IF Sickle cell disease child >2 y
THEN
  • Annual TCD MCA velocity screening.
  • >200 cm/s — primary stroke prevention with chronic transfusion (HbS <30 %).
  • 170–199 cm/s — repeat in 3 months.
IF NIRS rSO₂ <40 % or >25 % drop from baseline
THEN
  • Check airway, ventilation, hemoglobin, CO, MAP.
  • Treat reversible cause (hypotension, anemia, hypoxia).
  • Consider asymmetry as focal pathology.
IF P2 > P1 on ICP waveform despite "normal" mean ICP
THEN
  • Decreased intracranial compliance — high risk for sudden ICP rise.
  • Optimize sedation, head-of-bed elevation, normothermia.
  • Pre-emptive hyperosmolar therapy if trend worsens.
IF Lundberg A (plateau) wave
THEN
  • ICP >50 mmHg sustained — impending herniation.
  • Hyperosmolar therapy.
  • Brief hyperventilation as bridge.
  • Emergent surgical decompression consultation.

6. SAQ Bank — 40 Questions

SAQ 1. Define neuromonitoring and its goal.
Answer.
  • Continuous surveillance of CNS functional integrity.
  • Goal — detect potentially injurious physiologic derangements; modify management to prevent permanent damage.
  • Each modality has unique engineering and interpretive principles.
  • No single monitor improves pediatric outcome alone — multimodal integration is the direction.
SAQ 2. ICP catheter types and trade-offs.
Answer.
  • EVD — gold standard; intraventricular; recalibratable; CSF drainage; infection risk 5–10 %.
  • Parenchymal fiber-optic / strain-gauge — easier; no drainage; cannot rezero in situ; drift.
  • Subdural / epidural bolts — less accurate.
  • Lumbar — only if no obstruction; risks herniation in mass effect.
SAQ 3. Pediatric ICP monitoring indications (BTF 3rd ed).
Answer.
  • Severe TBI (GCS ≤8) with abnormal admission CT.
  • Severe TBI with normal CT plus 2 of — age, motor posturing, hypotension.
  • Selected post-arrest, post-stroke, fulminant CNS infection, hepatic encephalopathy cases.
SAQ 4. Age-adjusted CPP targets.
Answer.
  • Infant ≥40 mmHg.
  • Toddler ≥50 mmHg.
  • Child ≥60 mmHg.
  • Adolescent ≥70 mmHg.
  • Individualized via PRx-derived CPPopt is increasingly used.
SAQ 5. ICP waveform components and pathological signs.
Answer.
  • P1 — percussion (arterial), sharp.
  • P2 — tidal (compliance).
  • P3 — dicrotic (aortic valve closure).
  • Normal — P1 > P2 > P3.
  • P2 > P1 — decreased compliance, early warning.
  • Lundberg A waves — plateau >50 mmHg, sustained >5 min, ominous.
  • Lundberg B waves — 30 s–2 min oscillations, mild concern.
SAQ 6. Continuous EEG indications.
Answer.
  • Status epilepticus / suspected NCSE.
  • Unexplained altered mental status.
  • Post-cardiac-arrest care.
  • Severe TBI.
  • Suspected encephalitis / autoimmune.
  • Neonatal seizures.
  • Prognostication after anoxic injury.
SAQ 7. EEG background patterns and prognosis.
Answer.
  • Continuous normal voltage — better prognosis.
  • Discontinuous — intermediate.
  • Burst-suppression — poor prognosis (unless drug-induced).
  • Low-voltage / isoelectric — very poor.
  • Reactivity preserved — better prognosis.
  • Sleep architecture intact — better prognosis.
SAQ 8. aEEG features in neonatal HIE.
Answer.
  • CNV (continuous normal voltage) — best prognosis.
  • DNV (discontinuous normal voltage) — intermediate.
  • Burst-suppression — guarded.
  • Low-voltage / inactive — poor.
  • Used for hypothermia eligibility and prognostication.
  • Misses brief / low-amplitude seizures — pair with full cEEG when available.
SAQ 9. TCD principle and key parameters.
Answer.
  • Insonates intracranial arteries through bone windows (temporal, transorbital, suboccipital).
  • Measures peak systolic, end diastolic, mean flow velocity.
  • Pulsatility Index = (PSV − EDV) / Mean.
  • PI >1.4 suggests raised ICP or downstream resistance.
SAQ 10. STOP trial thresholds for sickle cell stroke prevention.
Answer.
  • Annual TCD MCA TAMM screening from age 2.
  • >200 cm/s — high risk; chronic transfusion to HbS <30 %.
  • 170–199 cm/s — repeat in 3 months.
  • TWiTCH — hydroxyurea can replace transfusion in selected stable patients.
SAQ 11. NIRS principle and rSO₂ baseline.
Answer.
  • Near-infrared light absorbed by oxy- vs. deoxy-hemoglobin.
  • Reports regional oxygen saturation (rSO₂) — venous-weighted (~75 %).
  • Reflects ~1–2 cm of frontal cortex.
  • Normal rSO₂ 55–75 %.
SAQ 12. NIRS action thresholds.
Answer.
  • rSO₂ <40 % — absolute trigger.
  • >25 % drop from individual baseline — relative trigger.
  • Bilateral asymmetry >10 % — focal pathology (catheter, embolism).
  • Investigate airway, ventilation, hemoglobin, CO, MAP.
SAQ 13. Brain tissue oxygen (PbtO₂) thresholds.
Answer.
  • Intraparenchymal probe in frontal lobe.
  • Normal >25 mmHg.
  • Mild ischemia 15–25 mmHg.
  • Severe ischemia <15 mmHg.
  • BOOST-3 trial did not show improved outcome from PbtO₂-guided care in adult TBI; pediatric data emerging.
SAQ 14. Pressure Reactivity Index (PRx).
Answer.
  • Pearson correlation of slow-wave MAP and ICP over moving 5-min window.
  • PRx <0 — preserved autoregulation.
  • PRx >0.3 — impaired.
  • Used to derive CPPopt — minimum PRx identifies optimal CPP.
SAQ 15. Cerebral microdialysis biomarkers.
Answer.
  • Glucose, lactate, pyruvate, glycerol.
  • Lactate/pyruvate ratio >25–40 suggests anaerobic metabolism.
  • Glycerol — membrane damage.
  • Limited pediatric use; research tool.
SAQ 16. Multimodal neuromonitoring concept.
Answer.
  • Combine ICP, CPP, PRx, PbtO₂, NIRS, cEEG, TCD.
  • Trends and patterns outweigh individual values.
  • Trigger intervention when ≥2 modalities suggest deterioration.
  • Protocol-driven response improves consistency.
SAQ 17. EVD complications.
Answer.
  • Infection (5–10 % rate, increases after 5 days).
  • Hemorrhage during placement.
  • Catheter occlusion / malposition.
  • Overdrainage with herniation upward (rare) or subdural hematoma.
  • Antibiotic-impregnated catheters and chlorhexidine bundle reduce infection.
SAQ 18. Burst-suppression EEG and treatment goal.
Answer.
  • Defined as bursts of activity alternating with low-voltage / isoelectric periods.
  • Drug-induced in pentobarbital coma for refractory SE — target suppression 90–95 % or 6–10 s background per minute.
  • Spontaneous burst-suppression suggests poor prognosis (anoxic injury, severe HIE).
SAQ 19. Lundberg waves.
Answer.
  • A waves — plateau >50 mmHg, lasting >5 min, ominous; impending herniation.
  • B waves — 30 s–2 min oscillations, mild concern; may reflect altered respiratory pattern.
  • C waves — small short oscillations, normal variant.
SAQ 20. ECMO neuromonitoring approaches.
Answer.
  • Continuous EEG — seizures, encephalopathy.
  • NIRS — regional cerebral oxygenation; asymmetry suggests focal stroke / cannula issue.
  • TCD — cerebral blood flow patterns; right vs. left.
  • Daily head ultrasound in infants while open fontanelle.
  • Cerebral autoregulation monitoring (PRx) under investigation.
SAQ 21. Brain death ancillary tests.
Answer.
  • EEG — electrocerebral inactivity for 30 min.
  • Cerebral blood flow study — radionuclide angiography showing no intracranial flow.
  • TCD — oscillating flow or systolic spikes only.
  • CT angiography — no opacification of cerebral arteries.
  • Used when apnea test cannot be safely completed.
SAQ 22. Pulsatility Index interpretation.
Answer.
  • PI = (PSV − EDV) / Mean velocity.
  • Normal ~0.8–1.0.
  • Elevated PI (>1.4) — high downstream resistance (raised ICP, vasoconstriction).
  • Low PI — vasodilation, hyperemia.
SAQ 23. TCD in vasospasm.
Answer.
  • Post-SAH or post-meningitis.
  • Increased MCA flow velocity (>200 cm/s) or Lindegaard ratio >3 (MCA / extracranial ICA).
  • Daily monitoring; consider angiography if symptomatic.
  • Treat — hemodynamic optimization, intra-arterial therapy if focal.
SAQ 24. Why is the EEG more sensitive than aEEG for seizure detection?
Answer.
  • aEEG is time-compressed and filtered — misses brief, low-amplitude, or focal seizures.
  • Full cEEG provides multiple channels with broader spatial coverage.
  • aEEG complement, not substitute, when full cEEG is available.
  • Sensitivity for seizure detection on aEEG ~25–80 % depending on seizure type.
SAQ 25. ICP catheter calibration.
Answer.
  • EVD — zero at level of tragus / external auditory canal; recalibrate regularly.
  • Parenchymal probe — zeroed before insertion; cannot recalibrate in situ.
  • Air-filled vs. fluid-filled — fluid-filled requires careful purging.
  • Drift >3 mmHg over 24 h — consider replacing parenchymal probe.
SAQ 26. Cerebral oximetry artifact sources.
Answer.
  • Sensor displacement.
  • External light interference (operating room lights).
  • Hematoma / scalp edema beneath sensor.
  • Methemoglobin / carboxyhemoglobin distort signal.
  • Hypothermia / vasoconstriction may alter values.
SAQ 27. CPPopt-guided care.
Answer.
  • PRx plotted against binned CPP forms U-shape.
  • CPP at nadir of curve is patient-specific CPPopt.
  • Maintaining CPP near CPPopt associated with better outcomes (observational pediatric data).
  • Replacing one-size-fits-all CPP thresholds.
SAQ 28. Cerebral oximetry use cases.
Answer.
  • Pediatric cardiac surgery.
  • ECMO monitoring.
  • TBI — secondary deterioration detection.
  • Neonatal HIE.
  • Cardiac arrest.
  • Sepsis with shock.
SAQ 29. Status epilepticus EEG patterns.
Answer.
  • Generalized periodic discharges (GPD).
  • Lateralized periodic discharges (LPD).
  • Rhythmic delta activity (RDA).
  • Stimulus-induced rhythmic, periodic, or ictal discharges (SIRPIDs).
  • Frank electrographic seizures with evolving frequency / morphology.
SAQ 30. Outline jugular venous oxygen saturation (SjvO₂).
Answer.
  • Catheter placed in internal jugular bulb (retrograde from jugular vein).
  • Reflects global cerebral oxygen extraction.
  • Normal 55–75 %.
  • <55 % — hypoperfusion.
  • >75 % — hyperperfusion or low metabolic demand.
  • Largely supplanted by NIRS and PbtO₂.
SAQ 31. Latency / lag of bedside monitors.
Answer.
  • Digital signal processing uses moving windows — values are averaged over past data.
  • Alarms fire after sustained breach for seconds.
  • Displayed values lag actual events by seconds.
  • Recovery also lags — values catch up slowly after intervention.
  • Trend the signal — single values may mislead.
SAQ 32. Cerebral autoregulation monitoring tools beyond PRx.
Answer.
  • Mean Velocity Index (Mx) — TCD MCA velocity vs. CPP.
  • Cerebral Oximetry Index (COx) — NIRS rSO₂ vs. MAP.
  • Pressure Reactivity Index (PRx) — ICP vs. MAP.
  • HVx — Hb volume index using NIRS.
  • Different indices have different strengths; PRx most studied.
SAQ 33. Continuous EEG montage and electrode standards.
Answer.
  • 10–20 system for electrode placement.
  • Pediatric — reduced montage if scalp small (8 vs. 21 electrodes).
  • Digital sampling 256 Hz minimum.
  • Quantitative EEG (qEEG) — spectrograms, density spectral array (DSA), color-density spectrograms — for trend interpretation.
SAQ 34. Sources of cerebral autoregulation impairment.
Answer.
  • TBI.
  • Hypoxic-ischemic injury.
  • Sepsis.
  • Prematurity (immature autoregulation).
  • Some anesthetics (volatile, propofol).
  • Severe hyperthermia.
  • Vasopressors (high doses).
SAQ 35. Optic nerve sheath diameter (ONSD) ultrasound.
Answer.
  • Non-invasive bedside marker of raised ICP.
  • Measured 3 mm posterior to globe.
  • Adult threshold >5.5–6 mm.
  • Pediatric thresholds age-dependent (~4–4.5 mm in children).
  • Useful screening when invasive ICP not available; not a replacement.
SAQ 36. Evoked potentials in PICU.
Answer.
  • Somatosensory evoked potentials (SSEPs) — useful in coma prognostication after anoxic injury.
  • Bilateral absent N20 cortical response — very poor prognosis.
  • Brainstem auditory evoked responses (BAERs) — brainstem integrity.
  • Visual evoked potentials (VEPs) — visual pathway integrity.
SAQ 37. Standardized critical care EEG terminology (ACNS 2021).
Answer.
  • Background — predominant pattern (continuous, discontinuous, burst-suppression, isoelectric).
  • Periodic discharges — generalized (GPD), lateralized (LPD), bilateral independent.
  • Rhythmic delta activity — generalized (GRDA), lateralized (LRDA).
  • "Plus" features — modifiers indicating ictal-leaning patterns.
  • Ictal-interictal continuum (IIC) — patterns of uncertain seizure status.
SAQ 38. Pitfalls of cerebral oximetry interpretation.
Answer.
  • Baseline varies among patients and devices.
  • Frontal cortex only — regional measure.
  • Cannot distinguish causes (hypoxia vs. ischemia vs. low O₂ extraction).
  • Hematoma / scalp swelling beneath sensor distort signal.
  • Trend matters more than absolute value.
SAQ 39. Multimodal alarm fatigue and how to mitigate.
Answer.
  • Multiple monitors → frequent alarms → desensitization.
  • Customize alarm thresholds per patient.
  • Integrate alarms with EHR.
  • Pattern-recognition algorithms reduce false alarms.
  • Bedside protocols for "real" alarm escalation.
SAQ 40. Future directions in pediatric neuromonitoring.
Answer.
  • Machine learning for early deterioration detection.
  • Individualized CPPopt protocols.
  • Closed-loop autoregulation-guided therapy.
  • Non-invasive ICP estimation (TCD-based, ONSD, pupillometry).
  • Biomarker-integrated dashboards (S100B, GFAP, neurofilament).

7. Case Bank — 25 Interpretation Cases

Case 1 — P2 > P1 on ICP waveform.
8-year-old TBI patient; ICP 15 mmHg but waveform shows P2 amplitude exceeding P1.
Q. Interpretation.
Answer.
  • Decreased intracranial compliance despite normal mean ICP.
  • Early warning — high risk of sudden ICP rise.
  • Optimize sedation, head-of-bed elevation, normothermia.
  • Pre-emptive hyperosmolar therapy if trend worsens.
Case 2 — Lundberg A wave.
12-year-old TBI; ICP rises to 60 mmHg and stays there for 8 minutes.
Q. Action.
Answer.
  • Lundberg A wave — impending herniation.
  • Hyperosmolar therapy (hypertonic saline 3 %).
  • Brief hyperventilation as bridge.
  • Emergent surgical decompression consultation.
  • Continuous EEG; treat seizures.
Case 3 — Sickle cell TCD screening.
5-year-old with sickle cell disease; annual TCD shows MCA TAMM 215 cm/s.
Q. Management.
Answer.
  • >200 cm/s — high stroke risk (STOP trial).
  • Chronic transfusion to HbS <30 %.
  • Continue annual TCD.
  • Once stable, can consider hydroxyurea per TWiTCH trial.
Case 4 — NIRS rSO₂ drop intraoperatively.
Pediatric cardiac surgery — NIRS rSO₂ drops from 70 % to 45 % bilaterally.
Q. Action.
Answer.
  • >25 % drop from baseline — structured response.
  • Check ETT position, ventilator settings, FiO₂.
  • Check hemoglobin, CO, MAP.
  • Increase MAP if low; transfuse if anemic.
  • Consider decompression of cannulae / cardiac issue.
  • Investigate surgical / mechanical causes.
Case 5 — Asymmetric NIRS on ECMO.
2-month-old on VA-ECMO; right NIRS 65 %, left NIRS 35 %.
Q. Implication.
Answer.
  • Asymmetry >10 % suggests focal pathology.
  • Possible — left carotid cannula occlusion, embolic stroke, intracranial hemorrhage.
  • Urgent head ultrasound (if open fontanelle) or CT.
  • Review cannula position and flow.
  • Notify perfusion and surgical team.
Case 6 — Continuous EEG burst-suppression.
6-year-old after cardiac arrest; cEEG shows burst-suppression 12 h post-ROSC.
Q. Implication and action.
Answer.
  • Spontaneous burst-suppression — poor prognostic indicator.
  • Confounders — sedatives (propofol, midazolam, pentobarbital), hypothermia.
  • Hold prognostication ≥72 h; reassess after sedation cleared and normothermia.
  • Continue supportive care.
  • Family meeting with palliative care.
Case 7 — Nonconvulsive status epilepticus.
9-year-old with persistent altered mental status after a focal seizure; clinical exam unremarkable but cEEG shows ongoing rhythmic discharges in the right temporal region.
Q. Diagnosis and management.
Answer.
  • NCSE.
  • Up to 50 % of seizures in critically ill children are nonconvulsive.
  • Treat per pediatric SE protocol — benzodiazepine → fosphenytoin / levetiracetam → midazolam infusion → pentobarbital coma.
  • Investigate etiology — MRI, LP, autoimmune panel.
  • Continuous EEG during management.
Case 8 — PRx-guided CPP optimization.
10-year-old severe TBI; PRx hovering at 0.4 at current CPP 65 mmHg.
Q. Action.
Answer.
  • Impaired autoregulation (PRx >0.3).
  • Plot PRx vs. CPP across the past 4 h to find CPPopt.
  • Titrate MAP / vasoactives to bring CPP near CPPopt.
  • Monitor cerebral oxygenation (PbtO₂, NIRS) in parallel.
  • Re-evaluate every 4–6 h.
Case 9 — TCD vasospasm post-meningitis.
3-year-old post-pneumococcal meningitis; new focal weakness day 7; MCA TAMM 240 cm/s.
Q. Interpretation and management.
Answer.
  • Vasospasm complicating bacterial meningitis.
  • Optimize hemodynamics — avoid hypotension.
  • Consider nimodipine (pediatric data limited).
  • MRI / MRA to confirm and rule out infarction.
  • Daily TCD; intra-arterial therapy if symptomatic and refractory.
Case 10 — Brain death ancillary test.
14-year-old after severe TBI; clinical brain death exam complete but apnea test cannot be safely done (severe hypoxia).
Q. Options.
Answer.
  • Ancillary tests — EEG (electrocerebral inactivity 30 min), radionuclide CBF scan (no intracranial flow), TCD (oscillating flow or systolic spikes only), CTA (no intracranial arterial opacification).
  • Choice depends on local availability and patient factors.
  • Confirmed ancillary test plus clinical exam supports brain death determination.
Case 11 — Parenchymal probe drift.
Day 5 of ICP monitoring; ICP gradually rising from baseline 10 to 18 mmHg, but clinical exam stable and CT unchanged.
Q. Differential.
Answer.
  • Parenchymal probe drift — cannot recalibrate in situ.
  • Verify with clinical correlation and CT.
  • If clinical exam stable, drift likely.
  • If progressive, consider replacing probe.
  • Trend matters more than absolute values for parenchymal probes.
Case 12 — EVD infection.
EVD placed 8 days ago; fever, CSF cell count 1200 with low glucose.
Q. Action.
Answer.
  • EVD-associated ventriculitis.
  • Empiric vancomycin + cefepime or meropenem (cover Pseudomonas).
  • CSF culture and PCR.
  • Remove or exchange catheter if possible.
  • Consider intrathecal antibiotics if persistent.
  • Risk factors — >5 days, repeated manipulation.
Case 13 — PbtO₂ low despite normal CPP.
15-year-old TBI; CPP 65 mmHg, ICP 18, PbtO₂ 12 mmHg.
Q. Action.
Answer.
  • Brain tissue hypoxia despite acceptable CPP — regional ischemia possible.
  • Check FiO₂; consider increasing oxygen.
  • Check Hb; transfuse if anemic.
  • Increase CPP target.
  • Reassess autoregulation (PRx) — pressure-passive flow may benefit from higher CPP.
  • Investigate probe placement on CT.
Case 14 — aEEG in HIE.
Term newborn with HIE; aEEG shows burst-suppression at 6 hours.
Q. Implication.
Answer.
  • Burst-suppression pattern — guarded prognosis.
  • Eligible for therapeutic hypothermia (already underway given Sarnat criteria).
  • Continue hypothermia 33.5 °C × 72 h.
  • Continuous full EEG when available.
  • Family discussion about uncertain prognosis.
Case 15 — Pulsatility Index high.
10-year-old TBI; MCA PI 1.8.
Q. Implications.
Answer.
  • Elevated PI (>1.4) — high downstream vascular resistance.
  • Suggests raised ICP or distal vasoconstriction.
  • Correlate with ICP monitor and imaging.
  • Address ICP if elevated.
  • Consider hypocapnia or vasopressor effect.
Case 16 — SSEP for post-arrest prognostication.
7-year-old, 72 h post-cardiac arrest, off sedation; SSEP shows bilaterally absent N20 cortical responses.
Q. Implication.
Answer.
  • Bilaterally absent N20 — very poor prognosis in normothermic adult-like data.
  • Pediatric data more limited.
  • Use as one component of multimodal prognostication (clinical exam, EEG, MRI, biomarkers).
  • Hold final prognosis until full multimodal assessment.
  • Family meeting with palliative care.
Case 17 — Microdialysis lactate/pyruvate.
Severe TBI patient; lactate/pyruvate ratio 55, glycerol 200.
Q. Interpretation.
Answer.
  • Elevated L/P ratio >40 — anaerobic metabolism / mitochondrial dysfunction.
  • High glycerol — membrane damage.
  • Consider increasing CPP, oxygenation.
  • Limited pediatric clinical use; research tool.
Case 18 — Generalized periodic discharges.
12-year-old in coma; cEEG shows generalized periodic discharges at 1 Hz with triphasic morphology.
Q. Differential.
Answer.
  • Encephalopathy patterns — hepatic, uremic, anoxic, drug-induced.
  • Triphasic morphology classic for hepatic encephalopathy.
  • Send ammonia, LFTs.
  • If ictal-interictal continuum, trial of benzodiazepine to assess for seizure component.
  • Treat underlying cause.
Case 19 — Optic nerve sheath diameter.
4-year-old with vomiting and headache; ultrasound ONSD 5.5 mm.
Q. Interpretation.
Answer.
  • ONSD elevated for age (>4.5 mm in young child).
  • Suggestive of raised ICP.
  • Confirm with imaging (CT/MRI) and clinical exam (fundoscopy for papilledema).
  • Manage as suspected raised ICP — hyperosmolar therapy if signs of herniation.
Case 20 — Asymmetric TCD on ECMO.
8-year-old on VA-ECMO; right MCA velocity 90, left MCA velocity 35.
Q. Action.
Answer.
  • Marked asymmetry — possible cannula occlusion of left carotid (often cannulated in pediatric VA-ECMO), embolus, or stroke.
  • Urgent head ultrasound (if open fontanelle) or CT.
  • Review cannula position with surgical team.
  • Adjust flow / cannula if obstruction.
  • Consider switch to bilateral cannulation strategy.
Case 21 — Hypothermia and brain death evaluation.
9-year-old after near-drowning, ROSC achieved; absent brainstem reflexes; rectal temp 32 °C.
Q. Approach.
Answer.
  • Cannot determine brain death until confounders corrected.
  • Rewarm to ≥36 °C.
  • Wait for sedatives / NMBA to clear.
  • Correct electrolyte abnormalities.
  • Then perform brain death examination per pediatric protocol.
  • Two separate exams by different physicians, with appropriate observation period.
Case 22 — Status epilepticus pentobarbital coma.
Refractory SE on pentobarbital infusion; cEEG shows continuous activity with no burst-suppression.
Q. Adjustment.
Answer.
  • Pentobarbital infusion not yet at target.
  • Increase dose to achieve burst-suppression (90–95 % suppression or 6–10 s background/minute).
  • Watch hemodynamics — pentobarbital causes hypotension and myocardial depression.
  • Continue cEEG; once 24–48 h of suppression, wean and reassess.
Case 23 — Cerebral autoregulation in sepsis.
5-year-old with septic shock and altered mental status; PRx 0.4 across all CPP ranges.
Q. Implication.
Answer.
  • Global autoregulation impairment in sepsis.
  • Pressure-passive CBF.
  • Maintain MAP carefully; avoid both hypotension and hypertension.
  • Consider higher MAP target (~70th percentile for age).
  • Monitor cerebral oxygenation in parallel.
Case 24 — Alarm fatigue scenario.
PICU patient triggers ~50 alarms per hour from multiple modalities.
Q. Approach.
Answer.
  • Customize alarm thresholds per patient — individualized rather than defaults.
  • Integrate alarms with EHR.
  • Multidisciplinary protocols for "real" alarm response.
  • Trend graphs over hours/days rather than instantaneous.
  • Education on artifact recognition.
Case 25 — TBI with ICP refractory to first-tier therapy.
12-year-old TBI; ICP 32 mmHg despite sedation, head-of-bed elevation, hyperosmolar therapy.
Q. Multimodal review.
Answer.
  • Confirm ICP signal — calibration, drift, waveform morphology.
  • Check CPPopt via PRx.
  • PbtO₂ to assess brain tissue oxygenation.
  • NIRS for regional oxygenation.
  • cEEG for status epilepticus.
  • Imaging to rule out evolving lesion.
  • Consider decompressive craniectomy.

10. Final One-Page Infographic

🧠 Neurologic Monitoring — Master Map

① The five core modalities

  • ICP — EVD (gold) or parenchymal probe; age-adjusted CPP target.
  • cEEG — NCSE detection; up to 50 % of seizures nonconvulsive in PICU.
  • TCD — flow velocity, PI; sickle cell stroke screen (>200 cm/s).
  • NIRS — regional rSO₂; <40 % or >25 % drop triggers action.
  • PbtO₂ — invasive tissue O₂; <15 mmHg suggests ischemia.

② Waveform pearls

  • P2 > P1 — decreased compliance, early warning.
  • Lundberg A wave — impending herniation; emergency.
  • Burst-suppression — sedative-induced OK; spontaneous = poor prognosis.
  • PI >1.4 — raised ICP / vasoconstriction.

③ Critical thresholds

  • CPP — infant ≥40, toddler ≥50, child ≥60, adolescent ≥70 mmHg.
  • ICP — infant >15, child >20 mmHg sustained.
  • TCD MCA >200 cm/s in SCD → transfuse.
  • rSO₂ <40 % or >25 % drop → investigate.
  • PbtO₂ <15 mmHg → optimize CPP, FiO₂, Hb.
  • PRx >0.3 → impaired autoregulation; titrate to CPPopt.

④ Red flags

  • P2 elevation precedes ICP rise.
  • Bilateral NIRS asymmetry >10 % → focal pathology.
  • Spontaneous burst-suppression off sedation → poor prognosis.
  • NCSE in unexplained altered mental status.
  • EVD infection signs after day 5.

⑤ Exam pearls

🔥🔥🔥 Up to 50 % of seizures in critically ill children are nonconvulsive — always cEEG.
🔥🔥🔥 STOP threshold MCA >200 cm/s → transfusion in SCD.
🔥🔥🔥 PRx-derived CPPopt is the emerging individualized target.
🔥🔥 NIRS — trend > absolute value; asymmetry matters.
🔥🔥 Lundberg A waves = emergency; not optional intervention.
🔥 Parenchymal probes drift — verify against clinical exam.
