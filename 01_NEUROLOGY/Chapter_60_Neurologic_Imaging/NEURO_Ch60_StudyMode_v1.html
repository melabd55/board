CHAPTER 60 ■ Neurologic Imaging — Key Points

Distilled message
  • CT is fast, widely available, ideal for acute hemorrhage and bony injury — but exposes to ionizing radiation.
  • MRI gives superior anatomic + functional detail (DWI, SWI, MRS) without ionizing radiation.
  • CT visualizes large hemorrhages and cervical fractures; MRI better for soft-tissue cord injury.
  • DWI detects cytotoxic edema within hours of ischemia and differentiates abscess from necrotic tumor.
  • SWI detects diffuse axonal injury and microhemorrhages.
  • MR spectroscopy reveals metabolic properties — adds prognostic value after TBI / ischemia.
  • CT may be normal up to 24 h after focal or global ischemia — MRI is more sensitive early.
  • MRI patterns inform inborn errors of metabolism diagnosis.
RCPSC pearl 🔥🔥🔥
  • The "right test at the right time" is high-yield exam content — when to choose CT vs. MRI, which sequence detects which pathology.
Pages 3124–3128 · CT Technique

Computed Tomography

Principles
  • X-ray beams from multiple angles → tissue attenuation → Hounsfield units (HU).
  • Air −1000 HU; fat −100; water 0; CSF ~0; brain 30–40; blood 50–80 (acute); bone +1000.
  • Helical / multi-detector scanners enable rapid acquisition (seconds), often without sedation.
  • Window settings — brain (W80, L40), bone (W2000, L500), subdural (W200, L60).
Strengths
  • Rapid, widely available.
  • Excellent for acute hemorrhage, bony injury, large mass effect, hydrocephalus, calcifications.
  • No need for prolonged sedation.
  • Useful when MRI contraindicated (pacemaker, metal foreign body).
Limitations
  • Ionizing radiation — cumulative cancer risk especially in young children.
  • Less sensitive for early ischemia (may be normal <24 h).
  • Posterior fossa and brainstem poorly seen (beam-hardening artifact).
  • Iodinated contrast — renal toxicity, allergy.
ICU layer 🔥🔥🔥
  • Image gently — As Low As Reasonably Achievable (ALARA) — lowest dose for diagnostic answer.
  • Use weight-based pediatric protocols, not adult defaults.
  • Limit repeat scans; consider MRI when stable.
Pages 3129–3134 · MRI Technique

Magnetic Resonance Imaging

Principles
  • Magnetic field aligns hydrogen protons; radiofrequency pulses excite them.
  • Different tissues relax at different rates — T1 (longitudinal) and T2 (transverse).
  • Field strength 1.5 T or 3 T common; higher = better resolution but more artifact.
  • Acquisition takes 20–60 minutes — sedation often needed in young children.

  • SWI is the sequence for diffuse axonal injury and microbleeds — looks like punctate dark dots.
Pages 3135–3140 · Trauma Imaging

Imaging in Pediatric TBI

CT findings
  • Epidural hematoma — biconvex (lens-shaped), does not cross sutures; arterial (middle meningeal artery) bleed.
  • Subdural hematoma — crescent-shaped, crosses sutures but not midline; venous (bridging vein) bleed.
  • Subarachnoid hemorrhage — sulcal hyperdensity; often along tentorium / interhemispheric fissure.
  • Contusion — focal hyperdensity in cortex.
  • Cerebral edema — sulcal effacement, ventricle compression, blurred gray-white differentiation.
  • Skull fracture — bone window; nondepressed vs. depressed; basilar fractures (CSF rhinorrhea / otorrhea).
MRI advantages in TBI
  • Diffuse axonal injury — SWI shows microhemorrhages in corpus callosum, brainstem, gray-white junction.
  • DWI — early cytotoxic edema; ischemic injury.
  • MRS — NAA decrease and lactate rise prognosticate worse outcome.
  • Spinal cord — soft-tissue injury, edema, hemorrhage not visible on CT.
ICU layer 🔥🔥🔥
  • Cervical spine imaging — CT for bony injury; MRI when neuro signs + normal CT.
  • Abusive head trauma — multilayered retinal hemorrhages on exam, subdurals of different ages on imaging, posterior rib / metaphyseal corner fractures on skeletal survey.
  • Repeat imaging — at 24 h if clinical deterioration; otherwise as guided by clinical course.
Pages 3141–3146 · Stroke and Vascular Imaging

Stroke Imaging

Stages on imaging
  • Hyperacute (0–6 h) — CT may be normal; DWI shows restriction within minutes.
  • Acute (6–24 h) — CT shows hypodensity, loss of gray-white differentiation, dense MCA sign.
  • Subacute (1–7 d) — well-demarcated hypodensity; mass effect peaks.
  • Chronic (>1 week) — encephalomalacia, gliosis.
Pediatric stroke etiologies and imaging
  • Arterial ischemic stroke — focal arterial territory infarct on DWI; MRA for occlusion / dissection / arteriopathy (FCA, moyamoya).
  • Cerebral sinovenous thrombosis (CSVT) — MRV is gold standard; "empty delta sign" on contrast CT.
  • Hemorrhagic stroke — CT for blood; MRA / CTA / DSA for AVM, aneurysm, cavernoma.
  • Sickle cell stroke — MCA territory infarct typical; MRA shows arteriopathy.
Exam trap
  • Hyperacute stroke — CT can be entirely normal. MRI DWI is the sensitive test.
  • Look for the "dense MCA sign" and "insular ribbon sign" on early CT.
Pages 3147–3152 · Infections, Inflammation, Demyelination

CNS Infection Imaging

  • Bacterial meningitis — meningeal enhancement on contrast MRI; complications (hydrocephalus, infarct, empyema, abscess).
  • Cerebral abscess — ring-enhancing lesion with central DWI restriction (distinguishes from necrotic tumor where center is dark on DWI but no restriction).
  • HSV encephalitis — temporal lobe / limbic system T2/FLAIR hyperintensity; often hemorrhagic on SWI.
  • Tuberculous meningitis — basilar meningitis, hydrocephalus, tuberculomas, infarctions.
  • Toxoplasma — ring-enhancing lesions in immunocompromised; "eccentric target sign."

Demyelinating / Autoimmune Disease

  • ADEM — multifocal large T2/FLAIR hyperintensities involving white and gray matter; may enhance.
  • Pediatric MS — periventricular ovoid lesions perpendicular to ventricles (Dawson fingers); cervical cord involvement.
  • NMOSD — longitudinally extensive transverse myelitis >3 vertebral segments; bilateral optic neuritis; area postrema lesions.
  • MOGAD — variable; may resemble ADEM in young children, NMOSD in older.
  • Anti-NMDA receptor encephalitis — often normal MRI; sometimes limbic / cerebellar T2 hyperintensity.
Pages 3153–3158 · Hypoxic-Ischemic and Metabolic Patterns

HIE Imaging Patterns (term neonate)

  • Mild — normal MRI or subtle changes.
  • Moderate — watershed pattern (parasagittal injury in older infants).
  • Severe — central pattern (basal ganglia, thalamus, perirolandic cortex, posterior limb internal capsule).
  • Profound asphyxia — global pattern with brainstem involvement.
  • Best imaged at 3–5 days post-injury (DWI peaks then pseudonormalizes).

Inborn Errors of Metabolism Imaging Patterns

  • Leigh syndrome — symmetric basal ganglia, brainstem, periaqueductal hyperintensity; MRS shows lactate.
  • Maple syrup urine disease — symmetric edema of corticospinal tracts.
  • Urea cycle defects — diffuse cerebral edema with cortical / insular involvement.
  • Glutaric aciduria — frontotemporal atrophy and widened Sylvian fissures ("bat-wing" pattern).
  • Metachromatic leukodystrophy — symmetric demyelination with sparing of subcortical U-fibers.
  • Krabbe — symmetric demyelination + thalamic / cerebellar changes.
  • X-linked adrenoleukodystrophy — bilateral posterior parieto-occipital demyelination with enhancement at edge.
Pages 3159–3170 · Tumors, Hydrocephalus, Brain Death + Conclusion

Tumors

  • Posterior fossa — common pediatric location.
  • Medulloblastoma — midline, vermis, restricts on DWI; common 5–7 y.
  • Pilocytic astrocytoma — cystic with enhancing mural nodule; common cerebellum.
  • Ependymoma — fourth ventricle, "plastic" extending through foramen of Magendie.
  • Brainstem glioma (DIPG) — diffuse expansion of pons.
  • Supratentorial — gliomas, atypical teratoid rhabdoid tumor (ATRT), ependymoma.

Hydrocephalus

  • Communicating — ventricles enlarge proportionally; post-meningitis, post-hemorrhage.
  • Obstructive (non-communicating) — focal obstruction (aqueductal stenosis, tumor, Chiari malformation).
  • Signs — ventricular dilation, periventricular edema, sulcal effacement, callosal thinning, third ventricular bowing.
  • VP shunt — head CT for shunt malfunction; tap if uncertain.

Brain Death Ancillary Imaging

  • Radionuclide cerebral blood flow scan — no intracranial uptake.
  • CT angiography — no opacification of major intracranial arteries.
  • TCD — oscillating flow or systolic spikes only.
  • Used when clinical apnea test cannot be safely performed.

Conclusion

  • Choose the right modality for the clinical question.
  • CT is fast and acute; MRI is sensitive and detailed.
  • Multimodal imaging integrated with clinical findings improves diagnostic and prognostic accuracy.
  • Minimize radiation exposure (ALARA); use pediatric-specific protocols.

Equations

Effective dose (head CT, pediatric). ~1–2 mSv (older children) — equivalent to ~1 year of background radiation; use ALARA.
Apparent Diffusion Coefficient (ADC). Measure of water diffusion. ADC dark in acute stroke (restricted diffusion); bright in vasogenic edema.
MRS NAA/Cr ratio. Normal ~1.5–2; reduced in neuronal injury (HIE, TBI). Lactate peak — anaerobic metabolism.

3. Global Evidence Layer — Full Citations

① ACR Appropriateness Criteria — Pediatric Head Trauma Imaging

  • Source: American College of Radiology · Updated 2022 · Ryan ME et al. (committee)
  • Link: ACR Appropriateness Criteria
  • Key findings:
    • Non-contrast head CT for moderate/severe TBI or PECARN-positive minor TBI.
    • MRI when neuro signs persist despite normal CT.
    • Spinal CT for bony injury; MRI for cord / soft tissue.

② PECARN Pediatric Head Injury Rule

  • Source: Kuppermann N et al. Lancet. 2009;374:1160–1170
  • Link: DOI 10.1016/S0140-6736(09)61558-0 · PMID 19758692
  • Key findings:
    • Validated rule for identifying children with minor head trauma at very low risk of clinically important TBI.
    • Separate algorithms for <2 y and ≥2 y.
    • Reduces unnecessary CT use.

③ MRI in Pediatric Stroke — AHA/ASA Statement

  • Source: Ferriero DM et al. Stroke. 2019;50:e51–e96
  • Link: DOI 10.1161/STR.0000000000000183
  • Key findings:
    • MRI with DWI preferred over CT for pediatric stroke.
    • MRA / MRV for etiology (arteriopathy, dissection, CSVT).
    • Time-sensitive — tPA/thrombectomy decision-making.

④ Neonatal HIE MRI Prognostication

  • Source: Shankaran S et al. N Engl J Med. 2012;366:2085–2092 (NICHD follow-up); de Vries LS & Groenendaal F. Neuroradiology. 2010
  • Link: Shankaran DOI 10.1056/NEJMoa1112066
  • Key findings:
    • MRI at 4–7 days post-cooling predicts 18–22 month outcome.
    • Basal ganglia / thalamus and posterior limb internal capsule injury associated with worse outcomes.
    • MRS lactate / NAA ratio quantitatively prognostic.

⑤ ASNR / ACR Practice Parameter — Pediatric MRI Sedation

  • Source: AAP / ASA / ACR joint guideline · multiple revisions
  • Link: ACR Practice Parameters
  • Key findings:
    • Pediatric MRI often requires sedation in <6 y.
    • Risk stratify — fasting, airway, cardiac, drug allergies.
    • Dedicated sedation team with rescue capability.
    • Feed-and-wrap protocol for very young infants when feasible.

⑥ FDA Warning — Gadolinium Retention

  • Source: FDA Drug Safety Communication 2017 · McDonald RJ et al. Radiology. 2015;275:772–782
  • Link: FDA gadolinium warning
  • Key findings:
    • Trace gadolinium retention in brain and bone, even with normal renal function.
    • Linear (older) agents — more retention than macrocyclic.
    • Clinical significance uncertain; use only when contrast adds diagnostic value.
    • Nephrogenic systemic fibrosis (NSF) risk in renal failure (eGFR <30) — avoid gadolinium.

⑦ Diffuse Axonal Injury Detection — SWI

  • Source: Tong KA et al. Ann Neurol. 2004;56:36–50 (foundational); subsequent pediatric work through 2024
  • Link: DOI 10.1002/ana.20123
  • Key findings:
    • SWI detects 2–6× more lesions than GRE.
    • Number, volume, location of microhemorrhages correlate with severity and outcome.
    • Important in pediatric TBI prognostication.

5. ICU Decision Engine

IF Acute pediatric head trauma with red flags (GCS <15, focal signs, loss of consciousness, severe mechanism)
THEN
  • Non-contrast head CT first (fast, detects hemorrhage and fractures).
  • Add cervical spine CT for high-risk mechanisms.
  • MRI brain + cervical cord if focal signs persist despite normal CT.
IF Suspected pediatric stroke
THEN
  • MRI with DWI is the preferred initial test (CT may be normal in hyperacute stroke).
  • Add MRA / CTA / MRV for vascular etiology.
  • Consider tPA / thrombectomy per AHA/ASA pediatric algorithms.
IF Suspected abusive head trauma
THEN
  • Non-contrast head CT immediately.
  • Fundoscopy for retinal hemorrhages.
  • Skeletal survey for occult fractures.
  • MRI 1–7 days after CT to look for DAI, microhemorrhages of different ages.
  • Report to child protection — mandatory.
IF Neonatal HIE
THEN
  • Initial cranial ultrasound to exclude hemorrhage / structural.
  • MRI at 4–7 days post-injury (DWI peaks, before pseudonormalization).
  • Include MRS for lactate / NAA ratios.
  • Patterns guide prognosis discussion.
IF Encephalopathy with multifocal white matter lesions
THEN
  • MRI with FLAIR, T2, post-gadolinium T1.
  • ADEM, MS, NMOSD, MOGAD considerations.
  • LP, anti-MOG, anti-AQP4 testing.
IF Ring-enhancing brain lesion
THEN
  • DWI key — central restriction → abscess; no restriction → necrotic tumor.
  • Consider metastasis, demyelination, infection.
  • MRS — choline peak (tumor) vs. lactate / amino acids (abscess).
IF Suspected CSVT
THEN
  • MRV is the gold standard.
  • Contrast CT — "empty delta sign" in superior sagittal sinus.
  • Anticoagulation per pediatric stroke guidelines.

6. SAQ Bank — 40 Questions

SAQ 1. When is CT preferred over MRI in pediatric neurocritical care?
Answer.
  • Acute trauma — fast detection of hemorrhage and fractures.
  • Suspected raised ICP / herniation.
  • Unstable patient.
  • Bony injury (skull, cervical spine).
  • MRI contraindicated (pacemaker, metal foreign body).
  • Before LP if focal signs / immunocompromised.
SAQ 2. List five advantages of MRI over CT.
Answer.
  • No ionizing radiation.
  • Greater anatomic and metabolic detail.
  • Better gray-white differentiation.
  • Better visualization of posterior fossa and brainstem.
  • Multiple sequences — DWI for early stroke, SWI for DAI, MRS for metabolism, MRA/MRV for vessels.
  • Better soft-tissue resolution of spinal cord.
SAQ 3. Hounsfield unit values for key tissues.
Answer.
  • Air −1000.
  • Fat −100.
  • CSF / water 0.
  • Brain (gray matter slightly higher than white) 30–40.
  • Acute blood 50–80.
  • Calcification / bone +1000.
SAQ 4. Why may CT be normal in hyperacute stroke?
Answer.
  • Tissue density changes take hours to develop.
  • CT may show subtle insular ribbon loss or dense MCA sign within first hours.
  • Frank hypodensity usually requires 6–24 h.
  • MRI DWI detects cytotoxic edema within minutes.
SAQ 5. Differentiate epidural vs. subdural hematoma on CT.
Answer.
  • Epidural — biconvex (lens-shaped), does not cross sutures; arterial (middle meningeal); often associated skull fracture.
  • Subdural — crescent-shaped, crosses sutures but not midline; venous (bridging vein); common in abusive head trauma.
  • Epidural may have "lucid interval" before deterioration.
SAQ 6. Why is SWI the key sequence in DAI?
Answer.
  • SWI is highly sensitive to magnetic susceptibility (deoxy-Hb, hemosiderin).
  • Detects microhemorrhages 2–6× more than GRE.
  • DAI distribution — gray-white junction, corpus callosum, brainstem.
  • Number, volume, location correlate with severity and outcome.
SAQ 7. How does DWI distinguish abscess from necrotic tumor?
Answer.
  • Both can appear as ring-enhancing lesions.
  • Abscess — central pus restricts diffusion (DWI bright, ADC dark).
  • Necrotic tumor — central necrosis does NOT restrict (DWI dark or T2 shine-through, ADC bright).
  • MRS supports — abscess shows amino acid / lactate / lipid; tumor shows choline.
SAQ 8. Neonatal HIE imaging patterns.
Answer.
  • Mild — normal MRI.
  • Moderate — watershed (parasagittal) pattern in older infants.
  • Severe — central pattern — basal ganglia, thalami, perirolandic cortex, posterior limb internal capsule.
  • Profound — global with brainstem.
  • Image at 4–7 days post-injury for best correlation with outcome.
SAQ 9. ADEM imaging features.
Answer.
  • Multifocal large T2/FLAIR hyperintensities.
  • Involves white matter AND gray matter.
  • Often with thalamic / basal ganglia / cortical involvement.
  • May enhance with gadolinium.
  • Differentiate from MS (Dawson fingers, periventricular ovoid) and NMOSD (LETM >3 segments).
SAQ 10. "Empty delta sign" significance.
Answer.
  • Triangular filling defect in superior sagittal sinus on contrast CT.
  • Indicates cerebral sinovenous thrombosis.
  • MRV is the gold standard for confirmation.
  • Treat with anticoagulation per pediatric guidelines.
SAQ 11. HSV encephalitis imaging features.
Answer.
  • Temporal lobe / limbic system T2/FLAIR hyperintensity.
  • Often asymmetric — unilateral temporal involvement classic.
  • Hemorrhagic component on SWI.
  • Cingulate gyrus may also be involved.
  • EEG — periodic lateralized discharges (PLEDs) over temporal region.
SAQ 12. Tuberculous meningitis imaging.
Answer.
  • Basilar meningitis with thick enhancement.
  • Hydrocephalus (communicating, sometimes obstructive).
  • Tuberculomas — ring-enhancing parenchymal lesions.
  • Infarctions — especially basal ganglia (perforator vasculitis).
  • "TB triad" — basilar exudates + hydrocephalus + basal ganglia infarct.
SAQ 13. Pediatric MS imaging features.
Answer.
  • Periventricular ovoid lesions perpendicular to ventricles (Dawson fingers).
  • Juxtacortical lesions.
  • Cervical cord involvement (focal short-segment).
  • Some enhancement of acute lesions.
  • Diagnosis requires dissemination in space and time.
SAQ 14. NMOSD imaging features.
Answer.
  • Longitudinally extensive transverse myelitis >3 vertebral segments.
  • Bilateral simultaneous optic neuritis.
  • Area postrema lesions (intractable nausea / vomiting).
  • Hypothalamic lesions.
  • Less commonly periventricular (vs. MS).
  • Anti-AQP4 antibody confirms.
SAQ 15. Inborn error patterns — Leigh syndrome.
Answer.
  • Mitochondrial disease.
  • Symmetric basal ganglia, brainstem, periaqueductal gray T2 hyperintensity.
  • MRS — elevated lactate.
  • Often progressive.
  • Avoid valproate (mitochondrial toxic) and prolonged propofol (PRIS).
SAQ 16. X-linked adrenoleukodystrophy imaging.
Answer.
  • Bilateral posterior parieto-occipital demyelination.
  • Enhancing leading edge of demyelination.
  • Symmetric, progressive.
  • Treatment — Lorenzo's oil (uncertain benefit), bone marrow transplant if early.
SAQ 17. Pediatric posterior fossa tumors.
Answer.
  • Medulloblastoma — midline / vermis; restricts on DWI; 5–7 y.
  • Pilocytic astrocytoma — cerebellar; cystic with enhancing mural nodule; very low DWI signal.
  • Ependymoma — fourth ventricle; "plastic" extending through foramen of Magendie.
  • DIPG — diffuse pontine expansion; non-enhancing.
SAQ 18. When to use gadolinium and when to avoid.
Answer.
  • Use — infection, inflammation, tumor, vascular evaluation.
  • Avoid — eGFR <30 mL/min/1.73m² (NSF risk).
  • Trace retention in brain / bone documented even with normal renal function — use only when contrast adds diagnostic value.
  • Prefer macrocyclic over linear agents.
SAQ 19. CT for cervical spine.
Answer.
  • Bony detail — fractures, dislocations.
  • High-risk pediatric mechanisms — high-speed MVC, fall >3 m, axial load, focal neuro signs.
  • Limitations — soft-tissue cord injury not seen on CT.
  • SCIWORA — spinal cord injury without radiographic abnormality, especially in young children with elastic spine; MRI required.
SAQ 20. PECARN head injury rule basics.
Answer.
  • Identifies very low risk children with minor head trauma.
  • Separate rules for <2 y and ≥2 y.
  • Predictors — GCS, mental status, scalp hematoma (location/size), loss of consciousness, mechanism, signs of basilar fracture, vomiting (≥2 y), headache.
  • If no predictors — CT not needed (sensitivity for clinically important TBI ~99 %).
SAQ 21. ALARA principle.
Answer.
  • As Low As Reasonably Achievable.
  • Minimize ionizing radiation dose for diagnostic value.
  • Pediatric protocols — weight-based, low-kVp, iterative reconstruction.
  • Use MRI when feasible to avoid CT.
  • Document indication; avoid repeat scans without clear change.
SAQ 22. SCIWORA features.
Answer.
  • Spinal cord injury without radiographic abnormality.
  • Common in young children (<8 y) — elastic spine, weak neck musculature.
  • CT / X-ray normal; MRI shows cord edema, hemorrhage, contusion.
  • Mechanism — sudden flexion / extension.
  • Spinal immobilization, MRI, neurosurgical consultation.
SAQ 23. Subarachnoid hemorrhage CT findings.
Answer.
  • Sulcal hyperdensity.
  • Often along tentorium / interhemispheric fissure.
  • Pediatric SAH — typically AVM, aneurysm, trauma.
  • CT sensitivity ~95 % within 6 h; falls thereafter.
  • If CT negative and clinical suspicion high, LP for xanthochromia.
SAQ 24. Cytotoxic vs. vasogenic edema on MRI.
Answer.
  • Cytotoxic (cellular swelling) — DWI bright, ADC dark; restricted diffusion; stroke.
  • Vasogenic (BBB leak) — DWI dark or T2 shine-through, ADC bright; tumor / abscess / PRES.
  • Distinction guides differential and management.
SAQ 25. Imaging in pediatric brain death evaluation.
Answer.
  • Radionuclide cerebral blood flow scan — no intracranial uptake.
  • CT angiography — no opacification of major intracranial arteries.
  • TCD — oscillating flow or systolic spikes only.
  • Used when clinical apnea test cannot be safely performed.
SAQ 26. MRS clinical applications.
Answer.
  • NAA — neuronal integrity (decreased in HIE, TBI).
  • Choline — membrane turnover (increased in tumor).
  • Creatine — energy reference; ratios reported.
  • Lactate peak — anaerobic metabolism (HIE, mitochondrial disease, stroke).
  • Lipid / amino acids — abscess.
  • Useful in HIE prognostication, tumor characterization, mitochondrial disease.
SAQ 27. Imaging strategy in suspected encephalitis.
Answer.
  • MRI with FLAIR, T2, DWI, post-gadolinium.
  • HSV — temporal lobe (asymmetric).
  • Autoimmune (NMDAR) — often normal early, sometimes limbic.
  • ADEM — multifocal large T2/FLAIR.
  • JE / enteroviral — thalamus, basal ganglia, brainstem.
  • Always pair with CSF and EEG.
SAQ 28. Anti-NMDAR encephalitis imaging.
Answer.
  • MRI often normal early.
  • Subtle limbic, hippocampal, cerebellar T2 hyperintensity in some cases.
  • EEG — extreme delta brush pattern characteristic.
  • CSF antibody positivity drives diagnosis.
  • Search for tumor (ovarian teratoma in older girls).
SAQ 29. Cerebral abscess imaging.
Answer.
  • Ring-enhancing lesion with central DWI restriction.
  • Surrounding vasogenic edema.
  • "Dual rim sign" on SWI — hypointense inner rim.
  • MRS — amino acids, lactate, lipid.
  • Differential — necrotic tumor (no DWI restriction), demyelination, metastasis.
SAQ 30. CSVT imaging diagnosis.
Answer.
  • MRV — gold standard; lack of flow in affected sinus.
  • CT — "empty delta sign" in superior sagittal sinus on contrast CT.
  • Non-contrast CT — hyperdense sinus / cord sign.
  • SWI — venous engorgement, microhemorrhages.
  • Anticoagulation per pediatric guidelines.
SAQ 31. Posterior reversible encephalopathy syndrome (PRES) imaging.
Answer.
  • Bilateral parieto-occipital white matter T2/FLAIR hyperintensity.
  • Vasogenic edema (DWI dark, ADC bright).
  • May involve cerebellum, brainstem (atypical PRES).
  • Causes — hypertension, immunosuppressants (tacrolimus, cyclosporine), eclampsia, chemotherapy.
  • Usually reversible with cause control.
SAQ 32. Hypoglycemic brain injury imaging.
Answer.
  • Parieto-occipital cortex affected — selectively vulnerable.
  • DWI restriction in cortex and white matter.
  • Basal ganglia / hippocampus may also be affected.
  • Posterior predominance distinguishes from HIE.
  • Prognosis variable; depends on duration and severity.
SAQ 33. Cerebral venous infarct imaging.
Answer.
  • Cortical / subcortical hemorrhage not in arterial distribution.
  • Often parasagittal (superior sagittal sinus thrombosis).
  • Vasogenic + cytotoxic edema (mixed DWI signal).
  • Treat underlying CSVT with anticoagulation.
SAQ 34. Glutaric aciduria type 1 imaging.
Answer.
  • Widened Sylvian fissures and frontotemporal atrophy ("bat-wing" pattern).
  • Subdural hematomas (mimic abusive head trauma).
  • Bilateral basal ganglia signal abnormality.
  • Macrocephaly common.
  • Important — distinguish from non-accidental trauma.
SAQ 35. Imaging in hypothalamic / pituitary tumors.
Answer.
  • Craniopharyngioma — suprasellar; calcifications on CT; cystic on MRI; enhancement.
  • Optic pathway glioma — bilateral in NF1.
  • Germinoma — pineal or suprasellar; restricts on DWI.
  • Hypothalamic hamartoma — non-enhancing isointense to gray matter; gelastic seizures.
SAQ 36. Imaging features of abusive head trauma.
Answer.
  • Subdural hematomas of different ages (mixed density).
  • Interhemispheric subdurals.
  • Retinal hemorrhages on fundoscopy.
  • Posterior rib fractures, metaphyseal corner fractures (skeletal survey).
  • Hypoxic-ischemic injury pattern.
  • Cortical contusions / shear injuries.
  • SWI shows microhemorrhages of different ages.
SAQ 37. MRI safety considerations.
Answer.
  • Pacemakers — MR-conditional newer models acceptable; non-conditional contraindicated.
  • Cochlear implants — generally contraindicated.
  • Metallic foreign bodies (intraocular) — check with screening before scan.
  • Aneurysm clips — confirm MR-compatibility.
  • Sedation risks — fasting status, airway, hemodynamic stability.
  • Hearing protection mandatory.
SAQ 38. Ultrasound use in PICU neonate.
Answer.
  • Open fontanelle enables transcranial ultrasound up to ~6 months.
  • Detects intraventricular hemorrhage, hydrocephalus, periventricular leukomalacia.
  • Daily ultrasound on ECMO for stroke / hemorrhage detection.
  • Bedside, no sedation, no radiation.
  • Limited for cortical and posterior fossa pathology.
SAQ 39. Why CT for skull fractures, MRI for cord injury?
Answer.
  • CT — superior bony detail (bone window).
  • MRI — superior soft-tissue / cord / ligament detail.
  • Both modalities often complement each other in trauma.
  • CT cervical spine cleared, but persistent neuro signs — proceed to MRI cervical / brain.
SAQ 40. Future directions in pediatric neuroimaging.
Answer.
  • Faster MRI sequences enabling unsedated scans in young children.
  • Machine learning for image interpretation and triage.
  • Quantitative imaging biomarkers (volumetrics, perfusion, diffusion metrics).
  • Functional MRI for cognitive / language mapping.
  • PET-MRI hybrid for metabolic + structural assessment.
  • Photon-counting CT for lower dose and higher resolution.

7. Case Bank — 25 Interpretation Cases

Case 1 — Hyperacute stroke with normal CT.
7-year-old with sudden right hemiparesis; CT head normal.
Q. Next imaging step?
Answer.
  • MRI with DWI / MRA emergently — CT may be normal in first 24 h of stroke.
  • Consider tPA / thrombectomy per pediatric protocols.
  • If sickle cell — exchange transfusion to HbS <30 % concurrently.
Case 2 — Epidural hematoma after fall.
10-year-old after falling off bike; lucid interval, then deterioration. CT — biconvex hyperdense collection.
Q. Diagnosis and management.
Answer.
  • Epidural hematoma — biconvex, does not cross sutures.
  • Often arterial bleed (middle meningeal artery).
  • Emergent neurosurgical evacuation if mass effect.
  • Volume >30 mL or thickness >15 mm in adults; pediatric thresholds variable.
Case 3 — Abusive head trauma.
5-month-old with seizures; CT shows interhemispheric subdural hematoma. Fundoscopy shows multilayered retinal hemorrhages.
Q. Imaging follow-up.
Answer.
  • MRI with SWI 3–5 days after CT — DAI, microhemorrhages of different ages.
  • Skeletal survey for occult fractures.
  • Report to child protection.
  • Multidisciplinary team.
Case 4 — Hypoxic-ischemic injury neonate.
Term newborn with HIE on hypothermia day 4.
Q. MRI plan.
Answer.
  • MRI at 4–7 days post-injury — DWI peak.
  • Include MRS for lactate / NAA ratios.
  • Patterns — central (BG, thalamus, PLIC), watershed, or global — drive prognosis.
  • Family discussion of imaging findings.
Case 5 — Ring-enhancing lesion.
9-year-old immunocompromised with fever and headache; MRI shows ring-enhancing right frontal lesion.
Q. Differential and key sequence.
Answer.
  • DWI — central restriction → abscess; no restriction → necrotic tumor.
  • MRS — amino acids / lactate for abscess; choline for tumor.
  • Tox PCR for organisms in immunocompromised (Toxoplasma, fungal).
  • Antibiotics + surgical drainage if abscess.
Case 6 — HSV encephalitis.
4-year-old with fever, seizures, altered mental status; MRI shows asymmetric right temporal lobe T2 hyperintensity with hemorrhagic component on SWI.
Q. Diagnosis and management.
Answer.
  • HSV encephalitis (temporal lobe predilection).
  • CSF HSV PCR.
  • Empiric acyclovir 30 mg/kg/day IV (60 mg/kg/d in neonates).
  • 21-day course.
  • Continuous EEG; monitor for cerebral edema.
Case 7 — CSVT with mastoiditis.
12-year-old with mastoiditis develops focal seizures; MRV shows lack of flow in left transverse sinus.
Q. Diagnosis and management.
Answer.
  • Cerebral sinovenous thrombosis (lateral sinus).
  • Treat mastoiditis — antibiotics, surgical drainage.
  • Anticoagulation (LMWH then warfarin/DOAC) for 3–6 months.
  • Treat seizures.
  • Follow-up MRV.
Case 8 — DAI on SWI.
14-year-old TBI; CT shows no overt hematoma but MRI SWI shows punctate microhemorrhages in corpus callosum and gray-white junction.
Q. Diagnosis.
Answer.
  • Diffuse axonal injury (DAI).
  • SWI is the most sensitive sequence.
  • Severity correlates with location (cortical-subcortical < corpus callosum < brainstem).
  • Prognostic — number, volume, location.
Case 9 — Pediatric MS first attack.
15-year-old female with optic neuritis; MRI brain shows periventricular ovoid lesions perpendicular to ventricles.
Q. Likely diagnosis.
Answer.
  • Pediatric multiple sclerosis (Dawson fingers).
  • Need dissemination in space and time for definite diagnosis.
  • Treat acute attack with high-dose steroids.
  • Disease-modifying therapy decision with neurology.
Case 10 — NMOSD.
14-year-old with bilateral optic neuritis and longitudinally extensive transverse myelitis over 4 vertebral segments.
Q. Diagnosis.
Answer.
  • NMOSD likely (anti-AQP4 antibody confirmation).
  • LETM >3 segments + bilateral ON are core features.
  • Acute — IV methylprednisolone, PLEX.
  • Maintenance — rituximab, eculizumab, satralizumab, inebilizumab.
  • Avoid β-interferons (worsen NMOSD).
Case 11 — ADEM.
7-year-old, 2 weeks post-URI, develops encephalopathy + multifocal neurological deficits; MRI shows multifocal large T2/FLAIR hyperintensities involving white and gray matter.
Q. Diagnosis and treatment.
Answer.
  • Acute disseminated encephalomyelitis (ADEM).
  • High-dose IV methylprednisolone 30 mg/kg/day × 3–5 days.
  • IVIG or PLEX if refractory.
  • Anti-MOG antibody testing.
  • Most pediatric cases recover well.
Case 12 — Leigh syndrome.
2-year-old with developmental regression and lactic acidosis; MRI shows symmetric T2 hyperintensity in basal ganglia and brainstem with MRS lactate peak.
Q. Diagnosis.
Answer.
  • Leigh syndrome (mitochondrial disease).
  • Genetic confirmation (mitochondrial vs. nuclear genes).
  • Avoid valproate, prolonged propofol.
  • Levetiracetam first-line for seizures.
  • L-arginine, CoQ10, L-carnitine supplementation.
Case 13 — X-linked adrenoleukodystrophy.
8-year-old boy with cognitive regression and visual problems; MRI shows bilateral posterior parieto-occipital demyelination with enhancing edge.
Q. Diagnosis and management.
Answer.
  • X-linked adrenoleukodystrophy (ALD).
  • Confirm with very long chain fatty acids (VLCFA) and genetic testing.
  • Bone marrow transplant if early in disease — best outcomes.
  • Adrenal insufficiency screening (Addison disease).
  • Family screening — X-linked.
Case 14 — Pilocytic astrocytoma.
6-year-old with morning vomiting and ataxia; MRI shows cystic posterior fossa lesion with enhancing mural nodule.
Q. Likely tumor type.
Answer.
  • Pilocytic astrocytoma (cerebellar).
  • WHO grade 1 — generally favorable prognosis.
  • Surgical resection is mainstay; complete resection often curative.
  • Manage hydrocephalus — EVD or VP shunt.
Case 15 — Medulloblastoma.
5-year-old with morning headache and vomiting; MRI shows midline posterior fossa mass restricting on DWI.
Q. Diagnosis and management.
Answer.
  • Medulloblastoma (WHO grade 4).
  • DWI restriction — hypercellular tumor.
  • Treatment — maximal safe surgical resection + radiation + chemotherapy.
  • Subtypes — WNT (best), SHH, group 3 (worst), group 4.
  • Avoid cranial RT in <3 y if possible.
Case 16 — Ependymoma.
4-year-old with progressive ataxia and cranial nerve dysfunction; MRI shows fourth ventricle mass extending through foramen of Magendie.
Q. Diagnosis.
Answer.
  • Ependymoma — "plastic" tumor.
  • Surgical resection + radiation.
  • Often residual disease due to brainstem adherence.
  • Manage hydrocephalus.
Case 17 — Tuberculous meningitis.
Adolescent immigrant with subacute headache, fever; MRI shows basilar meningeal enhancement, hydrocephalus, basal ganglia infarction.
Q. Diagnosis and management.
Answer.
  • Tuberculous meningitis (TB triad).
  • LP — lymphocytic pleocytosis, low glucose, high protein, AFB smear/PCR.
  • 4-drug regimen — isoniazid, rifampin, pyrazinamide, ethambutol.
  • Dexamethasone reduces mortality.
  • EVD / VP shunt for hydrocephalus.
Case 18 — PRES from tacrolimus.
14-year-old post-renal transplant on tacrolimus with hypertension, seizures, cortical blindness; MRI shows bilateral parieto-occipital T2/FLAIR hyperintensity.
Q. Diagnosis.
Answer.
  • Posterior reversible encephalopathy syndrome (PRES).
  • Control hypertension (nicardipine, labetalol).
  • Reduce or stop tacrolimus.
  • Treat seizures.
  • Usually reversible.
Case 19 — Glutaric aciduria mimicking AHT.
8-month-old with macrocephaly and subdural hematoma; MRI shows wide Sylvian fissures and frontotemporal atrophy.
Q. Differential.
Answer.
  • Glutaric aciduria type 1 — can mimic abusive head trauma.
  • Urine organic acids, acylcarnitine profile.
  • Genetic testing for GCDH gene.
  • Important to recognize — different management.
  • Treatment — lysine restriction, carnitine, riboflavin.
Case 20 — Anti-NMDAR encephalitis.
15-year-old with psychiatric symptoms, dyskinesias, seizures; MRI initially normal; CSF anti-NMDAR antibody positive.
Q. Imaging interpretation.
Answer.
  • MRI often normal in anti-NMDAR encephalitis — does not exclude.
  • Some cases show limbic or cerebellar T2 hyperintensity.
  • EEG — extreme delta brush.
  • Search for tumor (ovarian teratoma).
  • Treat — steroids + IVIG/PLEX first-line; rituximab/cyclophosphamide refractory.
Case 21 — Brain death CTA.
14-year-old after severe TBI; clinical brain death exam complete; CT angiography shows no opacification of intracranial arteries.
Q. Interpretation.
Answer.
  • Supports brain death determination.
  • Confirmed ancillary test plus clinical exam.
  • Family communication and organ donation discussion.
Case 22 — Craniopharyngioma.
10-year-old with growth failure, visual changes, polyuria; MRI shows suprasellar mass with cystic and solid components, calcifications on CT.
Q. Diagnosis.
Answer.
  • Craniopharyngioma.
  • Endocrine evaluation — hypopituitarism common.
  • Visual field testing.
  • Surgical resection ± radiation.
  • Stress-dose steroids perioperative; DI prophylaxis.
Case 23 — Hypoglycemic injury.
3-month-old with severe hypoglycemia; MRI shows parieto-occipital cortical / subcortical DWI restriction.
Q. Imaging interpretation.
Answer.
  • Hypoglycemic brain injury — selective posterior vulnerability.
  • Distinguishes from HIE which prefers basal ganglia / thalami.
  • Prognosis depends on duration and severity.
  • Workup for cause — hyperinsulinism, FAOD, gluconeogenesis defect.
Case 24 — Pediatric stroke etiology workup.
6-year-old with new right hemiparesis; MRI shows left MCA territory infarct, MRA shows narrowed M1 segment with beading.
Q. Most likely etiology.
Answer.
  • Focal cerebral arteriopathy (FCA) — most common pediatric AIS cause.
  • Post-VZV vasculitis frequently associated.
  • Antiplatelet therapy.
  • Steroids in some cases.
  • Monitor for progression / recurrence.
Case 25 — Moyamoya disease.
8-year-old with recurrent TIAs; MRA shows progressive bilateral ICA narrowing with collateral "puff of smoke" pattern.
Q. Diagnosis and management.
Answer.
  • Moyamoya disease (or syndrome).
  • Cerebral revascularization — direct (STA-MCA bypass) or indirect (encephaloduroarteriosynangiosis).
  • Avoid hypotension, hyperventilation, dehydration — trigger ischemia.
  • Aspirin for secondary prevention.
  • Investigate underlying conditions (NF1, sickle cell, Down syndrome).

10. Final One-Page Infographic

🧠 Pediatric Neurologic Imaging — Master Map

① CT vs. MRI choice

  • CT — acute trauma, suspected bleed, unstable, bony injury, MRI contraindicated.
  • MRI — subacute / chronic, stroke, encephalitis, white matter, posterior fossa, cord, metabolic.
  • Both — when one is incomplete or to add complementary info.

② Key MRI sequences

  • T1 — anatomy; T2 — edema; FLAIR — periventricular / cortical.
  • DWI/ADC — acute stroke (restricted), abscess vs. tumor.
  • SWI/GRE — DAI, microhemorrhages, cavernoma.
  • MRA/MRV — vessels.
  • MRS — NAA, Cho, Cr, lactate.

③ Critical patterns

  • Hyperacute stroke — CT normal, DWI bright.
  • Epidural — biconvex; subdural — crescent.
  • HSV — temporal lobe.
  • HIE — central (BG/thalamus/PLIC) vs. watershed.
  • ALD — bilateral posterior parieto-occipital with leading enhancement.
  • Leigh — symmetric BG and brainstem with MRS lactate.
  • Glutaric — bat-wing Sylvian fissures.
  • NMOSD — LETM >3 vertebral segments.
  • MS — Dawson fingers.

④ Red flags

  • Normal CT in suspected hyperacute stroke — need DWI MRI.
  • Subdurals of different ages — abusive head trauma.
  • Bat-wing pattern — consider glutaric aciduria, not just AHT.
  • Ring-enhancing lesion — DWI distinguishes abscess from tumor.
  • Gadolinium in eGFR <30 — NSF risk.

⑤ Exam pearls

🔥🔥🔥 DWI restricts in acute stroke and abscess; not in necrotic tumor.
🔥🔥🔥 SWI is the sequence for DAI and microhemorrhages.
🔥🔥🔥 ALARA — minimize radiation, especially in young children.
🔥🔥 HIE — image 4–7 days post-injury for best prognostic correlation.
🔥🔥 SCIWORA — normal CT does not exclude cord injury; get MRI.
🔥 Empty delta sign on contrast CT = CSVT until proven otherwise.
