CHAPTER 55 ■ Chronic Neuromuscular Disease — Key Points

Key points distilled
  • Chronic NMD may present as PICU "outliers" — neonatal respiratory distress, late-onset diaphragmatic weakness, bulbar motor dysfunction.
  • Common cause of chronic neurologic morbidity in childhood; many recurrent PICU admissions.
  • Most acute complications relate to respiratory insufficiency.
  • Natural history matters — some progressive, some stabilize or improve with age.
  • Recurrent decompensation may herald permanent deterioration → escalate to NIV or tracheostomy.
  • Multidisciplinary case manager mandatory from first PICU admission.
RCPSC relevance 🔥🔥🔥
  • Examiners pair this with Chapter 54 — decision-making about long-term ventilation, NIV vs. tracheostomy, anesthetic precautions, and ethical use of ICU.
Pages 2892–2893 · INTRODUCTION

INTRODUCTION

Setting
  • Chronic NMD children have recurrent PICU admissions and disproportionate resource use.
  • Major shortcoming of contemporary PICU practice — lack of continuity and long-term follow-up.
  • Critical partnership — intensivist + neuromuscular neurologist provides diagnosis, trajectory, prognosis, long-term plan.
ICU layer
  • The first PICU admission is a strategic inflection point — diagnosis-defining workup, goals-of-care conversation, planning for chronic respiratory support.
  • Plan must consider quality of life as well as survival.
Pages 2894–2896 · Respiratory phenotype outliers

Chronic Disorder Presenting in the Neonatal Period with Respiratory Distress

Differential of floppy infant + respiratory failure
  • SMA type 0/1 — most common; 5q SMN1 deletion.
  • SMARD1 — IGHMBP2 mutation; diaphragmatic phenotype.
  • Congenital myopathies — nemaline, centronuclear, multi-minicore.
  • Congenital muscular dystrophies — merosin-deficient, Ullrich.
  • Congenital myotonic dystrophy.
  • Congenital myasthenic syndromes.
  • Congenital phrenic palsy / birth trauma.
  • Neonatal transient MG.

Chronic Disorder Presenting with Later-Onset Diaphragmatic Weakness

Differential
  • Pompe disease (acid maltase deficiency; GAA gene) — late-onset form.
  • Late-onset SMA / X-linked SMA.
  • MuSK-MG.
  • Mitochondrial myopathy.
  • Congenital myopathies with variable expression.

Chronic Disorder Presenting with Bulbar Motor Dysfunction

Differential
  • Congenital myasthenic syndromes.
  • Oculopharyngeal muscular dystrophy.
  • Mitochondrial syndromes — Kearns-Sayre.
  • Bulbar SMA variants.
  • Riboflavin transporter deficiency (Brown-Vialetto-Van Laere).
  • Pediatric motor neuron disease (rare).
Pearl 🔥🔥🔥
  • The "clinical outlier" framing is examiner-favorite — they describe a child who fits an unusual phenotype and want you to construct a sensible chronic NMD differential before going to genetic testing.

ICU layer 🔥🔥🔥
  • Modifying therapies — nusinersen (intrathecal antisense), onasemnogene abeparvovec (one-time IV gene therapy AAV9-SMN1), risdiplam (oral SMN2 splicing modifier) — transformed prognosis.
  • Diagnosed SMA in the PICU is a critical-illness emergency for therapy initiation; outcomes correlate with earlier initiation.
  • Pre-symptomatic SMA via newborn screening gives best outcomes.

Congenital Hypomyelinating / Demyelinating Neuropathies

Phenotype
  • Charcot-Marie-Tooth type 3 (Dejerine-Sottas), congenital hypomyelinating neuropathy.
  • Floppy infant + areflexia + slow conduction velocities (often <5 m/s).
  • Genetic testing — PMP22, MPZ, EGR2.
  • No disease-modifying therapy; supportive care and rehab.

Myasthenic Syndromes (CMS)

Genetic NMJ disorders
  • Presynaptic — ChAT deficiency.
  • Synaptic — COLQ (endplate AChE deficiency).
  • Postsynaptic — epsilon AChR, RAPSN, DOK7, slow-channel, fast-channel.
  • Subtype-specific therapy critical — some worsen with anticholinesterases.
Pages 2901–2904 · CHRONIC NEUROMUSCULAR DISORDERS OF CHILDHOOD — Compartment Catalog

Anterior Horn Cell

  • SMA (5q SMN1) — most common.
  • SMARD1 (IGHMBP2) — diaphragmatic phenotype.
  • X-linked SMA (UBA1).
  • Riboflavin transporter deficiency (SLC52A2/A3) — bulbar SMA mimic; treatable with riboflavin.

Peripheral Nerve

  • Charcot-Marie-Tooth subtypes — CMT1A demyelinating, CMT2 axonal, CMT3 severe early-onset, CMT4 recessive.
  • Hereditary sensory and autonomic neuropathies.
  • Familial amyloid polyneuropathy.
  • Giant axonal neuropathy.

Neuromuscular Junction

  • Congenital myasthenic syndromes (presynaptic, synaptic, postsynaptic).
  • Juvenile myasthenia gravis (autoimmune).

Myopathies

  • Congenital myopathies — nemaline, centronuclear, multi-minicore, central core.
  • Congenital muscular dystrophies — merosin-deficient, Ullrich, Walker-Warburg, Fukuyama.
  • Duchenne / Becker muscular dystrophy.
  • Myotonic dystrophy DM1 (DMPK CTG repeat) and DM2 (CNBP CCTG repeat).
  • Limb-girdle muscular dystrophies.
  • Glycogen storage diseases — Pompe (GAA).
  • Mitochondrial myopathies.
  • Fatty acid oxidation defects — CPT2, VLCAD, LCHAD.
  • Periodic paralyses (channelopathies).
Pages 2905–2908 · CONGENITAL MYOPATHIES + CONGENITAL MUSCULAR DYSTROPHIES

CONGENITAL MYOPATHIES

Overview
  • Heterogeneous group with structural muscle abnormalities; variable severity.
  • Clinical clue — floppy infant with relatively static / slowly progressive course.
  • Biopsy patterns — nemaline rods, central nuclei, multiple cores.
  • Genetics — ACTA1, NEB, RYR1, MTM1, DNM2, BIN1.
  • Preterm birth and asphyxia exacerbate neonatal pulmonary issues.
  • Some severely hypotonic infants survive with little residual weakness.
ICU layer
  • Many congenital myopathies are RYR1-related → malignant hyperthermia susceptibility.
  • Anesthesia plan must avoid volatile anesthetics and succinylcholine.
  • Cardiac involvement unusual in most congenital myopathies; variable in centronuclear forms.

CONGENITAL MUSCULAR DYSTROPHIES

Subtypes
  • Merosin-deficient (LAMA2) — most common; central white-matter signal on MRI but normal cognition.
  • Ullrich (collagen VI; COL6A1/2/3) — distal hyperlaxity + proximal contractures; severe respiratory weakness without limb collapse.
  • Walker-Warburg (POMT1) — brain malformations and severe weakness; dystroglycan glycosylation defects.
  • Fukuyama (FKTN) — brain malformations; common in Japan.
Pages 2909–2912 · DUCHENNE MUSCULAR DYSTROPHY

Duchenne Muscular Dystrophy (DMD) — Respiratory Trajectory

Natural history
  • X-linked recessive (DMD gene).
  • Loss of ambulation typically age 8–12 y.
  • Respiratory decline accelerates after wheelchair dependence.
  • FVC peaks ~age 10 then declines ~5 % per year.
  • NIV threshold typically FVC <50 % predicted with nocturnal hypoventilation.
  • Daytime NIV when FVC <30 %.
  • Tracheostomy by age 18–25 in untreated patients.
ICU layer 🔥🔥🔥
  • DMD cardiomyopathy progresses independently of skeletal muscle.
  • Start carvedilol + ACE-inhibitor at age 10 regardless of cardiac symptoms.
  • Annual echo and cardiac MRI from age 10.
  • Steroids (deflazacort / prednisone) delay loss of ambulation and respiratory decline.
Newer therapies
  • Ataluren — read-through for nonsense mutations.
  • Eteplirsen / golodirsen / casimersen — exon-skipping.
  • Delandistrogene moxeparvovec — AAV-microdystrophin gene therapy (FDA 2023, expanded 2024).
Pages 2913–2916 · Acute respiratory decompensation

ICU MANAGEMENT — Acute Decompensation

Common triggers
  • Respiratory viruses — influenza, RSV, parainfluenza, COVID-19.
  • Aspiration pneumonia.
  • Atelectasis.
  • Scoliosis progression.
  • Post-anesthesia decompensation.
  • Secretion retention.
  • Cardiac decompensation (especially in DMD).
Vaccines and antimicrobials
  • Annual influenza, COVID-19, RSV monoclonal (nirsevimab) in eligible age groups.
  • Palivizumab historically.
  • Pneumococcal conjugate and 23-valent polysaccharide (PCV20 or PCV13 + PPSV23).
  • VZV, hepatitis B series.
  • Prompt antibiotics for bacterial superinfection.
  • Avoid live vaccines on high-dose steroids.
  • Household contacts vaccinated.
Trap 🔥🔥🔥
  • Administration of supplemental oxygen alone in advanced DMD respiratory failure may mask hypoventilation and risk CO₂ narcosis.
  • Start NIV first; titrate O₂ only after ventilation is supported.

  • Modes — BiPAP, AVAPS (average volume-assured pressure support), volume-controlled in older devices.
  • Complications — pneumothorax (rare), facial pressure ulcers, gastric distension, aerophagia, aspiration if bulbar dysfunction.

Pages 2921–2924 · Tracheostomy + nutrition + GI

Tracheostomy

  • NIV inadequate.
  • Bulbar weakness prevents NIV (mask leak, aspiration).
  • Need for >16 h/day ventilation.
  • Recurrent NIV failure.
  • Decision multidisciplinary; involves patient and family.

Nutrition & Gastrostomy

  • Gastrostomy considered when oral intake inadequate, swallow study positive for aspiration, prolonged feeding times, growth failure.
  • Best placed when FVC >50 % to minimize anesthetic risk.
  • Fundoplication considered if severe gastroesophageal reflux.
  • Use TIVA + MH precautions.

Constipation and Bladder Care

  • Risk of constipation increased by prolonged immobilization and use of opioids.
  • Bladder catheterization as part of nursing care for urinary retention.
  • Bowel program — laxatives, hydration, stool softeners, regular toileting.

Pain Management

  • First-line — paracetamol or NSAIDs.
  • Escalate to weak opioids if needed.
  • Avoid strong opioids if respiratory drive marginal.
  • Adjuvants — gabapentin, amitriptyline for neuropathic pain.
Pages 2925–2928 · Scoliosis + multidisciplinary table

Scoliosis & Spinal Surgery

  • Common in DMD, SMA, congenital myopathies.
  • Bracing limited in benefit.
  • Spinal fusion considered at Cobb angle 20–30° in DMD (early, before pulmonary decline) and 40–50° in slowly progressive disease.
  • Pre-op optimization — cardiology, NIV training, MH precautions.

Pages 2929–2932 · Cardiac, malignant hyperthermia, long-term ventilation

Cardiac Involvement

  • DMD / BMD — dilated cardiomyopathy, conduction abnormalities.
  • Myotonic dystrophy — progressive conduction blocks (1st-degree → 2nd-degree → BBB → complete heart block); sudden death risk.
  • Pompe (infantile) — hypertrophic cardiomyopathy.
  • Friedreich ataxia — hypertrophic cardiomyopathy.
  • Carnitine deficiency — dilated cardiomyopathy.
  • ECG and echo standard pre-anesthesia.

Malignant Hyperthermia Precautions

High-risk diseases
  • RYR1-related congenital myopathies, central core, multi-minicore, King-Denborough.
  • Native myotonic disorders.
Anesthesia plan
  • Avoid volatile anesthetics and succinylcholine.
  • Use TIVA (propofol + opioid + non-depolarizing NMBA).
  • Have dantrolene available.
  • Continuous ETCO₂ and core temperature monitoring.
  • Clean / vapor-free anesthesia machine or activated charcoal filters.

Long-term Ventilation Ethics

  • Once intubated, the question of when not to extubate (or when to withdraw) is among the most complex ICU decisions.
  • Build the framework before crisis — advance care planning, ceiling of care discussions, family meetings, palliative care integration, ethics review when needed.
  • Respect adolescent assent / autonomy where appropriate.
Pages 2933–2940 · Conclusion

CONCLUSION

  • Integrate the acute episode into a long-term trajectory.
  • Recognize outlier phenotypes — floppy neonate, late-onset diaphragmatic weakness, bulbar dysfunction.
  • Plan NIV / tracheostomy thresholds in advance.
  • Manage cardiac, orthopedic, GI, and developmental comorbidities.
  • Deploy modifying therapies when available (SMA, DMD).
  • Maintain rigorous anesthesia precautions (MH-risk, succinylcholine).
  • Integrate palliative care early.

② DMD International Care Consensus — 2018 / 2022 update

  • Authoring body: DMD Care Considerations Working Group (CDC + TREAT-NMD + World Duchenne Organization)
  • Source: Birnkrant DJ et al. Lancet Neurol. 2018;17:251–267 (Part 1 — diagnosis/neuromuscular); Birnkrant DJ et al. Lancet Neurol. 2018;17:347–361 (Part 2 — respiratory); Birnkrant DJ et al. Lancet Neurol. 2018;17:445–455 (Part 3 — primary care/psychosocial); updates 2022
  • Link: Part 1 DOI: 10.1016/S1474-4422(18)30024-3 · Part 2 DOI: 10.1016/S1474-4422(18)30025-5 · Part 3 DOI: 10.1016/S1474-4422(18)30026-7 · TREAT-NMD: treat-nmd.org · World Duchenne Organization: worldduchenne.org
  • Key findings:
    • Steroids (deflazacort 0.9 mg/kg/d or prednisone 0.75 mg/kg/d) from age 4–6 regardless of ambulation status.
    • Cardiac protection — start ACE-I ± carvedilol at age 10 regardless of LV function; annual echo + cardiac MRI from age 10.
    • Respiratory — sleep study annually from age 6; nocturnal NIV when FVC <50 % or nocturnal hypoventilation; daytime NIV at <30 %.
    • Bone health — DEXA from diagnosis on steroids; bisphosphonate for symptomatic vertebral fracture or low BMD.
    • Spinal fusion — Cobb 20–30°, before FVC declines below 30–40 %.
    • Exon-skipping and gene therapy for eligible mutations.
  • ICU impact: Defines respiratory and cardiac trajectory and pre-emptive intervention thresholds.

③ Pompe Disease Enzyme Replacement — COMET (Avalglucosidase Alfa)

  • Authoring body: COMET Investigators
  • Source: Diaz-Manera J, Kishnani PS, Kushlaf H, Ladha S et al. Lancet Neurol. 2021;20:1012–1026 · long-term extension 2023
  • Link: DOI 10.1016/S1474-4422(21)00241-6 · PMID 34800398 · Pompe registry: pomperegistry.com
  • Key findings:
    • Avalglucosidase alfa improves respiratory function (FVC) and motor function vs. standard alglucosidase alfa in late-onset Pompe.
    • Better lysosomal targeting via increased mannose-6-phosphate residues.
    • Infantile-onset Pompe — alglucosidase alfa established standard; CRIM-negative patients require immune-tolerance induction (rituximab + methotrexate ± IVIG).
  • ICU impact: Initiate early in infantile-onset to prolong survival; consider in late-onset with isolated diaphragmatic weakness.

④ ATS / ERS Pediatric NIV Statement

  • Authoring bodies: American Thoracic Society + European Respiratory Society
  • Source: Fauroux B, Khirani S, Amaddeo A et al. Eur Respir Rev. 2022;31:220012 (NIV in pediatric NMD); Castro-Codesal ML et al. Pediatr Pulmonol. 2024 (updates)
  • Link: DOI 10.1183/16000617.0012-2022 · ATS: thoracic.org/statements · ERS: ersnet.org/guidelines
  • Key findings:
    • NIV indicated in pediatric NMD with — nocturnal CO₂ >50 mmHg, sustained SpO₂ <88 % for >5 min, daytime symptoms, FVC <50 % in DMD.
    • AVAPS or volume-controlled modes preferred for fluctuating compliance.
    • Re-evaluate every 6 months with sleep study.
    • MI-E indicated when cough peak flow <160–270 L/min.
  • ICU impact: Anchors NIV titration and timing decisions.

⑤ Riboflavin Transporter Deficiency / Brown-Vialetto-Van Laere Syndrome — Treatment Cohort Studies

  • Source: Manole A, Jaunmuktane Z, Hargreaves I et al. Brain. 2017;140:2820–2837 (mechanistic); subsequent treatment series: O'Callaghan B et al. JIMD Rep. 2019; multinational cohort updates 2020–2024 in Neurology and J Neurol Neurosurg Psychiatry
  • Link: Foundational mechanism DOI: 10.1093/brain/awx231 · PMID 29053783 · MND association: mndassociation.org
  • Key findings:
    • SLC52A2 (RFVT2) and SLC52A3 (RFVT3) mutations cause Brown-Vialetto-Van Laere syndrome — bulbar weakness, sensorineural deafness, motor neuron–like presentation.
    • High-dose oral riboflavin (10–80 mg/kg/day, divided) can halt or reverse neurological decline.
    • Early initiation matters — delays correlate with residual deficits.
    • Treatable mimic of motor neuron disease and bulbar SMA.
  • ICU impact: Any pediatric bulbar-onset motor neuron syndrome should trigger empiric riboflavin and SLC52A2/A3 testing.

⑥ ELEVATE — Delandistrogene Moxeparvovec (AAV-microdystrophin) in DMD

  • Authoring body: Sarepta Therapeutics + Roche Clinical Development
  • Source: EMBARK (Phase 3) — Mendell JR, Sahenk Z, Lehman K et al. JAMA Neurol. 2024 (full results); FDA accelerated approval 2023, expanded indication 2024
  • Link: FDA approval page: FDA Elevidys approval · Sarepta product info: sarepta.com · EMBARK primary results in JAMA Neurol. 2024
  • Key findings:
    • AAV-rh74-microdystrophin gene therapy delivers truncated functional dystrophin.
    • FDA approved 2023 for ambulatory DMD aged 4–7; expanded 2024.
    • EMBARK Phase 3 missed primary endpoint (NSAA) but showed secondary benefits.
    • Adverse events — transaminitis, immune-mediated myocarditis, myositis, complement activation.
    • Pre-treat with corticosteroids; monitor LFTs, troponin, complement.
  • ICU impact: Emerging post-infusion monitoring; manage gene-therapy-related complications.

⑦ Engel CMS Working Group — Pharmacologic Treatment of Congenital Myasthenic Syndromes

  • Authoring body: International CMS Working Group — Engel AG (Mayo), Beeson D (Oxford) and collaborators
  • Source: Engel AG, Shen XM, Selcen D, Sine SM. Lancet Neurol. 2015;14:420–434 (foundational); updates 2020–2024 in Neuromuscul Disord. and Curr Opin Neurol.
  • Link: Foundational DOI: 10.1016/S1474-4422(14)70201-7 · PMID 25792100 · Muscle Gene Table: musclegenetable.fr
  • Key findings:
    • Subtype-specific pharmacotherapy — pyridostigmine + 3,4-DAP (postsynaptic, fast-channel, RAPSN); salbutamol or ephedrine (DOK7, COLQ); fluoxetine or quinidine (slow-channel).
    • Anticholinesterases worsen slow-channel, DOK7, COLQ subtypes.
    • Genetic panel + SFEMG drive correct choice.
  • ICU impact: A floppy infant who paradoxically worsens on pyridostigmine deserves CMS subtype testing.

⑧ Myotonic Dystrophy Type 1 Consensus Care Guidelines

  • Authoring body: Myotonic Dystrophy Foundation + International DM Consortium
  • Source: Ashizawa T, Gagnon C, Groh WJ et al. Neurol Clin Pract. 2018;8:507–520 (consensus on care); pediatric DM1 update: Johnson NE et al. Pediatr Neurol. 2023
  • Link: Foundational DOI: 10.1212/CPJ.0000000000000531 · Myotonic Dystrophy Foundation: myotonic.org/toolkits-publications
  • Key findings:
    • Annual ECG with low threshold for invasive electrophysiology study or implanted device.
    • Sleep study to detect sleep-disordered breathing.
    • Anesthesia — avoid suxamethonium, expect prolonged NMBA effect, post-op respiratory monitoring 24–48 h, maintain warmth.
    • Congenital DM1 — very large CTG repeat (>1000), maternally inherited; mother often subclinical.
  • ICU impact: Pre-anesthesia ECG/echo/sleep study mandatory; vigilance for sudden cardiac death even with normal LV function.

⑨ Friedreich Ataxia — Omaveloxolone (Nrf2 activator)

  • Authoring body: MOXIe Investigators
  • Source: Lynch DR, Chin MP, Delatycki MB et al. Ann Neurol. 2021;89:212–225 (MOXIe Part 2); FDA approval February 2023
  • Link: DOI 10.1002/ana.25934 · PMID 33068037 · Friedreich Ataxia Research Alliance: curefa.org
  • Key findings:
    • Omaveloxolone (SKYCLARYS) — first FDA-approved disease-modifying therapy for FA (≥16 y).
    • Slower progression on modified Friedreich Ataxia Rating Scale.
    • Cardiomyopathy remains primary cause of death — independent cardiac surveillance still required.
  • ICU impact: Cardiac decompensation and scoliosis surgery remain main PICU presentations; omaveloxolone now part of long-term care.

⑩ Spinal Surgery in Neuromuscular Scoliosis — Consensus

  • Authoring body: Pediatric Orthopaedic Society of North America (POSNA) + Scoliosis Research Society
  • Source: Cheuk DK et al. Cochrane Database Syst Rev. 2015 (DMD scoliosis); updated approach in Roye BD et al. J Pediatr Orthop. 2020
  • Link: Cochrane DOI: 10.1002/14651858.CD005375.pub4 · POSNA: posna.org · SRS: srs.org
  • Key findings:
    • DMD — operate at Cobb 20–30°, ideally with FVC >30–40 %.
    • SMA — operate at Cobb 40–50° depending on flexibility, often later.
    • Pre-op — cardiology clearance, NIV training, MH precautions, multidisciplinary planning.
    • Use tranexamic acid; consider cell saver.
    • Plan extubation directly to NIV.
  • ICU impact: Anchors pre-op optimization and post-op respiratory plan.

6. SAQ Bank — 40 Questions

SAQ 1. Define three outlier phenotypes suggesting chronic NMD in the PICU.
Answer.
  • Neonatal respiratory distress in a non-preterm infant without obvious lung disease.
  • Later-onset isolated diaphragmatic weakness.
  • Bulbar dysfunction (dysphagia, dysarthria, recurrent aspiration) without anatomic cause.
SAQ 2. SMA type 1 features and current modifying therapy.
Answer.
  • Onset <6 months, severe hypotonia, never sits, tongue fasciculations, intercostal recession with bell-shaped chest, areflexia.
  • Nusinersen — intrathecal antisense; 4 loading doses then every 4 months.
  • Onasemnogene abeparvovec — single IV gene therapy <2 years.
  • Risdiplam — oral splicing modifier (>2 months).
SAQ 3. DMD respiratory trajectory.
Answer.
  • FVC peaks ~age 10, declines ~5 %/year.
  • NIV at FVC <50 % or nocturnal hypoventilation.
  • Daytime NIV at <30 %.
  • Tracheostomy at <10 % or with bulbar failure.
  • Steroids and gene therapy flatten the slope.
SAQ 4. Cardiac surveillance in DMD.
Answer.
  • Annual ECG from diagnosis.
  • Annual echo + cardiac MRI from age 10.
  • Start ACE-I ± carvedilol at age 10 regardless of LV function.
  • Intensify with eplerenone or sacubitril/valsartan if dysfunction develops.
SAQ 5. Why is supplemental O₂ alone hazardous in advanced DMD respiratory failure?
Answer.
  • Hypercapnia is the primary problem.
  • O₂ corrects SpO₂ without addressing ventilation, masking deterioration.
  • Risks acute hypercapnic acidosis and CO₂ narcosis.
  • Always start NIV first; titrate O₂ only after ventilation is supported.
SAQ 6. Four congenital myopathy subtypes with biopsy / gene.
Answer.
  • Nemaline — rod bodies (NEB, ACTA1).
  • Centronuclear / myotubular — central nuclei (MTM1, DNM2, BIN1).
  • Multi-minicore — multiple small cores (SEPN1, RYR1).
  • Central core — single core (RYR1).
SAQ 7. Anesthesia precautions for RYR1-related myopathy.
Answer.
  • Avoid volatile anesthetics and succinylcholine.
  • TIVA (propofol + opioid + non-depolarizing NMBA, reduced dose).
  • Dantrolene available.
  • Continuous ETCO₂ + core temperature.
  • Pre-op MH consultation.
SAQ 8. Cough peak flow thresholds and action.
Answer.
  • >270 L/min — normal.
  • 160–270 — borderline; proactive techniques during illness.
  • <160 — initiate MI-E routinely.
  • High risk of mucus plugging during respiratory illness.
SAQ 9. Pompe disease and late-onset presentation.
Answer.
  • Acid α-glucosidase (GAA) deficiency causing glycogen accumulation in muscle.
  • Late-onset (LOPD) — proximal limb weakness, isolated diaphragmatic weakness, preserved cardiac function.
  • Treated with enzyme replacement (alglucosidase alfa, avalglucosidase alfa).
  • Infantile form — hypertrophic cardiomyopathy, profound hypotonia, death <1 year without ERT.
SAQ 10. Congenital myotonic dystrophy and its risks.
Answer.
  • DM1 with very large CTG repeat expansion (>1000); maternally inherited.
  • Floppy neonate, polyhydramnios, club feet, talipes, feeding difficulties, respiratory failure.
  • Mother often has only mild grip myotonia.
  • Cardiac conduction defects develop later.
  • Generalized weakness may improve in childhood then progress.
SAQ 11. DM1 cardiac conduction implications.
Answer.
  • Progressive conduction system disease — 1st-degree AV block → 2nd-degree → BBB → complete heart block.
  • Sudden cardiac death risk even with normal LV function.
  • Annual ECG mandatory.
  • Pacemaker / ICD per criteria.
  • Avoid QT-prolonging drugs.
SAQ 12. Four congenital muscular dystrophy subtypes.
Answer.
  • Merosin-deficient (LAMA2) — white matter signal on MRI; normal cognition.
  • Ullrich (collagen VI; COL6A1/2/3) — distal hyperlaxity + proximal contractures.
  • Walker-Warburg (POMT1) — brain malformations, severe early respiratory weakness.
  • Fukuyama (FKTN) — brain malformations, common in Japan.
SAQ 13. Role of NIV during acute exacerbation.
Answer.
  • First-line for hypoventilation.
  • Continue or initiate NIV during exacerbations.
  • AVAPS or volume-controlled mode for fluctuating compliance.
  • Add MI-E for secretions.
  • Avoid supplemental O₂ alone.
  • Failure — persistent acidosis, bulbar collapse, mental status decline → intubation with explicit extubation plan.
SAQ 14. Palliative care role in chronic NMD ICU.
Answer.
  • Integrate from first admission.
  • Clarify ceilings of care, advance directives.
  • Symptom management — dyspnea, pain, anxiety.
  • Family support.
  • School re-integration; transition planning to adult services.
  • After-death follow-up.
  • Early integration improves quality of life and reduces conflict.
SAQ 15. Scoliosis surgery timing in DMD.
Answer.
  • Cobb angle 20–30°.
  • Ideally before FVC falls below 30–40 % predicted.
  • Earlier surgery has better tolerance.
  • Pre-op — cardiology clearance, NIV training, MH precautions, multidisciplinary planning.
SAQ 16. Nusinersen mechanism and dosing.
Answer.
  • Antisense oligonucleotide modifying SMN2 splicing to include exon 7.
  • Increases functional SMN protein in motor neurons.
  • Intrathecal dosing (12 mg) — 4 loading doses (days 0, 14, 28, 63), then every 4 months.
SAQ 17. Onasemnogene mechanism and key AEs.
Answer.
  • Single IV AAV9-vector gene therapy delivering SMN1 to motor neurons.
  • Approved <2 years.
  • AEs — transaminitis (steroids), thrombocytopenia, troponin elevation, rare thrombotic microangiopathy.
  • Pre-treat prednisolone 1 mg/kg/d from day –1 with taper based on LFTs.
SAQ 18. When is gastrostomy indicated in chronic NMD?
Answer.
  • Inadequate oral intake, growth failure.
  • Prolonged feeding (>30–45 min).
  • Aspiration on swallow study.
  • Frequent meal-related desaturation.
  • Best placed when FVC >50 %.
  • Fundoplication added if severe reflux.
SAQ 19. Differentiate myasthenic crisis vs. acute exacerbation in chronic CMS.
Answer.
  • Both — worsening weakness + respiratory compromise.
  • Autoimmune MG — Ab-mediated; responds to IVIG/PLEX and immunotherapy.
  • CMS — genetic; requires subtype identification.
  • Some CMS subtypes (slow-channel, DOK7, COLQ) worsen with anticholinesterases.
  • SFEMG and genetic testing inform management.
SAQ 20. List CMS subtypes and their therapy.
Answer.
  • Presynaptic ChAT — pyridostigmine + manage episodic apnea.
  • Synaptic COLQ — anticholinesterases contraindicated; salbutamol / ephedrine.
  • Postsynaptic AChR-ε / RAPSN / fast-channel — pyridostigmine ± 3,4-DAP.
  • DOK7 — salbutamol / ephedrine; anticholinesterases worsen.
  • Slow-channel — fluoxetine / quinidine; anticholinesterases worsen.
SAQ 21. Riboflavin transporter deficiency.
Answer.
  • SLC52A2/A3 mutations → Brown-Vialetto-Van Laere syndrome.
  • Bulbar weakness, sensorineural deafness, motor neuron–like presentation.
  • Treatable with high-dose riboflavin 10–80 mg/kg/day.
  • Treatable mimic of bulbar SMA / motor neuron disease.
SAQ 22. Outline NIV initiation.
Answer.
  • Confirm indication — sleep study, daytime CO₂, FVC, symptoms.
  • Interface — nasal mask preferred; full-face for mouth breathers.
  • Mode — BiPAP S/T or AVAPS.
  • Initial settings — IPAP 8–10 over EPAP 4–5, titrate to relieve WOB and normalize CO₂.
  • Backup rate.
  • Acclimatize awake first.
  • Re-evaluate every 6 months with sleep study.
SAQ 23. Eight elements of a chronic NMD multidisciplinary care plan.
Answer.
  • Neuromuscular neurology.
  • Pulmonology + sleep + RT + MI-E.
  • Cardiology.
  • Orthopedic surgery.
  • PT / OT.
  • Speech-language pathology + GI + nutrition.
  • Medical genetics + family counselling.
  • Mental health + social work + school.
  • Palliative care.
  • Case manager / coordination.
SAQ 24. Diagnostic approach to a floppy newborn.
Answer.
  • History — pregnancy, family NMD.
  • Exam — tone, reflexes, antigravity movement, dysmorphism, mother's grip myotonia.
  • SMN1 deletion + SMN2 copy number.
  • Congenital myopathy / muscular dystrophy panel.
  • DM1 CTG repeat in mother.
  • CMS panel; mitochondrial / metabolic screen.
  • Brain MRI if dysmorphism / seizures.
  • Echo if cardiac suspicion.
SAQ 25. CMT type 3 (Dejerine-Sottas) in a neonate.
Answer.
  • Severe congenital hypomyelinating neuropathy.
  • Hypotonia, areflexia, very low motor and sensory conduction velocities (often <5 m/s).
  • Some require ventilatory support.
  • PMP22 / MPZ / EGR2.
  • No disease-modifying therapy; supportive care and rehab.
SAQ 26. Vaccines and prophylaxis in chronic NMD.
Answer.
  • Annual influenza, COVID-19.
  • PCV20 or PCV13 + PPSV23.
  • VZV, hepatitis B.
  • RSV (nirsevimab) for eligible.
  • Palivizumab historically.
  • Avoid live vaccines on high-dose steroids.
  • Household contacts vaccinated.
SAQ 27. MH susceptibility in pediatric NMD.
Answer.
  • RYR1-related, central core, multi-minicore — MH-susceptible.
  • Avoid volatile + succinylcholine.
  • TIVA with dantrolene available.
  • Continuous ETCO₂ + temperature.
  • Pre-op MH consultation; emergency surgery in unknown NMD — assume susceptibility.
SAQ 28. Cardiac involvement across pediatric NMD.
Answer.
  • DMD/BMD — dilated cardiomyopathy + conduction defects.
  • Myotonic dystrophy — progressive conduction, SCD risk.
  • Pompe (infantile) — hypertrophic cardiomyopathy.
  • Friedreich ataxia — hypertrophic cardiomyopathy.
  • Carnitine deficiency — dilated cardiomyopathy.
  • Congenital myopathies — usually spared.
SAQ 29. Steroid role in DMD.
Answer.
  • Deflazacort 0.9 mg/kg/d or prednisone 0.75 mg/kg/d.
  • Start age 4–6.
  • Delays loss of ambulation 2–3 years.
  • Slows respiratory decline; may delay cardiomyopathy.
  • Side effects — weight gain, growth restriction, osteoporosis, cataracts.
  • Never stop abruptly during acute illness.
SAQ 30. Ataluren and exon-skipping in DMD.
Answer.
  • Ataluren — read-through for nonsense (stop-codon) mutations.
  • Eteplirsen (exon 51), golodirsen (exon 53), casimersen (exon 45) — exon-skipping antisense oligonucleotides.
  • Restore reading frame in eligible mutations.
  • Modest dystrophin restoration; benefit debated.
SAQ 31. Delandistrogene moxeparvovec indications.
Answer.
  • AAV-microdystrophin gene therapy.
  • FDA-approved 2023 for ambulatory DMD 4–7 y; expanded 2024.
  • Single IV infusion.
  • AEs — hepatotoxicity, myocarditis, myositis.
  • Long-term data emerging.
SAQ 32. Tracheostomy indications in chronic NMD.
Answer.
  • NIV inadequate.
  • >16 h/day ventilation.
  • Bulbar weakness preventing mask seal.
  • Recurrent aspiration / pneumonia on NIV.
  • Intolerance of NIV.
  • Decision involves patient, family, multidisciplinary team, palliative care.
SAQ 33. Gastrostomy timing relative to respiratory decline.
Answer.
  • Best placed when FVC >50 % to minimize anesthetic risk.
  • Indications — aspiration, prolonged feeds, growth failure, poor weight gain, meal-related desaturation.
  • Laparoscopic where feasible; open if abdominal access needed.
  • Use TIVA + MH precautions.
SAQ 34. Typical NIV failure scenario and management.
Answer.
  • Acute bronchiolitis in a teenager with DMD — increased secretions, mask leak, escalating PCO₂.
  • Optimize MI-E, treat infection.
  • Escalate NIV — full-face mask, increased pressures, backup rate.
  • Broaden antibiotics.
  • Consider intubation with explicit plan for extubation to NIV.
  • If extubation fails — consider tracheostomy with prior consent.
SAQ 35. Long-term ventilation ethics.
Answer.
  • Patient assent / autonomy vs. parent decision-making.
  • Quality-of-life considerations.
  • Resource allocation.
  • Reversibility.
  • Advance care planning.
  • Withdrawal vs. withholding.
  • Palliative integration.
  • Family caregiver burden.
  • School re-integration.
SAQ 36. Approach to 16-year-old male with new isolated diaphragmatic weakness.
Answer.
  • History — family, weakness elsewhere, ptosis, fluctuation.
  • Sleep study, ABG, FVC.
  • EMG with diaphragm sampling.
  • GAA enzyme (Pompe).
  • AChR / MuSK Ab.
  • CMS panel.
  • Brain / spine MRI.
  • Cardiac assessment.
  • Treat underlying disorder — ERT for Pompe, immunotherapy for MG, subtype-specific for CMS.
  • NIV bridge.
SAQ 37. Friedreich ataxia features relevant to PICU.
Answer.
  • Progressive sensory ataxia.
  • Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy — leading cause of death.
  • Diabetes, kyphoscoliosis, sensorineural deafness, optic atrophy.
  • Admissions usually for cardiac decompensation or scoliosis surgery.
  • Anesthesia — cardiology clearance, avoid hypotension, MH-safe (not RYR1).
SAQ 38. Goal of SMA newborn screening.
Answer.
  • Identify pre-symptomatic SMA for immediate disease-modifying therapy.
  • Onasemnogene preferred <2 mo with 1–3 SMN2 copies.
  • Pre-symptomatic treatment gives near-normal motor outcomes.
  • Symptomatic treatment is much less effective.
  • US RUSP universal screening since 2018.
SAQ 39. Mitochondrial disease anesthetic considerations.
Answer.
  • Avoid prolonged fasting; give 10 % dextrose.
  • Avoid propofol infusion >1 h (PRIS risk).
  • Avoid lactate-containing fluids.
  • Limit volatile anesthetics.
  • Short-acting agents.
  • Plan for slower recovery and respiratory monitoring.
SAQ 40. Transition-of-care planning for adolescent chronic NMD.
Answer.
  • Begin at 14–16 y.
  • Multidisciplinary transition clinic.
  • Identify adult neuromuscular neurologist + pulmonologist + cardiologist.
  • Patient education and self-management.
  • Family role redefinition.
  • Financial / insurance / disability paperwork.
  • Educational / vocational planning.

Case 25 — Floppy newborn with polyhydramnios.
Floppy newborn; pregnancy had polyhydramnios.
Q. Top differential and key bedside test.
Answer.
  • Differentials — congenital DM1, SMA 0/1, severe congenital myopathy, CMD.
  • Bedside — examine mother for grip myotonia.
  • SMN1 deletion test.
  • Polyhydramnios reflects fetal swallow weakness.
Case 26 — SMA3 loses ambulation.
SMA type 3 ambulatory child loses ambulation at age 12 during a viral illness.
Q. Acute and chronic plan.
Answer.
  • Treat acute illness; intensive rehab.
  • Evaluate for modifying therapy intensification.
  • Plan ambulatory aids, school accommodations.
  • Cardiac and respiratory surveillance.
Case 27 — Home-vent DMD with mucus plug.
DMD on long-term home ventilation via tracheostomy with mucus plugging and desaturation.

Answer.

Case 28 — 3,4-DAP seizure.
CMS infant on chronic 3,4-DAP develops seizure.
Q. Mechanism and management.
Answer.
  • 3,4-DAP lowers seizure threshold.
  • Stop 3,4-DAP; treat with benzodiazepine.
  • Exclude metabolic seizure causes.
  • Restart at reduced dose if essential.
Case 29 — MELAS in status epilepticus.
Adolescent with mitochondrial myopathy (MELAS) in status epilepticus.
Q. Care priorities.
Answer.
  • Avoid valproate (mitochondrial / hepatotoxic).
  • Avoid prolonged propofol (PRIS).
  • Levetiracetam first-line.
  • Maintain glucose; treat acidosis.
  • Hydration; cardiac monitoring.
  • Arginine / CoQ10 / L-carnitine supplementation per protocol.
Case 30 — CMT1A trauma admission.
CMT1A adolescent post-trauma needs anesthesia.
Q. Anesthetic concerns.
Answer.
  • Generally MH-safe.
  • Avoid succinylcholine (hyperkalemia risk).
  • Sensitivity to non-depolarizing NMBA.
  • Post-op chest physio if respiratory muscle involvement.
Case 31 — DMD vertebral fracture.
DMD adolescent on long-term steroids has vertebral fracture from minor fall.
Q. Management.
Answer.
  • Pain management without compromising respiratory drive.
  • Bracing as tolerated.
  • Calcium / vitamin D optimization.
  • DEXA monitoring.
  • Bisphosphonate (zoledronate/pamidronate) for symptomatic fracture or low BMD.
  • Continue steroids — never stop abruptly.
Case 32 — Friedreich ataxia VT.
Friedreich ataxia adolescent admitted for ventricular tachycardia.
Q. Management.
Answer.
  • Treat VT (amiodarone; defibrillation if unstable).
  • Echo / MRI for HCM.
  • ICD consideration.
  • Cardiology referral.
  • Avoid beta-blocker boluses that drop BP excessively.
Case 33 — Centronuclear myopathy apnea.
Centronuclear myopathy adolescent post-anesthesia has prolonged apnea.
Q. Mechanism, future planning.
Answer.
  • Sensitivity to opioids and NMBA — reduce doses.
  • Plan TIVA next time; regional anesthesia where feasible.
  • Post-op respiratory monitoring 24 h.
  • MH-safe but verify subtype genetics.
Case 34 — Post-onasemnogene bronchiolitis.
SMA1 toddler 6 months post-onasemnogene develops new bronchiolitis.
Q. Continue modifying therapy?
Answer.
  • Onasemnogene was a single dose — no further dosing.
  • Continue supportive care, NIV/MI-E as needed.
  • Reassess motor outcomes; consider add-on nusinersen or risdiplam if motor progression suboptimal.

Answer.

  • Clarify medical realities (transplant unlikely candidate; prognosis).

  • Engage palliative care alongside active treatment, not opposed.
  • Document plan; revisit periodically.

③ ICU management thresholds

  • Cough peak flow <160 L/min → MI-E.
  • FVC (DMD) <50 % → nocturnal NIV; <30 % → daytime NIV.
  • Sleep CO₂ >50 mmHg → NIV.
  • Cobb angle (DMD) 20–30° → spinal fusion.
  • Echo (DMD) annual from 10 y; ACE-I + carvedilol at 10 y.
  • SMA gene therapy — onasemnogene <2 y; nusinersen/risdiplam any age.

④ Red flags

  • Supplemental O₂ alone in DMD respiratory failure → CO₂ narcosis.
  • Volatile anesthetic / succinylcholine in RYR1 myopathy → MH.
  • Succinylcholine in chronic NMD or post-onset weakness >24 h → hyperkalemia.
  • Pyridostigmine dose escalation in slow-channel / DOK7 / COLQ CMS → worsens weakness.
  • Valproate / prolonged propofol in mitochondrial disease → hepatic failure / PRIS.
  • Stopping steroids abruptly during DMD acute illness → adrenal insufficiency.

⑤ Exam pearls
