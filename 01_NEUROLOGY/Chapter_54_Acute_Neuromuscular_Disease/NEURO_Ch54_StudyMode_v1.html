CHAPTER 54 ■ Acute Neuromuscular Disease — Key Points

11 key points distilled
  • "Acute flaccid paralysis" (AFP) defined as rapid-onset weakness frequently involving respiratory and bulbar muscles — mandatory polio surveillance for any AFP <15 y.
  • AFP is an umbrella term — paralytic polio, transverse myelitis, AFM, GBS, toxic neuropathy, muscle disorders.
  • GBS dominates pediatric AFP; annual incidence in <9 y ≈ 0.62 / 100 000.
  • Classical GBS — progressive, ascending, symmetric, areflexic weakness of more than one limb plus autonomic dysfunction.
  • Subtype distribution varies geographically — AIDP 85–90 % in Europe / North America; AMAN dominant in China, Japan, Bangladesh, Mexico.
  • GBS is uncommon in PICU and rare in the very young — thoracic pump failure drives mechanical ventilation; bulbar dysfunction drives airway protection.
  • Diagnosis is clinical + NCS abnormalities + CSF cytoalbuminologic dissociation.
  • PICU admission triggers — rapid motor progression with respiratory muscle involvement, ventilatory insufficiency, pneumonia, severe bulbar weakness, autonomic instability, arrhythmia, bradycardia.
  • Autonomic dysfunction is an important cause of death (hemodynamic instability + arrhythmia).
  • Succinylcholine-induced hyperkalemia / arrhythmia complicates intubation.
  • First-line therapy — plasma exchange (50 mL/kg) or IVIG (400 mg/kg/day × 5 = 2 g/kg). IVIG preferred when line access difficult.
  • PLEX preferred over IVIG in IgA deficiency (anaphylaxis risk with IVIG).
RCPSC relevance 🔥🔥🔥
  • Examiners weight this chapter heavily — GBS + MG crisis stems with explicit thresholds for intubation, drug avoidance, and immunotherapy choice.

INTRODUCTION — Anatomic Classification

Five-compartment framework
  • Brain — encephalitis, brainstem stroke / locked-in.
  • Spinal cord — transverse myelitis, AFM, trauma.
  • Peripheral nerve — GBS variants, toxic neuropathy, CIP.
  • Neuromuscular junction — MG, botulism, organophosphates, envenomation.
  • Muscle — acute myositis, periodic paralysis, rhabdomyolysis, CIM.
ICU layer
  • A floppy, areflexic, ventilator-dependent child without sensory level points to peripheral nerve or NMJ.
  • Keep central causes on the differential only if pyramidal signs, altered consciousness, or sensory level.
  • Localization drives both diagnosis and management — name the compartment before the disease.
Pearl 🔥🔥🔥
  • The 5-compartment framework is the structural answer to "approach to acute weakness" stems.

RESPIRATORY ASSESSMENT IN NEUROMUSCULAR WEAKNESS — Clinical & Laboratory

Clinical parameters
  • Tachypnea and shallow breathing.
  • Paradoxical breathing — chest in / abdomen out on inspiration.
  • Single-breath count — counting numbers on a single breath; <15 indicates significant weakness (correlates with FVC <30 % predicted).
  • Head-drop, weak cough, secretion pooling.
  • Nasal voice, dysphagia, aspiration.
  • Repeat single-breath count every 2–4 h in evolving GBS/MG — trend matters.
Laboratory / mechanics parameters
  • FVC <20 mL/kg or <30 % predicted → intubate.
  • MIP less negative than –30 cmH₂O → intubate.
  • MEP <40 cmH₂O → cough failure.
  • SpO₂ sustained <94 % on room air — late sign.
  • PaCO₂ >45 mmHg and rising — end-stage; intubate emergently.
Pearl 🔥🔥🔥
  • "20/30/40 rule" — FVC 20 mL/kg, MIP –30 cmH₂O, MEP 40 cmH₂O.
  • Hypoxia and hypercapnia are LATE, not early, signs in neuromuscular respiratory failure.
Exam trap
  • Normal ABG is not reassurance — minute ventilation falls before hypercapnia appears.

ACUTE FLACCID PARALYSIS — Umbrella Differential

Definition + WHO surveillance
  • Pragmatic case definition — rapid-onset weakness frequently involving respiratory and bulbar muscles.
  • WHO mandates polio surveillance reporting for any AFP <15 y of age.
  • Two stool samples 24–48 h apart for poliovirus PCR — mandatory.
Causes by compartment
  • AHC — poliomyelitis, AFM, West Nile, Japanese encephalitis, Murray Valley, St Louis, Russian spring-summer.
  • Cord — transverse myelitis (NMOSD, MOGAD, MS, idiopathic).
  • Peripheral nerve — GBS variants, toxic, diphtheric, tick paralysis.
  • NMJ — botulism, MG, organophosphate, envenomation.
  • Muscle — acute myositis (viral, immune), periodic paralysis, rhabdomyolysis.
ICU layer
  • Always report AFP to public health regardless of working diagnosis.
  • Workup runs in parallel — MRI cord, NCS, antibodies, viral PCR, CSF — do not wait for one to start the next.

GUILLAIN-BARRÉ SYNDROME — Epidemiology and Pathogenesis

Epidemiology
  • Pediatric incidence <9 y ≈ 0.62 / 100 000 / year.
  • Bimodal age distribution; less common but more severe in toddlers.
  • AIDP dominates in Western countries; AMAN dominates in Asia, parts of Central America.
Pathogenesis — molecular mimicry
  • Antecedent infection (Campylobacter jejuni, CMV, EBV, mycoplasma, Zika, SARS-CoV-2) generates antiganglioside antibodies.
  • Anti-GM1 / GD1a — AMAN, AMSAN.
  • Anti-GQ1b — Miller-Fisher, Bickerstaff.
  • For AIDP, specific autoantibodies remain elusive.
  • Autoantibodies bind myelin antigens and produce demyelination via complement-mediated mechanisms.
Table 54.1 — Antecedent infections reported with GBS
  • Campylobacter jejuni (especially AMAN).
  • CMV, EBV, mycoplasma pneumoniae.
  • Influenza, hepatitis E.
  • Zika virus, SARS-CoV-2.
  • Post-vaccination — rare; benefit / risk balance favors immunization.

GBS — Clinical Features & Pathology

Clinical progression
  • Onset typically 5–14 days after antecedent infection.
  • Ascending symmetric weakness over hours to days; nadir within 4 weeks (90 % within 2 weeks).
  • Paresthesias frequently precede weakness.
  • Cranial nerves VII, IX, X, XII commonly involved.
  • Autonomic dysfunction — sinus tachycardia, BP swings, ileus, urinary retention.
  • Half of patients have CNS involvement signs (cerebellar ataxia, extensor plantar, absent reflexes).
Pathology
  • AIDP — demyelinating lesions along the entire peripheral nerve length, motor > sensory.
  • AMAN — axonal degeneration of motor fibers with little inflammation.
  • Sometimes early conduction block with rapid reversibility — accounts for some rapid recoveries.

GBS — Diagnosis

Brighton criteria (levels of certainty)
  • Level 1 (highest) — bilateral flaccid weakness, decreased / absent reflexes, monophasic course (nadir 12 h–28 days), CSF protein >0.45 g/L with cells <50/μL, electrodiagnostic evidence of GBS.
  • Lower levels relax electrodiagnostic and CSF requirements.
Diagnostic tools
  • NCS — slow CV, prolonged distal latency, absent / prolonged F-waves, conduction block (AIDP); low CMAP amplitudes (AMAN).
  • CSF — cytoalbuminologic dissociation; up to 50 % normal in week 1.
  • MRI spine — enhancement of cauda equina / ventral nerve roots (post-gadolinium).
  • Antiganglioside antibodies — confirmatory, not screening.
Trap
  • Early CSF is often normal — do not exclude GBS based on early CSF alone; F-waves are more sensitive.

GBS — Differential Diagnosis

Major differentials
  • Transverse myelitis — sensory level, sphincter involvement, MRI cord lesion.
  • AFM — asymmetric flaccid, cord gray-matter T2 lesion.
  • Poliomyelitis / AFP from other enteroviruses — preserved SNAP, asymmetric.
  • Myasthenic crisis — fatigability, ocular features.
  • Botulism — descending paralysis, autonomic, pupil involvement.
  • Toxic neuropathy — heavy metals, organophosphates, diphtheria.
  • Acute myositis — proximal pain, raised CK.
  • Tick paralysis — examine skin.

GBS — ICU Management

Indications for PICU admission
  • Rapid progression of motor weakness involving respiratory muscles.
  • FVC trending toward 20 mL/kg.
  • Bulbar weakness with secretion-handling failure.
  • Autonomic instability (BP swings, arrhythmia, ileus).
  • Inability to ambulate within 24 h of admission.
  • Pneumonia / aspiration.
Adult predictors of mechanical ventilation (proxy in older children)
  • Time from onset to admission <7 days.
  • Inability to lift elbows above bed.
  • Inability to lift head off bed.
  • Inability to stand.
  • Inability to cough.
  • Vital capacity <60 % predicted.
Pearl 🔥🔥🔥
  • Intubation is elective, not emergent.
  • Avoid succinylcholine — risk of hyperkalemia from upregulated extrajunctional AChRs and denervated muscle.
  • Use rocuronium with sugammadex reversal available.

GBS — Autonomic Management & Tracheostomy

Autonomic dysfunction
  • Sinus tachycardia, hypertension, postural hypotension, urinary retention, ileus, abnormal sweating.
  • Continuous ECG and BP monitoring mandatory.
  • Use only short-acting antihypertensives (e.g., labetalol IV) for end-organ symptoms — autonomic lability is bidirectional; long-acting drugs cause sustained hypotension.
  • Bradyarrhythmias on suctioning — pre-treat with atropine 20 mcg/kg IV; gentle suctioning technique.
  • Consider transcutaneous or transvenous pacing standby in severe dysautonomia.
  • Aggressive ileus prophylaxis; address urinary retention; thromboprophylaxis.
Tracheostomy considerations
  • Typically considered after >14 days of ventilation with no clinical improvement.
  • Benefits — comfort, oral feeding, rehab facilitation, may shorten ICU stay.
  • Decision multidisciplinary, involves family.

MYASTHENIA GRAVIS — Pathogenesis and Subtypes

Pathogenesis
  • Postsynaptic NMJ antibody-mediated disease.
  • Polyclonal IgG against AChR, MuSK, or LRP4.
  • Complement-mediated destruction of postsynaptic folds.
Subtypes in pediatrics
  • Juvenile MG (JMG) — AChR antibody most common in older children.
  • Neonatal transient MG — transplacental maternal AChR Ab in 10–20 % of MG mothers' infants; resolves 2–8 weeks.
  • MuSK-Ab MG — prominent bulbar / respiratory weakness; facial wasting; less responsive to anticholinesterases; better with PLEX and rituximab.
  • Congenital myasthenic syndromes — genetic (epsilon AChR, RAPSN, DOK7, COLQ, slow-channel) — covered also in Chapter 55.
  • Seronegative MG — diagnosed by clinical + SFEMG when antibodies negative.

MG — Clinical Features & Crisis

Clinical features
  • Fatigable weakness worse at end of day.
  • Ocular — ptosis, diplopia (often presenting).
  • Bulbar — dysphonia, dysphagia, dysarthria.
  • Limb — proximal fatigability.
  • Respiratory — pump and bulbar combined.
  • Pupils are SPARED in autoimmune MG (vs. botulism where they are involved).
Myasthenic crisis
  • Definition — acute respiratory failure / severe bulbar weakness requiring intubation or NIV.
  • Triggers — infection, surgery, drugs (see avoid list), pregnancy, missed pyridostigmine, emotional stress.
Cholinergic crisis
  • Over-treatment with anticholinesterase.
  • SLUDGE — salivation, lacrimation, urination, diarrhea, GI cramping, emesis, bradycardia, miosis.
  • Plus nicotinic weakness.
  • Edrophonium (Tensilon) test — improves myasthenic but worsens cholinergic crisis; rarely used today.
  • Practical approach — stop pyridostigmine, support ventilation, distinguish by clinical course.

MG — Diagnosis

Diagnostic approach
  • Bedside fatigability — sustained upgaze, repeated arm abduction, single-breath count.
  • Ice-pack test — ice on ptotic lid for 2 minutes improves ptosis >2 mm; specificity 80–90 %.
  • AChR-Ab — positive ~85 % generalized JMG, ~50 % ocular.
  • MuSK-Ab — positive ~40 % of AChR-negative MG.
  • Anti-LRP4 antibody — emerging in seronegative MG.
  • RNS at 3 Hz — >10 % decrement.
  • SFEMG — increased jitter ± blocking; most sensitive.
  • Edrophonium / neostigmine — largely supplanted; bradyarrhythmia risk.
  • CT thorax — thymoma in adolescents (rare under 10 y).

MG — Management Graded Approach

Stepwise therapy
  • Symptomatic — pyridostigmine 0.5–1.5 mg/kg PO every 4–6 h (start low; titrate).
  • Crisis rescue — IVIG 2 g/kg over 2–5 days or PLEX 50 mL/kg × 5–6 sessions.
  • Maintenance immunotherapy — prednisone (expect transient worsening; start in-hospital), azathioprine, mycophenolate, cyclosporine, methotrexate.
  • Refractory — rituximab, eculizumab (anti-C5), efgartigimod (FcRn inhibitor).
  • Thymectomy — indicated for thymoma; consider in generalized AChR-positive non-thymomatous MG in adolescents (MGTX trial extension).
Intubation pearls 🔥🔥🔥
  • Avoid succinylcholine — variable response; risk of arrhythmia / hyperkalemia in chronic disease.
  • Rocuronium — reduce dose ~50 %; patients are exquisitely sensitive.
  • Plan sugammadex reversal.
  • Avoid magnesium for tocolysis or hypertension in MG patients.

ACUTE PORPHYRIAS

Overview
  • Hepatic porphyrias — acute intermittent porphyria (AIP), hereditary coproporphyria, variegate, ALA-dehydratase deficiency.
  • Present in late childhood / adolescence.
  • Classic tetrad — abdominal pain, altered mental status, autonomic dysfunction, motor neuropathy progressing to bulbar / respiratory failure.
  • Pain in limbs, back, neck, and chest in up to 70 %.
  • Hyponatremia (SIADH).
  • Port-wine urine on standing.
Triggers
  • Drugs inducing hepatic CYP450 — barbiturates, sulfonamides, rifampin, phenytoin, carbamazepine, valproate, OCPs, ergots.
  • Fasting, infection, stress, hormonal cycles.
Diagnosis
  • Urine PBG and ALA on a fresh, light-protected sample.
  • Erythrocyte PBG deaminase activity (low in AIP, but normal does not exclude).
  • Genetic confirmation (HMBS, CPOX, PPOX, ALAD).
Table 54.4 — Drugs unsafe in porphyria
  • Barbiturates, sulfonamides, sulfonylureas, ergots, rifampin, dapsone, griseofulvin, danazol, OCPs.
  • Phenytoin, carbamazepine, valproate, primidone.
  • Methyldopa, hydralazine, theophylline (sometimes), pyrazinamide.
  • Always cross-check via American Porphyria Foundation database.
Treatment
  • Remove triggers — stop offending drugs, infection control, hydration, electrolyte correction.
  • High-carbohydrate intake (≥300 g glucose/day) suppresses ALA-synthase; IV dextrose 10 % if PO not feasible.
  • Heme arginate 3 mg/kg/day × 4 days — first-line in severe attacks.
  • Givosiran (RNAi against ALA-synthase 1; adult approval, emerging pediatric data) for recurrent attacks.
  • Pain control — morphine; nausea — ondansetron; seizures — lorazepam (safe).
  • Treat hyponatremia cautiously to prevent osmotic demyelination.

DIAPHRAGMATIC PALSY + CONCLUSION

Causes
BilateralUnilateral
SMARD1, AHC disease, GBS, MG, congenital myopathy, central hypoventilationPostsurgical phrenic injury (cardiac surgery, neck surgery), birth trauma (with Erb's palsy), tumors, eventration
Presentation
  • Tachypnea, paradoxical abdominal movement, recurrent extubation failure.
  • Lower lobe atelectasis.
  • Pleural effusion in postoperative cases.
Diagnosis
  • CXR — hemidiaphragm elevation (limited sensitivity).
  • Real-time diaphragm ultrasound — excursion <4 mm or paradoxical movement is abnormal; thickening fraction adjunctive.
  • Fluoroscopic sniff test.
  • Phrenic nerve conduction; diaphragm EMG.
Management
  • Conservative — nasal CPAP / BiPAP for unilateral palsy if tolerated.
  • Diaphragmatic plication in young children with significant ventilator dependence — typically after 2–4 weeks of failed weaning.
  • Definitive treatment of underlying disease (neostigmine for MG, IVIG for GBS).
Conclusion
  • Acute neuromuscular disease in the PICU is dominated by GBS.
  • MG, acute porphyria, ICU-AW, and diaphragmatic palsy are critical secondary considerations.
  • Four pillars — localize the lesion, vigilance for respiratory and autonomic failure, careful airway management, prompt immunotherapy.

① EAN / PNS Guideline on the Diagnosis and Treatment of Guillain-Barré Syndrome

  • Authoring bodies: European Academy of Neurology (EAN) + Peripheral Nerve Society (PNS) joint task force
  • Source: Journal of the Peripheral Nervous System · 2023 · Van Doorn PA, Van den Berg B, Hughes RAC and colleagues
  • Link: DOI 10.1111/jns.12541 · PNS portal: pnsociety.com · EAN guidelines: ean.org/research/ean-guidelines
  • Key findings:
    • IVIG 2 g/kg over 2–5 days OR plasma exchange 50 mL/kg × 5 sessions as first-line in adults and children.
    • No benefit from combining IVIG + PLEX.
    • Corticosteroids alone or with IVIG not recommended.
    • Treatment-related fluctuation within 8 weeks — repeat course of same agent.
    • Early NCS guides prognosis, especially when AMAN suspected.
    • Recognize Miller-Fisher, Bickerstaff, pharyngo-cervico-brachial as discrete subtypes.
  • ICU impact: Anchors IVIG vs. PLEX selection and dosing; defines TRF management.
  • RCPSC relevance: Foundation reference for GBS management answers.

② WHO Global Polio Eradication Initiative — Acute Flaccid Paralysis Surveillance Standards

  • Authoring body: World Health Organization + Global Polio Eradication Initiative (GPEI)
  • Source: WHO Standard Procedures for Detecting and Reporting AFP · 2018 (current operative version) · annual surveillance bulletins through 2024
  • Link: polioeradication.org · WHO polio: who.int/health-topics/poliomyelitis · WHO AFP standards PDF: surveillance indicators
  • Key findings:
    • Any child <15 y with AFP — mandatory reporting to public health.
    • Two stool samples collected 24–48 h apart, refrigerated, sent to WHO-accredited polio reference lab.
    • Adequate sample = both within 14 days of paralysis onset.
    • Surveillance performance indicators — ≥2 / 100 000 children <15 y AFP detection rate, ≥80 % adequate stool sampling.
  • ICU impact: Public-health reporting is a duty regardless of working diagnosis (GBS / AFM / TM).
  • RCPSC relevance: Stool-sampling rule is a recurring exam factoid.

③ International Consensus Guidance for Management of Myasthenia Gravis (2020 Update)

  • Authoring body: Myasthenia Gravis Foundation of America (MGFA) International Task Force
  • Source: Narayanaswami P, Sanders DB, Wolfe G, Benatar M, Cea G, Evoli A et al. Neurology. 2021;96(3):114–122 · DOI 10.1212/WNL.0000000000011124 · PMID 33144515
  • Link: MGFA: myasthenia.org/Health-Professionals/Treatment-Guidance
  • Key findings:
    • First-line crisis therapy — IVIG 2 g/kg over 2–5 days or PLEX 5 sessions; comparable efficacy.
    • Maintenance — prednisone (anticipate transient worsening) plus steroid-sparing agent (azathioprine, mycophenolate, methotrexate, cyclosporine).
    • Refractory MG — rituximab (especially MuSK-positive), eculizumab (AChR-positive complement-mediated), efgartigimod (FcRn inhibitor).
    • Thymectomy — indicated in thymoma; consider in generalized AChR-positive adolescents.
    • Comprehensive drug-avoidance list with magnesium, aminoglycosides, fluoroquinolones, beta-blockers, etc.
  • ICU impact: Defines crisis rescue and refractory escalation pathway.
  • RCPSC relevance: Drug-avoidance list and graded therapy are core exam content.

④ MGTX — Randomized Trial of Thymectomy in Myasthenia Gravis (Extension)

  • Authoring body: MGTX Study Group
  • Source: Wolfe GI, Kaminski HJ, Aban IB, Minisman G et al. N Engl J Med. 2016;375:511–522 (original) · Extension: Wolfe GI et al. Lancet Neurol. 2019;18:259–268
  • Link: Original DOI: 10.1056/NEJMoa1602489 · PMID 27509100 · Extension DOI: 10.1016/S1474-4422(18)30392-2
  • Key findings:
    • Trans-sternal thymectomy plus prednisone superior to prednisone alone for non-thymomatous AChR-positive MG.
    • Lower quantitative MG score and less prednisone over 3 years.
    • Extension confirms durable benefit at 5 years.
  • ICU impact: Supports adolescent thymectomy consideration in generalized AChR-positive MG.

⑤ ADAPT — Efgartigimod (FcRn inhibitor) Phase 3 in Generalized MG

  • Authoring body: ADAPT Investigators
  • Source: Howard JF Jr, Bril V, Vu T, Karam C et al. Lancet Neurol. 2021;20:526–536
  • Link: DOI 10.1016/S1474-4422(21)00159-9 · PMID 34146511
  • Key findings:
    • Efgartigimod reduces total IgG including pathogenic anti-AChR antibody.
    • Improved MG-ADL response in 67.7 % vs. 29.7 % placebo at week 4.
    • Approved for adults with generalized AChR-positive MG; pediatric extensions in progress.

⑥ REGAIN — Eculizumab in Refractory AChR-Positive MG

  • Authoring body: REGAIN Investigators
  • Source: Howard JF Jr, Utsugisawa K, Benatar M, Murai H et al. Lancet Neurol. 2017;16:976–986
  • Link: DOI 10.1016/S1474-4422(17)30369-1 · PMID 29066163
  • Key findings:
    • Complement C5 inhibition reduces clinical worsening in refractory generalized AChR-positive MG.
    • Requires meningococcal vaccination before initiation.
    • Ravulizumab (longer half-life C5 inhibitor) approved 2022 as alternative.
  • ICU impact: Refractory MG crisis option; meningococcal vaccination required.

⑦ ENVISION — Givosiran (siRNA against ALA-synthase 1) in Acute Hepatic Porphyria

  • Authoring body: ENVISION Investigators
  • Source: Balwani M, Sardh E, Ventura P, Peiró PA et al. N Engl J Med. 2020;382:2289–2301 · open-label extension data through 2024
  • Link: DOI 10.1056/NEJMoa1913147 · PMID 32521132
  • Key findings:
    • Subcutaneous givosiran reduces annual rate of porphyria attacks by ~70 %.
    • Reduces hemin (heme arginate) use and pain severity.
    • Adverse events — injection-site reactions, transaminitis, hyperhomocysteinemia.
    • FDA approved 2019 for adults; pediatric extension expanding 2023–2024.
  • ICU impact: Acute attack management remains hemin + carbohydrate loading + supportive care; givosiran for chronic prevention.

⑧ California Infant Botulism Treatment and Prevention Program — BabyBIG

  • Authoring body: California Department of Public Health · IBTPP
  • Source: Arnon SS, Schechter R, Maslanka SE, Jewell NP, Hatheway CL. N Engl J Med. 2006;354:462–471 · with registry updates 2010–2023
  • Link: Program/24-hour hotline: infantbotulism.org · Pivotal trial: DOI 10.1056/NEJMoa051926 · PMID 16452559
  • Key findings:
    • Single IV BIG-IV dose shortens hospital stay by ~3 weeks and ventilation by ~2 weeks.
    • Greatest benefit when given within ≤3 days of admission.
    • Empiric treatment on clinical suspicion + supportive RNS is standard — do not delay for stool toxin.
  • ICU impact: Drives the empiric dosing strategy for floppy infant with descending paralysis.

⑨ ESPNIC / PALISI Consensus on Pediatric ICU-Acquired Weakness

  • Authoring bodies: European Society of Paediatric and Neonatal Intensive Care (ESPNIC) + Pediatric Acute Lung Injury and Sepsis Investigators (PALISI)
  • Source: Intensive Care Medicine / Pediatric Critical Care Medicine · 2023 consensus statement; adult criteria origin — Stevens RD et al. Crit Care Med. 2009;37(10 Suppl):S299–S308 · DOI 10.1097/CCM.0b013e3181b6e64c
  • Link: ESPNIC: espnic-online.org · PALISI: palisi.org
  • Key findings:
    • Criteria — MRC sum score <48/60 (or pediatric proxy) on two examinations ≥24 h apart, ventilator dependence, electrodiagnostic confirmation when feasible.
    • Prevention bundle — daily sedation interruption, minimize NMBA, glycemic control 6–10 mmol/L, early mobilization from day 1, minimize corticosteroid duration, treat sepsis source, protein-adequate nutrition, MRC sum-score screening.
    • Mobilization feasible on ECMO.
    • No pharmacotherapy has proven benefit.
  • ICU impact: Codifies screening + confirmation + prevention pathway.

⑩ CDC Acute Flaccid Myelitis Surveillance & Clinical Management Review

  • Authoring body: CDC AFM Task Force
  • Source: CDC AFM site + MMWR · 2014–2024 surveillance · Murphy OC, Messacar K, Benson L et al. Lancet. 2021;397:334–346
  • Link: cdc.gov/acute-flaccid-myelitis · Clinical review DOI: 10.1016/S0140-6736(20)32723-9 · PMID 33357469
  • Key findings:
    • Biennial outbreak pattern linked to enterovirus D68.
    • Asymmetric flaccid weakness + cord T2 hyperintensity in gray matter.
    • No proven disease-modifying therapy — supportive + aggressive rehab.
    • Nerve transfer surgery at 6–12 months for residual upper-limb paralysis improves function.
  • ICU impact: Distinguishing AFM from AIDP avoids futile IVIG/PLEX.

⑪ COVID-19–associated GBS — Systematic Reviews

⑫ AAN Guideline on Management of Childhood Transverse Myelitis

5. ICU Decision Engine

IF child <15 y presents with acute flaccid paralysis
THEN
  • Report to public health.
  • Send two stool samples 24–48 h apart for poliovirus PCR (WHO surveillance).
  • Run parallel workup — MRI cord, NCS, antibodies, CSF.
IF ascending symmetric areflexic weakness with autonomic features
THEN
  • Work up GBS — NCS (F-waves first), CSF after 7–10 days if early CSF normal, spinal MRI to exclude TM.
  • Start IVIG 2 g/kg if progressive.
IF FVC <20 mL/kg, MIP > –30, MEP <40, or rapid deterioration
THEN
  • Intubate electively.
  • Avoid succinylcholine.
  • Use rocuronium with reduced dose if MG; sugammadex available.
IF bulbar weakness with secretion failure
THEN
  • Intubate early — do not wait for hypoxia / hypercapnia.
IF dysautonomia (BP swings, arrhythmias, ileus) in GBS
THEN
  • Use short-acting agents only when end-organ symptoms develop.
  • Avoid long-acting antihypertensives.
  • Pre-medicate suctioning with atropine if bradycardia history.
  • Pacing standby in severe dysautonomia.
IF myasthenic crisis (respiratory failure + bulbar weakness)
THEN
  • Intubate electively.
  • IVIG 2 g/kg or PLEX 50 mL/kg × 5.
  • Hold pyridostigmine during ventilation.
  • Start steroids in-hospital with monitoring for transient worsening.
IF MG patient deteriorating after starting an antibiotic
THEN
  • Stop suspect agent (macrolides, aminoglycosides, fluoroquinolones).
  • Ventilatory support.
  • Consider IVIG / PLEX rescue.
IF AFM suspected (asymmetric flaccid, cord T2 hyperintensity)
THEN
  • Supportive care.
  • Consider IVIG (weak evidence).
  • Plan rehabilitation.
  • Nerve-transfer evaluation at 6–12 months if residual.
IF floppy infant with descending paralysis, constipation, ptosis
THEN
  • Treat as infant botulism — BabyBIG immediately.
  • Avoid aminoglycosides.
  • Supportive ventilation.
IF adolescent with abdominal pain, dysautonomia, motor neuropathy, after barbiturate / OCP exposure
THEN
  • Spot urine PBG and ALA.
  • IV dextrose 10 % bridge.
  • Heme arginate 3 mg/kg/day × 4 days.
  • Avoid unsafe drugs.
IF failure to wean after sepsis on day 14
THEN
  • Diagnose ICU-AW — MRC sum score, EDX (CMAP/SNAP/DMS).
  • Tighten prevention bundle.
  • Plan slow wean with PT/OT.
IF failure to extubate after cardiac surgery with elevated hemidiaphragm
THEN
  • Diaphragmatic ultrasound.
  • Phrenic NCS.
  • Consider plication if persistent >2–4 weeks.

6. SAQ Bank — 40 Questions

SAQ 1. Define acute flaccid paralysis and list main causes by compartment.
Answer.
  • Rapid-onset weakness frequently involving respiratory and bulbar muscles.
  • AHC — polio, AFM, WNV, JE.
  • Cord — TM, trauma.
  • Nerve — GBS variants, toxic, diphtheric, tick paralysis.
  • NMJ — botulism, MG, organophosphate, envenomation.
  • Muscle — acute myositis, periodic paralysis, rhabdomyolysis.
SAQ 2. What public-health step is mandatory for any child <15 y with AFP?
Answer.
  • Report to public health under WHO AFP surveillance.
  • Collect two stool samples 24–48 h apart for poliovirus PCR.
  • Required even if GBS or AFM is the working diagnosis.
SAQ 3. List the 20/30/40 rule for intubation.
Answer.
  • FVC <20 mL/kg.
  • MIP less negative than –30 cmH₂O.
  • MEP <40 cmH₂O.
  • Any breached, with rapid decline or bulbar dysfunction → intubate electively.
SAQ 4. Why are hypoxia and hypercapnia late signs in NMD respiratory failure?
Answer.
  • Tidal volume falls before respiratory rate compensation fails.
  • Minute ventilation drops substantially before PaCO₂ rises and SpO₂ falls.
  • Mechanics (FVC, MIP, MEP) and clinical signs (single-breath count, paradoxical breathing) detect deterioration earlier.
SAQ 5. Why is succinylcholine contraindicated in chronic NMD or GBS?
Answer.
  • Upregulated extrajunctional AChRs in denervated muscle.
  • Depolarization releases massive K⁺ → life-threatening hyperkalemia and arrhythmia.
  • Use rocuronium (reduced dose in MG) with sugammadex available.
SAQ 6. Pediatric annual incidence of GBS <9 y.
Answer.
  • ~0.62 per 100 000 per year.
SAQ 7. List six GBS subtypes with one distinguishing feature each.
Answer.
  • AIDP — symmetric demyelinating ascending paralysis.
  • AMAN — pure motor axonal; anti-GM1; post-Campylobacter.
  • AMSAN — severe sensorimotor axonal.
  • Miller-Fisher — ophthalmoplegia + ataxia + areflexia; anti-GQ1b.
  • Bickerstaff brainstem encephalitis — MFS + altered consciousness.
  • Pharyngo-cervico-brachial — bulbar + arm weakness, legs spared.
SAQ 8. CSF profile in GBS and its temporal pitfall.
Answer.
  • Cytoalbuminologic dissociation — elevated protein with cells <10/μL.
  • Up to 50 % CSF normal in week 1.
  • Repeat after 7–10 days if early sample normal but clinical suspicion remains.
SAQ 9. Earliest electrodiagnostic abnormality in AIDP and why.
Answer.
  • Absent or prolonged F-waves.
  • Demyelination affects proximal nerve roots first — where F-waves are generated.
  • Distal latency and CV slow only later.
SAQ 10. Indications for PICU admission in pediatric GBS.
Answer.
  • Rapidly progressive weakness.
  • FVC trending <20 mL/kg.
  • Bulbar weakness with secretion failure.
  • Autonomic instability.
  • Inability to lift head or ambulate within 24 h.
  • Pneumonia / aspiration.
SAQ 11. Compare IVIG and PLEX — dose, indications, contraindications.
Answer.
  • IVIG 2 g/kg over 2–5 days — preferred in children, easier access; check IgA.
  • PLEX 50 mL/kg × 5 sessions — requires large vascular access; contraindicated in hemodynamic instability and severe coagulopathy.
  • Combining offers no advantage.
SAQ 12. Why are corticosteroids not recommended in GBS?
Answer.
  • RCT evidence shows no benefit and possible delayed recovery.
  • Steroids reserved for CIDP, the chronic counterpart.
SAQ 13. What is treatment-related fluctuation in GBS?
Answer.
  • Worsening within 8 weeks of initial improvement after IVIG/PLEX.
  • Occurs in ~10 %.
  • Treat with a repeat course of the same agent.
  • Not failure or CIDP.
SAQ 14. Autonomic management priorities in GBS.
Answer.
  • Continuous ECG and BP monitoring.
  • Short-acting antihypertensives only for end-organ symptoms.
  • Atropine pre-suction in bradycardia history.
  • Aggressive ileus prophylaxis.
  • Address urinary retention.
  • Thromboprophylaxis.
SAQ 15. Differentiate AIDP from acute transverse myelitis.
Answer.
  • AIDP — areflexic ascending, no sensory level, autonomic features, sphincters spared early.
  • TM — sensory level on torso, sphincter involvement, hyperreflexia below the level after spinal shock, MRI cord T2 hyperintensity.
SAQ 16. Define myasthenic crisis and list triggers.
Answer.
  • Acute respiratory failure or severe bulbar dysfunction in MG requiring intubation / NIV.
  • Triggers — infection, surgery, drugs (aminoglycosides, macrolides, fluoroquinolones, beta-blockers, magnesium, phenytoin, D-penicillamine, hydroxychloroquine, NMBAs, contrast), pregnancy, missed doses, stress.
SAQ 17. Differentiate myasthenic from cholinergic crisis.
Answer.
  • Both — severe weakness + respiratory failure.
  • Cholinergic crisis — adds SLUDGE (salivation, lacrimation, urination, diarrhea, GI cramping, emesis, bradycardia, miosis).
  • Edrophonium improves myasthenic, worsens cholinergic.
  • Practical — stop pyridostigmine, support ventilation, distinguish by course.
SAQ 18. Five drug classes precipitating MG crisis.
Answer.
  • Aminoglycosides.
  • Macrolides.
  • Fluoroquinolones.
  • Beta-blockers.
  • Magnesium sulfate.
  • Also — phenytoin, D-penicillamine, hydroxychloroquine, lithium, contrast, NMBAs, steroids (transient initial worsening).
SAQ 19. Role of pyridostigmine during MG crisis on the ventilator.
Answer.
  • Hold or reduce — increases pharyngeal secretions and complicates care.
  • Restart graded as weaning is contemplated.
SAQ 20. Describe neonatal transient MG.
Answer.
  • Transplacental maternal AChR antibody.
  • 10–20 % of infants of MG mothers symptomatic.
  • Onset hours–days after birth.
  • Resolves spontaneously 2–8 weeks.
  • Supportive — NG feeds, NIV / ventilator if needed, short-course pyridostigmine if symptomatic.
  • Distinct from congenital MG (genetic, persistent).
SAQ 21. MuSK-Ab MG features.
Answer.
  • Predominantly bulbar and respiratory weakness with limb sparing.
  • Frequent facial wasting.
  • Poor response to anticholinesterases; some worsen.
  • Better response to PLEX and rituximab.
  • RNS limb often normal; facial muscle RNS abnormal.
SAQ 22. Thymectomy indications in pediatric MG.
Answer.
  • Thymoma — absolute.
  • Generalized AChR-positive non-thymomatous MG in adolescents — selectively (MGTX trial extension).
  • Usually deferred in pre-pubertal children.
  • MuSK-MG and ocular MG generally not thymectomized.
SAQ 23. Describe the ice-pack test.
Answer.
  • Ice on ptotic lid for 2 minutes.
  • Improvement >2 mm in ptosis supports MG.
  • Cold transiently improves NMJ transmission.
  • Specificity 80–90 %, easy and safe.
SAQ 24. Antibodies tested in MG and positive rates.
Answer.
  • AChR-Ab — ~85 % of generalized JMG, ~50 % of ocular MG.
  • MuSK-Ab — ~40 % of AChR-negative MG; more bulbar.
  • Anti-LRP4 — emerging in seronegative.
SAQ 25. Newer biologics for refractory MG.
Answer.
  • Eculizumab and ravulizumab — C5 complement inhibitors.
  • Efgartigimod, rozanolixizumab — FcRn inhibitors reducing IgG (including pathogenic antibody).
  • Rituximab — established second-line, especially in MuSK-MG.
SAQ 26. Classic clinical features of acute porphyria.
Answer.
  • Severe abdominal pain, often without peritonism.
  • Altered mental status.
  • Autonomic dysfunction — tachycardia, hypertension.
  • Motor neuropathy → bulbar / respiratory failure.
  • Hyponatremia (SIADH).
  • Psychiatric — anxiety, hallucinations, depression.
  • Port-wine urine on standing.
SAQ 27. Diagnostic approach to suspected acute porphyria.
Answer.
  • Spot urine PBG and ALA — light-protected.
  • Erythrocyte PBG deaminase (low in AIP; normal does not exclude).
  • Confirmatory genetic testing — HMBS, CPOX, PPOX, ALAD.
SAQ 28. Five drugs unsafe in porphyria.
Answer.
  • Barbiturates.
  • Sulfonamides.
  • Phenytoin, carbamazepine, valproate.
  • OCPs, danazol.
  • Rifampin, ergots, griseofulvin.
  • Cross-check via American Porphyria Foundation database.
SAQ 29. Acute porphyria treatment.
Answer.
  • Stop precipitants, treat infection, hydrate.
  • High-carbohydrate intake suppresses ALA-synthase; IV dextrose 10 % if oral not possible.
  • Heme arginate 3 mg/kg/day × 4 days — first-line severe.
  • Givosiran for recurrent attacks.
  • Pain — morphine; nausea — ondansetron; seizures — lorazepam.
  • Treat hyponatremia cautiously.
SAQ 30. Define ICU-acquired weakness.
Answer.
  • Generalized symmetric flaccid weakness after onset of critical illness, no other identifiable cause.
  • Subtypes — CIP, CIM, CINM, prolonged NMB.
  • Clinical + electrodiagnostic criteria.
SAQ 31. Five risk factors for ICU-AW.
Answer.
  • Sepsis / SIRS.
  • Multi-organ failure.
  • Hyperglycemia.
  • Corticosteroid exposure.
  • NMBA exposure.
  • Prolonged ventilation and immobilization.
  • Severity-of-illness.
SAQ 32. ICU-AW prevention bundle.
Answer.
  • Daily sedation interruption.
  • Minimize NMBA.
  • Glycemia 6–10 mmol/L.
  • Early mobilization.
  • Minimize corticosteroid duration.
  • Aggressive sepsis source control.
  • Protein-adequate nutrition.
  • Routine MRC sum-score screening.
SAQ 33. Differentiate CIP from CIM electrodiagnostically.
Answer.
  • CIP — reduced CMAP and SNAP, preserved velocity (sensorimotor axonal).
  • CIM — reduced CMAP, normal SNAP, low neCMAP/dmCMAP ratio <0.5.
  • EMG in CIM — short-duration MUPs ± fibrillations.
SAQ 34. Causes and workup of pediatric diaphragmatic palsy.
Answer.
  • Causes — postsurgical phrenic injury, birth trauma (with Erb's), tumors, eventration, SMA, GBS, MG, congenital myopathy, central hypoventilation.
  • Workup — CXR (limited), real-time diaphragm ultrasound, fluoroscopic sniff test, phrenic NCS, diaphragm EMG.
SAQ 35. When should diaphragmatic plication be considered?
Answer.
  • Young children (especially <2 y) with confirmed phrenic palsy after cardiac surgery.
  • Persistent ventilator dependence beyond 2–4 weeks of failed weaning.
  • Reduces paradox and facilitates extubation.
  • Older children often tolerate unilateral palsy without surgery.
SAQ 36. Three antecedent infections most commonly preceding pediatric GBS.
Answer.
  • Campylobacter jejuni — especially AMAN.
  • Respiratory viruses — rhinovirus, influenza.
  • EBV and CMV.
  • Also — mycoplasma, Zika, SARS-CoV-2, hepatitis E.
SAQ 37. Describe Brighton diagnostic criteria for GBS.
Answer.
  • Tiered certainty levels.
  • Level 1 — bilateral flaccid weakness, decreased / absent reflexes, monophasic course (nadir 12 h–28 days), CSF protein >0.45 g/L with cells <50/μL, EDX evidence.
  • Lower levels relax electrodiagnostic and CSF requirements.
SAQ 38. Five long-term sequelae of severe pediatric GBS.
Answer.
  • Persistent distal weakness or sensory deficit.
  • Chronic fatigue.
  • Neuropathic pain.
  • Motor incoordination.
  • Residual respiratory limitations / sleep-disordered breathing.
  • Anxiety / depression.
  • School absenteeism with delayed academic progress.
SAQ 39. Role of MRI spine in pediatric AFP.
Answer.
  • Excludes transverse myelitis (cord T2 hyperintensity).
  • Excludes AFM (gray-matter cord lesions).
  • In GBS — post-gadolinium enhancement of cauda equina / ventral nerve roots supports diagnosis.
  • MRI brain if Bickerstaff or central involvement suspected.
SAQ 40. Post-PICU rehabilitation priorities after severe acute NMD.
Answer.
  • Multidisciplinary rehab — PT, OT, speech therapy.
  • Pulmonary rehab — assisted cough, NIV bridges.
  • Psychosocial support.
  • School re-integration.
  • Pain management.
  • Long-term follow-up for recurrence (MG, porphyria, CIDP).

10-year-old, 10 days post-bloody diarrhea, rapidly progressive flaccid quadriparesis over 3 days. NCS — preserved velocity, low CMAP, normal SNAPs. Anti-GM1 positive.
Q. Subtype, prognosis, treatment.
Answer.
  • AMAN.
  • Variable prognosis — some rapid reversible conduction failure; some prolonged axonal recovery.
  • IVIG 2 g/kg.

Case 3 — Miller-Fisher syndrome.
8-year-old, 2 weeks post-URI, bilateral ophthalmoplegia, gait ataxia, areflexia over 5 days. Limb strength preserved.
Q. Diagnosis, antibody, treatment.
Answer.
  • Miller-Fisher syndrome (GBS variant).

  • Supportive ± IVIG.
  • Watch for evolution to Bickerstaff brainstem encephalitis.
Case 4 — Bickerstaff brainstem encephalitis.
14-year-old develops ophthalmoplegia, ataxia, areflexia, plus drowsiness and upgoing toes. MRI brainstem hyperintensity.
Q. Diagnosis, management.
Answer.
  • Bickerstaff brainstem encephalitis — central + peripheral autoimmune.
  • Anti-GQ1b.
  • IVIG 2 g/kg ± steroids in CNS-overlap form.
  • Monitor for respiratory failure.
Case 5 — Pharyngo-cervico-brachial variant.
16-year-old develops acute bulbar weakness, neck flexion weakness, arm weakness over 3 days. Legs relatively spared.
Q. Diagnosis and key risk.
Answer.
  • Pharyngo-cervico-brachial GBS variant.
  • Anti-GT1a / GQ1b.
  • Risk — bulbar / airway compromise without much limb weakness.
  • Intubate proactively for secretion management.
  • IVIG.
Case 6 — Acute transverse myelitis.
9-year-old develops paraparesis with sensory loss at T6, urinary retention, hyperreflexia below the level after spinal shock. MRI — T2 cord hyperintensity over 3 segments.
Q. Differentiate from GBS; management.
Answer.
  • Transverse myelitis — sensory level, sphincter involvement, MRI cord lesion.
  • IV methylprednisolone 30 mg/kg/day × 5; PLEX if poor response.
  • Investigate AQP4 (NMOSD) and MOG antibodies, MS.
Case 7 — Acute Flaccid Myelitis.
4-year-old presents in September with right arm flaccid paralysis after a febrile URI. MRI — cord gray matter T2 hyperintensity. SNAPs preserved, CMAP low.
Q. Diagnosis and management.
Answer.
  • AFM — likely enterovirus D68 outbreak season.
  • Send NP / stool / CSF for enterovirus PCR.
  • Supportive care; IVIG sometimes given (weak evidence).
  • Aggressive rehab; nerve transfer surgery at 6–12 months for residual.
  • Report to public health.
Case 8 — Suxamethonium hyperkalemia.
11-year-old with subacute GBS gets RSI with succinylcholine → VF; K⁺ 8 mmol/L.
Q. Mechanism and lesson.
Answer.
  • Upregulated extrajunctional AChRs in denervated muscle → massive K⁺ release.
  • Treat — calcium gluconate, insulin/dextrose, salbutamol, bicarbonate if acidotic, defibrillation.
  • Lesson — never use succinylcholine in chronic NMD, GBS, immobilization >24 h, burns, denervation.
  • Use rocuronium + sugammadex.
Case 9 — GBS dysautonomia with cardiac arrest.
13-year-old on day 7 of GBS — bradycardia during ETT suctioning, progressing to asystole; resuscitated.
Q. Mechanism and prevention.
Answer.
  • Vagal hyperresponsiveness during airway manipulation in autonomic dysfunction.
  • Pre-treat with atropine 20 mcg/kg IV.
  • Pre-oxygenate; gentle short suctioning.
  • Maintain transcutaneous or transvenous pacing standby in severe dysautonomia.
Case 10 — GBS with IgA deficiency.
7-year-old with known IgA deficiency presents with classic AIDP requiring intubation.
Q. First-line therapy and why.
Answer.
  • PLEX preferred over IVIG — anaphylaxis risk from anti-IgA antibodies.
  • If IVIG used — IgA-depleted preparation, premedicate, epinephrine ready.
Case 11 — Treatment-related fluctuation.
12-year-old initially improved after IVIG for AIDP, then 4 weeks later worsens — weakness returns, FVC falls.
Q. Diagnosis and management.
Answer.
  • Treatment-related fluctuation — recurrence within 8 weeks.
  • Repeat IVIG 2 g/kg.
  • If multiple relapses or progression beyond 8 weeks, reconsider A-CIDP.
Case 12 — Acute-onset CIDP masquerading as GBS.
10-year-old presents like GBS but at week 12 continues to deteriorate after two IVIG courses.
Q. Likely revised diagnosis and treatment.
Answer.
  • Acute-onset CIDP.
  • Start corticosteroids (prednisone).
  • IVIG maintenance every 3–6 weeks.
  • Plan long-term immunosuppression.
Case 13 — Juvenile MG in crisis.
15-year-old known generalized MG presents with progressive dyspnea, ptosis, dysphagia, single-breath count 8. AChR positive. Started azithromycin yesterday for pneumonia.
Q. Manage.
Answer.
  • Stop azithromycin (macrolide).
  • Elective intubation with rocuronium at 50 % dose; sugammadex available.
  • Hold pyridostigmine on ventilator.
  • IVIG 2 g/kg over 5 days or PLEX.
  • Treat pneumonia with macrolide-free regimen (e.g., ceftriaxone).
  • Plan steroid maintenance with monitoring for transient worsening.
Case 14 — Cholinergic crisis.
13-year-old MG patient self-increased pyridostigmine and presents with weakness, miosis, salivation, sweating, diarrhea, fasciculations.
Q. Diagnosis and management.
Answer.
  • Cholinergic crisis.
  • Stop pyridostigmine.
  • Supportive ventilation — rocuronium / sugammadex; avoid succinylcholine.
  • Low-dose atropine for muscarinic symptoms.
  • Re-titrate pyridostigmine from low dose once recovered.
Case 15 — Magnesium contraindication.
17-year-old MG patient receives magnesium sulfate for severe asthma exacerbation and develops respiratory failure.
Q. Mechanism and action.
Answer.
  • Magnesium blocks presynaptic ACh release and worsens NMJ transmission.
  • Stop magnesium; support ventilation; calcium gluconate may partially counteract.
  • IVIG or PLEX if persistent weakness.
Case 16 — Neonatal transient MG.
Term infant born to a mother with AChR-positive MG. At 24 h — poor feeding, weak cry, ptosis, shallow respirations.
Q. Diagnosis and treatment.
Answer.
  • Neonatal transient MG.
  • Supportive — NG feeds, NIV or intubation if needed, short-course pyridostigmine 0.5 mg/kg PO.
  • Resolves spontaneously in 2–8 weeks.
  • Cord-blood antibody titer correlates with severity.
Case 17 — CMS paradoxical response.
Infant with persistent weakness improves on pyridostigmine, then worsens after dose increase. AChR antibody negative.
Q. Likely CMS subtype, management.
Answer.
  • Slow-channel CMS or DOK7 / COLQ CMS.
  • Anticholinesterases worsen these subtypes.
  • Switch — fluoxetine / quinidine (slow channel) or salbutamol / ephedrine (DOK7).
  • Genetic panel confirms subtype.
Case 18 — MuSK-Ab MG.
16-year-old AChR-negative, severe bulbar weakness, facial wasting, mild limb involvement. RNS limb normal; SFEMG facial positive.
Q. Subtype and therapy.
Answer.
  • MuSK-Ab MG.
  • Limited response to pyridostigmine (sometimes worsens).
  • PLEX or rituximab preferred.
  • Steroid maintenance.
Case 19 — Infant botulism.
4-month-old with 5 days of constipation, weak cry, hypotonia, ptosis, sluggish pupils, descending paralysis. Bedside RNS — low CMAP with 80 % increment at 50 Hz.
Q. Treatment and contraindication.
Answer.
  • Infant botulism.
  • BabyBIG immediately — do not wait for stool toxin.
  • Avoid aminoglycosides.
  • Supportive ventilation 2–4 weeks; swallow recovery gates extubation.
Case 20 — Foodborne adolescent botulism.
17-year-old after eating home-canned vegetables develops diplopia, dysphagia, descending paralysis.
Q. Treatment differs from infant.
Answer.
  • Equine heptavalent botulism antitoxin (BAT) — not BabyBIG.
  • Skin test before infusion.
  • Supportive ICU; ileus management.
  • Report to public health; trace canning source.
Case 21 — Tick paralysis.
5-year-old returning from a camping trip presents with rapidly ascending paralysis and ataxia over 24 h. CSF normal.
Q. Crucial step.
Answer.
  • Full skin inspection for an attached tick.
  • Removal is diagnostic and curative — improvement within hours.
  • Mimics GBS but reverses dramatically.
Case 22 — Acute intermittent porphyria.
17-year-old on OCP and recent phenobarbital for seizures presents with severe diffuse abdominal pain, tachycardia, hypertension, paresthesia, port-wine urine, progressive weakness.
Q. Diagnosis and immediate management.
Answer.
  • Acute intermittent porphyria.
  • Stop OCP and phenobarbital.
  • Urine PBG and ALA (light-protected).
  • IV dextrose 10 % bridge.
  • Heme arginate 3 mg/kg/day × 4 days.
  • Avoid unsafe drugs.
  • Treat hyponatremia cautiously.
  • Morphine for pain, ondansetron, lorazepam if seizures.
Case 23 — Variegate porphyria with skin signs.
15-year-old presents with photosensitive skin blistering and acute abdominal pain after sulfa antibiotic.
Q. Differentiating feature and action.
Answer.
  • Variegate porphyria — both skin photosensitivity and acute attacks.
  • Stop sulfa drug.
  • Manage acute attack as above.
  • Long-term sun protection.
  • Genetic counselling for PPOX mutation.
Case 24 — Diphtheric neuropathy.
8-year-old recently arrived from a region with low vaccination coverage develops palatal paralysis 3 weeks after a pharyngeal pseudomembrane illness, progressing to peripheral neuropathy at week 6.
Q. Diagnosis and treatment.
Answer.
  • Diphtheric polyneuropathy — toxin demyelinates cranial nerves first then peripheral.
  • Diphtheria antitoxin ASAP.
  • Erythromycin or penicillin to eradicate.
  • Supportive ICU.
  • Vaccinate household contacts.
Case 25 — Lead intoxication neuropathy.
12-year-old develops a painful sensorimotor neuropathy with abdominal pain and basophilic stippling on smear. Lives near a smelter.
Q. Diagnosis and management.
Answer.
  • Lead intoxication.
  • Blood lead level.
  • Chelation — succimer (DMSA) for moderate; calcium-EDTA + dimercaprol for severe with encephalopathy.
  • Source identification / remediation.
  • Iron-deficiency check.
Case 26 — Organophosphate poisoning.
9-year-old from a farm presents with miosis, salivation, bronchorrhea, fasciculations, respiratory failure 30 min after pesticide exposure.
Q. Management.
Answer.
  • Decontaminate.
  • Atropine titrated to dry secretions.
  • Pralidoxime.
  • Intubate — avoid succinylcholine (markedly prolonged).
  • Benzodiazepines for seizures.
  • Monitor for intermediate syndrome 24–96 h later.
Case 27 — Snake envenomation.
6-year-old bitten by an elapid snake develops bilateral ptosis, ophthalmoplegia, descending paralysis 2 h after the bite.
Q. Management.
Answer.
  • Region-specific antivenom ASAP.
  • Supportive ventilation.
  • Some postsynaptic toxins partially reverse with anticholinesterases.
  • Tetanus prophylaxis.
  • Manage coagulopathy / nephrotoxicity.
  • Avoid succinylcholine.
Case 28 — Acute myositis with rhabdomyolysis.
7-year-old with influenza B develops severe bilateral calf pain, refuses to walk. CK 80 000 U/L, dark urine.
Q. Diagnosis and management.
Answer.
  • Benign acute childhood myositis.
  • Hydration to prevent AKI; monitor K⁺, Ca²⁺, phosphate.
  • Resolves spontaneously in 1–2 weeks.
  • Investigate atypical / persistent cases (JDM, immune necrotizing myopathy).
Case 29 — Juvenile dermatomyositis.
10-year-old with weeks of fatigue, heliotrope rash, Gottron papules, proximal weakness, dysphagia, respiratory distress.
Q. Diagnosis and management.
Answer.
  • Juvenile dermatomyositis.
  • MRI muscle T2 hyperintensity; CK often moderate.
  • IV methylprednisolone, methotrexate or IVIG.
  • Rule out interstitial lung disease (HRCT, anti-MDA5).
  • PICU for severe respiratory or aspiration risk.
Case 30 — Periodic paralysis.
14-year-old wakes with quadriparesis after heavy carbohydrate dinner. K⁺ 2.4 mmol/L. Family history.
Q. Diagnosis, treatment, long-term.
Answer.
  • Hypokalemic periodic paralysis.
  • Oral K⁺ 0.5–1 mEq/kg; cautious IV only if cardiac arrhythmia; watch rebound hyperkalemia.
  • Long-term — avoid heavy carbohydrates, acetazolamide or dichlorphenamide prophylaxis.
Case 31 — Thyroid storm myopathy.
15-year-old with newly diagnosed Graves has thyroid storm and severe proximal weakness.
Q. Mechanism and management.
Answer.
  • Thyrotoxic myopathy.
  • Treat thyroid storm — PTU, beta-blocker (propranolol acceptable), iodine, steroids.
  • Myopathy resolves with euthyroid restoration.
Case 32 — Critical illness polyneuropathy.
9-year-old, 21 days post-meningococcal sepsis, persistent flaccid quadriparesis. EDX — reduced CMAP and SNAP, preserved velocity.
Q. Diagnosis, bundle, prognosis.
Answer.
  • CIP.
  • Tighten prevention bundle — sedation interruption, glycemia, mobilization, minimize NMBA/steroids.
  • No pharmacotherapy.
  • Children recover better than adults; partial recovery over months.
Case 33 — Critical illness myopathy.
11-year-old asthma exacerbation, paralyzed 5 days with rocuronium, high-dose methylprednisolone. Fails to wean. Proximal weakness, normal reflexes, CK 4 000.
Q. Diagnosis and EDX.
Answer.
  • Acute necrotizing CIM (NMBA + steroid).
  • EDX — low CMAP, normal SNAP, EMG myopathic with fibrillations, low neCMAP/dmCMAP.
  • Stop NMBA, taper steroids, mobilize.
  • Recovery slow but generally favorable in children.
Case 34 — Prolonged NMB.
5-year-old in renal failure received vecuronium infusion 48 h; fails to wake. TOF 0/4.
Q. Mechanism and action.
Answer.
  • Prolonged NMB due to renal/hepatic accumulation.
  • Co-factors — hypothermia, acidosis, steroid co-administration, certain antibiotics.
  • Wait for clearance, supportive ventilation, monitor TOF.
  • Sugammadex 16 mg/kg can reverse rocuronium-class NMB; cautious with vecuronium.
Case 35 — Phrenic nerve palsy post Norwood.
2-month-old post-Norwood fails extubation 3 times. Right hemidiaphragm elevated. Sniff test paradoxical.
Q. Diagnosis and management.
Answer.
  • Right phrenic nerve injury.
  • Ultrasound and phrenic NCS confirm.
  • Trial extubation with NIV; if recurrent failure beyond 2–4 weeks, diaphragmatic plication.
Case 36 — Bilateral diaphragmatic palsy.
3-month-old presents with tachypnea, paradoxical breathing, both hemidiaphragms elevated. Family history of consanguinity. IUGR at birth.
Q. Differential and key test.
Answer.
  • SMARD1 (IGHMBP2).
  • Differentials — severe SMA, congenital myopathy, central hypoventilation (PHOX2B).
  • Genetic panel for IGHMBP2 / SMN1 / PHOX2B.
  • Long-term ventilatory support.
Case 37 — Erb's palsy with phrenic injury.
Term neonate after shoulder dystocia has right arm flaccid in waiter's-tip position and respiratory distress. Right hemidiaphragm elevated.
Q. Diagnosis and management.
Answer.
  • Brachial plexus injury (C5–C6 / Erb's palsy) with phrenic injury (C3–C5).
  • NIV / mechanical ventilation as needed; trial weaning.
  • Many improve over weeks; plication if persistent.
  • Brachial plexus rehab and surgery if no improvement at 3 months.
Case 38 — Post-COVID GBS.
12-year-old, 14 days post-COVID-19, develops areflexic ascending paralysis. NCS demyelinating features.
Q. Diagnosis and management.
Answer.
  • Post-COVID AIDP.
  • Treat as standard GBS — IVIG 2 g/kg, supportive ICU.
  • Outcomes similar to other parainfectious GBS.
Case 39 — Recurrent GBS / CIDP.
13-year-old has third episode of GBS-like illness in 18 months, each responsive to IVIG but recurring on weaning.
Q. Revised diagnosis and management.
Answer.
  • CIDP.
  • Maintenance IVIG every 3–6 weeks ± corticosteroid.
  • Consider rituximab or SC immunoglobulin.
  • Long-term neurology follow-up.
Case 40 — Locked-in syndrome mimicking peripheral paralysis.
15-year-old with sudden quadriplegia, anarthria, but vertical eye movements preserved. MRI — basilar artery thrombosis with pontine infarct.
Q. Critical bedside clue and management.
Answer.
  • Locked-in syndrome — preserved vertical gaze and consciousness distinguish from peripheral paralysis.
  • Time-sensitive thrombolysis / endovascular thrombectomy if within window.
  • Long-term tracheostomy and assistive communication.

③ Critical ICU thresholds

  • FVC <20 mL/kg → intubate.
  • MIP > –30 cmH₂O → intubate.
  • MEP <40 cmH₂O → cough failure.
  • Single-breath count <15.
  • IVIG 2 g/kg over 5 days.
  • PLEX 50 mL/kg × 5.
  • Heme arginate 3 mg/kg/day × 4.
  • MRC sum-score <48/60 supports ICU-AW.

④ Red flags

  • Succinylcholine in chronic NMD / GBS / immobility >24 h → hyperkalemia.
  • Aminoglycoside / macrolide / magnesium in MG or botulism → respiratory failure.
  • Barbiturate / sulfa / OCP in known porphyria → attack.
  • Asymmetric AFP → consider AFM / polio; report to public health.
  • Pupils fixed and dilated in a floppy infant → botulism, not GBS.
  • Drug-induced cholinergic crisis mimics myasthenic crisis — stop pyridostigmine.

⑤ Exam pearls

🔥🔥🔥 The 20/30/40 rule and elective (not emergent) intubation define expert NMD airway management.
🔥🔥🔥 IVIG 2 g/kg over 5 days = first-line pediatric GBS; PLEX equivalent.
🔥🔥🔥 Steroids hurt — not help — GBS.
🔥🔥 Treatment-related fluctuation ≠ failure. Repeat IVIG/PLEX.
🔥🔥 Hold pyridostigmine during MG crisis on the ventilator.
🔥🔥 Magnesium can kill in MG.
🔥 Phrenic palsy plication after 2–4 weeks of failed weaning in young children.
