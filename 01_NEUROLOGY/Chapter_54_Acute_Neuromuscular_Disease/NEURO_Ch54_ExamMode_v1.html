Mock RCPSC Block A — 10 SAQs (timed)

⏱ 30 min total · 3 min per SAQ
SAQ A-1
List the "20/30/40 rule" for intubation in neuromuscular respiratory failure and explain why hypoxia and hypercapnia are late signs.
Show Answer
  • FVC <20 mL/kg.
  • MIP less negative than –30 cmH₂O.
  • MEP <40 cmH₂O.
  • Tidal volume falls before respiratory rate fully compensates.
  • Minute ventilation drops substantially before PaCO₂ rises and SpO₂ falls.
  • Mechanics and clinical signs (single-breath count, paradoxical breathing) detect deterioration earlier than gases.
SAQ A-2
Why is succinylcholine contraindicated in chronic NMD or GBS?
Show Answer
  • Upregulated extrajunctional acetylcholine receptors in denervated / disused muscle.
  • Depolarization triggers massive K⁺ release → life-threatening hyperkalemia and arrhythmia.
  • Use rocuronium (reduced dose in MG) with sugammadex reversal.
SAQ A-3
Compare IVIG and PLEX for pediatric GBS — dose, indications, contraindications.
Show Answer
  • IVIG 2 g/kg over 2–5 days (0.4 g/kg/day) — preferred in children; easier line access; check IgA before infusion.
  • PLEX 50 mL/kg/session × 5 sessions — effective but requires large vascular access; contraindicated in hemodynamic instability and severe coagulopathy.
  • Combining offers no benefit.
SAQ A-4
List five drugs that precipitate myasthenic crisis.
Show Answer
  • Aminoglycosides.
  • Macrolides.
  • Fluoroquinolones.
  • Beta-blockers.
  • Magnesium sulfate.
  • Also — phenytoin, D-penicillamine, hydroxychloroquine, lithium, iodinated contrast, non-depolarizing NMBAs.
  • Corticosteroids cause transient initial worsening.
SAQ A-5
Outline the ICU-AW prevention bundle.
Show Answer
  • Daily sedation interruption.
  • Minimize NMBA.
  • Glycemia 6–10 mmol/L.
  • Early mobilization from day 1.
  • Minimize corticosteroid duration.
  • Aggressive sepsis source control.
  • Protein-adequate nutrition.
  • Routine MRC sum-score screening.
SAQ A-6
Differentiate AIDP from AMAN electrodiagnostically and clinically.
Show Answer
  • AIDP — demyelinating; prolonged distal latency; slowed CV; absent F-waves; conduction block; common in West.
  • AMAN — axonal; low CMAP amplitudes with preserved velocity; often post-Campylobacter; anti-GM1/GD1a; common in Asia.
  • Both treated with IVIG or PLEX.
SAQ A-7
Outline acute porphyria treatment.
Show Answer
  • Stop precipitant drugs; treat infection; hydrate.
  • High-carbohydrate intake (≥300 g/day) or IV dextrose 10 % suppresses ALA-synthase.
  • Heme arginate 3 mg/kg/day × 4 days — first-line severe.
  • Opioids for pain; ondansetron; lorazepam if seizures.
  • Watch hyponatremia; avoid rapid sodium correction.
  • Givosiran for recurrent attacks.
SAQ A-8
What public-health step is mandatory for any child <15 y with AFP?
Show Answer
  • Notify public health under WHO AFP surveillance.
  • Collect two stool samples 24–48 h apart for poliovirus PCR.
  • Required even when GBS or AFM is the working diagnosis.
SAQ A-9
Why are corticosteroids not recommended in GBS, and what is the contrast with CIDP?
Show Answer
  • RCTs show no benefit and possible delayed recovery in GBS.
  • In CIDP (chronic counterpart), steroids are first-line maintenance.
  • Differentiation is by clinical course beyond 8 weeks and repeated relapses.
SAQ A-10
Differentiate myasthenic from cholinergic crisis.
Show Answer
  • Both — severe weakness with respiratory failure.
  • Cholinergic crisis adds muscarinic excess (SLUDGE: salivation, lacrimation, urination, diarrhea, GI cramping, emesis, bradycardia, miosis) from anticholinesterase overdose.
  • Practical — stop pyridostigmine, support ventilation, distinguish by clinical course.

Mock RCPSC Block B — 5 Sequential ICU Cases

⏱ 30 min total · 6 min per case
CASE B-1
12-year-old, 2 weeks post-Campylobacter gastroenteritis, ascending weakness over 4 days, areflexia, FVC 18 mL/kg, paradoxical breathing, BP swings 80→160 mmHg. CSF protein 0.7 g/L, 2 cells/μL.
Q. Immediate management.
Show Answer
  • Elective intubation — FVC <20 mL/kg and paradoxical breathing.
  • Rocuronium + sugammadex available; avoid succinylcholine.
  • IVIG 2 g/kg over 5 days.
  • Continuous ECG / BP; short-acting agents only for symptomatic hypertension; atropine pre-suctioning.
  • PT / OT from day 1, contracture prevention, thromboprophylaxis.
CASE B-2
15-year-old with known generalized MG presents with dyspnea, ptosis, dysphagia, single-breath count 8. AChR positive. Started azithromycin yesterday for pneumonia.
Q. Manage.
Show Answer
  • Stop azithromycin (macrolide).
  • Elective intubation with reduced-dose rocuronium; sugammadex available.
  • Hold pyridostigmine on ventilator.
  • IVIG 2 g/kg over 5 days or PLEX 50 mL/kg × 5.
  • Treat pneumonia with macrolide-free regimen (e.g., ceftriaxone).
  • Start steroid maintenance in-hospital with monitoring for transient worsening.
CASE B-3
4-month-old with 5 days of constipation, weak cry, hypotonia, ptosis, sluggish pupils, descending paralysis. Bedside RNS: low CMAP with 80 % increment at 50 Hz.
Q. Treatment and drugs to avoid.
Show Answer
  • Infant botulism.
  • BabyBIG immediately — do not wait for stool toxin.
  • Avoid aminoglycosides (worsen presynaptic block).
  • Supportive ventilation 2–4 weeks; swallow recovery gates extubation.
CASE B-4
17-year-old female on OCP and recent phenobarbital presents with severe diffuse abdominal pain, tachycardia, hypertension, paresthesia, port-wine urine, and progressive weakness.
Q. Diagnosis and immediate management.
Show Answer
  • Acute intermittent porphyria.
  • Stop OCP and phenobarbital.
  • Spot urine PBG and ALA (light-protected).
  • IV dextrose 10 % bridge.
  • Heme arginate 3 mg/kg/day × 4 days.
  • Avoid unsafe drugs.
  • Treat hyponatremia cautiously.
  • Pain control with morphine; lorazepam if seizures.
CASE B-5
2-month-old post-Norwood fails extubation 3 times. Right hemidiaphragm elevated. Sniff test paradoxical movement.
Q. Diagnosis and management.
Show Answer
  • Right phrenic nerve injury.
  • Confirm with diaphragmatic ultrasound (excursion, thickening fraction) and phrenic NCS.
  • Trial extubation with NIV.
  • If recurrent failure beyond 2–4 weeks, proceed with diaphragmatic plication.

Block C — Remaining SAQs (randomized)

Q C-1
CSF profile in GBS and its temporal pitfall.
Show Answer
  • Cytoalbuminologic dissociation — elevated protein with normal cell count.
  • Up to 50 % normal in week 1.
  • Repeat after 7–10 days if early sample normal but clinical suspicion remains.
Q C-2
Define ICU-acquired weakness and its diagnostic criteria.
Show Answer
  • Symmetric flaccid weakness developing after onset of critical illness.
  • MRC sum-score <48/60 on two examinations ≥24 h apart.
  • Ventilator dependence.
  • Other causes excluded.
  • Electrodiagnostic evidence supports subtype (CIP, CIM, CINM, prolonged NMB).
Q C-3
List six GBS subtypes with one distinguishing feature each.
Show Answer
  • AIDP — symmetric demyelinating ascending.
  • AMAN — pure motor axonal; anti-GM1; post-Campylobacter.
  • AMSAN — severe sensorimotor axonal.
  • Miller-Fisher — ophthalmoplegia + ataxia + areflexia; anti-GQ1b.
  • Bickerstaff — MFS + altered consciousness.
  • Pharyngo-cervico-brachial — bulbar + arm, legs spared.
Q C-4
Describe the ice-pack test.
Show Answer
  • Ice pack on the more ptotic eyelid for 2 minutes.
  • Improvement >2 mm in ptosis supports MG.
  • Cold transiently improves NMJ transmission.
  • Specificity 80–90 %; simple and safe.
Q C-5
Antibodies tested in MG and their positive rates.
Show Answer
  • AChR-Ab — ~85 % of generalized JMG; ~50 % of ocular MG.
  • MuSK-Ab — ~40 % of AChR-negative MG; more bulbar.
  • Anti-LRP4 — emerging seronegative.
Q C-6
Describe MuSK-Ab MG features.
Show Answer
  • Predominantly bulbar and respiratory weakness with limb sparing.
  • Frequent facial wasting.
  • Poor response to anticholinesterases — some worsen.
  • Better response to PLEX and rituximab.
  • RNS may be normal in limbs but abnormal in facial muscles.
Q C-7
Neonatal transient MG.
Show Answer
  • Transplacental maternal AChR antibody.
  • 10–20 % of infants of MG mothers symptomatic.
  • Onset hours–days; resolves in 2–8 weeks.
  • NG feeds, NIV / ventilator if needed.
  • Short-course pyridostigmine if symptomatic.
Q C-8
Thymectomy indications in pediatric MG.
Show Answer
  • Thymoma — absolute.
  • Generalized AChR-positive non-thymomatous MG in adolescents — selectively per MGTX trial extension.
  • Usually deferred pre-puberty.
  • MuSK-MG and ocular MG generally not thymectomized.
Q C-9
Newer biologics for refractory MG.
Show Answer
  • Eculizumab and ravulizumab — C5 complement inhibitors.
  • Efgartigimod and rozanolixizumab — FcRn inhibitors reducing pathogenic IgG.
  • Rituximab — established second-line, especially MuSK-MG.
Q C-10
Classic clinical features of acute porphyria.
Show Answer
  • Severe abdominal pain, often without peritonism.
  • Altered mental status.
  • Autonomic dysfunction.
  • Motor neuropathy progressing to bulbar / respiratory failure.
  • Hyponatremia (SIADH).
  • Psychiatric symptoms.
  • Port-wine urine on standing.
Q C-11
Diagnostic approach to acute porphyria.
Show Answer
  • Spot urine PBG and ALA — light-protected.
  • Erythrocyte PBG deaminase (low in AIP, but normal does not exclude).
  • Confirmatory genetic testing.
Q C-12
Five drugs unsafe in porphyria.
Show Answer
  • Barbiturates.
  • Sulfonamides.
  • Phenytoin, carbamazepine, valproate.
  • OCPs, danazol.
  • Rifampin, ergots.
  • Cross-check via porphyria drug-safety database.
Q C-13
Five risk factors for ICU-AW.
Show Answer
  • Sepsis / SIRS.
  • Multi-organ failure.
  • Hyperglycemia.
  • Corticosteroids.
  • NMBA exposure.
  • Prolonged ventilation.
  • Prolonged immobilization.
  • Severity-of-illness.
Q C-14
Differentiate CIP from CIM electrodiagnostically.
Show Answer
  • CIP — reduced CMAP and SNAP, preserved velocity (sensorimotor axonal).
  • CIM — reduced CMAP, normal SNAP, low neCMAP/dmCMAP ratio <0.5.
  • EMG in CIM — short-duration MUPs ± fibrillations.
Q C-15
Causes and workup of pediatric diaphragmatic palsy.
Show Answer
  • Causes — postsurgical phrenic injury, birth trauma (with Erb's), tumors, eventration, SMA, GBS, MG, congenital myopathy, central hypoventilation.
  • Workup — CXR (limited), real-time diaphragm ultrasound (excursion / thickening fraction), fluoroscopic sniff test, phrenic NCS, diaphragm EMG.
Q C-16
When should diaphragmatic plication be considered?
Show Answer
  • Young children (<2 y) with confirmed phrenic palsy after cardiac surgery.
  • Persistent ventilator dependence beyond 2–4 weeks of failed weaning.
  • Older children often tolerate unilateral palsy without surgery.
Q C-17
Three antecedent infections most commonly preceding pediatric GBS.
Show Answer
  • Campylobacter jejuni (especially AMAN).
  • Respiratory viruses (rhinovirus, influenza).
  • EBV / CMV.
  • Also — mycoplasma, Zika, SARS-CoV-2, hepatitis E.
Q C-18
Describe Brighton diagnostic criteria for GBS.
Show Answer
  • Tiered certainty levels.
  • Level 1 — bilateral flaccid weakness, decreased / absent reflexes, monophasic course (nadir 12 h–28 days), CSF protein >0.45 g/L with cells <50/μL, EDX evidence.
  • Lower levels relax EDX and CSF requirements.
Q C-19
Five long-term sequelae of severe pediatric GBS.
Show Answer
  • Persistent distal weakness or sensory deficit.
  • Chronic fatigue.
  • Neuropathic pain.
  • Motor incoordination.
  • Residual respiratory limitations.
  • Anxiety / depression.
  • School absenteeism.
Q C-20
Role of MRI spine in pediatric AFP.
Show Answer
  • Excludes transverse myelitis (cord T2 hyperintensity).
  • Excludes AFM (gray-matter cord lesions).
  • In GBS, post-gadolinium enhancement of cauda equina / ventral nerve roots supports diagnosis.
  • MRI brain if central involvement suspected.
Q C-21
Post-PICU rehabilitation priorities after severe acute NMD.
Show Answer
  • Multidisciplinary rehab — PT, OT, speech.
  • Pulmonary rehab with assisted cough and NIV bridges.
  • Psychosocial support.
  • School re-integration.
  • Pain management.
  • Long-term follow-up for recurrence.
Q C-22
Earliest electrodiagnostic abnormality in AIDP and why.
Show Answer
  • Absent or prolonged F-waves.
  • Demyelination affects proximal nerve roots first where F-waves are generated.
  • Distal CMAP latency and CV slow later.
Q C-23
Indications for PICU admission in pediatric GBS.
Show Answer
  • Rapidly progressive weakness.
  • FVC trending <20 mL/kg.
  • Bulbar weakness with secretion failure.
  • Autonomic instability.
  • Inability to lift head or ambulate within 24 h.
Q C-24
Define myasthenic crisis and list triggers.
Show Answer
  • Acute respiratory failure or severe bulbar dysfunction requiring intubation or NIV.
  • Triggers — infection, surgery, drugs (aminoglycosides, macrolides, fluoroquinolones, beta-blockers, magnesium, phenytoin), pregnancy, missed doses, stress.
Q C-25
What is treatment-related fluctuation in GBS?
Show Answer
  • Worsening within 8 weeks of initial improvement after IVIG/PLEX.
  • Occurs in ~10 %.
  • Treat with a repeat course of the same agent.
Q C-26
Autonomic management priorities in GBS.
Show Answer
  • Continuous ECG and BP monitoring.
  • Short-acting antihypertensives only for end-organ symptoms.
  • Atropine pre-suction in bradycardia history.
  • Aggressive ileus prophylaxis.
  • Address urinary retention.
  • Thromboprophylaxis.
Q C-27
Differentiate AIDP from acute transverse myelitis.
Show Answer
  • AIDP — areflexic ascending, no sensory level, autonomic features, sphincters spared early.
  • TM — sensory level, sphincter involvement, hyperreflexia below level after spinal shock, MRI cord T2 hyperintensity.
Q C-28
Describe the ICU-AW prevention bundle.
Show Answer
  • Daily sedation interruption.
  • Minimize NMBA.
  • Glycemia 6–10 mmol/L.
  • Early mobilization.
  • Minimize corticosteroids.
  • Treat sepsis source.
  • Protein-adequate nutrition.
  • MRC sum-score screening.
Q C-29
Role of pyridostigmine during MG crisis on the ventilator.
Show Answer
  • Hold or reduce — increases pharyngeal secretions and complicates care.
  • Restart graded as weaning is contemplated.
Q C-30
Define acute flaccid paralysis and list causes by compartment.
Show Answer
  • Rapid-onset weakness frequently involving respiratory and bulbar muscles.
  • AHC — polio, AFM, WNV, JE.
  • Cord — TM, trauma.
  • Nerve — GBS variants, toxic, diphtheric, tick paralysis.
  • NMJ — botulism, MG, organophosphate, envenomation.
  • Muscle — acute myositis, periodic paralysis, rhabdomyolysis.

Block D — Remaining Case Bank (randomized)

CASE D-1
10-year-old, 10 days post-bloody diarrhea, rapidly progressive flaccid quadriparesis over 3 days. NCS — preserved velocity, low CMAP, normal SNAPs. Anti-GM1 positive.
Q. Subtype, prognosis, treatment.
Show Answer
  • AMAN.
  • Variable prognosis — rapid reversible conduction failure in some; prolonged axonal recovery in others.
  • IVIG 2 g/kg.
  • Anticipate longer rehab; possible residual weakness.
CASE D-2
8-year-old, 2 weeks post-URI, bilateral ophthalmoplegia, gait ataxia, areflexia. Limb strength preserved.
Q. Diagnosis, antibody, treatment.
Show Answer
  • Miller-Fisher syndrome.
  • Anti-GQ1b.
  • Supportive ± IVIG.
  • Watch for evolution to Bickerstaff brainstem encephalitis.
CASE D-3
14-year-old develops ophthalmoplegia, ataxia, areflexia plus drowsiness and upgoing toes. MRI brainstem hyperintensity.
Q. Diagnosis, management.
Show Answer
  • Bickerstaff brainstem encephalitis — central + peripheral autoimmune.
  • Anti-GQ1b.
  • IVIG 2 g/kg ± steroids in CNS-overlap form.
  • Monitor for respiratory failure.
CASE D-4
16-year-old develops acute bulbar weakness, neck flexion weakness, arm weakness over 3 days, legs relatively spared.
Q. Diagnosis and key risk.
Show Answer
  • Pharyngo-cervico-brachial GBS variant.
  • Anti-GT1a / GQ1b.
  • Major risk — bulbar / airway compromise without much limb weakness.
  • Intubate proactively for secretion management.
  • IVIG.
CASE D-5
9-year-old develops paraparesis with sensory loss at T6, urinary retention, hyperreflexia below the level after spinal shock. MRI — T2 cord hyperintensity over 3 segments.
Q. Diagnosis vs. GBS; management.
Show Answer
  • Transverse myelitis — sensory level, sphincter involvement, cord lesion on MRI.
  • IV methylprednisolone 30 mg/kg/day × 5.
  • PLEX if poor response.
  • Investigate AQP4 / MOG, multiple sclerosis.
CASE D-6
4-year-old presents in September with right arm flaccid paralysis after a febrile URI. MRI — cord gray matter T2 hyperintensity. SNAPs preserved, CMAP low.
Q. Diagnosis, management.
Show Answer
  • AFM — likely enterovirus D68.
  • NP / stool / CSF for enterovirus PCR.
  • Supportive care; IVIG sometimes given (weak evidence).
  • Aggressive rehab.
  • Nerve transfer surgery at 6–12 months for residual.
  • Report to public health.
CASE D-7
11-year-old with subacute GBS gets RSI with succinylcholine → ventricular fibrillation; K⁺ 8 mmol/L.
Q. Mechanism and lesson.
Show Answer
  • Massive K⁺ release from upregulated extrajunctional AChRs in denervated muscle.
  • Treat — calcium gluconate, insulin/dextrose, salbutamol, bicarbonate, defibrillation.
  • Lesson — never use succinylcholine in chronic NMD, GBS, prolonged immobilization, burns >24 h, denervation.
  • Use rocuronium + sugammadex.
CASE D-8
13-year-old on day 7 of GBS — bradycardia during ETT suctioning → asystole; resuscitated.
Q. Mechanism and prevention.
Show Answer
  • Vagal hyperresponsiveness in autonomic dysfunction.
  • Pre-treat with atropine 20 mcg/kg IV.
  • Pre-oxygenate; gentle short suctioning.
  • Maintain transcutaneous or transvenous pacing standby in severe dysautonomia.
CASE D-9
7-year-old with IgA deficiency presents with classic AIDP requiring intubation.
Q. First-line therapy.
Show Answer
  • PLEX preferred — IVIG carries anaphylaxis risk from anti-IgA antibodies.
  • If IVIG used — IgA-depleted preparation, premedicate, epinephrine ready.
CASE D-10
12-year-old initially improved after IVIG for AIDP, then 4 weeks later worsens.
Q. Diagnosis and management.
Show Answer
  • Treatment-related fluctuation.
  • Repeat IVIG 2 g/kg.
  • If multiple relapses or progression beyond 8 weeks, reconsider acute-onset CIDP and start steroids.
CASE D-11
10-year-old presents like GBS but at week 12 continues to deteriorate after two IVIG courses.
Q. Likely revised diagnosis and treatment.
Show Answer
  • Acute-onset CIDP.
  • Start corticosteroids (prednisone).
  • Maintain IVIG every 3–6 weeks.
  • Plan long-term immunosuppression.
CASE D-12
13-year-old MG patient self-increased pyridostigmine; presents with weakness, miosis, salivation, sweating, diarrhea, fasciculations.
Q. Diagnosis and management.
Show Answer
  • Cholinergic crisis.
  • Stop pyridostigmine.
  • Supportive ventilation (rocuronium/sugammadex; avoid succinylcholine).
  • Low-dose atropine for muscarinic symptoms.
  • Re-titrate pyridostigmine from low dose once recovered.
CASE D-13
17-year-old MG patient receives magnesium sulfate for severe asthma exacerbation; develops respiratory failure.
Q. Mechanism and action.
Show Answer
  • Magnesium blocks presynaptic ACh release and worsens NMJ transmission — potentially lethal in MG.
  • Stop magnesium; support ventilation; calcium gluconate may partially counteract.
  • IVIG or PLEX if persistent weakness.
CASE D-14
Term infant born to a mother with AChR-positive MG. At 24 h — poor feeding, weak cry, ptosis, shallow respirations.
Q. Diagnosis and treatment.
Show Answer
  • Neonatal transient MG.
  • NG feeds, NIV / intubation if needed, short-course pyridostigmine.
  • Resolves in 2–8 weeks.
  • Cord-blood antibody titer correlates with severity.
CASE D-15
Infant with persistent weakness improves on pyridostigmine, then worsens dramatically after dose increase. AChR antibody negative.
Q. Likely CMS subtype and management.
Show Answer
  • Slow-channel CMS or DOK7/COLQ CMS — anticholinesterases worsen these.
  • Switch — fluoxetine/quinidine (slow channel) or salbutamol/ephedrine (DOK7).
  • Genetic panel confirms subtype.
CASE D-16
16-year-old, AChR-negative; severe bulbar weakness, facial wasting, mild limb involvement. RNS limb normal; SFEMG facial positive.
Q. Subtype and therapy.
Show Answer
  • MuSK-Ab MG.
  • Limited pyridostigmine response.
  • PLEX or rituximab preferred.
  • Steroid maintenance.
CASE D-17
17-year-old after home-canned vegetables develops diplopia, dysphagia, descending paralysis.
Q. Treatment difference from infant botulism.
Show Answer
  • Equine heptavalent botulism antitoxin (BAT) — not BabyBIG.
  • Skin test before infusion.
  • Supportive ICU; ileus management.
  • Report to public health; trace canning source.
CASE D-18
5-year-old returning from camping presents with rapidly ascending paralysis and ataxia over 24 h. CSF normal.
Q. Crucial step.
Show Answer
  • Full body skin inspection for an attached tick (scalp, axillae, groin, behind ears).
  • Removal is diagnostic and curative.
  • Improvement within hours.
CASE D-19
15-year-old with photosensitive skin blistering and acute abdominal pain after sulfa antibiotic.
Q. Diagnosis and action.
Show Answer
  • Variegate porphyria — skin + acute attacks.
  • Stop sulfa drug.
  • Manage as acute porphyria.
  • Sun protection; genetic counselling.
CASE D-20
8-year-old recently arrived from a region with low vaccination coverage develops palatal paralysis 3 weeks after a pharyngeal pseudomembrane illness, progressing to peripheral neuropathy at week 6.
Q. Diagnosis and treatment.
Show Answer
  • Diphtheric polyneuropathy.
  • Diphtheria antitoxin ASAP.
  • Erythromycin or penicillin to eradicate.
  • Supportive ICU.
  • Vaccinate household contacts.
CASE D-21
12-year-old with painful sensorimotor neuropathy, abdominal pain, basophilic stippling on smear. Lives near smelter.
Q. Diagnosis and management.
Show Answer
  • Lead intoxication.
  • Blood lead level.
  • Chelation — succimer (DMSA) for moderate; calcium-EDTA + dimercaprol for severe.
  • Source remediation; iron-deficiency check.
CASE D-22
9-year-old from farm with miosis, salivation, bronchorrhea, fasciculations, respiratory failure 30 min after pesticide exposure.
Q. Management.
Show Answer
  • Decontaminate.
  • Atropine titrated to dry secretions.
  • Pralidoxime.
  • Intubate — avoid succinylcholine (markedly prolonged).
  • Benzodiazepines for seizures.
  • Monitor for intermediate syndrome 24–96 h later.
CASE D-23
6-year-old bitten by elapid snake develops bilateral ptosis, ophthalmoplegia, descending paralysis 2 h after bite.
Q. Management.
Show Answer
  • Region-specific antivenom ASAP.
  • Supportive ventilation.
  • Some postsynaptic toxins reverse partially with anticholinesterases.
  • Tetanus prophylaxis.
  • Manage coagulopathy / nephrotoxicity.
  • Avoid succinylcholine.
CASE D-24
7-year-old with influenza B develops severe bilateral calf pain, refuses to walk. CK 80 000 U/L, dark urine.
Q. Diagnosis, management.
Show Answer
  • Benign acute childhood myositis.
  • Hydration to prevent AKI; monitor electrolytes.
  • Resolves 1–2 weeks.
  • Investigate atypical / persistent cases (JDM, immune necrotizing myopathy).
CASE D-25
10-year-old with fatigue, heliotrope rash, Gottron papules, proximal weakness, dysphagia, respiratory distress.
Q. Diagnosis and management.
Show Answer
  • Juvenile dermatomyositis.
  • MRI muscle T2 hyperintensity.
  • IV methylprednisolone, methotrexate or IVIG.
  • Rule out interstitial lung disease (HRCT, anti-MDA5).
  • PICU for severe respiratory or aspiration risk.
CASE D-26
14-year-old wakes with quadriparesis after heavy carbohydrate dinner. K⁺ 2.4 mmol/L. Family history.
Q. Diagnosis, treatment, long-term.
Show Answer
  • Hypokalemic periodic paralysis.
  • Oral K⁺ 0.5–1 mEq/kg; cautious IV only if arrhythmia; watch rebound hyperkalemia.
  • Long-term — avoid heavy carbohydrates; acetazolamide or dichlorphenamide prophylaxis.
CASE D-27
15-year-old with new Graves disease, thyroid storm, severe proximal weakness.
Q. Mechanism and management.
Show Answer
  • Thyrotoxic myopathy.
  • Treat thyroid storm — PTU, propranolol, iodine, steroids.
  • Myopathy resolves with euthyroid state.
CASE D-28
9-year-old, 21 days post-meningococcal sepsis, persistent flaccid quadriparesis. EDX — reduced CMAP and SNAP, preserved velocity.
Q. Diagnosis, bundle, prognosis.
Show Answer
  • CIP.
  • Tighten prevention bundle.
  • No pharmacotherapy.
  • Children recover better than adults; partial recovery over months.
CASE D-29
11-year-old asthma exacerbation, paralyzed 5 days with rocuronium, high-dose methylprednisolone, fails to wean. Proximal weakness, normal reflexes, CK 4 000.
Q. Diagnosis and EDX.
Show Answer
  • Acute necrotizing CIM (NMBA + steroid).
  • Low CMAP, normal SNAP, low neCMAP/dmCMAP ratio, EMG myopathic with fibrillations.
  • Stop NMBA, taper steroids, mobilize.
  • Recovery slow.
CASE D-30
5-year-old in renal failure received vecuronium infusion 48 h; fails to wake on stopping. TOF 0/4.
Q. Mechanism, action.
Show Answer
  • Prolonged NMB due to renal/hepatic accumulation.
  • Co-factors — hypothermia, acidosis, steroid co-administration, certain antibiotics.
  • Wait for clearance; supportive ventilation; monitor TOF.
  • Sugammadex 16 mg/kg can reverse rocuronium; caution with vecuronium and recurrence.
CASE D-31
3-month-old with tachypnea, paradoxical breathing, both hemidiaphragms elevated. Family consanguinity. IUGR at birth.
Q. Differential and key test.
Show Answer
  • SMARD1 (IGHMBP2).
  • Consider severe SMA, congenital myopathy, central hypoventilation (PHOX2B).
  • Genetic panel.
  • Long-term ventilatory support if SMARD1.
CASE D-32
Term neonate after shoulder dystocia has right arm flaccid in waiter's-tip position and respiratory distress. Right hemidiaphragm elevated.
Q. Diagnosis, management.
Show Answer
  • Brachial plexus injury (C5–C6 / Erb's) with phrenic injury (C3–C5).
  • NIV / mechanical ventilation as needed; trial weaning.
  • Many improve over weeks; plication if persistent.
  • Brachial plexus rehab/surgery if no improvement at 3 months.
CASE D-33
12-year-old, 14 days post-COVID, develops areflexic ascending paralysis. NCS demyelinating.
Q. Diagnosis and management.
Show Answer
  • Post-COVID AIDP.
  • IVIG 2 g/kg; supportive ICU.
  • Outcomes similar to other parainfectious GBS.
CASE D-34
13-year-old has third episode of GBS-like illness in 18 months, each responsive to IVIG but recurring on weaning.
Q. Revised diagnosis, management.
Show Answer
  • CIDP.
  • Maintenance IVIG every 3–6 weeks ± corticosteroid.
  • Consider rituximab or SC immunoglobulin.
  • Long-term neurology follow-up.
CASE D-35
15-year-old with sudden quadriplegia, anarthria, but vertical eye movements preserved. MRI — basilar artery thrombosis with pontine infarct.
Q. Critical bedside clue, management.
Show Answer
  • Locked-in syndrome — preserved vertical gaze and consciousness distinguish from peripheral paralysis.
  • Time-sensitive thrombolysis / endovascular thrombectomy if within window.
  • Long-term tracheostomy and assistive communication.
