ZIMMERMAN Ch 118 — Traumatic Brain Injury — EXAM MODE

SOURCE: ZIMMERMAN   Companion to: ZIMMERMAN_118 STUDY MODE

Focus: Mock RCPSC SEAP examination on severe pediatric TBI — BTF 2019 algorithm, secondary injury cascades, neuroprotective RSI, herniation pathway, Marshall CT, multimodal monitoring.

Block A — Mock RCPSC SAQ (10 questions, 12 minutes total)

A1. List the intubation criteria for the head-injured child (Zimmerman Box 118.1 framework).

Show answer
  • GCS ≤ 8 or deterioration of ≥ 2 points from baseline.
  • Inability to protect airway (absent gag/cough, copious secretions).
  • Apnea or irregular respiratory pattern; hypoxemia despite supplemental O₂.
  • Signs of impending herniation — anisocoria, Cushing triad, motor posturing.
  • Need for sedation/analgesia precluding spontaneous ventilation.
  • Need for transport/CT in patient at risk of clinical deterioration.
  • Combative behaviour preventing safe assessment or imaging.

A2. Outline a neuroprotective RSI strategy stratified by hemodynamic status.

Show answer
  • Pre-oxygenate FiO₂ 1.0; in-line cervical stabilization; avoid hypotension and hypoxia at all costs.
  • Pre-treatment: lidocaine 1–1.5 mg/kg IV optional; fentanyl 1–3 µg/kg blunts sympathetic surge if normotensive.
  • Normotensive: etomidate 0.3 mg/kg OR thiopental/propofol with caution; rocuronium 1.2 mg/kg or succinylcholine.
  • Hypotensive/shocked: ketamine 1–2 mg/kg IV (acceptable in TBI — no rise in ICP when ventilation controlled); rocuronium 1.2 mg/kg.
  • Avoid propofol bolus in shock.
  • Continue post-intubation sedation/analgesia (midazolam or propofol infusion + fentanyl); paralysis only if needed for ICP/ventilation.
  • End-tidal CO₂ target 35–38 mm Hg; SpO₂ ≥ 94%; MAP age-appropriate for CPP target.

A3. Describe the three categories of secondary injury after pediatric TBI.

Show answer
  • Excitotoxicity / energy failure: glutamate release → NMDA/AMPA overactivation → Ca²⁺ influx → mitochondrial dysfunction → ATP depletion → necrosis/apoptosis; ROS, lipid peroxidation.
  • Cerebral swelling: cytotoxic edema (cellular Na⁺/water accumulation, AQP4-mediated astrocyte swelling), vasogenic edema (BBB disruption), hyperemia (vasoparalysis in some children).
  • Traumatic axonal injury (TAI): mechanical stretch → cytoskeletal disruption, axolemmal Ca²⁺ entry → axonal swelling, transport disruption, secondary axotomy over hours-days.
  • Targets: avoid hypotension/hypoxia/hyperventilation; hyperosmolar therapy; temperature control; seizure control.

A4. State BTF 2019 pediatric CPP and ICP thresholds.

Show answer
  • ICP treatment threshold > 20 mm Hg for sustained > 5 min.
  • CPP minimum target ≥ 40 mm Hg in infants/young children; ≥ 50 mm Hg in school-age; ≥ 60 in adolescents (age-stratified).
  • Use age-appropriate MAP target (50th percentile or higher) to maintain CPP.
  • Multimodal data — PRx/CPPopt — increasingly favored for individualized targets.

A5. List BTF Tier 1 first-line therapies for ICP > 20 mm Hg.

Show answer
  • Adequate sedation and analgesia (avoid propofol > 4 mg/kg/h or > 48 h — PRIS risk).
  • Drain CSF if EVD in place.
  • Hyperosmolar therapy: hypertonic saline 3% 3–5 mL/kg bolus (target Na 150–160), or mannitol 0.25–1 g/kg.
  • Neuromuscular blockade if shivering/dyssynchrony.
  • Mild head-of-bed 30°, normocapnia (PaCO₂ 35–38), normothermia 36–37°C.
  • Treat seizures (cEEG); maintain Hb ≥ 7 g/dL (higher if low PbtO₂).

A6. Describe Marshall CT classification and its prognostic implications.

Show answer
  • I: Diffuse injury — no visible pathology.
  • II: Diffuse injury — cisterns present, midline shift 0–5 mm, no mass > 25 mL.
  • III: Diffuse injury, swelling — cisterns compressed/absent, shift 0–5 mm.
  • IV: Diffuse injury, shift > 5 mm, no mass > 25 mL.
  • V: Evacuated mass lesion (surgical).
  • VI: Non-evacuated mass lesion (> 25 mL, not surgical).
  • Higher grades (III–VI) → worse outcome; compressed cisterns associated with elevated ICP.
  • Pediatric-specific findings: AHT subdural patterns, growing skull fracture, age-specific shear injury.

A7. Summarize the ICP-monitoring outcome debate in pediatric TBI.

Show answer
  • Forsyth (UK) and Gerber (NY State) cohorts — ICP monitoring associated with improved outcomes in severe pediatric TBI.
  • Chesnut BEST TRIP RCT (adults, Latin America) — no overall benefit of ICP monitoring vs imaging/clinical protocol.
  • Bennett pediatric propensity-matched cohort — no clear benefit of monitoring in mortality outcomes.
  • Current BTF 2019 — Level III recommendation to consider ICP monitoring in severe TBI (GCS ≤ 8).
  • Practice variability remains; ADAPT trial documented heterogeneous use.
  • Key: ICP data only useful if integrated into protocolized algorithm — monitor + act.

A8. A 6-year-old with severe TBI develops blown left pupil 4 hours after arrival. Outline immediate management.

Show answer
  • Recognize impending herniation — call surgical team STAT.
  • Head-of-bed 30°, head midline, ensure ventilation; brief controlled hyperventilation PaCO₂ 30–35 only as bridge.
  • Hyperosmolar bolus: 3% saline 5 mL/kg IV (or mannitol 1 g/kg if Na high).
  • Drain CSF if EVD; ensure adequate sedation/paralysis to reduce metabolic demand.
  • Stat non-contrast CT head.
  • Neurosurgical consultation — decompressive craniectomy or evacuation if mass lesion.
  • Maintain age-appropriate CPP — vasopressor support if needed.
  • Avoid hypotension, hypoxia, hyperthermia, hyponatremia, glucose dysregulation.

A9. List BTF Tier 2 options for refractory intracranial hypertension.

Show answer
  • Barbiturate coma (high-dose pentobarbital) with EEG burst-suppression target; requires hemodynamic support.
  • Decompressive craniectomy — early in pediatric AHT or unilateral mass; consider in diffuse swelling refractory to medical therapy.
  • Therapeutic moderate hypothermia 32–34°C — Cool Kids trial and Hutchison's earlier trial did not show benefit and may worsen outcomes; reserved for ICP control only.
  • Aggressive controlled hyperventilation PaCO₂ 25–30 — only with multimodal monitoring (PbtO₂, SjvO₂) to avoid ischemia.
  • Lumbar drainage in select cases with open basal cisterns.

A10. Describe advanced multimodal neuromonitoring in pediatric severe TBI.

Show answer
  • PbtO₂ (brain tissue O₂) — target ≥ 15–20 mm Hg; supplements CPP/ICP.
  • SjvO₂ (jugular bulb oximetry) — target 55–75%; assesses global oxygen extraction.
  • Cerebral microdialysis — lactate/pyruvate ratio, glutamate, glycerol; energy failure markers.
  • PRx (pressure reactivity index) — moving correlation ICP vs MAP; identifies CPPopt.
  • cEEG — non-convulsive seizures common (30–40% pediatric severe TBI); also for burst suppression in barbiturate coma.
  • NIRS (cerebral oximetry) — non-invasive but evidence less robust.
  • Integrate data into protocol-driven decisions, not pursue each value in isolation.

Block B — Oral Cases (5 sequential, 35–40 minutes total)

B1. A 7-year-old boy is unrestrained in a high-speed MVC. EMS finds him obtunded (GCS 6). HR 130, BP 75/40, SpO₂ 88% on NRB. Pupils 4 mm reactive bilaterally.

B1a — Approach in the trauma bay.
  • Primary survey: cervical spine immobilization; airway with manual in-line stabilization.
  • RSI per Box 118.1 (GCS < 8 + airway risk + shock) — ketamine 1–2 mg/kg + rocuronium 1.2 mg/kg given hypotension; AVOID propofol bolus.
  • Fluid resuscitation isotonic NS 20 mL/kg; transfuse if hemorrhagic shock; vasopressor support if needed (norepinephrine).
  • Targets: SpO₂ ≥ 94%, age-appropriate MAP for CPP ≥ 50, PaCO₂ 35–38, normothermia 36–37, glucose 100–180.
  • Stat CT head (after stabilization) + cervical spine + FAST.
  • Send labs: type/cross, CBC, coags, fibrinogen, BMP, lactate, ABG, ethanol/tox screen.
B1b — CT shows diffuse swelling with compressed basal cisterns, midline shift 4 mm. ICP monitor placed reads 28 mm Hg. Tier 1 management?
  • Deepen sedation/analgesia: midazolam + fentanyl infusion; check for shivering/dyssynchrony.
  • Drain CSF if EVD in place (preferred over parenchymal in young child).
  • 3% saline 5 mL/kg bolus → infusion to maintain Na 150–160 (cap ≤ 170, osm ≤ 360).
  • Or mannitol 0.5–1 g/kg if euvolemic (check osm gap).
  • Head-of-bed 30°, neutral neck; PaCO₂ 35–38; normothermia 36–37 (cooling blanket).
  • cEEG to rule out non-convulsive seizures; treat if present (levetiracetam first-line for prophylaxis).
  • Maintain age-appropriate CPP ≥ 50 mm Hg with vasopressor as needed.
B1c — ICP persistently > 25 despite Tier 1. Escalation?
  • Reassess: ensure no surgical lesion missed (repeat CT); confirm normothermia, Na, glucose, PaCO₂; rule out seizure on cEEG.
  • Tier 2 options:
    • Decompressive craniectomy — consider early in pediatric population; effective in diffuse swelling with shift; family discussion of risks.
    • High-dose pentobarbital with EEG burst-suppression; requires invasive hemodynamic monitoring + vasopressor support.
    • Moderate hypothermia 32–34°C — only for ICP control; do not use for neuroprotection alone (Hutchison + Cool Kids negative).
    • Aggressive hyperventilation PaCO₂ 25–30 — bridge with PbtO₂/SjvO₂ monitoring to avoid ischemia.
  • Add advanced neuromonitoring (PbtO₂, microdialysis) if available.
B1d — Day 3: ICP controlled but PbtO₂ persistently 12 mm Hg despite CPP 60. Interpretation and action?
  • Low PbtO₂ with adequate CPP suggests microcirculatory failure, anemia, hypocarbia, or metabolic mismatch.
  • Check: Hb (transfuse to ≥ 9–10 if anemic), PaCO₂ (avoid hyperventilation), MAP/CPP, FiO₂.
  • Increase FiO₂ trial; consider increasing CPP target (CPPopt if PRx available).
  • Reduce metabolic demand: sedation, normothermia, treat seizures.
  • Persistent low PbtO₂ portends worse outcome.
B1e — Family meeting at day 5: discuss goals of care given uncertain prognosis.
  • Avoid premature prognostication — multimodal data over time, ideally off sedation.
  • MRI when stable for structural injury extent and DTI signs.
  • Multidisciplinary meeting: PICU, neurosurgery, neurology, palliative care, rehab, social work, chaplaincy.
  • Honest communication acknowledging uncertainty and trajectory.
  • Address parental needs (PICS-Family); offer second opinion if requested.
  • Plan trajectory: continued aggressive care vs WLST vs tracheostomy/PEG; revisit frequently.

B2. A 4-month-old infant presents with apnea and seizures. Subdural hemorrhages and retinal hemorrhages noted on imaging.

B2a — Assessment.
  • Concern for abusive head trauma (AHT) — must be considered when history is incongruous with injury severity.
  • Mandatory child protection consult and reporting per local statute.
  • Full skeletal survey + repeat in 2 weeks; ophthalmology for dilated retinal exam.
  • Coagulation studies, metabolic workup (rule out mimics).
  • Document carefully; speak with family non-accusatorially while protective services act.
B2b — ICU management priorities.
  • Same BTF principles with pediatric-specific caveats — high mortality in AHT.
  • Seizure prophylaxis with levetiracetam; cEEG (high rate of subclinical seizures in AHT).
  • Open fontanelle/sutures do NOT prevent herniation — full ICP management still indicated.
  • Consider early decompressive craniectomy in infants (small skull volume, rapid decompensation).
  • Hyperosmolar therapy with caution for hyponatremia/hypernatremia in infants.
  • Strict glucose 100–180; avoid hypothermia < 36 in young infants (limited evidence).
B2c — How does AHT differ from accidental TBI pathophysiologically?
  • Often multi-event/multi-mechanism; combined rotational + impact forces.
  • Higher rates of bilateral subdural hemorrhage (commonly mixed-age), diffuse axonal injury, hypoxic-ischemic component (from apnea/strangulation).
  • Greater cytotoxic edema and worse outcomes than mechanism-matched accidental TBI.
  • Retinal hemorrhages (multilayered, extending to ora serrata) highly specific.
  • Outcomes worse: higher mortality, severe long-term disability common.
B2d — Long-term plan if infant survives with severe disability.
  • Tracheostomy and PEG planning if prolonged disability.
  • Inpatient pediatric rehabilitation referral early.
  • Early Intervention services post-discharge.
  • Multidisciplinary follow-up: rehab medicine, neurology, ophthalmology, developmental pediatrics, mental health.
  • Coordination with child protection placement (foster care or relative).
  • Document everything for legal proceedings; be available for court testimony.

B3. A 14-year-old struck by a car has GCS 4, fixed dilated right pupil, decerebrate posturing. CT shows large right epidural hematoma with 9 mm midline shift.

B3a — Initial action sequence.
  • This is a surgical emergency — call neurosurgery STAT for craniotomy and evacuation.
  • RSI immediately with cervical immobilization; ketamine if hypotensive, etomidate if stable; rocuronium 1.2 mg/kg.
  • 3% saline 5 mL/kg bolus while preparing for OR; mannitol 1 g/kg alternative.
  • Brief controlled hyperventilation PaCO₂ 30–35 as bridge to OR — NOT prolonged.
  • Head-of-bed 30°, neck midline; ensure SpO₂ 100%; vasopressor for age-appropriate MAP/CPP.
  • Transfer directly to OR — do not delay for additional imaging.
B3b — Post-op admission to PICU. What now?
  • ICP monitor placed intraoperatively — target ICP < 20, CPP ≥ 60.
  • Sedation and analgesia infusion; paralysis if needed; avoid propofol prolonged/high-dose.
  • Normothermia 36–37; PaCO₂ 35–38; head-of-bed 30°.
  • cEEG for non-convulsive seizures; consider prophylactic levetiracetam first 7 days.
  • Serial neuro exams off sedation if feasible; repeat CT at 6–24 h.
  • Anticipate post-traumatic vasospasm and delayed ischemia.
B3c — Day 2 develops polyuria 8 mL/kg/h, Na 152, urine SG 1.002.
  • Central diabetes insipidus from hypothalamic/pituitary axis injury.
  • DDAVP 0.5–1 µg IV (or intranasal); titrate to UOP.
  • Replace free water deficit cautiously over 48 h (avoid rapid Na correction → cerebral edema).
  • Screen anterior pituitary (cortisol, T4) — empiric stress-dose hydrocortisone if hypotensive.
  • Endocrine consult; monitor for triphasic response (DI → SIADH → permanent DI).
B3d — Decision to wean and extubate.
  • Wean sedation; assess neurological exam.
  • Pass SBT with adequate respiratory mechanics, cuff leak.
  • Ensure airway protection (gag, cough, manageable secretions).
  • Plan rehabilitation: PT/OT/speech early; assess swallow.
  • Family education; arrange inpatient pediatric rehab.

B4. A 9-year-old with severe TBI on day 4 has refractory ICP 32 mm Hg despite Tier 1. PRx 0.6 (positive — impaired autoregulation).

B4a — Interpret PRx and CPPopt.
  • PRx = moving correlation between mean ICP and mean MAP; positive PRx (> 0.25) means impaired autoregulation.
  • CPPopt = CPP at which PRx is most negative (best autoregulation).
  • Targeting CPPopt rather than fixed CPP may individualize care.
  • In this case, increase CPP to identify CPPopt or accept current value if surgical/medical Tier 2 needed.
B4b — Tier 2 plan.
  • Decompressive craniectomy strongly consider — early decompression in pediatric severe TBI may improve outcome.
  • Pentobarbital coma with EEG burst suppression target 1 burst per 10 s; pressor support needed.
  • Hypothermia 32–34 only for ICP control; rewarming slowly 0.25°C/h.
  • Hyperventilation PaCO₂ 25–30 only with PbtO₂/SjvO₂.
  • Discuss palliative trajectory with family if escalation continues.
B4c — Family asks about hypothermia. What do you tell them?
  • Hypothermia 32–34°C did not improve outcomes in two pediatric trials (Hutchison 2008; Adelson Cool Kids 2013) and may worsen outcomes.
  • Strict normothermia (36–37°C) remains the goal.
  • Hypothermia can be used as a tier 2 tool for ICP control alone, not for primary neuroprotection.
  • Aggressive normothermia is appropriate — fever worsens secondary injury.
B4d — Post-decompressive craniectomy day 7 — develops fever 39.5, WBC 22.
  • Differential: ventriculitis (if EVD), wound infection, pneumonia, line infection, central fever.
  • Send CSF (if EVD): cell count, glucose, protein, Gram stain, culture.
  • Blood cultures, urinalysis, CXR; remove/exchange suspect lines.
  • Empiric vancomycin + cefepime (or meropenem) for ventriculitis coverage.
  • Wound inspection by neurosurgery; CT for collection if neurologic change.
  • Aggressive normothermia — fever worsens TBI secondary injury.

B5. An 11-year-old with severe TBI develops paroxysmal sympathetic hyperactivity (PSH) on day 6 with episodes of hypertension, tachycardia, diaphoresis, and dystonic posturing.

B5a — Recognition and differential.
  • PSH classic syndrome after severe DAI; commonly emerges day 5–14.
  • Episodes of paroxysmal sympathetic surge (HR, BP, RR, T) + dystonic posturing.
  • Differential: pain, withdrawal, seizure (non-convulsive), sepsis, infection, intra-abdominal pathology.
  • Rule out: cEEG, infection workup, exam, abdominal evaluation, pain assessment.
B5b — Acute management.
  • Identify and remove triggers (stimulation, suctioning, position changes).
  • Acute episode: morphine 0.05–0.1 mg/kg IV (often very effective for PSH); short-acting BZD adjunct.
  • Prevent: scheduled gabapentin (effective for paroxysmal events), propranolol (β-blockade), clonidine, baclofen (intrathecal in severe cases).
  • Bromocriptine, dantrolene reserve.
  • Avoid neuroleptics that worsen dystonia.
B5c — Patient develops contractures and severe spasticity.
  • Aggressive PT/OT with range-of-motion exercises.
  • Splinting/serial casting.
  • Oral baclofen, tizanidine; intrathecal baclofen if refractory.
  • Botulinum toxin for focal spasticity.
  • Rehab medicine consult; plan inpatient rehabilitation.
B5d — Plan for transition out of PICU.
  • Tracheostomy and PEG if prolonged dependence.
  • Wean sedation; transition to oral PSH medications.
  • Pediatric rehabilitation transfer.
  • Family training: tracheostomy care, PEG, suctioning, medications, seizure management.
  • Long-term follow-up: neurology, rehab medicine, neuropsychology, school reintegration plan, PICS-p screening.

Block C — Randomized SAQ Drill (25 questions)

C1. BTF 2019 pediatric ICP treatment threshold.

Show
  • > 20 mm Hg sustained > 5 min.

C2. CPP target in 3-year-old per BTF 2019.

Show
  • ≥ 40 mm Hg (age-stratified minimum); use higher (50) when feasible.

C3. Best induction agent in hypotensive TBI child.

Show
  • Ketamine 1–2 mg/kg IV — does not raise ICP when ventilation controlled.

C4. Hypertonic saline pediatric TBI dose.

Show
  • 3% saline 3–5 mL/kg bolus; target Na 150–160, osm ≤ 360.

C5. Mannitol dose and concerns.

Show
  • 0.25–1 g/kg; risk renal failure, hypovolemia; check osm gap; avoid in hypovolemic/hypotensive.

C6. Propofol cautions in TBI.

Show
  • Avoid > 4 mg/kg/h or > 48 h — propofol infusion syndrome (PRIS); acidosis, rhabdo, cardiac failure.

C7. Steroid use in severe TBI.

Show
  • NOT recommended (CRASH-1 showed harm in adults; no pediatric benefit).

C8. TXA in severe TBI.

Show
  • CRASH-3 (adults): mortality benefit if given within 3 hours of mild-moderate TBI; pediatric extrapolation supports use in moderate TBI with hemorrhage.

C9. Seizure prophylaxis in pediatric severe TBI.

Show
  • Levetiracetam first 7 days post-injury (or phenytoin); reduces early post-traumatic seizures.

C10. Marshall CT grade III definition.

Show
  • Diffuse injury with swelling — cisterns compressed/absent, midline shift 0–5 mm.

C11. Hutchison 2008 hypothermia trial outcome.

Show
  • Hypothermia 32–34 × 24 h after pediatric severe TBI — NO mortality/outcome benefit; possible harm.

C12. Cool Kids (Adelson) trial outcome.

Show
  • Stopped early — no benefit of 48 h hypothermia 32–33°C in pediatric severe TBI.

C13. PbtO₂ target.

Show
  • ≥ 15–20 mm Hg.

C14. SjvO₂ target.

Show
  • 55–75% (low → ischemia; high → hyperemia or arrest).

C15. PRx interpretation.

Show
  • Positive (> 0.25) → impaired autoregulation; negative → preserved.

C16. Hb threshold for transfusion in pediatric TBI.

Show
  • ≥ 7 g/dL standard; consider higher (9–10) if low PbtO₂ or ongoing ischemia.

C17. Glucose target in pediatric TBI.

Show
  • 100–180 mg/dL (5.6–10 mmol/L); avoid hypo and severe hyperglycemia.

C18. Decompressive craniectomy indication.

Show
  • Mass lesion with shift; refractory diffuse ICP; consider early in pediatric AHT and unilateral lesions.

C19. Retinal hemorrhage in AHT — pattern.

Show
  • Multilayered, extending to ora serrata, bilateral — highly specific for AHT.

C20. DAI imaging finding.

Show
  • Multiple punctate hemorrhages at gray-white junction, corpus callosum, brainstem on MRI (GRE/SWI); DTI changes.

C21. Central DI post-TBI dose.

Show
  • DDAVP 0.5–1 µg IV or 1–2 µg intranasal; titrate UOP; replace FWD over 48 h.

C22. Cerebral salt wasting vs SIADH.

Show
  • CSW: hypovolemic hyponatremia, polyuria, treat with NaCl + volume. SIADH: euvolemic hyponatremia, low UOP, treat with fluid restriction.

C23. PSH (paroxysmal sympathetic hyperactivity) treatment.

Show
  • Morphine acutely; gabapentin scheduled; β-blocker (propranolol); clonidine; baclofen (intrathecal severe).

C24. PaCO₂ target initial TBI care.

Show
  • 35–38 mm Hg (normocapnia); avoid prophylactic hyperventilation.

C25. When may controlled hyperventilation (PaCO₂ 30–35) be used?

Show
  • Acute herniation bridge; refractory ICH with multimodal monitoring (PbtO₂/SjvO₂) — not prolonged.

Block D — Randomized PICU Cases (20)

D1. 3-year-old fell from 2nd floor; GCS 5; CT shows acute SDH with 8 mm shift.

Show
  • Emergent neurosurgery; RSI ketamine + rocuronium; HTS bolus; controlled hyperventilation bridge; CPP ≥ 40.

D2. 12-year-old bicycle crash, GCS 7, ICP 22.

Show
  • BTF Tier 1: sedation, EVD drain, HTS, head-of-bed 30°, normothermia, normocapnia, cEEG; CPP ≥ 50.

D3. 6-week-old with seizures, bilateral SDH, multilayered retinal hemorrhages.

Show
  • AHT; mandatory CPS report; skeletal survey; cEEG; levetiracetam; full BTF; early craniectomy threshold low.

D4. 14-year-old GCS 4 with epidural hematoma, blown pupil.

Show
  • STAT craniotomy; HTS or mannitol bolus; brief hyperventilation bridge; CPP ≥ 60.

D5. Post-TBI day 5, polyuria, Na 152, dilute urine.

Show
  • Central DI; DDAVP; replace FWD over 48 h; screen anterior pituitary; endocrine consult.

D6. Post-TBI day 4, Na 124 with high UNa.

Show
  • Differentiate CSW (hypovolemic) vs SIADH (euvolemic); CSW → salt + volume; SIADH → fluid restriction; correct Na slowly to avoid CPM.

D7. Post-TBI day 7 paroxysms of tachycardia, hypertension, posturing.

Show
  • PSH; morphine acutely; scheduled gabapentin + propranolol + clonidine; rule out seizure on cEEG.

D8. Refractory ICP 35 mm Hg day 3, autoregulation impaired (PRx + 0.5).

Show
  • Tier 2: decompressive craniectomy; pentobarbital coma; consider hypothermia for ICP control only; titrate to CPPopt.

D9. CT shows diffuse axonal injury pattern with corpus callosum hemorrhages.

Show
  • DAI; MRI for DTI; predict prolonged disorders of consciousness; rehab medicine early.

D10. Penetrating gunshot wound to head, GCS 6.

Show
  • Stat neurosurgery; broad-spectrum antibiotics (cefazolin/cefepime + metronidazole + vanco); tetanus; CT angio for vascular injury.

D11. Severe TBI + INR 1.8 + platelets 90.

Show
  • Coagulopathy worsens outcomes; reverse rapidly: FFP/PCC for warfarin or coagulopathy; platelets to ≥ 100; fibrinogen ≥ 1.5 g/L; consider TEG-guided therapy.

D12. Severe TBI day 2 develops new fever 39.

Show
  • Fever worsens secondary injury; aggressive normothermia 36–37; rule out infection (cultures, EVD CSF); central fever diagnosis of exclusion.

D13. EVD draining excessive CSF causing IVH "siphoning".

Show
  • Level EVD to appropriate height (10–15 cmH₂O above tragus); avoid excessive drainage to prevent collapse and acute subdural; close drain during transport.

D14. Severe TBI on pentobarbital coma develops hypotension.

Show
  • Anticipated effect; vasopressor (norepinephrine); ensure euvolemia; consider easing pentobarbital if necessary.

D15. Pediatric AHT with cardiac arrest at scene.

Show
  • Post-arrest bundle (TTM 32–34 or 36–37); BTF concurrent; high mortality and disability; document carefully; child protection involvement.

D16. CT shows large bifrontal contusions; planning extubation.

Show
  • Wean sedation; assess airway protection; expect frontal lobe disinhibition/agitation; rehab medicine; cognitive evaluation post-extubation.

D17. Severe TBI with thoracic spine injury.

Show
  • Maintain MAP for both cerebral and cord perfusion (CPP and spinal cord perfusion pressure); spinal precautions; coordinate with orthopedics/neurosurgery.

D18. Bullet trajectory crosses midline of brain.

Show
  • Very high mortality; consider goals of care discussion; surgical debridement; antibiotics; antiseizure prophylaxis; family meeting early.

D19. Severe TBI with skull base fracture and CSF rhinorrhea.

Show
  • β-2 transferrin testing of fluid; head-of-bed 30°; avoid nasal instrumentation/NG tube (oral instead); consider lumbar drain; neurosurgery for persistent leak.

D20. Severe TBI surviving with disorder of consciousness day 14.

Show
  • Trach + PEG planning; rehab medicine; pediatric neurology; family meeting; PICS-p screening; long-term support plan.

Top 5 traps

  • Prophylactic hyperventilation in pediatric severe TBI (causes ischemia — only as acute herniation bridge or with multimodal monitoring).
  • Therapeutic hypothermia 32–34 for neuroprotection (Hutchison + Cool Kids negative; possibly harmful — only for ICP control).
  • Steroids in severe TBI (CRASH-1 showed harm in adults; not recommended in pediatrics).
  • Adult-pattern CPP targets in young children (must age-stratify 40/50/60 mm Hg).
  • Open fontanelle/sutures do NOT prevent herniation — full ICP management required.
  • Missing AHT in young infant with apparent low-mechanism injury — must screen with skeletal survey, ophthalmology, CPS report.
  • Confusing CSW (hypovolemic hyponatremia) with SIADH (euvolemic) — opposite treatment.
