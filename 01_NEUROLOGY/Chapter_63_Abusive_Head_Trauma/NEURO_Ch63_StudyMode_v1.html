1 · Structured Rogers Mirror (verbatim headings + ICU commentary)

KEY POINTS (pages 3313–3315)
  • AHT spans all cultures and is the leading cause of severe TBI in infants.
  • Mechanisms include shaking, blunt impact, hypoxic-ischemic injury; many cases involve more than one.
  • Consider AHT in any infant with intracranial hemorrhage — caregiver history may be unreliable or absent.
  • Mandatory reporting in all US states/Canadian provinces; physicians are legally protected when reporting in good faith.
  • Pediatric BTF guidelines (Kochanek 2019, 3rd ed.) apply: ICP <20, CPP target age-dependent.
  • Seizures common (up to 70% of severe AHT), often subclinical → cEEG critical.
  • Mortality and disability higher than for noninflicted TBI of the same GCS.
ICU layer:
  • Treat the brain injury per BTF tier 1/tier 2 in parallel with the safeguarding pathway — neither delays the other.
  • Activate Child Protection Team, social work, ophthalmology, neurosurgery, radiology, forensic medicine on day 0.
  • Document examination findings verbatim with objective language; chain-of-custody mindset.
RCPSC angle:
  • Safeguarding leadership is examinable: who reports, when, what is documented, how parents are notified.
  • Probability-style differential ("is this AHT?") cannot be made by intensivist alone — but suspicion is sufficient to trigger reporting.
BASICS OF AHT RESUSCITATION AND CARE (page 3315)
  • Standard ABCs with pediatric trauma overlay.
  • Airway protection for GCS ≤8 or imaging concerning for intracranial hypertension/herniation.
  • Avoid hyperoxemia and unnecessary hyperventilation (both worsen secondary injury).
  • Vascular access for resuscitation fluids, blood products, vasopressors, and BP monitoring.
  • Obtain accurate GCS once paralytics and sedatives have cleared.
  • Mild/moderate AHT (GCS 9–15): expectant surveillance for deterioration; complete diagnostic workup.
  • Severe AHT (GCS ≤8): full BTF severe TBI protocol — ICP monitor, surgical lesion evacuation, tiered ICP therapy, antiseizure prophylaxis, nutritional planning.
  • Comprehensive evaluation for extracerebral injuries (fractures, abdominal, ocular).
ICU layer: Treat the GCS 9–11 cohort with extra caution — Shein et al. showed ~9.5% mortality in this band, similar to severe noninflicted TBI. Lower threshold for ICU admission, cEEG, and serial neuro exam.
Trap: Discharging a "well-appearing" infant with isolated subdural after a vague history — risk of return with catastrophic re-injury or missed retinal hemorrhages.
INTRODUCTION (pages 3316–3317)
  • Nomenclature has evolved: "whiplash shaken infant syndrome" (Caffey 1974) → "shaken baby syndrome" → "inflicted/nonaccidental TBI" → "abusive head trauma" (AAP 2009 consensus, Christian & Block).
  • AHT term recognizes that injury is rarely a single mechanism — it integrates shaking, blunt impact, hypoxia, and sometimes upper-cervical spinal cord injury.
  • Term shifts focus from mechanism (which is often contested medico-legally) to etiology (abusive).
RCPSC angle: Know the term lineage — examiners may ask why we say "AHT" instead of "shaken baby." Answer: integrates multiple mechanisms + medico-legally neutral as to specific act.
EPIDEMIOLOGY (page 3317)
  • Incidence 15–30 per 100 000 infants per year — almost certainly an underestimate.
  • Most common in infants <1 year, with peak incidence parallel to peak infant crying (2–4 months).
  • ~25% of AHT cases are in children >1 year — do not exclude based on age.
  • Male preponderance (consistent with noninflicted TBI patterns).
  • Frequent under-recognition: vague symptoms like vomiting may be misdiagnosed as gastroenteritis or reflux (Jenny 1999 JAMA — landmark "missed cases" paper).
ICU layer: COVID-19 era saw documented rise in AHT (Sidpra 2021); be alert during community stressors (economic crisis, deployment, natural disaster).
Risk Factors (page 3318)
  • Child-intrinsic: age <2 years, male sex, prematurity, colic/excessive crying, prior abuse.
  • Caregiver-intrinsic: young parental age, poor impulse control, lower maternal education, substance/alcohol abuse, mental illness, intimate partner violence.
  • Community stressors: military deployment, natural disaster, unemployment, pandemics.
  • Absence of risk factors does not exclude AHT — all young children are vulnerable.
Trap: Anchoring on a "low-risk" demographic profile. AHT occurs across all socioeconomic strata.
Mechanisms of Injury (pages 3319–3321)
  • Shaking (acceleration–deceleration): tears bridging veins → subdural hemorrhage; shear injury to retina → retinoschisis and multilayered retinal hemorrhages.
  • Blunt force trauma: alone or combined with shaking; combined "shaken–impact" produces highest rotational force (biomechanical evidence).
  • Hypoxic-ischemic injury: from apnea (brainstem/cervicomedullary injury), delayed presentation, airway obstruction.
  • Cervical spinal cord injury: subdural in cervical canal in >60% of AHT vs <1% in noninflicted TBI (Choudhary et al.). May explain apnea via respiratory center damage.
  • Trigeminal nerve hypothesis: proposed mechanism for "big black brain" pattern — sensory afferents in dura/subarachnoid triggered by repeated insult release vasoactive neuropeptides → hyperemia, BBB breakdown, neuroinflammation.

DIAGNOSIS (pages 3321–3325)

History

  • Caregiver history is unreliable in AHT — perpetrator may conceal, other caregivers may be unaware.
  • Recognize unreliability EARLY; do not be lulled by absence of "trauma" in the story.
  • Document the chronology meticulously: last well, first symptom, who was present, what was done, when help was sought.
  • Document the words used — to enable comparison if the story later changes.

Physical examination

  • Primary and secondary trauma surveys.
  • Examine for bruising in non-mobile infant, ear/neck/torso pattern bruising, frenulum tears, posterior rib markings, scalp swelling.
  • Pediatric GCS adapted for verbal stage (see Chapter 62).
  • Greenes & Schutzman 1998: 19% of <2-year-olds with SDH/contusion/edema had GCS 15 → mild presentation does not exclude serious AHT.

Imaging

  • Initial: non-contrast head CT for hemorrhage, mass effect, fracture.
  • Subacute: brain MRI with DWI/SWI within 72 h — identifies ischemia, DAI, microhemorrhages, ages collections.
  • Spine MRI — cervical subdural is a strong AHT marker.
  • Rapid MRI protocols may reduce CT use by >90% in screening (Flom 2017).

Other essential tests (Table 63.1)

  • Skeletal survey (now and repeat at 10–14 days).
  • Dilated ophthalmologic exam by pediatric ophthalmology — retinal hemorrhage patterns.
  • ALT/AST screen — values >80 IU/L trigger abdominal imaging (Lindberg).
  • Bleeding workup: CBC, platelets, PT/PTT, INR, fibrinogen, d-dimer, Factor VIII, Factor IX.
  • Additional: glutaric aciduria screen, urine toxicology when clinically appropriate.

Clinical prediction rules

  • PIBIS (Pittsburgh Infant Brain Injury Score): 4 variables — abnormal dermatologic exam, age ≥3 months, head circumference >85th percentile, Hb <11.2 g/dL → ~90% sensitivity for abnormal neuroimaging in well-appearing infants.
  • PediBIRN 4-variable screen: respiratory compromise, bruising of ears/neck/torso, bilateral/interhemispheric subdural, nondiastatic-nonlinear skull fracture → ~90% sensitivity, 50% specificity for AHT diagnosis.
Figure 63.3 (described): Diagnostic algorithm — well-appearing infant with symptom → apply PIBIS screen → high risk → head CT → if positive → AHT workup (skeletal survey, ophthalmology, MRI, labs, social work).
ICU layer: The intensivist often anchors the diagnostic process — do not delegate the integration of history, exam, imaging, and labs entirely to the Child Protection Team.
Differential Diagnosis (pages 3325–3326)
  • Most common mimic: noninflicted TBI (large fall, MVC, sports). Distinguished by consistency of history with injury.
  • Glutaric aciduria type I: macrocephaly, bilateral subdurals, may mimic AHT — screen with amino acids/organic acids.
  • Hemophagocytic lymphohistiocytosis (HLH): bleeding diathesis, cytopenia, hyperferritinemia.
  • Hemorrhagic disease of the newborn: vitamin K deficiency.
  • Arteriovenous malformation/aneurysm: consider when SAH or unusual hematoma morphology.
  • Bleeding disorders: hemophilia, von Willebrand, ITP; consider if persistent/severe coagulopathy beyond what TBI alone explains.
  • Mild PT/PTT prolongation is common after AHT and noninflicted TBI — does not equal bleeding diathesis.
RCPSC angle: Be able to name 4–5 mimics and how you would test for each — examiners will probe.
MANAGEMENT / TREATMENT (pages 3326–3329)
  • Mild/moderate AHT: supportive, prevent secondary insults, complete diagnostic and safeguarding workup.
  • Severe AHT (GCS ≤8): apply BTF pediatric severe TBI guidelines (Kochanek 2019, 3rd ed.).
  • ICP monitoring (EVD preferred when ventricular access feasible).
  • CPP target debated; recent data suggest CPP <45 mm Hg predicts poor outcome in AHT-predominant cohort.
  • PbtO₂ monitoring often technically difficult in infants (unossified skull, bolt fixation issues).
  • Tiered ICP therapy: HOB 30°, midline neck, sedation/analgesia, NMB, mild hyperventilation (rescue only), hyperosmolar therapy (3% saline preferred), barbiturates.
  • Surgical lesions: epidural/subdural evacuation, decompressive craniectomy for refractory ICH or impending herniation.
  • Seizures: ~73% of severe AHT present with seizures (Hasbani 2013); 25% have nonconvulsive status — cEEG essential; load levetiracetam.
  • Nutrition: early enteral once hemodynamics permit; avoid hyperglycemia.
  • Screen for and treat all extracerebral injuries.
ICU layer (high yield):
  • Open fontanelle does NOT prevent intracranial hypertension or herniation once compliance is exhausted.
  • Use age-appropriate CPP targets: ≥40 (infant) → ≥50 (school age) → ≥60 (adolescent); avoid CPP <40 at any age.
  • 3% NaCl 3–5 mL/kg bolus first-line hyperosmolar; mannitol second-line.
  • Levetiracetam preferred to phenytoin in pediatric AHT for tolerability.
  • Cool Kids RCT — therapeutic hypothermia not beneficial; maintain normothermia.
OUTCOMES (page 3329)
  • Standardized outcome assessment: GOS-E Peds (validated pediatric Glasgow Outcome Scale Extended).
  • Shein et al. — initial GCS 3 → 60% mortality; mortality decreases step-wise with rising GCS.
  • At 1 year, >50% of survivors have abnormal development (>2 SD below population mean).
  • Mortality and morbidity worse than noninflicted TBI at matched GCS.
  • Drivers of worse outcome: younger age, delayed presentation, prior maltreatment, concurrent hypoxia-ischemia, developmental immaturity, recurrent abuse.
RCPSC angle: Memorize the outcome disparity — examiners often ask "why is AHT outcome worse than non-AHT TBI at same GCS?" — answer: delay to care, repeated injury, hypoxic-ischemic component, age-related vulnerability.
REPORTING AND LEGAL ISSUES (pages 3330–3331)
  • Mandatory reporting in all US states and Canadian provinces (each with local statute) — physicians protected from civil/criminal liability if good faith.
  • Report should describe medical facts in plain language ("bruise" not "ecchymosis").
  • Indicate severity (life-threatening or not) and confidence ("concern for abuse" vs "diagnostic of abuse").
  • Reports are dynamic — update as new data emerge.
  • Physician's role is NOT to identify the perpetrator.
  • Notify caregivers of the report being made, when safe and appropriate.
  • If other children at home, do not delay reporting to gather more data.
  • Canadian context: each province/territory has its own Child, Youth and Family Services Act — know your jurisdiction.
ICU layer: Documentation chain-of-custody — date, time, photograph injuries (with consent), preserve clothing/objects per institutional policy.
PREVENTION (page 3332)
  • Primary prevention is societal: parental education programs (e.g., "Period of PURPLE Crying"), home visiting, support for at-risk families.
  • Intensivist's role is secondary prevention: recognize, report, prevent re-injury and harm to siblings.
CONCLUSIONS AND FUTURE DIRECTIONS (page 3332)
  • AHT remains a leading cause of mortality and disability from child abuse.
  • Better classification systems incorporating biomarkers and imaging phenotypes are needed for trial enrichment.
  • AHT-specific therapies (anti-edema, anti-neuroinflammatory) under investigation.
  • Inclusion of AHT patients in TBI trials is ethically permissible and scientifically essential.

3 · Equations & Numerical Thresholds

Cerebral perfusion pressure
CPP = MAP − ICP
Age-dependent minimum: ≥40 (infant) → ≥50 (school age) → ≥60 (adolescent). AHT-specific data suggest CPP <45 mm Hg predicts poor outcome.
Hyperosmolar saline bolus
3% NaCl: 3–5 mL/kg IV over 10–20 min (PRN for ICP spikes).
Continuous 3% NaCl: titrate to Na 150–160 mmol/L; ceiling osmolality ≤360 mOsm/kg.
Mannitol
0.25–1 g/kg IV bolus.
Osmolar ceiling ≈ 320 mOsm/kg (above this → AKI risk, rebound ICH).
Levetiracetam loading dose
40–60 mg/kg IV over 15 min, then 20–30 mg/kg q12h maintenance.
Abdominal injury screen (Lindberg)
ALT or AST > 80 IU/L → obtain abdominal CT (validated for occult intra-abdominal injury in abuse).
PediBIRN sensitivity / specificity
Sensitivity ≈ 90%, Specificity ≈ 50%, Negative LR ≈ 0.2 — useful as rule-out but not rule-in alone.

7 · 40 SAQs — Chapter 63 (STUDY MODE — answers visible)

SAQ 1. Define abusive head trauma (AHT) and explain why this term replaced "shaken baby syndrome."

  • AHT = brain injury in an infant or young child resulting from inflicted violence — shaking, blunt impact, hypoxia-ischemia, alone or combined.
  • Term endorsed by AAP 2009 (Christian & Block).
  • Reasons for change: integrates multiple mechanisms; medico-legally neutral as to specific act; less prejudicial in court.

SAQ 2. List four risk factors for AHT.

  • Infant age <2 y, male sex, prematurity, colic.
  • Young or single parents, low maternal education, substance abuse, mental illness.
  • Community stress: deployment, natural disaster, economic crisis, pandemic.
  • Intimate partner violence in the home.

SAQ 3. Why does absence of risk factors NOT exclude AHT?

  • AHT occurs across all sociodemographic strata.
  • Anchoring on demographics → missed cases (Jenny 1999 — 31% missed at first contact).
  • Examination findings and imaging drive diagnosis, not socioeconomics.

SAQ 4. List the three principal mechanisms of injury in AHT and one signature feature of each.

  • Shaking → bridging-vein subdural hemorrhage; retinal shear injury.
  • Blunt impact → skull fracture, contusion, scalp hematoma.
  • Hypoxia-ischemia → "big black brain" diffuse DWI restriction, cervicomedullary apneic injury.

SAQ 5. Why is cervical spine MRI essential in suspected AHT?

  • Cervical subdural hemorrhage occurs in >60% of AHT vs <1% of noninflicted TBI (Choudhary).
  • Supports diagnosis when cranial imaging alone is ambiguous.
  • May explain apnea via cervicomedullary respiratory center injury.

SAQ 6. Describe the retinal hemorrhage pattern characteristic of AHT.

  • Multilayered (pre-retinal, intraretinal, subretinal).
  • Extending to periphery (not just posterior pole).
  • Often bilateral.
  • Retinoschisis (especially macular) highly specific.
  • Document by pediatric ophthalmology with retinal photography within 72 h.

SAQ 7. Why must dilated retinal exam be deferred until after pupillary assessment for ICP?

  • Mydriatics dilate pupils for hours.
  • If used early, lose value of pupillary exam for detecting herniation.
  • Time exam once neurologic status is stable or ICP monitoring in place.
  • However — don't delay so long that hemorrhages resolve (some patterns clear within 72 h).

SAQ 8. List 4 components of the recommended workup for suspected AHT.

  • Brain and spine MRI (DWI/SWI).
  • Skeletal survey + repeat 10–14 days later.
  • Dilated ophthalmologic exam.
  • Labs: CBC, platelets, PT/PTT/INR, fibrinogen, d-dimer, factor VIII/IX, ALT/AST.
  • Consider: urine organic acids, plasma amino acids, urine toxicology.

SAQ 9. Name the PediBIRN 4-variable rule.

  • Respiratory compromise (apnea, intubation on scene).
  • Bruising of ears, neck, or torso.
  • Bilateral or interhemispheric subdural fluid collection/hemorrhage.
  • Non-diastatic, non-linear skull fracture.
  • ≥1 variable in a PICU-admitted child ≤3 y triggers full AHT workup.

SAQ 10. Name the PIBIS variables.

  • Abnormal dermatologic exam (2 pts).
  • Age ≥3 months (1 pt).
  • Head circumference >85th percentile (1 pt).
  • Hb <11.2 g/dL (1 pt).
  • Score ≥2 → screening head CT in a well-appearing infant with vague symptoms.

SAQ 11. List 5 mimics of AHT and one discriminating feature of each.

  • Noninflicted TBI — consistent history with injury severity.
  • Glutaric aciduria type I — macrocephaly, metabolic acidosis, abnormal organic acids.
  • HLH — cytopenia, hyperferritinemia, splenomegaly.
  • Hemorrhagic disease of newborn — vitamin K deficiency, exclusive breastfeeding without K prophylaxis.
  • AVM/aneurysm — vascular imaging shows nidus.
  • Bleeding disorders — persistent or disproportionate coagulopathy, family history.

SAQ 12. Why is mild PT/PTT prolongation common after AHT and NOT diagnostic of bleeding disorder?

  • Acute TBI causes consumptive coagulopathy from tissue factor release.
  • Also dilutional from resuscitation fluids.
  • Bleeding diathesis suggested by persistent, disproportionate, or escalating coagulopathy, or family history.

SAQ 13. What is the BTF 2019 first-tier therapy bundle for severe AHT?

  • HOB 30°, head midline.
  • Sedation/analgesia, neuromuscular blockade.
  • Normocapnia, normothermia, normoxia.
  • Hyperosmolar therapy (3% NaCl preferred; mannitol if hyperemia).
  • CSF drainage via EVD.
  • Optimize CPP per age band.

SAQ 14. What is the BTF 2019 second-tier escalation for refractory ICP?

  • Mild controlled hyperventilation (PaCO₂ 30–35) with PbtO₂ or jugular venous monitoring.
  • Barbiturate coma (pentobarbital) with cEEG burst suppression.
  • Decompressive craniectomy.
  • Avoid hypothermia <35°C as routine (Cool Kids RCT negative).

SAQ 15. Why is mild hyperventilation NOT used prophylactically?

  • Causes cerebral vasoconstriction → reduced CBF → secondary ischemia.
  • Particularly harmful in the first 24 h when CBF is already depressed.
  • Use only as rescue bridge for impending herniation, with monitoring.

SAQ 16. What CPP target supports best outcome in AHT-predominant cohorts?

  • Avoid CPP <45 mm Hg (Allen 2014 — pediatric AHT data).
  • Aim age-stratified: ≥40 infant, ≥50 school age, ≥60 adolescent.
  • Avoid CPP <40 at any age.

SAQ 17. Why is open fontanelle no guarantee against intracranial hypertension?

  • Once compensatory mechanisms (CSF displacement, venous outflow) are exhausted, ICP rises.
  • Pressure-volume curve still steep beyond compensation.
  • Open fontanelle delays — does not prevent — herniation.

SAQ 18. Why is PbtO₂ monitoring technically difficult in young infants with AHT?

  • Commercial probes require cranial fixation bolt/screw.
  • Unossified infant skull (<2 y) cannot reliably hold bolt.
  • Risk of fracture during placement.
  • Alternatives: NIRS-derived measures (still investigational).

SAQ 19. What proportion of severe AHT cases present with seizures?

  • Approximately 70% (Hasbani 2013).
  • 25% of these have nonconvulsive status epilepticus.
  • Justifies continuous EEG monitoring.

SAQ 20. First-line antiseizure prophylaxis after severe AHT.

  • Levetiracetam 40–60 mg/kg load → 20–30 mg/kg q12h.
  • Continue for 7 days post-injury per BTF guidelines.
  • Preferred over phenytoin due to fewer hemodynamic and dermatologic side effects in pediatrics.

SAQ 21. When is reporting to Child Protective Services mandatory?

  • "Reasonable suspicion" — does not require diagnostic certainty.
  • All US states have mandatory reporting laws for physicians.
  • Canada — each province under Child, Youth and Family Services Act equivalent.
  • Good-faith reporting confers legal protection.

SAQ 22. What language conventions should be used in CPS reports?

  • Plain language ("bruise" not "ecchymosis"; "bleeding inside the head" not "intracerebral hemorrhage").
  • State severity (life-threatening or not).
  • State confidence level ("concern for abuse" vs "diagnostic of abuse").
  • Reports are dynamic — update as new findings emerge.

SAQ 23. Is it the physician's role to identify the perpetrator?

  • No. Physician identifies and documents injuries and reports concern.
  • Investigative agencies (CPS, police) identify perpetrator.
  • Avoid attributing acts to specific persons in medical records.

SAQ 24. Should reporting be delayed pending further data?

  • If other children in home → report immediately to protect siblings.
  • If no siblings at risk and diagnosis uncertain → reasonable to gather data over hours-to-days.
  • Better to file a low-confidence report than to delay and risk re-injury.

SAQ 25. Why is AHT outcome worse than noninflicted TBI at matched GCS?

  • Younger age → developmental vulnerability.
  • Delay to medical attention by perpetrator → prolonged secondary injury.
  • Hypoxic-ischemic component.
  • Prior maltreatment / chronic injury.
  • Recurrent abuse risk if undetected.

SAQ 26. Mortality in AHT at GCS 3 vs GCS 9–11?

  • GCS 3 → 60% mortality.
  • GCS 9–11 → 9.5% mortality (similar to severe noninflicted TBI).
  • Source: Shein 2014.

SAQ 27. What is GOS-E Peds?

  • Glasgow Outcome Scale Extended for Pediatrics.
  • Validated for pediatric TBI common data element outcome reporting.
  • Used in pediatric TBI trials including AHT subsets.

SAQ 28. Name the "big black brain" pattern.

  • Multifocal leukoencephalomalacia with extensive diffuse low attenuation on CT, often after AHT.
  • Postulated mechanism: trigeminal-mediated vasodilation, hyperemia, BBB breakdown, neuroinflammation.
  • Carries very poor prognosis.

SAQ 29. Describe the typical SDH morphology in AHT.

  • Bilateral, interhemispheric (along falx), or convexity crescents.
  • Mixed densities suggest different ages of blood → repeated injury.
  • Differs from noninflicted TBI which is more often unilateral and focal.

SAQ 30. What ALT/AST cut-off triggers abdominal imaging in suspected AHT?

  • >80 IU/L (Lindberg 2013).
  • 1/3 of abused children with abdominal injury have no bruising or tenderness.
  • Abdominal CT is investigation of choice.

SAQ 31. Describe rapid MRI protocol for AHT screening.

  • Axial T2 fast spin echo + axial GRE/SWI + coronal T1 IR + axial DWI.
  • Total scan ~5 minutes.
  • Reduces head CT use by >90% in selected infants.
  • Best for stable patients screened for occult AHT.

SAQ 32. What is the trigeminal nerve hypothesis?

  • Sensory afferents in dura and subarachnoid release vasoactive neuropeptides on triggering.
  • Leads to hyperemia, BBB permeability, neuroinflammation.
  • Proposed mechanism for "big black brain" pattern uniquely seen in AHT.

SAQ 33. Should methylprednisolone be given in AHT?

  • No. Steroids not recommended for any TBI (Edwards Lancet 2005 — CRASH steroid trial).
  • AHT included.
  • Steroids worsen mortality in TBI.

SAQ 34. When can TXA be considered in AHT?

  • If presents within 3 h of suspected event AND has TBI with hemorrhage (per CRASH-3 extrapolation).
  • Pediatric AHT data limited — extrapolation only.
  • Avoid if >3 h post-injury (signal of harm).

SAQ 35. List 4 elements of multidisciplinary AHT team.

  • Pediatric intensivist (case lead, ICU management).
  • Pediatric neurosurgery (surgical lesions, ICP monitoring).
  • Pediatric ophthalmology (retinal exam).
  • Pediatric radiology (imaging interpretation).
  • Child protection / safeguarding team (social work, child abuse specialist).
  • Forensic pediatrics, social services, hospital legal counsel.

SAQ 36. What is the "Period of PURPLE Crying" program?

  • Universal parental education program from National Center on Shaken Baby Syndrome.
  • Normalizes infant crying behavior and teaches coping strategies.
  • Cluster RCTs (Barr 2009, Dias 2017) show reduction in AHT hospitalizations.
  • Primary prevention strategy intensivists should advocate locally.

SAQ 37. How is hypoxic-ischemic injury distinguished from primary impact injury in AHT imaging?

  • HII pattern: diffuse cortical and white-matter DWI restriction, watershed pattern, "big black brain."
  • Primary impact: focal contusion, hematoma, fracture overlying.
  • AHT often has BOTH because delay to care or apneic episode contributes.
  • HII pattern portends poor outcome.

SAQ 38. What is the role of biomarkers in AHT diagnosis?

  • Investigational: GFAP, UCH-L1, S100B, NSE.
  • Serum profile in AHT resembles HII more than focal TBI (Berger 2011).
  • Not yet ready for routine clinical use; promising for screening.

SAQ 39. What happens to retinal hemorrhages over time?

  • Superficial retinal hemorrhages may resolve within 72 h.
  • Deeper, subretinal, and macular schisis lesions persist longer (weeks).
  • Therefore: dilated exam should occur within 72 h of presentation.

SAQ 40. What is the most appropriate way to notify caregivers of a CPS report?

  • Notify directly when possible (safety permitting).
  • Brief, respectful, forthright communication.
  • Focus on child's well-being.
  • Include social work or child protection specialist.
  • Don't delay reporting waiting for parents — if not available, report and notify later.

Case 1. A 4-month-old presents with vomiting and lethargy; mother reports the baby "rolled off the couch." HC at 95th centile. CT shows bilateral SDH.

  • History inconsistent with mechanism — couch fall does not cause bilateral SDH; non-mobile infant cannot roll.
  • Stabilize; admit PICU; full AHT workup.
  • Skeletal survey, MRI brain+spine, ophthalmology, labs.
  • Notify CPS — siblings status?
  • Inform parents of CPS report with social work present.

Case 2. 11-month-old with apnea on scene, GCS 5, large right SDH with midline shift.

  • Intubate using neuro-protective induction (etomidate/ketamine + rocuronium).
  • 3% NaCl 5 mL/kg bolus while moving to OR.
  • Emergent neurosurgery for evacuation / craniectomy.
  • Begin AHT workup in parallel; report to CPS.
  • Goal: prevent secondary injury, maintain CPP ≥40 mm Hg in infant.

Case 3. 6-week-old "found unresponsive in crib"; CT: bilateral chronic and acute SDH, retinal hemorrhages.

  • Mixed-age SDH = repeated injury → AHT.
  • Resuscitate; document fully; CPS report.
  • Pediatric ophthalmology for retinal photography.
  • MRI brain+spine; skeletal survey now and at 10–14 d.
  • Siblings: ensure protection.

Case 4. 3-month-old, mother says "started seizing while feeding." GCS 14 on arrival.

  • Differential includes febrile seizure, electrolyte, metabolic, AHT.
  • Apply PIBIS: check dermatology, age, HC, Hb.
  • Head CT if PIBIS ≥2 or any concern.
  • Bilateral SDH if found → AHT pathway.

Case 5. 8-month-old, intubated for severe AHT, ICP 28 on tier-1.

  • Confirm sedation depth; bolus if light.
  • 3% NaCl 3 mL/kg bolus.
  • CSF drainage via EVD if available.
  • NMB trial; recheck after each maneuver.
  • Escalate to tier 2 if persistent: barbiturate coma, controlled HV, decompressive craniectomy.

Case 6. 2-year-old, presented after MVC. GCS 6, isolated severe TBI. No bruising of unusual pattern.

  • Mechanism plausible; treat as noninflicted TBI initially.
  • Still ensure documentation; child protection consult if any concerning features.
  • Full BTF tier 1 management.

Case 7. 5-month-old with macrocephaly, bilateral chronic SDH, mild lethargy.

  • Differential: AHT vs glutaric aciduria type I.
  • Send urine organic acids, plasma amino acids, glutarylcarnitine.
  • Skeletal survey; ophthalmology; MRI.
  • Report to CPS while metabolic workup proceeds — do not delay reporting if siblings.

Case 8. 9-month-old AHT with INR 1.4, PTT 50, mild bruising.

  • Mild coagulopathy common in AHT.
  • Persistent or severe → factor VIII, IX, vWF level.
  • If isolated finding without other AHT markers, also rule out hemophilia.
  • Do not exclude AHT on coag findings alone.

Case 9. 7-month-old severe AHT day 3 — develops Na 156, polyuria 6 mL/kg/h, urine sg 1.003.

  • Central DI from hypothalamic injury.
  • Differentiate from CSW (urine Na, volume status).
  • DDAVP cautiously; replace free water deficit over 48 h.
  • Screen anterior pituitary axes.

Case 10. 10-month-old AHT, on cEEG develops electrographic seizures despite levetiracetam.

  • Add second agent: fosphenytoin or valproate.
  • If status: continuous infusion midazolam.
  • Continuous EEG to confirm seizure cessation.
  • Re-image if new neuro change.

Case 11. 3-week-old, mother reports "ate fine, then suddenly turned blue and limp." HC 75th centile, GCS 11.

  • ALTE / BRUE workup AND AHT screening.
  • PIBIS criteria, head CT.
  • Apnea history raises concern for cervicomedullary injury.
  • MRI brain+spine within 72 h.

Case 12. 2-month-old, frenulum tear, irritability, no history of trauma.

  • Frenulum tear in pre-mobile infant is highly suspicious for inflicted injury.
  • Full physical exam, photo documentation, CT head.
  • Skeletal survey, ophthalmology, MRI.
  • CPS report.

Case 13. 9-month-old AHT — refractory ICP day 2, family asks about decompressive craniectomy.

  • Discuss benefits (ICP control, mortality reduction) and risks (survival with severe disability).
  • Pediatric data (Taylor 2001) supportive in refractory cases.
  • Multidisciplinary family meeting with neurosurgery, palliative care.

Case 14. 4-month-old AHT day 5, parents asking why CPS still investigating.

  • Explain that the CPS investigation is independent of medical care.
  • Reaffirm child's medical needs are the priority.
  • Maintain professional, respectful tone.
  • Refer to social work for parental support.

Case 15. 6-month-old severe AHT, posterior rib fractures on skeletal survey.

  • Posterior rib fractures in pre-ambulatory infant are highly specific for inflicted injury (squeezing thorax).
  • Strong evidence for AHT diagnosis.
  • Document; CPS notified; pediatric orthopedic consult.

Case 16. 11-month-old AHT — repeat skeletal survey at 14 days shows new metaphyseal corner fractures.

  • "Bucket-handle" / metaphyseal fractures classic for inflicted injury (twisting).
  • Healing fractures of different ages indicate repeated abuse.
  • Strengthen CPS report with new findings.

Case 17. 3-month-old AHT, ALT 95, AST 110, no abdominal bruising.

  • Cut-off >80 met → obtain abdominal CT.
  • 1/3 of abused children with abdominal injury have no bruising/tenderness.
  • Liver laceration grades 1–2 may not require intervention but support AHT diagnosis.

Case 18. 5-month-old AHT, "big black brain" pattern on MRI.

  • Multifocal leukoencephalomalacia → extremely poor prognosis.
  • Continue full ICU care; do not prognosticate prematurely.
  • Integrate palliative care.
  • Communicate with family with uncertainty acknowledged.

Case 19. 8-month-old AHT, GCS 11, hemodynamically stable.

  • "Moderate" GCS 9–11 carries 9.5% mortality — admit PICU.
  • Serial neuro exams; cEEG; consider ICP monitoring if exam deteriorates.
  • Full AHT workup proceeds.

Case 20. 14-month-old AHT, ophthalmology shows few unilateral preretinal hemorrhages.

  • Pattern less specific than bilateral multilayered hemorrhages.
  • Combine with other workup findings.
  • Re-photograph if pattern evolves.

Case 21. 8-month-old AHT, fever 38.8°C day 2, no obvious source.

  • Septic workup (cultures); cover empirically only if unwell.
  • Aggressive normothermia (fever worsens secondary injury).
  • Consider central fever once infection excluded.

Case 22. 4-month-old AHT, parents have other children at home.

  • Report immediately — do not delay.
  • CPS will assess household safety.
  • Document concerns about siblings.

Case 23. 3-week-old AHT, family declines further workup ("we want to take her home").

  • Explain medical necessity; involve social work, child protection team.
  • Physician's role is to advocate for child — emergency safeguarding orders if needed.
  • CPS report regardless of family wishes.
  • Do not discharge against medical advice in suspected AHT.

Case 24. 10-month-old AHT day 6 — preparing for extubation.

  • Adequate GCS-M, gag/cough, off vasopressors, normocapnia.
  • Spontaneous breathing trial.
  • Plan discharge destination — likely foster care or extended family per CPS.

Case 25. 7-month-old AHT, MRI also shows old healing parenchymal contusions.

  • Chronic findings = prior episode of abuse.
  • Strong evidence for ongoing maltreatment.
  • Document timeline of injuries with radiology assistance.

Case 26. 9-month-old AHT — develops Na 126 with weight gain, low urine output.

  • SIADH.
  • Fluid restriction with isotonic maintenance.
  • Slow correction.
  • Hypertonic saline only if seizing or symptomatic.

Case 27. 12-month-old AHT, brain death exam being considered.

  • Per pediatric brain death determination protocol (Nakagawa 2011, updated).
  • Two independent exams + apnea test.
  • Ancillary studies if confounded (sedation, hemodynamic instability).
  • Discuss with family and organ donation organization at appropriate time.

Case 28. 6-month-old AHT, recovering — discharge planning.

  • CPS placement decision (foster, kinship, return-home pending investigation).
  • Rehab consult (developmental delays expected in >50% of survivors).
  • Ophthalmology, neurology, neurosurgery follow-up.
  • Long-term developmental pediatric follow-up.

Case 29. 2-year-old AHT — family medical history reveals hemophilia A.

  • Test factor VIII level; involve hematology.
  • Hemophilia can produce spontaneous SDH but typically NOT bilateral or interhemispheric pattern.
  • Integrate other findings; do not exclude AHT on family history alone.

Case 30. 5-month-old "found seizing"; CT normal.

  • Normal CT does not exclude AHT.
  • Consider MRI within 72 h (rapid protocol).
  • Continue safeguarding workup if clinical or social concerns.

Case 31. 7-month-old AHT, on cEEG — burst suppression target for barbiturate coma.

  • Pentobarbital infusion titrated to burst suppression with bursts every 5–10 seconds.
  • Vasopressor support anticipated.
  • Monitor levels, watch for ileus, infections, hypothermia.

Case 32. 9-month-old AHT — coags improve but persistent thrombocytopenia.

  • Rule out HLH (ferritin, fibrinogen, triglycerides, marrow if needed).
  • Rule out ITP, sepsis, marrow suppression.
  • Continue AHT diagnostic pathway.

Case 33. 3-month-old AHT — caregivers refusing to leave child's bedside, demanding records.

  • Maintain professional, respectful boundaries.
  • Involve hospital legal and social work.
  • Do not provide medical records to caregivers without institutional protocol.
  • Notify CPS of behavior if escalating.

Case 34. 10-month-old AHT, intubated, develops VAP day 5.

  • Empiric broad-spectrum antibiotics per local antibiogram.
  • Optimize ventilator bundle (HOB 30°, oral care, cuff pressure).
  • Do not allow VAP to delay extubation planning.

Case 35. 5-month-old AHT, urine toxicology positive for methamphetamine.

  • Document; report to CPS.
  • Consider toxic exposure as part of injury picture.
  • Refer parents/caregivers to substance use services.

Case 36. 8-month-old AHT, mortality discussion with family.

  • Integrate clinical exam (off sedation), MRI, EEG, ICP trajectory.
  • Avoid early definitive prognostication; reassess at 72 h–7 d.
  • Engage palliative care and chaplaincy.
  • Maintain CPS involvement and documentation.

Case 37. 4-month-old AHT, perpetrator (father) has been arrested. Mother is sole caregiver and is supportive.

  • Continue medical care; mother remains decision-maker unless CPS removes custody.
  • Engage mother in care plan; provide emotional support.
  • Coordinate discharge to safe environment with CPS.

Case 38. 11-month-old AHT in coma 7 days. Repeat MRI shows progression of leukoencephalomalacia.

  • Worsening imaging consistent with evolving secondary injury.
  • Discuss goals of care with family.
  • Consider tracheostomy/PEG vs withdrawal of life support per family/team consensus.
  • Palliative care integration.

Case 39. 2-month-old AHT, planning for organ donation after determination of death by neurologic criteria.

  • Confirm brain death determination per pediatric protocol (two exams + apnea test).
  • Engage Organ Donation Organization (OPO/OPDP).
  • Family consent; cultural and religious considerations.
  • CPS investigation continues; coordinate with forensic medicine.

Case 40. 5-month-old AHT — survives; pre-discharge multidisciplinary meeting.

  • Members: PICU, neurology, ophthalmology, social work, CPS, rehab, primary care, foster placement worker.
  • Plan: medications (lev x weeks-months), follow-up imaging, developmental monitoring.
  • CPS placement: documented safe destination.
  • Long-term: GOS-E Peds at 6 m, neuropsych at 1 y, school readiness assessment.

11 · One-Page Infographic — Chapter 63

Pathophysiology Flow

  • Shaking + blunt impact + hypoxia-ischemia → primary axonal/vascular/retinal injury.
  • Apnea / delayed presentation → secondary HIE.
  • Bridging vein rupture → bilateral / interhemispheric SDH.
  • Retinal shear → multilayered retinal hemorrhages + retinoschisis.
  • Cervicomedullary injury → respiratory center failure.

Diagnostic Algorithm

  • Suspicious infant → stabilize → head CT → if positive: skeletal survey, MRI brain+spine, ophthalmology, labs, social work.
  • Apply PediBIRN (admitted) / PIBIS (well-appearing) screens.
  • Repeat skeletal survey at 10–14 d.
  • ALT/AST >80 → abdominal CT.

ICU Management Steps

  • GCS ≤8: intubate, ICP monitor, BTF tier 1.
  • Hyperosmolar 3% NaCl (first), mannitol (second).
  • Lev for seizure prophylaxis.
  • cEEG mandatory.
  • Normothermia (NOT hypothermia — Cool Kids negative).
  • Tier 2: barbiturate coma, decompressive craniectomy, controlled HV.

Critical Thresholds

  • ICP >20 → tier 1.
  • CPP minimum: 40 (infant), 50 (school age), 60 (adolescent).
  • Na target: 150–160; osmolality ≤360.
  • ALT/AST: >80 IU/L = imaging trigger.
  • Hb: <11.2 → PIBIS point.

Red Flags

  • Bilateral/interhemispheric SDH in <1 y.
  • Multilayered retinal hemorrhages.
  • History inconsistent with injury or developmental stage.
  • Bruising in non-mobile infant.
  • Posterior rib or metaphyseal fractures.
  • Mixed-density SDH (different ages).
  • Frenulum tear in infant.

Exam Pearls

  • Suspicion (not certainty) triggers mandatory report.
  • You are NOT identifying the perpetrator — that's CPS/police.
  • Notify caregivers of report when safe.
  • Do NOT delay reporting if siblings at home.
  • AHT outcome worse than noninflicted at matched GCS.
  • Open fontanelle does NOT prevent herniation.
  • Steroids: never in TBI/AHT (CRASH steroid).
  • Therapeutic hypothermia: not routine (Cool Kids negative).
