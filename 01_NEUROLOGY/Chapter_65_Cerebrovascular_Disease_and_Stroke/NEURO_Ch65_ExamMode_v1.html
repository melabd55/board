Block A — Mock RCPSC SAQ (10 questions, 12 minutes)

A1. An 8-year-old presents with sudden right hemiparesis and aphasia 90 minutes ago. Outline a pediatric Code Stroke pathway with target times.

Show answer
  • 0–15 min: ABCs, focused neuro exam, PedNIHSS scoring, activate Code Stroke, bedside glucose, IV access, labs (CBC, coags, ferritin, hypercoag panel).
  • 15–30 min: rapid MRI (DWI/FLAIR/GRE/MRA) within 30 min door-to-imaging; CT/CTA if MRI unavailable.
  • 30–60 min: type-specific intervention — AIS: ASA 3–5 mg/kg/day; consider tPA case-by-case <4.5 h; thrombectomy <6–24 h for LVO.
  • Multidisciplinary stroke team (neurology, neurosurgery, hematology, ICU, radiology).
  • PICU admission with full neurologic monitoring.

A2. List the five major etiologic categories of pediatric arterial ischemic stroke and one feature of each.

Show answer
  • Focal cerebral arteriopathy (~25%) — unilateral, monophasic; post-varicella classic.
  • Cardioembolic (~25–30%) — CHD, post-Fontan, mechanical valve, endocarditis, PFO.
  • Sickle cell disease (~10–15%) — large-artery vasculopathy with progressive stenosis.
  • Dissection (~10–20%) — extracranial carotid or vertebral; trauma or connective tissue disease.
  • Prothrombotic / vasculitis / cryptogenic — hypercoag panel essential.

A3. A 10-year-old with HbSS presents with acute left hemiparesis 2 hours ago. What is your immediate management?

Show answer
  • Emergent exchange transfusion to HbS <30% within hours.
  • If exchange unavailable: simple transfusion to Hb 10 g/dL (avoid hyperviscosity above this).
  • Avoid IV tPA — increased bleeding risk in SCD.
  • Standard stroke supportive care: normothermia, normoglycemia, permissive HTN.
  • Plan chronic transfusion for secondary prevention (SWiTCH 2012 — HU inferior to transfusion for secondary prevention).
  • HSCT discussion with hematology.

A4. Explain the STOP and TWiTCH trials and their clinical implications.

Show answer
  • STOP (Adams NEJM 1998): RCT in HbSS children 2–16 y with TCD >200 cm/s; chronic transfusion reduced first stroke risk by >90% vs observation.
  • Established standard of care: annual TCD screening 2–16 y; if >200 cm/s → chronic transfusion.
  • TWiTCH (Ware Lancet 2016): after stabilization on transfusion with normal TCD, hydroxyurea non-inferior for maintaining low velocity.
  • Allows transition from transfusion to HU in stable primary-prevention patients.
  • SWiTCH (Ware Blood 2012) — opposite finding for secondary prevention: HU inferior after stroke; continue transfusion.

A5. A 12-year-old with mastoiditis develops headache, seizures, and altered mental status. MRV shows transverse sinus thrombosis with venous infarction containing petechial hemorrhage. Is anticoagulation safe?

Show answer
  • Yes — anticoagulation is indicated even with hemorrhagic component.
  • deVeber NEJM 2001 — landmark Canadian study; outcomes worse without treatment.
  • Start LMWH (enoxaparin 1 mg/kg q12h) or UFH; aim anti-Xa 0.5–1.0.
  • Treat underlying cause: IV antibiotics for mastoiditis, ENT consult for surgical drainage.
  • Anticoagulation duration 3–6 months; repeat imaging at 3 months.
  • Treat seizures per standard tier.

A6. A 7-year-old post-transplant on tacrolimus develops headache, seizures, BP 180/110. MRI shows bilateral parieto-occipital FLAIR hyperintensity without DWI restriction. Diagnosis and management?

Show answer
  • Posterior reversible encephalopathy syndrome (PRES / RPLS).
  • Typical triggers: hypertension, calcineurin inhibitors (cyclosporine, tacrolimus), eclampsia, sepsis, renal failure.
  • Imaging: bilateral parieto-occipital vasogenic edema — FLAIR ↑, no DWI restriction (distinguishing from AIS).
  • Management: control BP with labetalol or nicardipine (target gradual reduction).
  • Hold or reduce calcineurin inhibitor; consult transplant team.
  • Treat seizures (levetiracetam load).
  • Usually reversible if treated promptly.

A7. A 6-year-old with NF1 has had three TIAs in three months. MRA shows bilateral distal ICA narrowing with collaterals. Discuss diagnosis and management.

Show answer
  • Moyamoya syndrome (NF1 is a known cause; moyamoya disease refers to primary form).
  • Bilateral distal ICA stenosis with "puff of smoke" collaterals.
  • Suzuki staging I–VI.
  • Treatment: surgical revascularization referral.
  • Indirect: EDAS (encephaloduroarteriosynangiosis), pial synangiosis.
  • Direct: STA-MCA bypass.
  • Aspirin 3–5 mg/kg/day pre- and post-op.
  • Avoid hyperventilation, hypotension, dehydration — all reduce CBF.

A8. A 3-year-old develops sudden ataxia after minor neck trauma. Suspect what, and how do you investigate and treat?

Show answer
  • Vertebral artery dissection — atlantoaxial region most common.
  • Imaging: MRI brain + MRA neck + T1 fat-sat (look for crescent of intramural hematoma).
  • CT/CTA as alternative.
  • Extracranial dissection: anticoagulation 3–6 months (LMWH or UFH).
  • Intracranial dissection: antiplatelet preferred (rupture risk with anticoagulation).
  • Workup for connective tissue disease: Ehlers-Danlos (vascular type), Marfan, fibromuscular dysplasia.
  • Follow-up imaging at 3–6 months.

A9. An 11-year-old with massive left MCA infarction has GCS 9, midline shift 7 mm, and worsening exam over 6 hours. What is the next step?

Show answer
  • Malignant MCA syndrome with mass effect.
  • Intubate using neuro-protective induction.
  • HOB 30°, midline neck, sedation.
  • Hyperosmolar therapy: 3% NaCl 3–5 mL/kg bolus.
  • Emergent decompressive craniectomy within 48 h — DECIMAL/DESTINY/HAMLET pooled analysis (Vahedi 2007).
  • Pediatric outcomes generally better than adults due to neuroplasticity.
  • Cranioplasty at 6–12 weeks.

A10. A 7-year-old presents with cryptogenic AIS. Outline an essential workup beyond standard imaging.

Show answer
  • Echocardiogram with bubble study (PFO, ASD, mural thrombus); TEE if TTE non-diagnostic.
  • Hypercoagulability panel — proteins C and S, antithrombin, factor V Leiden, prothrombin G20210A, antiphospholipid antibodies, homocysteine.
  • Ferritin — iron deficiency anemia is an independent risk factor.
  • Vasculitis: ESR, CRP, ANA, ANCA, complement.
  • Drug screen.
  • Genetic testing for Fabry, NF1, CADASIL if clinically suggestive.
  • Lumbar puncture for cPACNS (CSF pleocytosis, protein).
  • MRA neck for dissection; consider vessel wall MRI.

Block B — Mock RCPSC Oral Cases (5 sequential, 40 minutes)

B1. A 9-year-old with HbSS comes to the ED with sudden onset right hemiparesis and slurred speech 90 minutes ago.

B1a — Immediate management.
  • ABCs, IV access, glucose check, vitals.
  • Activate Code Stroke; involve hematology and neurology immediately.
  • Send CBC with reticulocyte count, type and crossmatch, baseline HbS level.
  • Rapid MRI (DWI/FLAIR/GRE/MRA) within 30 min.
  • Begin preparation for exchange transfusion target HbS <30%.
  • Avoid IV tPA — increased bleeding risk in SCD.
  • Standard supportive: normothermia, normoglycemia, SpO₂ >94%, permissive HTN.
B1b — MRI confirms acute left M2 MCA infarct. Discuss exchange transfusion.
  • Exchange transfusion to HbS <30% urgently (within hours).
  • If exchange unavailable: simple transfusion to Hb 10 g/dL (avoid hyperviscosity above 10).
  • Provide HbS-negative, leukoreduced, antigen-matched (E, C, Kell) packed red cells.
  • Monitor for transfusion reactions, fluid overload.
  • Reassess HbS post-transfusion.
B1c — Plan secondary prevention.
  • Chronic transfusion regimen indefinitely — target HbS <30%.
  • Per SWiTCH (2012) — hydroxyurea alone inferior for secondary prevention.
  • Monitor for iron overload (ferritin, transferrin saturation); chelation when indicated (deferasirox, deferoxamine).
  • Consider hematopoietic stem cell transplant (HSCT) — curative option in selected patients.
  • Annual neurologic follow-up; periodic MRI surveillance.
B1d — Family asks about prevention for other SCD children at home.
  • Annual TCD screening 2–16 y in HbSS / HbSβ⁰.
  • If TCD >200 cm/s → chronic transfusion (STOP, NEJM 1998).
  • After stabilization with normal TCD: can transition to hydroxyurea per TWiTCH (Lancet 2016).
  • Adherence to hydroxyurea, hydration, infection prevention (penicillin prophylaxis, immunizations).
  • MRI surveillance for silent infarcts; if found, chronic transfusion (SIT, NEJM 2014).
B1e — Long-term outcome and rehabilitation.
  • Recurrence risk reduced significantly with transfusion regimen.
  • Expected sequelae: hemiparesis, cognitive impairment, school difficulties.
  • Pediatric PT/OT/SLP; constraint-induced movement therapy.
  • Neuropsychology assessment; school support plan (IEP).
  • Pediatric Stroke Outcome Measure (PSOM) for tracking.
  • Multidisciplinary follow-up: hematology, neurology, stroke clinic, rehab.

B2. A 5-year-old presents 2 weeks after varicella with abrupt right hemiparesis. CT is negative.

B2a — Working diagnosis and next imaging.
  • Post-varicella focal cerebral arteriopathy (FCA) is the leading consideration.
  • CT often negative early — proceed to rapid MRI with DWI/FLAIR/GRE/MRA.
  • Expected pattern: unilateral basal ganglia / lentiform infarct + ipsilateral M1/distal ICA narrowing.
  • MRA neck if extracranial dissection possible.
B2b — MRI confirms left basal ganglia infarct with M1 stenosis. Treatment plan?
  • Aspirin 3–5 mg/kg/day for ≥12 months.
  • Standard supportive care; treat seizures if they occur.
  • Acyclovir if active varicella or CSF VZV PCR positive.
  • Steroids generally NOT used (no proven benefit in FCA).
  • Multidisciplinary follow-up.
B2c — Family asks about recurrence and long-term plan.
  • FCA is often monophasic — most cases improve over 3–6 months.
  • Repeat MRA at 3–6 months to track stenosis evolution.
  • Recurrence risk highest in first 6–12 months — continue ASA.
  • Steinlin Stroke 2017 — progressive form versus transient form impacts long-term plan.
  • Long-term rehab; school support; neuropsych assessment.
B2d — At 6 months, MRA shows progression. What does that change?
  • Reclassify as progressive FCA (versus transient) — overlaps with chronic vasculopathy.
  • Continue ASA; consider extended duration or switch to clopidogrel if recurrence.
  • Consider conventional angiography to characterize fully.
  • Rule out evolving moyamoya — if bilateral progression, surgical revascularization referral.
  • Vasculitis workup if not previously done (LP, ANA, ANCA, complement).

B3. A 14-year-old known nephrotic syndrome relapse presents with severe headache, vomiting, and papilledema for 3 days.

B3a — Diagnosis and imaging.
  • Cerebral sinovenous thrombosis (CSVT) — nephrotic syndrome causes hypercoagulability through urinary loss of antithrombin III and protein S.
  • MRV (preferred) or CTV.
  • Look for absence of flow void or filling defect in sinuses (transverse, sigmoid, sagittal).
  • MRI also for venous infarct or hemorrhage.
B3b — MRV confirms superior sagittal and bilateral transverse sinus thrombosis with small left parietal venous infarct (no hemorrhage). Treatment?
  • Anticoagulation: LMWH (enoxaparin 1 mg/kg q12h SC) or UFH; anti-Xa target 0.5–1.0 IU/mL.
  • Duration 3–6 months.
  • Treat underlying nephrotic syndrome: steroids, immunosuppression; address proteinuria.
  • Hydration; antihypertensives if indicated.
  • Seizure prophylaxis only if seizures occur (not routine).
  • Repeat MRV at 3 months to assess recanalization.
B3c — Day 2 patient develops new focal seizure and worsening headache.
  • Repeat imaging — concern for evolving venous infarct, hemorrhage, or extension of thrombosis.
  • Treat seizure per algorithm (BZD then levetiracetam).
  • Reassess anticoagulation — continue unless major bleeding identified.
  • cEEG; ICP assessment if increased ICP suspected.
  • Neurosurgery consult if mass effect or hemorrhage requiring intervention.
B3d — Discharge planning at day 10.
  • Continue LMWH or transition to warfarin (INR 2–3) per hematology.
  • Duration 3–6 months; reassess based on resolution.
  • Follow-up MRV at 3 months.
  • Nephrology follow-up for ongoing management of nephrotic syndrome.
  • Counsel on hydration, signs of recurrence (headache, vision changes, weakness).
  • Hypercoagulability panel after anticoagulation completed.

B4. A 13-year-old presents with thunderclap headache, brief loss of consciousness, and now nuchal rigidity. CT shows diffuse SAH.

B4a — Initial management.
  • ABCs, airway protection if GCS depressed.
  • Activate neurosurgery; CTA to identify aneurysm or AVM.
  • BP control: target SBP <160 mm Hg with short-acting agents (labetalol, nicardipine).
  • Reverse any coagulopathy; type and crossmatch.
  • Avoid extremes of BP — also avoid hypotension which worsens ischemia.
  • Nimodipine 60 mg PO q4h (or weight-based equivalent) for vasospasm prophylaxis.
B4b — CTA reveals a 7 mm anterior communicating artery aneurysm. Treatment options?
  • Multidisciplinary decision: endovascular coiling or surgical clipping.
  • Coiling generally preferred when feasible (ISAT trial in adults).
  • Consider WEB device, stent-assisted coiling, or flow diversion based on neck and morphology.
  • Pediatric aneurysms are rare; experienced center essential.
  • Pre-procedural imaging optimization; baseline neuro exam.
B4c — Day 5 patient develops new focal deficit (left hemiparesis). Approach?
  • Suspect delayed cerebral ischemia from vasospasm (peak risk day 4–14 post-SAH).
  • Continue nimodipine.
  • Permissive hypertension (allow HTN to maintain CPP); ensure euvolemia.
  • Transcranial Doppler to detect vasospasm; CTA for confirmation.
  • Repeat CT to exclude rebleed.
  • Intra-arterial vasodilator therapy or angioplasty if confirmed and severe.
B4d — Long-term follow-up.
  • Surveillance imaging at 3 and 12 months, then annually.
  • Counsel on family history — screen first-degree relatives if familial pattern suggests.
  • Rehab as needed for any deficit.
  • Neuropsych assessment.
  • Hypertension control; smoking cessation counseling (adolescent).

B5. A 4-year-old with sudden hemiplegia 6 hours ago. MRI shows complete left M1 MCA occlusion with extensive but salvageable penumbra on perfusion imaging.

B5a — Discussion of treatment options.
  • Mechanical thrombectomy is feasible in selected pediatric LVO cases.
  • Time window 6–24 h with mismatch imaging supports salvageable tissue.
  • Multidisciplinary discussion: pediatric stroke neurology, interventional neuroradiology, neurosurgery, ICU.
  • Family discussion: risks (vessel injury, hemorrhage), benefits (potential recovery).
  • Refer to experienced center if local resources insufficient.
  • IV tPA case-by-case if <4.5 h (this case is at 6 h — tPA window closed).
B5b — Post-thrombectomy with TICI 2b reperfusion. Post-procedure care?
  • PICU admission; serial neuro exams (PedNIHSS).
  • BP control: post-reperfusion target SBP <160 mm Hg to reduce hemorrhagic conversion.
  • Aspirin 3–5 mg/kg/day starting 24 h post-procedure (delayed if hemorrhagic conversion).
  • Femoral access site monitoring.
  • Repeat MRI at 24 h to assess infarct extent.
  • Etiology workup: echo with bubble, hypercoag panel, vessel wall imaging.
B5c — Day 2 develops focal seizure on cEEG. Manage.
  • Treat seizure per algorithm: lorazepam 0.1 mg/kg IV.
  • Load levetiracetam 40–60 mg/kg IV.
  • Continue cEEG ≥24 h.
  • Repeat imaging if seizure persists or new deficit emerges.
  • Address electrolytes, fever, glucose.
B5d — Long-term plan and family discussion.
  • Discuss expected recovery — pediatric outcomes generally better than adults due to neuroplasticity.
  • Rehabilitation: PT, OT, SLP, constraint-induced movement therapy.
  • Continue ASA per etiology guidelines.
  • Cardiac source workup conclusion drives anticoagulation decision.
  • Annual MRI/MRA if vasculopathy.
  • Neuropsychology assessment; school support plan.
  • Pediatric Stroke Outcome Measure (PSOM) at intervals.

Block C — Randomized SAQ Drill (30 questions)

C1. WHO definition of stroke.

Show answer
  • Rapidly developing focal (or global) cerebral dysfunction lasting ≥24 h or causing death, with no apparent cause other than vascular origin.

C2. List 4 differential diagnoses for acute focal deficit in a child.

Show answer
  • AIS, HS, CSVT; hemiplegic migraine; Todd's paralysis; focal seizure; ADEM; tumor; PRES; MELAS; functional disorder.

C3. Pediatric stroke mortality and recurrence rates.

Show answer
  • Mortality ~20%; recurrence ~20%; disability 50–80%.
  • Among top 10 causes of childhood death.

C4. DWI/ADC pattern of hyperacute infarction.

Show answer
  • DWI bright (high signal); ADC dark (low signal).
  • Positive within minutes; pseudonormalizes after 7–10 days.

C5. Moyamoya angiographic features.

Show answer
  • Progressive bilateral distal ICA / proximal MCA/ACA stenosis.
  • "Puff of smoke" collateral plexus.
  • Suzuki staging I–VI.

C6. Moyamoya secondary causes.

Show answer
  • NF1, sickle cell, Down syndrome, post-radiation, autoimmune disease.
  • Primary form: RNF213 mutation, East Asian predominance.

C7. SCD TCD velocity thresholds.

Show answer
  • Normal: <170 cm/s.
  • Conditional: 170–199 cm/s.
  • Abnormal: ≥200 cm/s → chronic transfusion.

C8. SWiTCH vs TWiTCH.

Show answer
  • SWiTCH (2012): HU inferior for secondary prevention.
  • TWiTCH (2016): HU non-inferior for primary prevention after stable TCD.

C9. SCD acute stroke management.

Show answer
  • Emergent exchange transfusion to HbS <30%.
  • Simple transfusion to Hb 10 g/dL if exchange unavailable.
  • Avoid tPA.

C10. tPA in pediatric AIS — current status.

Show answer
  • No RCT data (TIPS feasibility closed early).
  • Case-by-case in older children >2 y, within 4.5 h, large-vessel AIS, PedNIHSS ≥4.
  • Dose 0.9 mg/kg (max 90 mg).
  • Multidisciplinary decision required.

C11. Default antiplatelet for pediatric AIS.

Show answer
  • Aspirin 3–5 mg/kg/day PO.
  • Duration ≥12 months for FCA; longer for chronic vasculopathy.

C12. Anticoagulation indications in pediatric stroke.

Show answer
  • CSVT, extracranial dissection, cardioembolic (mechanical valve, mural thrombus, cardiomyopathy), prothrombotic state.
  • LMWH (1 mg/kg q12h) or UFH; anti-Xa 0.5–1.0.

C13. CSVT treatment with hemorrhagic infarct.

Show answer
  • Still anticoagulate (deVeber NEJM 2001).
  • Outcomes worse without treatment.
  • Duration 3–6 months; repeat MRV at 3 months.

C14. Decompressive craniectomy indication and timing.

Show answer
  • Malignant MCA infarction with mass effect.
  • Within 48 h (DECIMAL/DESTINY/HAMLET pooled analysis).
  • Pediatric outcomes better than adults.

C15. BP target in acute AIS.

Show answer
  • Permissive HTN.
  • Treat only if SBP >220 or DBP >120 (or per protocol).
  • Avoid hypotension.

C16. BP target in acute ICH/SAH.

Show answer
  • SBP <160 mm Hg.
  • Use labetalol or nicardipine.
  • Avoid hypotension.

C17. PRES — imaging pattern.

Show answer
  • Bilateral parieto-occipital vasogenic edema.
  • FLAIR hyperintensity; NO DWI restriction.
  • Distinguishes from AIS.

C18. PRES triggers.

Show answer
  • Hypertension; calcineurin inhibitors (cyclosporine, tacrolimus); eclampsia; sepsis; renal failure.

C19. Dissection treatment by location.

Show answer
  • Extracranial: anticoagulation 3–6 months.
  • Intracranial: antiplatelet (rupture risk).

C20. Most common cause of pediatric spontaneous ICH.

Show answer
  • Arteriovenous malformation (AVM).
  • Diagnosed on CTA/MRA/DSA.
  • Treatment: surgery, embolization, SRS per Spetzler-Martin grade.

C21. PedNIHSS purpose.

Show answer
  • Pediatric NIH Stroke Scale; 15 items, 0–42.
  • PedNIHSS ≥4 typically threshold for thrombolysis/thrombectomy consideration.

C22. Iron deficiency anemia and stroke.

Show answer
  • Independent risk factor for AIS and CSVT in children.
  • Mechanisms: thrombocytosis, hyperviscosity, endothelial dysfunction.
  • Always check ferritin in cryptogenic stroke.

C23. Prothrombotic disorders associated with pediatric stroke.

Show answer
  • Protein C/S, antithrombin deficiency.
  • Factor V Leiden, prothrombin G20210A.
  • Antiphospholipid syndrome.
  • Hyperhomocysteinemia, MTHFR variants.

C24. Cryptogenic stroke workup essentials.

Show answer
  • Echo with bubble study (PFO, ASD).
  • Hypercoag panel.
  • Ferritin.
  • Vasculitis panel.
  • Genetic (Fabry, NF1, CADASIL).
  • LP for cPACNS.

C25. FCA classic example.

Show answer
  • Post-varicella angiopathy.
  • Unilateral distal ICA / proximal MCA narrowing with basal ganglia infarct.
  • Often transient — improves over 3–6 months.

C26. FCA treatment.

Show answer
  • ASA 3–5 mg/kg/day for ≥12 months.
  • Acyclovir if active VZV.
  • Repeat MRA at 3–6 months.

C27. RCVS features.

Show answer
  • Thunderclap headache.
  • Reversible segmental cerebral vasoconstriction — "string of beads" on MRA.
  • Triggers: sympathomimetics, postpartum, drug exposure.
  • Treatment: nimodipine, supportive.

C28. Neonatal stroke key features.

Show answer
  • Incidence ~1/1600–1/4000 live births.
  • Often presents with focal seizures 24–72 h after birth.
  • Left MCA territory classic.
  • MRI DWI for diagnosis.
  • Generally no anticoagulation or tPA in neonatal AIS unless specific indication.

C29. LMWH dosing in children.

Show answer
  • Enoxaparin 1 mg/kg q12h SC.
  • 1.5 mg/kg q12h in <2 mo.
  • Anti-Xa target 0.5–1.0 IU/mL (peak 4 h post-dose).

C30. Why hyperventilation harmful in moyamoya.

Show answer
  • Lowers PaCO₂ → cerebral vasoconstriction → reduced CBF.
  • Moyamoya has limited cerebral reserve.
  • Can precipitate TIA or stroke.
  • Also avoid hypotension and dehydration.

  • Annual MRA.

D24. 15-yo cocaine use, severe headache.

Show plan
  • Differential: ICH, SAH, RCVS, AIS.
  • CT + CTA/MRA.
  • BP control; drug counseling.

Show plan
  • Differential: prothrombotic, CHD, sepsis, AHT, metabolic.

  • AHT screening.

Show plan

  • Target HbS <30%.
  • After 1 y stable normal TCD: consider HU per TWiTCH.

D27. 14-yo on OCP with headache + papilledema.

Show plan
  • CSVT — MRV.
  • Stop OCPs.
  • Anticoagulation 3–6 mo.
  • Hypercoag workup.

Show plan
  • Traumatic dissection.
  • CTA neck or T1 fat-sat MRI.

Show plan
  • Septic/inflammatory arteriopathy.
  • Continue antibiotics; MRI + MRA + MRV.
  • ASA; steroids if vasculitis pattern.

Show plan
  • Switch to anticoagulation (warfarin or DOAC).
  • Hematology + rheumatology consult.
  • Confirm lupus anticoagulant, anti-β2GPI, anticardiolipin.

Show plan

D32. 13-yo MIS-C with hemiparesis.

Show plan
  • COVID-19/MIS-C hypercoagulability.
  • MRI + MRA + MRV.
  • Anticoagulation per pediatric MIS-C protocols.
  • IVIG, steroids.

D33. 3-yo cerebellar AIS.

Show plan
  • Vertebral artery dissection or cardioembolic.
  • MRA neck mandatory.
  • Watch for posterior fossa edema → fourth ventricle compression → emergent decompression.

D34. 9-yo AIS day 5 with worsening hemiparesis.

Show plan
  • Repeat MRI for extension, hemorrhagic conversion, edema.
  • EEG for NCSE.
  • If hemorrhagic conversion: stop antiplatelet, reverse anticoag.
  • Hyperosmolar; consider decompressive craniectomy.

D35. 5-yo post-decompressive craniectomy for malignant MCA.

Show plan
  • Pediatric outcomes generally favorable.
  • Expect motor deficit; possible epilepsy; cognitive challenges.
  • Cranioplasty at 6–12 weeks.
  • Long-term rehab and school support.
