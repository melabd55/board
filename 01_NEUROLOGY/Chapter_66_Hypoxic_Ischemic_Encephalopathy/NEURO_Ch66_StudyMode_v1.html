Chapter 66 — Hypoxic-Ischemic Encephalopathy — STUDY MODE

Rogers' Textbook of Pediatric Intensive Care, Chapter 66 (Fink, Manole, Herrmann, Clark). Pages 3472–3537. Classification: DISEASE / ICU. Adaptive distribution: 40 SAQs + 40 PICU cases. Cross-links to Chapters 57 (Molecular Biology of Brain Injury), 58 (Coma), 59 (Neurologic Monitoring), 60 (Imaging), 64 (Status Epilepticus), 65 (Stroke).

Classification & Yield

  • Type: DISEASE / ICU.
  • Yield: 🔥🔥🔥 — RCPSC core: post-arrest care, THAPCA targeted temperature management, prognostication, cEEG, brain death determination.
  • Mortality: Out-of-hospital cardiac arrest survival 6–12%; in-hospital ~40%; majority of survivors with neurologic disability.

1 · Structured Rogers Mirror

KEY POINTS (pages 3472–3473)
  • HIE arises from heterogeneous etiologies; cardiac arrest is the dominant PICU cause.
  • Outcomes — death and severe neurologic disability — remain high; survivors carry a wide spectrum of disability.
  • Post-arrest CBF goes through four characteristic phases: no-reflow, hyperemia, delayed hypoperfusion, recovery.
  • Selective neuronal vulnerability: hippocampal CA1, cortical layers 3 and 5, basal ganglia, cerebellar Purkinje cells.
  • High-quality CPR plus rigorous post-resuscitation care to prevent secondary insults (hypoxia, hypotension, hyperthermia, hyperexcitability).
  • No effective specific HIE treatment yet — early post-arrest window remains a target for experimental neuroprotection.
ICU layer: Mission after ROSC is to eliminate the secondary injury catalysts — oxygenation extremes, hypotension, fever, seizures, glycemic excursions.
RCPSC angle: Examiners expect the four CBF phases by name and the canonical vulnerable regions list.
INTRODUCTION (page 3473)
  • HIE = brain injury from hypoxia, ischemia, or both, with or without reperfusion.
  • Variables shaping outcome: age, developmental stage, regional vulnerability, etiology, post-arrest care quality.
  • Therapeutic hypothermia is established in neonatal HIE (term newborns); paediatric (post-infancy) data more nuanced (THAPCA).
  • Existing post-resuscitation guidelines emphasize bundles rather than single agents.
CORE PATHOPHYSIOLOGY AND PATHOBIOLOGY (pages 3473–3486)

Primary insult

  • Global cessation of oxygen and blood flow during the arrest.
  • Outcome correlates strongly with duration of no-flow.
  • Cellular targets: neurons (most vulnerable), astrocytes, oligodendrocytes, microglia.

Secondary cerebral insults (post-resuscitation)

  • Hypoperfusion from cardiac dysfunction, vasoplegia, microcirculatory failure.
  • Cerebral edema, raised ICP, impaired autoregulation.
  • Fever — every 1°C increase amplifies secondary injury.
  • Hyperglycemia and hypoglycemia.
  • Hypoxemia or hyperoxemia (FiO₂ titration matters).
  • Seizures — clinical and electrographic — common after pediatric arrest.
  • Inflammation and oxidative stress.

Cell death cascades

  • Excitotoxicity: glutamate release → NMDA/AMPA receptor activation → Ca²⁺ influx → mitochondrial collapse.
  • Calcium overload activates proteases, lipases, nucleases.
  • Reactive oxygen and nitrogen species during reperfusion.
  • Apoptosis (caspase-dependent and -independent) and necroptosis.
  • Neuroinflammation: microglial activation, cytokine release, BBB breakdown.
  • Autophagy — both protective and injurious depending on intensity.

Endogenous neuroprotection

  • Heat shock proteins, hypoxia-inducible factors (HIF-1α), neurotrophic factors (BDNF, NGF), anti-oxidant systems.
  • These pathways form the rationale for "preconditioning" research.

CEREBRAL BLOOD FLOW (CBF) PHASES (pages 3486–3490)
  • Phase 1 — No-reflow (immediate post-ROSC): microvascular failure prevents adequate brain reperfusion despite restored circulation. Causes: endothelial swelling, leukocyte plugging, microthrombi.
  • Phase 2 — Hyperemia (~15 min): reactive hyperaemia — high CBF, often exceeding metabolic demand. Risks: edema, hemorrhagic conversion.
  • Phase 3 — Delayed hypoperfusion (~30 min to 24 h): sustained low CBF, mismatched with metabolic demand. Depth and duration correlate with severity of ischemia in animal models.
  • Phase 4 — Recovery (≥24 h): CBF returns toward baseline; ongoing risk of secondary insult.
RCPSC angle: Be able to name and time the four phases; explain why MAP must be defended through delayed hypoperfusion to maintain CPP.
SELECTIVE VULNERABILITY (pages 3490–3494)
  • Hippocampus — CA1 region ("Sommer sector"): highly susceptible due to dense glutamate receptor expression and metabolic demand.
  • Cerebral cortex — layers 3 and 5: pyramidal neurons especially vulnerable.
  • Basal ganglia — caudate and putamen: common imaging signature on diffusion MRI.
  • Cerebellum — Purkinje cells: often involved in severe HIE.
  • Thalamus — lateral and ventral nuclei.
  • Watershed zones (border zones): hypotension-induced injury between vascular territories.
  • Different age groups show different patterns — neonatal HIE preferentially involves basal ganglia and thalamus (severe asphyxia) or watershed (partial prolonged).
POSTRESUSCITATION CARE (pages 3494–3510)

Oxygenation and ventilation

  • SpO₂ 94–99%; avoid both hypoxia and hyperoxia.
  • PaCO₂ 35–45 mm Hg — normocapnia; avoid prophylactic hyperventilation.
  • Lung-protective ventilation if ARDS develops.

Hemodynamics

  • Target age-appropriate MAP to maintain CPP; commonly MAP ≥5th percentile for age.
  • Avoid hypotension at all costs in first 24 h.
  • Vasopressors (epinephrine, norepinephrine, vasopressin) as needed; consider inotropic support for myocardial stunning.
  • Treat reversible causes of arrest (Hs and Ts).

Temperature management (TTM)

  • AHA/ILCOR (2020): for comatose children after ROSC, either targeted normothermia (36–37.5°C) or therapeutic hypothermia (32–34°C) is reasonable; both superior to allowing fever.
  • THAPCA-OH (NEJM 2015) and THAPCA-IH (NEJM 2017): no significant difference between hypothermia 33°C and normothermia 36.8°C for 1-year survival with favorable functional outcome.
  • Maintain TTM ≥72 h.
  • Avoid fever ≥38°C aggressively — every degree above 37°C worsens outcome.

Glucose

  • Avoid hypoglycemia (<3.3 mmol/L) and severe hyperglycemia (>10 mmol/L).
  • Modest insulin infusion if persistent glucose >10 mmol/L.

Seizures

  • 30–47% of pediatric arrest survivors have post-arrest seizures; many subclinical.
  • Continuous EEG ≥24 h per AHA scientific statement (Topjian 2019).
  • Treat clinical and electrographic seizures aggressively per Chapter 64 algorithm.

Sedation/analgesia

  • Adequate to minimize stress, shivering during cooling, prevent ICP spikes.
  • Reassess off sedation periodically for neurologic exam.

ICP monitoring

  • Routinely NOT performed in pure HIE.
  • Consider if cerebral edema with mass effect on imaging.
  • Hyperosmolar therapy if signs of herniation.
NEUROMONITORING (pages 3510–3520)
  • Continuous EEG: detects subclinical seizures; pattern (burst suppression, isoelectric, status, malignant patterns) carries prognostic weight.
  • Near-infrared spectroscopy (NIRS): regional cerebral oxygenation (rSO₂) trend; values <40% concerning.
  • Transcranial Doppler (TCD): CBF velocity surrogate; helps detect hyperemia or hypoperfusion.
  • Pupillometry / quantitative pupillometry: emerging tool to track brainstem function.
  • Brain MRI within 3–7 days: DWI restriction in selectively vulnerable regions, ADC values, MR spectroscopy for lactate.
  • Somatosensory evoked potentials (SSEPs): bilateral absent N20 strongly predictive of poor outcome (extrapolated from adults; pediatric data limited).
  • Serum biomarkers: NSE, S100B, GFAP — investigational in pediatrics.
PROGNOSTICATION (pages 3520–3528)
  • Multimodal approach — no single marker is definitive in the first 72 h.
  • Recommended: clinical examination off sedation, cEEG, MRI within 7 days, biomarkers if available.
  • Clinical features predicting poor outcome:
    • Prolonged CPR (>30 min).
    • Persistent absent pupillary, corneal, motor responses at 72 h.
    • Status myoclonus within 24 h.
  • EEG features predicting poor outcome:
    • Isoelectric or low-voltage background.
    • Burst suppression at >24 h.
    • Refractory status epilepticus.
  • MRI features predicting poor outcome:
    • Diffuse cortical and basal ganglia/thalamus DWI restriction.
    • Loss of grey–white differentiation.
    • Diffuse cerebral edema.
  • Avoid prognostic decisions in first 24–72 h; reassess serially.
  • Communicate uncertainty; multidisciplinary family discussions with palliative care.
DETERMINATION OF DEATH BY NEUROLOGIC CRITERIA (pages 3528–3534)
  • Pediatric brain death determination per Nakagawa (2011, updated 2023) and SCCM/CNS guidelines.
  • Prerequisites: known irreversible cause; correction of confounders (sedation, paralytics, hypothermia, hemodynamics, electrolytes).
  • Two independent clinical exams by qualified physicians with required interval (age-dependent).
  • Examination: coma, absent brainstem reflexes (pupillary, corneal, oculocephalic/oculovestibular, gag, cough), absent respiratory drive on apnea test.
  • Ancillary studies if exam confounded: cerebral perfusion scan, CT angiography, EEG.
  • Discuss organ donation through Organ Donation Organization at appropriate time, separate from determination team.
EXPERIMENTAL NEUROPROTECTION (pages 3534–3537)
  • Targets being investigated:
    • NMDA antagonists (memantine, ketamine).
    • Anti-oxidants (N-acetylcysteine, mitochondrial-targeted antioxidants).
    • Anti-apoptotic agents (caspase inhibitors).
    • Anti-inflammatory (anakinra, tocilizumab, IL-1 antagonists).
    • Erythropoietin (neonatal HIE adjunct — variable trial results).
    • Xenon (Phase II data — anesthetic gas with NMDA antagonism).
    • Stem cell therapy (preclinical).
  • No agent currently endorsed for routine clinical use in pediatric HIE.

3 · Equations & Targets

CPP
CPP = MAP − ICP. Maintain age-appropriate CPP post-arrest; defend MAP through delayed hypoperfusion phase.
Targeted Temperature Management
Hypothermia 32–34°C OR normothermia 36–37.5°C × ≥72 h.
Slow rewarming 0.25–0.5°C/h.
Strict normothermia thereafter to 5 days total post-arrest.
Neonatal HIE cooling
≥36 weeks gestation, ≤6 h of life, evidence of perinatal hypoxic-ischemic event + moderate to severe encephalopathy.
33.5°C × 72 h, slow rewarming.
Blood gas targets
SpO₂ 94–99% (avoid hypoxia and hyperoxia).
PaCO₂ 35–45 mm Hg (normocapnia).
Glucose targets
Avoid <3.3 mmol/L and >10 mmol/L.
Modest insulin infusion if persistent >10.
cEEG and prognostication windows
cEEG: minimum 24 h; extend if persistent altered mental status.
MRI: 3–7 days post-arrest (DWI peak signal).
Multimodal prognostic decisions: delay until ≥72 h off sedation when possible.

7 · 40 SAQs — Chapter 66 (STUDY MODE — answers visible)

SAQ 1. Define hypoxic-ischemic encephalopathy and list 4 common etiologies in the PICU.

  • HIE = brain injury from hypoxia and/or ischemia, with or without reperfusion.
  • Common causes: cardiac arrest (dominant), severe respiratory failure with cardiac arrest, drowning, asphyxiation, perinatal asphyxia (neonatal), strangulation, severe sepsis with shock, cardiac surgery with circulatory arrest.

SAQ 2. Name the four post-arrest CBF phases with approximate timing.

  • No-reflow: immediate post-ROSC, minutes.
  • Hyperemia: ~first 15 min.
  • Delayed hypoperfusion: ~30 min to 24 h — duration correlates with outcome.
  • Recovery: ≥24 h.

SAQ 3. List the four canonical selectively vulnerable brain regions.

  • CA1 region of the hippocampus (Sommer sector).
  • Cortical layers 3 and 5 (pyramidal neurons).
  • Basal ganglia (caudate and putamen).
  • Cerebellar Purkinje cells.
  • (Thalamus and watershed zones additionally vulnerable.)

SAQ 4. Outline the cell death cascade in HIE.

  • ATP depletion from primary insult.
  • Membrane depolarization → glutamate release → NMDA/AMPA receptor activation.
  • Calcium influx → mitochondrial collapse → activation of proteases, lipases, nucleases.
  • Reactive oxygen and nitrogen species during reperfusion.
  • Apoptosis (caspase-dependent and -independent), necroptosis (RIPK1/3/MLKL).
  • Neuroinflammation: microglial activation, cytokines, BBB breakdown.

SAQ 5. Describe the AHA 2019 post-arrest bundle (Topjian scientific statement).

  • SpO₂ 94–99%; PaCO₂ 35–45.
  • Age-appropriate MAP; avoid hypotension.
  • TTM 32–34°C or 36–37.5°C for ≥72 h; avoid fever.
  • Glucose 3.3–10 mmol/L.
  • cEEG ≥24 h; treat seizures aggressively.
  • MRI 3–7 days post-arrest.
  • Multimodal prognostication ≥72 h.

SAQ 6. Explain the THAPCA-OH trial design and result.

  • Moler NEJM 2015; pediatric out-of-hospital cardiac arrest, comatose patients.
  • Randomized to hypothermia 33°C × 48 h vs normothermia 36.8°C.
  • Primary outcome: survival with favorable functional outcome at 1 year.
  • No significant difference between groups; both superior to fever.
  • Conclusion: either approach reasonable, fever must be prevented.

SAQ 7. THAPCA-IH key result.

  • Moler NEJM 2017; pediatric in-hospital cardiac arrest cohort.
  • Hypothermia 33°C vs normothermia 36.8°C × 48 h.
  • No significant difference in favorable outcome.
  • Reinforces flexibility of TTM choice.

SAQ 8. Why must hyperoxia be avoided post-ROSC?

  • Excess oxygen drives ROS production during reperfusion.
  • Worsens oxidative injury to selectively vulnerable neurons.
  • Observational data link hyperoxia to worse outcome.
  • Target SpO₂ 94–99%.

SAQ 9. Why is normocapnia preferred over hyperventilation post-arrest?

  • Hyperventilation lowers PaCO₂ → cerebral vasoconstriction → reduced CBF.
  • Worsens delayed hypoperfusion in damaged brain.
  • Target PaCO₂ 35–45 mm Hg.
  • Only use brief hyperventilation as rescue for impending herniation.

SAQ 10. MAP target post-pediatric cardiac arrest.

  • Maintain MAP ≥5th centile for age.
  • Avoid hypotension at all costs in first 24 h (Topjian CCM 2018, JAMA Peds 2018).
  • Use volume and vasopressors as needed (epinephrine, norepinephrine).
  • Manage myocardial stunning with inotropes if needed.

SAQ 11. Why is fever harmful post-arrest?

  • Every 1°C rise in core temperature amplifies excitotoxicity, oxidative stress, and cerebral metabolism.
  • Each febrile episode independently associated with worse outcome.
  • Treat aggressively with antipyretics and cooling devices.
  • Maintain strict normothermia for at least 5 days post-arrest.

SAQ 12. cEEG indications and duration post-pediatric cardiac arrest.

  • Indicated for all comatose post-arrest patients (AHA 2019).
  • Minimum 24 h; extend if seizures or persistent altered mental status.
  • 30–47% have post-arrest seizures, many nonconvulsive.

SAQ 13. Treat post-arrest seizures with which agents?

  • Standard SE tier from Chapter 64.
  • BZD → lev or fosphenytoin → anesthetic infusion.
  • cEEG to confirm electrographic resolution.

SAQ 14. Multimodal prognostication after pediatric HIE.

  • Clinical exam off sedation (pupil, corneal, motor, brainstem reflexes).
  • cEEG (background, malignant patterns, SE).
  • MRI 3–7 days (DWI, ADC, MR spectroscopy).
  • SSEPs (bilateral absent N20 strongly predictive).
  • Biomarkers (NSE, S100B — investigational).
  • Delay definitive prognostic decisions to ≥72 h; account for confounders.

SAQ 15. List 4 EEG features associated with poor outcome.

  • Isoelectric or low-voltage background.
  • Burst suppression persisting >24 h.
  • Refractory status epilepticus.
  • Generalized periodic discharges on flat background.
  • Lack of reactivity / sleep architecture.

SAQ 16. List 4 MRI features associated with poor outcome.

  • Diffuse cortical DWI restriction.
  • Basal ganglia + thalamic involvement.
  • Loss of grey-white differentiation.
  • Diffuse cerebral edema with effaced sulci/cisterns.
  • Cerebellar Purkinje cell injury.

SAQ 17. Define status myoclonus and its prognostic implication.

  • Continuous or recurrent rhythmic myoclonic jerks within the first 24 h post-arrest.
  • Strongly associated with poor neurologic outcome.
  • Should still be treated (lev, propofol, midazolam) — do not equate with futility.

SAQ 18. SSEPs in HIE prognostication.

  • Bilateral absent N20 cortical response at ≥72 h post-arrest is a strong predictor of poor outcome (in adults).
  • Pediatric data more limited; integrate with multimodal assessment.
  • Affected by sedatives, hypothermia, hemodynamic state.

SAQ 19. Pediatric brain death determination prerequisites.

  • Known irreversible cause.
  • Correction of confounders: sedation, paralytics, hypothermia (rewarm to ≥36°C), hemodynamic instability, severe electrolyte/acid-base derangements.
  • Sufficient observation time per age (per Nakagawa 2011 / Greer 2023).

SAQ 20. Pediatric brain death clinical exam components.

  • Coma (no response to noxious stimuli).
  • Absent brainstem reflexes: pupillary, corneal, oculocephalic/oculovestibular, gag, cough.
  • Apnea test demonstrating no respiratory drive at PaCO₂ ≥60 mm Hg (or 20 mm Hg above baseline).
  • Two independent exams by qualified physicians with required interval (typically 24 h for term-30 d; 12 h for >30 d).

SAQ 21. Ancillary tests for pediatric brain death when exam is confounded.

  • Cerebral perfusion scan (radionuclide or CT angiography).
  • EEG demonstrating electrocerebral silence (less reliable in young infants).
  • Transcranial Doppler (variable utility).
  • Choice per institutional protocol.

SAQ 22. Neonatal HIE Sarnat staging.

  • I (mild): hyperalert, mild hypotonia, brisk reflexes, no seizures.
  • II (moderate): lethargy, hypotonia, weak reflexes, seizures common.
  • III (severe): stupor/coma, flaccid, absent reflexes, brainstem dysfunction.
  • Cooling indicated for Sarnat II and III in ≥36 wk infants ≤6 h of life.

SAQ 23. Eligibility criteria for therapeutic hypothermia in neonatal HIE.

  • Gestational age ≥36 weeks.
  • Postnatal age ≤6 h.
  • Perinatal event (acidosis, low Apgar, need for resuscitation).
  • Evidence of moderate–severe encephalopathy (Sarnat II–III).
  • Target 33.5°C × 72 h; slow rewarming.

SAQ 24. HEAL trial — finding and implication.

  • Wu NEJM 2022 — RCT of erythropoietin + hypothermia vs placebo + hypothermia for neonatal HIE.
  • No reduction in death or neurodevelopmental impairment.
  • EPO not recommended as routine adjunct.

SAQ 25. Why is cooling NOT performed in preterm neonates <36 weeks?

  • Higher risk of intraventricular hemorrhage and other complications.
  • Limited evidence of benefit.
  • Preterm Hypothermia Trial (Laptook 2017) suggested risk > benefit.

SAQ 26. Drowning HIE — pearls.

  • Outcome correlates with submersion time, water temperature, time to first BLS.
  • Cold-water submersion historically allowed prolonged resuscitation due to neuroprotection.
  • Apply standard post-arrest bundle once ROSC achieved.
  • Watch for ARDS, electrolyte imbalances (esp. salt water vs fresh water).

SAQ 27. Carbon monoxide poisoning and HIE.

  • CO binds Hb >200× O₂ affinity → tissue hypoxia.
  • Treat with 100% O₂ via non-rebreather; intubate if severe.
  • Hyperbaric oxygen for severe (LOC, GCS <15, pregnancy, ischemia, COHb >25%).
  • Delayed neuropsychiatric syndrome 2–40 days post-exposure.

SAQ 28. Cyanide and metabolic HIE.

  • Inhibits cytochrome c oxidase — cells cannot use oxygen.
  • Suspected with sudden coma + acidosis + house fire exposure.
  • Treatment: hydroxocobalamin 70 mg/kg IV (max 5 g); sodium thiosulfate as alternative.

SAQ 29. Hypoglycemia and HIE — overlap.

  • Severe hypoglycemia causes selective neuronal injury, especially in parietal-occipital cortex and hippocampus.
  • Compound injury with concurrent hypoxia-ischemia.
  • Treat immediately with D10; maintain euglycemia.

SAQ 30. Glycemic targets post-ROSC.

  • Avoid hypoglycemia (<3.3 mmol/L) and severe hyperglycemia (>10 mmol/L).
  • Both worsen secondary injury.
  • Avoid tight glycemic control given hypoglycemia risk.

SAQ 31. Causes of refractory hypotension post-ROSC.

  • Myocardial stunning — requires inotropic support.
  • Vasoplegic shock from systemic inflammatory response.
  • Hypovolemia from third-spacing or hemorrhage.
  • Underlying cause of arrest (sepsis, anaphylaxis, tamponade).
  • Adrenal insufficiency.

SAQ 32. ECPR (extracorporeal CPR) — when to consider in pediatrics.

  • Witnessed in-hospital cardiac arrest with refractory rhythm and identifiable reversible cause.
  • Cardiac surgical patients (post-bypass).
  • Severe myocarditis, sudden severe respiratory failure.
  • AHA PALS 2020 supports consideration in selected cases.
  • Decision must be rapid (minutes) to be effective.

SAQ 33. Outcome assessment tools for pediatric HIE.

  • Pediatric Cerebral Performance Category (PCPC) — global function.
  • Pediatric Overall Performance Category (POPC).
  • Vineland Adaptive Behavior Scales.
  • Bayley Scales of Infant Development.
  • PSOM (Pediatric Stroke Outcome Measure) — also applicable in some HIE.

SAQ 34. Rehabilitation considerations post-HIE.

  • Early initiation in PICU once stable.
  • PT, OT, SLP; neurodevelopmental pediatric assessment.
  • Behavioral health support; family education.
  • Long-term follow-up with neurology, rehab, and primary care.
  • School integration plan with IEP.

SAQ 35. Endogenous neuroprotective mechanisms.

  • Heat shock proteins (HSP72) — stress-induced cytoprotection.
  • Hypoxia-inducible factors (HIF-1α) — adaptive gene expression.
  • Neurotrophic factors: BDNF, NGF.
  • Anti-oxidant systems: glutathione, superoxide dismutase, catalase.
  • Rationale for "ischemic preconditioning" research.

SAQ 36. Experimental neuroprotective strategies in pediatric HIE.

  • Xenon (Phase II — NMDA antagonism, no respiratory depression).
  • N-acetylcysteine (anti-oxidant).
  • Caspase inhibitors (anti-apoptotic).
  • Anakinra, tocilizumab (anti-inflammatory).
  • Erythropoietin (neonatal HIE — negative HEAL trial; ongoing research in older children).
  • Stem cells (preclinical).

SAQ 37. When to consider organ donation after pediatric brain death.

  • After determination of death by neurologic criteria is complete.
  • Approach by Organ Donation Organization, NOT by determination team.
  • Family decision-making; cultural and religious considerations.
  • Coordinate with forensic medicine in suspected abuse cases.

SAQ 38. Communication with family in early HIE phase.

  • Honest acknowledgment of uncertainty.
  • Avoid early definitive prognostic statements.
  • Daily structured updates with consistent providers.
  • Integrate palliative care from day 1 (alongside curative efforts).
  • Multidisciplinary family meetings at 72 h with all data integrated.

SAQ 39. Define WLST (withdrawal of life-sustaining therapy) in HIE context.

  • Decision to discontinue life-sustaining therapies based on prognostic data and family wishes.
  • Should follow ≥72 h of optimal care + multimodal prognostic assessment.
  • Not equivalent to brain death determination.
  • Includes comfort care, family support, organ donation considerations.

SAQ 40. Top 5 examiner traps in pediatric HIE.

  • Permissive hypotension post-ROSC.
  • Prophylactic hyperventilation in absence of herniation.
  • Hyperoxia from forgetting to wean FiO₂.
  • Failure to start cEEG (missing NCSE).
  • Premature prognostication (<72 h, with sedation on board).

Case 1. 8-year-old after witnessed out-of-hospital cardiac arrest with 12 min CPR before ROSC.

  • Apply post-arrest bundle: SpO₂ 94–99%, PaCO₂ 35–45, MAP ≥5th centile.
  • Initiate TTM 33°C or 36–37.5°C × 72 h.
  • cEEG within hours.
  • Address reversible causes (Hs and Ts).
  • Inotropic support for myocardial stunning.
  • MRI at 3–7 days for prognostic data.

Case 2. 3-year-old drowning victim, 10 min submersion, GCS 3 at scene, ROSC after 20 min CPR.

  • Standard post-arrest bundle.
  • Watch for ARDS — lung-protective ventilation.
  • cEEG — drowning carries high seizure risk.
  • Anticipate cerebral edema; image if neuro change.
  • Realistic prognostic discussion — drowning post-arrest often poor.

Case 3. Term infant Sarnat II HIE at 4 h of life.

  • Initiate therapeutic hypothermia 33.5°C × 72 h.
  • Sedation if needed; treat seizures.
  • cEEG monitoring.
  • MRI between days 4–7 for prognostic information.
  • Slow rewarming; avoid fever post-cooling.

Case 4. Preterm 32-week infant with severe HIE.

  • Do NOT cool — preterm HIE not in current guidelines (risk > benefit).
  • Apply post-resuscitation bundle: normothermia, oxygen targeting, glucose, seizure management.
  • Treat seizures with phenobarbital first-line.
  • cEEG; serial imaging.
  • Family discussion about prognosis.

Case 5. 12-year-old post-ROSC with persistent hypotension despite 60 mL/kg fluid.

  • Start epinephrine or norepinephrine.
  • Echocardiogram for myocardial stunning, RV function.
  • Consider inotrope (dobutamine, milrinone) if low cardiac output.
  • Stress-dose hydrocortisone if persistent.
  • Treat reversible causes; check lactate, central venous saturation.

Case 6. 7-year-old post-arrest, intubated and paralyzed, develops twitching of face.

  • Initiate cEEG immediately.
  • Treat electrographic seizures with lev 40–60 mg/kg.
  • Continue cEEG ≥24 h.
  • Common pattern post-arrest.

Case 7. 14-year-old after submersion, day 3 post-arrest, family asks about prognosis.

  • Defer definitive prognostic statements until ≥72 h.
  • Integrate clinical exam off sedation, cEEG, MRI.
  • Multidisciplinary meeting with palliative care.
  • Honest uncertainty communication.

Case 8. 5-year-old post-cardiac surgery, post-cardiopulmonary bypass arrest.

  • Standard post-arrest bundle.
  • cEEG — post-cardiac-surgery HIE risk high.
  • Echo for residual cardiac function.
  • MRI when stable — look for watershed and basal ganglia injury.

Case 9. 2-year-old in PICU with severe HIE day 5; MRI shows diffuse cortical + basal ganglia + thalamic injury.

  • Major contributor to multimodal poor-outcome assessment.
  • Integrate with cEEG and clinical exam.
  • Multidisciplinary family meeting to discuss prognosis and goals of care.
  • Palliative care integration.

Case 10. 9-year-old post-arrest day 7, no brainstem reflexes, no spontaneous respirations.

  • Initiate pediatric brain death determination protocol.
  • Confirm prerequisites: known irreversible cause, normothermia, no sedation/paralytics, BP adequate, electrolytes corrected.
  • Two independent exams + apnea test, separated per age.
  • Ancillary testing if exam confounded.
  • ODO involvement for organ donation discussion.

Case 11. 6-year-old after CO poisoning from house fire, GCS 6.

  • 100% O₂ via non-rebreather or intubation; hyperbaric O₂ for severe.
  • Check COHb; methemoglobin; cyanide consideration.
  • Hydroxocobalamin 70 mg/kg IV if cyanide suspected.
  • Standard post-arrest care if arrest occurred.
  • Watch for delayed neuropsychiatric syndrome 2–40 days.

Case 12. 4-year-old with severe DKA arrest, post-ROSC.

  • Address cerebral edema — DKA cerebral edema can cause arrest.
  • 3% NaCl 5 mL/kg bolus or mannitol if signs of edema.
  • Slow IV fluids; reduce insulin.
  • Standard post-arrest bundle.
  • cEEG; MRI when stable.

Case 13. 8-year-old in TTM at 33°C develops shivering.

  • Increase sedation depth (fentanyl, midazolam).
  • Neuromuscular blockade if shivering uncontrolled (rocuronium infusion).
  • Address Mg, calcium, and adequate analgesia.
  • Continue cEEG to detect breakthrough seizures masked by NMB.

Case 14. 14-year-old post-ROSC with persistent fever 39°C despite ice packs.

  • Aggressive normothermia: surface or intravascular cooling device.
  • Antipyretics (acetaminophen).
  • Septic workup if no clear non-infectious cause.
  • Central fever consideration once infection excluded.
  • Continue ≥5 days post-arrest.

Case 15. 3-year-old post-arrest, status myoclonus on day 1.

  • Negative prognostic marker but not definitive in pediatrics.
  • Treat with lev, propofol, or midazolam.
  • cEEG to characterize and titrate therapy.
  • Integrate with multimodal prognostication.

Case 16. 11-year-old severe HIE, MRI shows extensive cortical DWI restriction. Family considering organ donation.

  • If patient meets brain death criteria → DBD pathway.
  • If not brain death but family choosing WLST → DCD pathway.
  • ODO leads donation conversation, separate from determination team.
  • Cultural, religious, family considerations.

Case 17. 2-month-old infant with severe RSV, cardiac arrest from hypoxic respiratory failure.

  • Post-arrest bundle adapted for infant.
  • MAP target ≥5th centile for age.
  • TTM (33°C or 36–37.5°C); cEEG; treat seizures.
  • Manage respiratory failure (lung-protective).
  • RSV treatment supportive.

Case 18. 9-year-old post-anaphylaxis arrest with ROSC at 8 min.

  • Standard post-arrest bundle.
  • Continue epinephrine; biphasic anaphylaxis risk.
  • Steroids, antihistamines (anaphylaxis-specific).
  • Allergy/immunology consult; identify trigger.
  • Provide EpiPen at discharge; action plan.

Case 19. 6-year-old post-electrocution arrest.

  • Standard post-arrest bundle.
  • ECG for arrhythmia; cardiac enzymes.
  • Rhabdomyolysis (CK, urine myoglobin) — IV fluids.
  • Compartment syndrome assessment.
  • cEEG; MRI when stable.

Case 20. 10-year-old commotio cordis after baseball strike to chest.

  • VF arrest; treat with defibrillation.
  • Post-arrest bundle.
  • Echo for cardiac injury; troponin trend.
  • cEEG and MRI for HIE.
  • Long-term cardiology follow-up.

Case 21. 3-year-old with prolonged cold-water submersion (45 min). ROSC achieved after extended CPR.

  • Cold-water submersion historically allows extended resuscitation.
  • Rewarm gradually if <30°C — cardiopulmonary bypass for severe.
  • Standard post-arrest bundle once normothermic.
  • Aggressive cEEG and MRI.
  • Outcome reports document survivors with intact neurologic function.

Case 22. 7-year-old post-arrest day 4 — minimal neurologic improvement, family meeting.

  • Integrate clinical exam off sedation, cEEG, MRI.
  • Honest discussion of prognosis with uncertainty acknowledged.
  • Palliative care, social work, chaplaincy.
  • Discuss goals of care: tracheostomy/PEG vs WLST.
  • Allow time for family decisions.

Case 23. 5-year-old after asphyxiation from neck strangulation.

  • Standard post-arrest bundle.
  • Airway assessment — laryngeal/tracheal trauma.
  • Spine assessment — cervical injury.
  • cEEG; MRI brain + neck vessels (carotid dissection from neck pressure).
  • Forensic medicine if NAI suspected.

Case 24. 2-year-old post-arrest from refractory status asthmaticus.

  • Standard post-arrest bundle.
  • Continue asthma management: SABA, steroids, magnesium.
  • Lung-protective ventilation; permissive hypercapnia (titrate to safe range).
  • cEEG; MRI.

Case 25. 11-year-old in TTM day 1 develops INR 1.8, platelets 70.

  • Hypothermia-induced coagulopathy mild and common.
  • Correct only if active bleeding or planned invasive procedure.
  • Continue cooling if benefit anticipated.
  • Monitor with TEG/ROTEM.

Case 26. 8-year-old post-arrest with refractory hypotension and high lactate.

  • Echo for cardiac function; consider ECMO if refractory cardiogenic shock.
  • Add vasopressin to NE.
  • Stress-dose hydrocortisone.
  • Reassess for ongoing source (sepsis, hemorrhage, tamponade).
  • Multidisciplinary discussion.

Case 27. 15-year-old after fentanyl overdose with respiratory arrest, ROSC after naloxone + CPR.

  • Continue naloxone infusion if respiratory depression recurs.
  • Standard post-arrest bundle.
  • cEEG; MRI; social work.
  • Mental health and addiction services post-recovery.

Case 28. 4-year-old severe HIE day 3; tracheostomy and PEG being discussed.

  • Multidisciplinary family meeting.
  • Trach/PEG decision per family wishes and prognostic data.
  • If decided for aggressive care: schedule procedures, plan rehab.
  • If undecided: bridge with cuffed ETT for additional days.

Case 29. 12-year-old post-arrest, family declines withdrawal despite poor prognostic indicators.

  • Respect family wishes; continue aggressive care.
  • Address concerns and values explicitly.
  • Continue daily updates; involve palliative care.
  • Plan for chronic care path: trach, PEG, rehab.

Case 30. 10-year-old in TTM cohort with breakthrough seizures during rewarming.

  • Rewarm more slowly (0.25°C/h).
  • Add second-line ASM (fosphenytoin or valproate).
  • Continue cEEG.
  • Repeat imaging if exam changes.

Case 31. 7-year-old post-arrest with persistent bilateral fixed dilated pupils at 72 h off sedation.

  • Major contributor to multimodal poor-outcome assessment.
  • Confirm with cEEG (isoelectric?) and MRI.
  • Move toward brain death determination if exam complete.
  • Family meeting with palliative care.

Case 32. 3-year-old in TTM with high CK and dark urine.

  • Rhabdomyolysis from prolonged CPR or cause of arrest.
  • IV fluids to maintain UOP >1.5 mL/kg/h.
  • Treat hyperkalemia, acidosis.
  • Monitor renal function; CRRT if needed.

Case 33. 2-year-old with HIE from sudden ALTE/BRUE event with no clear etiology.

  • Standard post-arrest bundle.
  • Comprehensive workup: cardiac (long QT), metabolic, infection, AHT.
  • EEG, cardiac monitoring, echo.
  • Skeletal survey + ophthalmology if AHT suspected.

Case 34. 6-year-old post-ROSC with new asymmetric pupils.

  • Urgent CT for new mass effect or hemorrhage.
  • Hyperosmolar therapy if signs of herniation.
  • Reassess cause of arrest — intracranial event?
  • Neurosurgery consult.

Case 35. 9-year-old with severe HIE on day 14 — wean from anesthetic infusion, persistent NCSE on cEEG.

  • Super-refractory SE in HIE context.
  • Continue or re-escalate anesthetic; add ketogenic diet within 7 days of onset.
  • Empiric immunotherapy if FIRES-like inflammatory signal.
  • Realistic prognostic conversation.

Case 36. 10-year-old post-ROSC, EEG day 3 shows alpha coma pattern.

  • Alpha coma — diffuse alpha frequency, unreactive — poor prognostic indicator after HIE.
  • Integrate with other multimodal data.
  • Avoid premature definitive prognostication.
  • Continue full ICU support.

Case 37. 5-year-old severe HIE with sepsis-driven arrest.

  • Standard post-arrest bundle.
  • Aggressive sepsis management: broad-spectrum antibiotics, source control, fluids, vasopressors.
  • Watch for septic encephalopathy compounding HIE.
  • cEEG; MRI; multidisciplinary team.

Case 38. 8-year-old post-arrest, day 4, transitioning to comfort care after multidisciplinary decision.

  • Discontinue vasopressors and life-sustaining therapies per family wishes.
  • Continue ASMs for seizure symptom control.
  • Pain and anxiety control.
  • Family presence; chaplaincy; bereavement support.
  • Coordinate with ODO and forensic medicine as applicable.

Case 39. 13-year-old survives severe HIE with persistent vegetative state, planning long-term care.

  • Trach + PEG.
  • Rehab consult and long-term placement planning (home with skilled care, rehab facility, long-term ventilator unit).
  • Family education and support.
  • Periodic reassessment for emergence from PVS to minimally conscious state.

Case 40. 4-year-old recovers from HIE with mild deficits — discharge plan.

  • Rehab: PT, OT, SLP.
  • Neurodevelopmental pediatric follow-up.
  • Neuropsychology assessment at 6 and 12 months.
  • School integration plan with IEP.
  • Annual neurology follow-up for late-emerging epilepsy, behavioral issues.
  • Family education on second-arrest prevention if applicable.

11 · One-Page Infographic — Chapter 66

Pathophysiology Flow

  • Primary insult: hypoxia + ischemia → ATP collapse → excitotoxicity, Ca²⁺ overload, ROS, inflammation, apoptosis/necroptosis.
  • Post-arrest CBF phases: no-reflow → hyperemia → delayed hypoperfusion → recovery.
  • Selective vulnerability: CA1 hippocampus, cortex 3/5, basal ganglia, Purkinje cells, thalamus, watershed.

Diagnostic & Monitoring Algorithm

  • cEEG ≥24 h for all comatose post-arrest patients.
  • MRI 3–7 days post-arrest (DWI peak).
  • SSEPs at ≥72 h (adjunct).
  • Pupillometry, NIRS, TCD as adjuncts.
  • Multimodal prognostic decisions ≥72 h.

Critical Thresholds & Doses

  • SpO₂ 94–99%; PaCO₂ 35–45 mm Hg.
  • MAP ≥5th centile for age.
  • TTM 32–34°C or 36–37.5°C × ≥72 h.
  • Neonatal: 33.5°C × 72 h, ≥36 wk, ≤6 h.
  • Glucose 3.3–10 mmol/L.
  • Strict normothermia ≥5 days post-arrest.

Red Flags / Poor Prognostic Indicators

  • Prolonged CPR (>30 min).
  • Persistent absent pupillary/corneal/motor at 72 h.
  • Status myoclonus <24 h.
  • Isoelectric / burst suppression EEG >24 h.
  • Refractory status epilepticus.
  • Bilateral absent N20 SSEPs at 72 h.
  • Diffuse cortical + basal ganglia DWI restriction on MRI.

Exam Pearls

  • THAPCA: 33°C ≈ 36.8°C — both better than fever.
  • No room for hypotension or hyperoxia post-ROSC.
  • cEEG mandatory for all comatose post-arrest patients.
  • Defer prognostication ≥72 h with confounders managed.
  • Neonatal HIE cooling: term, early, moderate-severe; HEAL trial — EPO negative.
  • Pediatric brain death: Nakagawa 2011 / Greer 2023 — two exams + apnea test.
