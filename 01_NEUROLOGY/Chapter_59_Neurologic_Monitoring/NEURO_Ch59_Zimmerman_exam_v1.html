ZIMMERMAN Ch 63 — ICP & Monitoring — EXAM MODE

SOURCE: ZIMMERMAN   Companion to: ZIMMERMAN_63 STUDY MODE

Block A — Mock RCPSC SAQ (10 questions, 12 min)

A1. Name and describe the four Plum-Posner stages of central rostrocaudal deterioration.

Show answer
  • Diencephalic (early to late): agitation → drowsiness → stupor; deep sighs/yawns, Cheyne-Stokes; small reactive pupils (1–3 mm); brisk OC/OV; normal-to-↑ tone, possible decorticate posturing late.
  • Midbrain–upper pontine: coma; central neurogenic hyperventilation; midposition sluggish pupils (3–5 mm); dysconjugate eyes; decorticate → decerebrate.
  • Lower pontine–upper medullary: deep coma; apneustic/cluster breathing; midposition fixed pupils; absent OC/OV; flaccid with extensor plantars.
  • Medullary (terminal): deep coma; irregular gasps → apnea; pupils may be unequal; flaccid; cardiac arrest imminent.

A2. Apnea-test–negative scenarios — list five types of cerebral herniation and one hallmark of each.

Show answer
  • Uncal (transtentorial): ipsilateral blown pupil + contralateral hemiparesis (Kernohan's notch may reverse to ipsilateral).
  • Central transtentorial: Plum-Posner rostrocaudal sequence.
  • Subfalcine (cingulate): contralateral leg weakness from ACA compression.
  • Tonsillar (downward): respiratory irregularity, lower CN palsies, sudden cardiorespiratory arrest.
  • Upward cerebellar: paralysis of upward gaze, fixed dilated pupils, midbrain dysfunction.
  • Transcalvarial: brain extrusion through skull defect.

A3. Lundberg waves — A, B, and C — significance.

Show answer
  • A (plateau): sustained ICP > 50 mm Hg for 5–20 min. Pathologic; herniation risk; emergency tier-2 escalation.
  • B: 0.5–2 cycles/min, 5–25 mm Hg oscillations. Variable significance, often related to vasomotor tone.
  • C: 4–8 cycles/min, low amplitude. Physiologic Traube-Hering-Mayer waves.

A4. ICP waveform morphology and intracranial compliance.

Show answer
  • Normal: P1 (percussion — arterial) > P2 (tidal — compliance) > P3 (dicrotic).
  • Reduced compliance / rising ICP: P2 > P1, rounded morphology, increased pulse amplitude.
  • RAP index > 0.6 (correlation of mean ICP and pulse amplitude) = exhausted compliance.
  • P2 dominance signals approach of plateau waves and need to tighten tier-1 measures.

A5. Explain PRx and its clinical use.

Show answer
  • Pressure Reactivity Index — moving correlation between slow waves of MAP and ICP.
  • Positive (e.g., +0.5) = pressure-passive cerebrovasculature → impaired autoregulation; worse outcomes.
  • Negative (e.g., –0.3) = intact autoregulation.
  • CPPopt = CPP at which PRx is most negative; tailor individual CPP target rather than fixed number.

A6. List 6 modalities used in multimodal neuromonitoring and one unique contribution of each.

Show answer
  • ICP/CPP: threshold-based management, backbone of BTF protocol.
  • PbtO₂: regional brain tissue oxygen (target > 20 mm Hg); detects ischemia despite adequate CPP.
  • SjvO₂: global O₂ extraction (< 50% ischemia, > 75% hyperemia).
  • Microdialysis: lactate/pyruvate ratio > 25 = metabolic crisis.
  • TCD: CBF velocity, vasospasm detection (Lindegaard ratio), noninvasive ICP estimation.
  • NIRS: regional cerebral O₂ saturation; easy to deploy but less specific.
  • cEEG: nonconvulsive seizure detection; burst suppression titration.
  • Pupillometry: quantitative reactivity (NPi); trends before clinical herniation.

A7. Outline the principle of noninvasive ICP estimation using TCD.

Show answer
  • Hydrodynamic / electrical-analog model using MCA CBF velocity + ABP waveform.
  • Kashif 2012 — adult validation; mean error 1.6 mm Hg.
  • Fanelli 2019 — pediatric & young adult validation; bias 1.0 mm Hg, RMSE 5.2 mm Hg.
  • Real-time continuous estimation.
  • Useful when invasive ICP contraindicated (coagulopathy, skin infection).
  • Investigational — not yet routine standard.
  • Adjunct: ONSD ultrasound > 5.5 mm suggests raised ICP.

A8. Davson equation and clinical implications.

Show answer
  • ICP_CSF = (CSF outflow resistance × CSF formation rate) + sagittal sinus pressure.
  • Therapeutic targets:
    • Reduce outflow resistance — EVD, ETV.
    • Reduce CSF formation — acetazolamide (chronic; limited acute role).
    • Reduce venous pressure — HOB 30°, treat venous obstruction.

A9. A patient with severe TBI has ICP 22, CPP 65 mm Hg, but PbtO₂ 8 mm Hg. Manage.

Show answer
  • Tissue hypoxia despite adequate CPP — likely regional or oxygen delivery problem.
  • Optimize O₂ delivery — check Hb (transfuse if < 7–8 g/dL), SaO₂, MAP, cardiac output.
  • Recheck PaO₂; consider higher FiO₂ short-term.
  • Reduce metabolic demand — increase sedation, control fever.
  • Consider raising CPP via CPPopt if PRx data available.
  • Investigate for distributive issue (anemia, fever, hyperventilation).
  • If persistent, escalate to tier-2 measures (controlled hyperventilation with PbtO₂ monitor; barbiturate coma; decompressive craniectomy).

A10. Why is CN VI palsy considered a false localizing sign?

Show answer
  • Long intracranial course makes abducens nerve vulnerable to stretch with generalized raised ICP.
  • Does NOT necessarily indicate brainstem pathology at the level of CN VI nucleus.
  • Common in pseudotumor cerebri, hydrocephalus, and any cause of raised ICP.
  • Examiner trap — don't anchor on "brainstem lesion."

Block B — Oral Cases (5 sequential, 35 min)

B1. 10-year-old severe TBI day 2, GCS 5, ICP 22 with P2 > P1 waveform, CPP 60.

B1a — Interpret and manage.
  • Reduced intracranial compliance (P2 > P1) — at risk for plateau waves.
  • Optimize tier 1: HOB 30°, deepen sedation, normocapnia, 3% NaCl 3–5 mL/kg.
  • Consider adding PbtO₂ for regional oxygenation.
  • Calculate PRx if continuous waveform tools available — identify CPPopt.
  • cEEG to exclude NCSE.
B1b — Develops Lundberg A wave (ICP 55 for 10 minutes).
  • Emergency — herniation risk.
  • Tier-2 measures: barbiturate coma (pentobarbital), controlled hyperventilation with PbtO₂, consider decompressive craniectomy.
  • Reassess imaging.
  • Family meeting given trajectory.
B1c — PRx becomes positive at 0.4.
  • Pressure-passive cerebrovasculature; autoregulation impaired.
  • Find CPPopt — try different CPP targets and monitor PRx.
  • Use PRx-guided CPP rather than fixed BTF threshold.
  • Avoid hyperventilation in this state — reduces CBF without autoregulation to compensate.
B1d — Family decision on decompressive craniectomy.
  • Discuss benefits (ICP reduction, mortality reduction) vs risks (survival with severe disability).
  • Pediatric data supports use in refractory cases.
  • Cranioplasty at 6–12 weeks post-craniectomy.
  • Multidisciplinary family meeting with neurosurgery and palliative care.

B2. 14-year-old post-arrest day 3, comatose, examiner notes agitation → drowsiness → small reactive pupils with Cheyne-Stokes breathing.

B2a — Diagnose the syndrome.
  • Plum-Posner diencephalic stage of central rostrocaudal deterioration.
  • Increasing ICP; herniation in progress.
  • Urgent intervention required.
B2b — Immediate management.
  • Intubate if not already; protect airway.
  • HOB 30°, midline neck.
  • 3% NaCl 3–5 mL/kg bolus or mannitol 0.25–1 g/kg.
  • Brief controlled hyperventilation as rescue.
  • Emergent CT to assess for surgical lesion.
  • Neurosurgery consult.
B2c — CT shows diffuse cerebral edema; ICP probe placed reads 32.
  • BTF tier-1 measures.
  • Escalate to tier 2 if persistent: barbiturate coma, controlled HV with PbtO₂, decompressive craniectomy.
  • cEEG.
  • Multidisciplinary family discussion regarding prognosis.
B2d — Patient progresses to midbrain stage (central hyperventilation, dysconjugate gaze, decerebrate posturing).
  • Further rostrocaudal progression.
  • Continue aggressive ICH management.
  • Family meeting with palliative care for goals of care discussion.
  • Reassess clinical exam and imaging.

B3. 8-year-old severe coagulopathy contraindicating ICP monitor placement, suspected raised ICP.

B3a — Noninvasive monitoring options.
  • Optic nerve sheath diameter (ONSD) ultrasound > 5.5 mm suggests ICH.
  • TCD-based hydrodynamic ICP estimation (where validated).
  • Pupillometry quantitative NPi.
  • cEEG for seizures and pattern.
  • Clinical exam serial.
B3b — ONSD measures 6.5 mm.
  • Suggestive of raised ICP.
  • Begin empiric tier-1 measures: HOB 30°, hyperosmolar.
  • Treat coagulopathy — if reversible, place invasive monitor.
  • Repeat ONSD after intervention to track response.
B3c — Develops decerebrate posturing.
  • Severe brainstem dysfunction.
  • Urgent CT.
  • Hyperosmolar therapy + brief hyperventilation.
  • Neurosurgery for potential decompression.
  • Treat coagulopathy aggressively.
B3d — Family discussion regarding prognosis.
  • Decerebrate posturing carries poor prognosis.
  • Multimodal data integration — clinical, imaging, EEG.
  • Avoid premature definitive prognostication; reassess at 72 h.
  • Palliative care, social work, chaplaincy involved.

B4. 9-year-old post-aneurysm clip for SAH, day 5, develops new right hemiparesis.

B4a — Differential diagnosis.
  • Delayed cerebral ischemia from vasospasm (peak day 4–14).
  • Rebleeding (less likely after clipping).
  • Hydrocephalus.
  • Seizure with post-ictal deficit.
  • Imaging change (re-image).
B4b — TCD shows MCA velocity 220 cm/s.
  • Calculate Lindegaard ratio: MCA / extracranial ICA velocity.
  • If ratio > 3 → vasospasm.
  • Treatment: nimodipine, permissive HTN, euvolemia.
  • CTA or DSA for confirmation.
  • Intra-arterial vasodilator or angioplasty if severe.
B4c — cEEG shows new electrographic seizures.
  • Treat with lev 40–60 mg/kg.
  • Consider underlying ischemia as etiology.
  • Continue cEEG.
  • Maintain CPP; avoid hyperventilation.
B4d — ICP rises to 25.
  • Possible vasospasm + edema or evolving hydrocephalus.
  • Imaging.
  • Hyperosmolar therapy.
  • EVD if hydrocephalus.
  • Continue vasospasm management.

B5. 11-year-old in pentobarbital coma for refractory ICP, on cEEG with burst suppression.

B5a — Targets and monitoring.
  • Target interburst interval 5–10 seconds.
  • Continuous cEEG.
  • Vasopressor support anticipated; daily echo for cardiac function.
  • Watch for ileus, gastric stasis, infections.
  • Pentobarbital level monitoring.
B5b — Develops refractory hypotension despite norepinephrine.
  • Add vasopressin or epinephrine.
  • Echo for cardiac function.
  • Stress-dose hydrocortisone.
  • Reduce pentobarbital dose carefully if able.
  • Add ketamine for hemodynamic-preserving sedation.
B5c — Day 7, attempting wean.
  • Slow wean with continuous cEEG observation.
  • Add maintenance ASM during wean (lev, fosphenytoin).
  • Watch for ICP rebound.
  • Reassess multimodal monitoring.
B5d — During wean, ICP rebounds to 28.
  • Re-deepen pentobarbital infusion.
  • Reassess for surgical lesion.
  • Consider decompressive craniectomy if not already performed.
  • Family meeting given trajectory.

Block C — Randomized SAQ Drill (20)

C1. Diencephalic stage features.

Show
  • Agitation → stupor; deep sighs, Cheyne-Stokes; small reactive pupils 1-3 mm; brisk OC/OV; normal-to-↑ tone.

C2. Lundberg A wave criteria.

Show
  • ICP > 50 mm Hg, 5-20 min, pathologic, herniation risk.

C3. P2 > P1 implications.

Show
  • Reduced intracranial compliance; impending plateau waves.

C4. PRx interpretation 0.5 vs -0.3.

Show
  • +0.5 pressure-passive (impaired autoregulation); -0.3 intact.

C5. CPPopt definition.

Show
  • CPP at which PRx is most negative; individualized target.

C6. Uncal herniation features.

Show
  • Ipsilateral blown pupil, contralateral hemiparesis (or ipsilateral via Kernohan's notch).

C7. Tonsillar herniation features.

Show
  • Medullary compression — respiratory irregularity, lower CN palsies, sudden cardiopulmonary arrest.

C8. PbtO₂ target.

Show
  • > 20 mm Hg; < 10 severe hypoxia; < 15 concerning.

C9. SjvO₂ interpretation.

Show
  • < 50% ischemia; > 75% hyperemia or impaired extraction.

C10. Microdialysis L/P ratio threshold.

Show
  • > 25 = metabolic crisis; > 40 = severe anaerobic metabolism.

C11. Lindegaard ratio.

Show
  • MCA / extracranial ICA mean velocity; > 3 vasospasm, > 6 severe.

C12. ONSD threshold.

Show
  • > 5.5 mm (3 mm posterior to globe) in older children suggests raised ICP.

C13. Davson equation components.

Show
  • ICP_CSF = (outflow resistance × CSF formation) + sagittal sinus pressure.

C14. Monro-Kellie doctrine.

Show
  • Fixed skull volume; brain + blood + CSF in equilibrium; compensatory mechanisms exhausted before steep rise in ICP.

C15. CN VI palsy in raised ICP.

Show
  • False localizing sign — long course stretches abducens nerve.

C16. Hyperventilation risks.

Show
  • Vasoconstriction → reduced CBF → secondary ischemia, especially first 24 h post-injury.

C17. Pentobarbital infusion targets.

Show
  • Burst suppression on cEEG with interburst 5–10 sec; vasopressor support anticipated.

C18. Fanelli 2019 noninvasive ICP performance in pediatrics.

Show
  • Bias 1.0 mm Hg, RMSE 5.2 mm Hg; within 7 mm Hg of invasive reference.

C19. Open fontanelle and herniation.

Show
  • Delays but does NOT prevent herniation once compensation exhausted.

C20. Neurogenic pulmonary edema mechanism.

Show
  • Massive sympathetic surge from severe ICH causing pulmonary capillary leak.

Block D — Randomized PICU Case Drill (15)

D1. 10-yo TBI day 1, ICP 22, P2 > P1.

Show
  • Reduced compliance; optimize sedation; HOB 30°; 3% NaCl 3-5 mL/kg; consider PbtO₂.

D2. 14-yo HIE, ICP 28 sustained 10 min.

Show
  • Plateau wave; tier-2 (barbiturate coma, controlled HV with PbtO₂, decompressive craniectomy); family meeting.

D3. 7-yo SAH day 5, MCA 240/ICA 60.

Show
  • Lindegaard 4 = vasospasm; permissive HTN; nimodipine; CTA; consider intra-arterial therapy.

D4. 12-yo ICP 18 CPP 65 but PbtO₂ 8.

Show
  • Tissue hypoxia; optimize O₂ delivery (Hb, SaO₂, MAP); recheck PaO₂; reduce metabolic demand; raise CPP via CPPopt.

D5. 3-yo diffuse cerebral edema, exam agitation→stupor→decerebrate.

Show
  • Central rostrocaudal — midbrain stage; tier 1/2; imaging; neurosurgery; family communication.

D6. 15-yo coagulopathy, invasive ICP contraindicated.

Show
  • ONSD US; TCD-based estimation; pupillometry; cEEG; treat coagulopathy.

D7. 5-yo hydrocephalus + new vomiting/lethargy.

Show
  • Shunt malfunction; imaging; neurosurgery; hyperosmolar as bridge.

D8. 9-yo on barbiturate coma, ICP 12.

Show
  • cEEG for burst suppression target; vasopressor support; daily echo; plan slow wean.

D9. 2-yo neurogenic pulmonary edema post-TBI.

Show
  • Sympathetic surge; control ICP; lung-protective ventilation; PEEP carefully.

D10. 11-yo L/P ratio 35, ICP 12, CPP 70.

Show
  • Metabolic crisis; review O₂ delivery, glucose, temp; CPPopt; investigate seizures; hyperthermia control.

D11. Infant bulging fontanelle + irritability.

Show
  • Open fontanelle does NOT exclude ICH; urgent imaging; neurosurgery.

D12. 13-yo post-craniotomy + new aphasia day 2.

Show
  • Imaging for hematoma, edema, infarct; cEEG; neurosurgery; steroid escalation as appropriate.

D13. 8-yo PbtO₂ probe in penumbra after focal TBI.

Show
  • Regional measure; interpret with placement; combine with global measure; trends matter.

D14. 14-yo refractory ICP, decompressive craniectomy decision.

Show
  • Tier-2 for malignant ICH; pediatric data supportive; multidisciplinary family meeting; cranioplasty at 6-12 wk.

D15. 9-yo uncal herniation (blown L pupil + R hemiparesis).

Show
  • Immediate hyperosmolar bolus; brief HV; emergent CT and neurosurgery; anticipate Cushing's.

Top 5 traps

  • Treating CPP without autoregulation context.
  • Missing Plum-Posner sequential rostrocaudal signs.
  • Prophylactic hyperventilation.
