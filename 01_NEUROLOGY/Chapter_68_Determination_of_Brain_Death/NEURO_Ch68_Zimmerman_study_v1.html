ZIMMERMAN Chapter 69 — Acute Rehabilitation & Early Mobility in the PICU

SOURCE: ZIMMERMAN   Authors: Lenker, Kim, Wieczorek, Kudchadkar

Status: NEW CHAPTER — no direct Rogers counterpart in chapters 53–68

Cross-links to Rogers: Chapter 58 (Comatose Child — rehab after coma), Chapter 62 (TBI rehab), Chapter 65 (Stroke rehab), Chapter 66 (HIE outcomes)

Classification & Yield

  • Type: THEORY / DISEASE-ICU hybrid (rehabilitation science applied to critical care).
  • Yield: 🔥🔥 — RCPSC examines team-based care, PICS prevention, sedation strategy, delirium screening.
  • Why it matters: Post-intensive care syndrome (PICS-p) is an emerging quality outcome metric; early mobility programs are the dominant evidence-based intervention.

1 · Structured Zimmerman Mirror (verbatim headings + commentary)

PEARLS
  • PICU survival has improved; short- and long-term morbidities have risen, especially in children undergoing active neurocognitive development.
  • Harms of bed rest and immobility outweigh risks of early mobilization in most critically ill children.
  • Early mobility spans a spectrum tailored to acuity and premorbid function.
  • A culture of mobility requires minimal-but-effective sedation, delirium prevention, sleep hygiene, family engagement.
  • Engage rehabilitation team within first 72 hours of admission, regardless of patient acuity.
  • Key disciplines: PT, OT, speech-language pathology (SLP), physiatry.
  • Positioning and mobilization are among the most important interventions to reduce ventilator-associated pneumonia (VAP).
  • Augmentative/assistive communication tools optimize child's ability to express needs and emotions.
ICU layer: The "culture shift" point is examinable. Without minimal sedation, you cannot mobilize. Without mobility, you cannot prevent ICU-acquired weakness, delirium, and PICS-p.
RCPSC angle: Know the 72-hour window for rehab consultation, the four-discipline team composition, and that 20% of children admitted >72 h in US PICUs are completely immobile (Choong 2018).
REHABILITATION TEAM MEMBERS
  • Physical therapist (PT): diagnosis and treatment of movement-related conditions; gait, balance, strength.
  • Occupational therapist (OT): ADLs (feeding, self-care, play, school readiness); positioning and splinting; sensorimotor / oral motor stimulation in infants.
  • Speech-language pathologist (SLP): swallow safety, speech, cognitive assessment, augmentative communication (Passy-Muir valve, eye-gaze systems).
  • Physiatrist (PM&R): physician oversight of rehab plan; coordinates with PICU intensivist.
  • Bedside nursing and respiratory therapy are essential extended members of the rehab team.
ENVIRONMENTAL BARRIERS TO MOBILITY
  • Room setup: lines, drains, monitor cables limit transfer to chair or sitting at edge of bed.
  • Respiratory support side of bed vs IV pumps side — placement affects feasibility.
  • Bright lights, alarm noise — disrupt sleep-wake cycles, contribute to delirium.
  • Crib confinement limits play and motor exploration.
  • Deep sedation culture — perceived "safer" but increases delirium and weakness.
Trap: Defaulting to deep sedation "for safety" actually increases adverse outcomes — delirium, weakness, prolonged ventilation, longer LOS.
SEDATION & EARLY MOBILITY — RESTORE Trial
  • RESTORE (Curley JAMA 2015) — pediatric RCT of targeted sedation with daily arousal assessments vs usual care.
  • Intervention group: no increase in unplanned extubation or line removal.
  • Demonstrated feasibility of pediatric goal-directed sedation.
  • Adult precedent: ABCDEF bundle (awakening, breathing, choice of sedation, delirium, early mobility, family engagement).
  • Validated tools:
    • State Behavioral Score (SBS) or Richmond Agitation-Sedation Scale (RASS) for sedation depth.
    • Pediatric Confusion Assessment Method (pCAM-ICU), Preschool CAM-ICU (psCAM-ICU), Cornell Assessment of Pediatric Delirium (CAPD) for delirium.
ICU-ACQUIRED WEAKNESS (ICU-AW)
  • Adult ICU-AW literature: multifactorial (critical illness myopathy + neuropathy); associated with prolonged LOS and disability.
  • Pediatric ICU-AW underreported; Field-Ridley 2016 — large database analysis linked ICU-AW with respiratory and infectious diagnoses.
  • ICU-AW survivors often require inpatient rehab post-discharge.
  • Prevention: early mobility, minimal sedation, glycemic control, nutrition optimization.
INFANT NEURODEVELOPMENT IN THE PICU
  • Synactive Theory of Development (Als): autonomic + motor + state regulation + attention + self-regulation systems all interact with environment.
  • Optimal positioning <3 months: physiologic flexion (in-utero fetal posture), upper and lower extremities flexed, hands toward face, midline head.
  • Cranial deformities (plagiocephaly, brachycephaly) impact later motor skills — rotate supine/side/prone positioning frequently.
  • Kangaroo care (skin-to-skin): reduces stress hormones, increases oxytocin in parent and infant; cognitive benefits up to age 10 (Charpak 2017).
  • Oral motor stimulation: shortens transition to oral feeding, prevents oral aversion after prolonged intubation.
EARLY MOBILITY PROGRESSION (Zimmerman framework)
  • Level 1 — Passive ROM, positioning, splinting (deeply sedated/paralyzed patients).
  • Level 2 — Active-assisted ROM, side-lying play, mobile turning.
  • Level 3 — Sitting at edge of bed with support, dangling.
  • Level 4 — Bed-to-chair transfer (with or without ventilator).
  • Level 5 — Standing, marching in place.
  • Level 6 — Ambulating with assistance ± ventilator.
  • Level 7 — Independent ambulation in unit.
  • Advance one level per day as tolerated; communicate goal at multidisciplinary rounds.
VALIDATED OUTCOME MEASURES
  • Functional Status Scale (FSS) — overall functional outcome.
  • WeeFIM II — gross motor function in 6 mo–12 y.
  • Timed Up and Go (TUG) — 5–13 y, balance/gait.
  • Six-Minute Walk Test (6MWT) — 3–18 y, endurance.
  • 30-second Walk Test (30sWT) — 5–13 y, gait mechanics.
  • Pediatric Evaluation of Disability Inventory–Computer Adaptive Test (PEDI-CAT).
  • Vineland Adaptive Behavior Scales — broader function.
PICS-p (POST-INTENSIVE CARE SYNDROME – PEDIATRIC)
  • Manning 2018 — proposed framework for pediatric post-ICU sequelae:
    • Physical: weakness, fatigue, deconditioning.
    • Cognitive: attention, memory, executive function deficits.
    • Emotional/psychological: anxiety, depression, PTSD.
    • Social: school re-entry, peer relationships.
    • Family: caregiver mental health (PICS-Family).
  • Early rehab + minimal sedation + delirium prevention + family engagement reduces PICS-p.
COMMUNICATION ADJUNCTS
  • Passy-Muir speaking valve (in tracheostomized patient with deflated cuff).
  • Eye-gaze systems (Tobii, EyeCommand).
  • Communication boards (yes/no, pain scales, picture exchange).
  • Tablet apps for ADHD/speech (Proloquo2Go).
  • Family involvement in interpreting nonverbal cues.

3 · Equations & Thresholds

CAPD score
0–21; 8 items × 0–4 each. Score ≥ 9 = positive delirium screen. Sensitivity ~94%, specificity ~79% (Traube 2014).
State Behavioral Scale (SBS)
–3 (unresponsive) to +2 (agitated). Target typically –1 to 0 in mechanically ventilated children unless deep sedation indicated.
RESTORE intervention components
Daily arousal assessment + targeted sedation + sedation interruption + analgesia-first approach. Goal: minimum effective sedation.
Early mobility safety checklist (Wieczorek 2015 algorithm)
Hemodynamic stability (no new vasopressor >1 h), respiratory stability (FiO₂ ≤ 0.6, PEEP ≤ 10), neurologic stability (no active ICP crisis), no new arrhythmia, no actively bleeding line.

4 · Evidence Layer

Curley MAQ, Wypij D, Watson RS, et al. (RESTORE Investigators). Protocolized sedation vs usual care in pediatric patients mechanically ventilated for acute respiratory failure: a randomized clinical trial. JAMA. 2015;313(4):379–389.
DOI: 10.1001/jama.2014.18399 · PMID: 25602358
  • Key findings: Targeted sedation protocol did not shorten ventilator duration (primary outcome) but reduced opioid exposure and was safe; foundation for pediatric goal-directed sedation.
  • ICU impact: Validates that decreasing sedation in pediatrics is feasible without harm.

DOI: 10.1097/PCC.0000000000000160

DOI: 10.1097/PCC.0000000000000983

6 · 20 SAQs — Zimmerman Ch 69 (STUDY MODE)

SAQ 1. List four members of the PICU rehabilitation team and their primary roles.

  • Physical therapist — movement, strength, gait.
  • Occupational therapist — ADLs, positioning, splinting, oral motor stimulation in infants.
  • Speech-language pathologist — swallow safety, speech, augmentative communication.
  • Physiatrist (PM&R physician) — coordinated rehab plan oversight.

SAQ 2. Within what time frame should rehab consultation be initiated after PICU admission?

  • Within 72 hours of admission.
  • Regardless of patient acuity (even deeply sedated/paralyzed).
  • Reflects evidence that bed rest harm exceeds mobilization risk in most cases.

SAQ 3. Name the ABCDEF bundle elements.

  • A — Assess, prevent, and manage pain.
  • B — Both spontaneous awakening and breathing trials.
  • C — Choice of analgesia and sedation.
  • D — Delirium screen and manage.
  • E — Early mobility.
  • F — Family engagement and empowerment.

SAQ 4. RESTORE trial — purpose and key finding.

  • Curley JAMA 2015 — pediatric RCT of targeted sedation with daily arousal vs usual care.
  • No significant difference in ventilator duration (primary outcome).
  • Significant reduction in opioid exposure and benzodiazepine use; no increase in unplanned extubation.
  • Demonstrates safety and feasibility of pediatric goal-directed sedation.

SAQ 5. List the five PICS-p domains.

  • Physical (weakness, fatigue, deconditioning).
  • Cognitive (attention, memory, executive function).
  • Emotional/psychological (anxiety, depression, PTSD).
  • Social (school, peer relationships).
  • Family (caregiver mental health — PICS-Family).

SAQ 6. CAPD cut-off and validation.

  • Score ≥ 9 of 21 = positive delirium screen.
  • Sensitivity ~94%, specificity ~79% (Traube 2014).
  • Validated across all pediatric ages including non-verbal and intubated children.
  • Score every 12 hours minimum during PICU stay.

SAQ 7. Sedation depth scales used in pediatric ICU.

  • State Behavioral Scale (SBS): –3 to +2.
  • Richmond Agitation-Sedation Scale (RASS): –5 to +4.
  • COMFORT-Behavior scale (especially in infants).
  • Target typically light sedation (SBS –1 to 0, RASS –2 to 0) unless deep sedation specifically indicated.

SAQ 8. Optimal positioning for infants <3 months in PICU.

  • Physiologic flexion (in-utero fetal posture).
  • Upper and lower extremities flexed.
  • Hands toward face for self-calming.
  • Head in midline.
  • Rotate supine/side/prone positions to prevent plagiocephaly.

SAQ 9. Kangaroo care benefits.

  • Reduces stress hormones (cortisol).
  • Increases oxytocin in parent and infant.
  • Long-term cognitive and social benefits up to age 10–20 years (Charpak 2017).
  • Improves bonding; reduces parental anxiety.
  • Recommended ≥ 1 hour per day when medically feasible.

SAQ 10. What is the Passy-Muir valve and its role?

  • One-way speaking valve placed on tracheostomy tube with cuff deflated.
  • Allows phonation by directing exhaled air through vocal cords.
  • Improves communication, swallow, and respiratory mechanics in some patients.
  • Requires SLP supervision and patient tolerance assessment.

SAQ 11. Why is positioning one of the most important interventions for VAP prevention?

  • HOB 30° reduces aspiration of gastric and oropharyngeal contents.
  • Improves diaphragmatic and accessory muscle use → better ventilation.
  • CDC and international VAP bundles include semi-recumbent positioning.
  • Combined with oral care, sedation interruption, and DVT prophylaxis.

SAQ 12. Safety criteria for initiating early mobility in critically ill child.

  • Hemodynamic stability — no new vasopressor in > 1 h.
  • Respiratory stability — FiO₂ ≤ 0.6, PEEP ≤ 10.
  • Neurologic stability — no active ICP crisis.
  • No active arrhythmia.
  • No actively bleeding line or unstable surgical site.
  • Adequate sedation depth for cooperation when active mobility planned.

SAQ 13. PICU Up! program — implementation framework.

  • Wieczorek 2016 at Johns Hopkins.
  • Tiered mobility levels (similar to 7-level ladder).
  • Daily goal-setting at multidisciplinary rounds.
  • QI-driven; increased rehab consults and advanced level of mobility achieved.
  • Safety demonstrated — no increase in adverse events.

SAQ 14. Functional outcome measures for post-PICU children.

  • Functional Status Scale (FSS).
  • WeeFIM II (6 mo–12 y).
  • Pediatric Evaluation of Disability Inventory–CAT.
  • Vineland Adaptive Behavior Scales.
  • Six-Minute Walk Test (3–18 y).
  • Timed Up and Go (5–13 y).

SAQ 15. Sedation choice and delirium — pediatric considerations.

  • Benzodiazepines (midazolam infusions) strongly associated with delirium risk.
  • Dexmedetomidine preferred for many post-cardiac surgery and ventilated children.
  • Propofol limited by PRIS in pediatrics (avoid >4 mg/kg/h or >48 h).
  • Ketamine adjunct; preserves hemodynamics.
  • Match sedation depth to actual need; daily arousal assessment.

SAQ 16. ICU-acquired weakness — risk factors and mechanisms.

  • Adult: critical illness myopathy + neuropathy.
  • Pediatric: respiratory and infectious diagnoses common (Field-Ridley 2016).
  • Risk factors: prolonged paralysis, steroids, hyperglycemia, sepsis, immobility.
  • Associated with prolonged LOS, post-discharge inpatient rehab need.
  • Prevention: minimize NMB duration, early mobility, glycemic control, optimal nutrition.

SAQ 17. Oral motor stimulation — purpose and timing.

  • OT-led intervention for infants with prolonged intubation or PICU stay.
  • Tactile, kinesthetic, auditory, and olfactory stimulation around mouth.
  • Prevents oral aversion.
  • Shortens transition to oral feeding post-extubation.
  • Soothes infants; improves physiologic stability.

SAQ 18. Sleep promotion strategies in PICU.

  • Clustering of care to allow uninterrupted sleep blocks.
  • Dimmed lights at night; daylight exposure during day.
  • Minimize alarm noise (silent monitoring where safe).
  • Family presence at bedside.
  • Avoid late-evening sedative wake-ups when possible.
  • Melatonin in selected children (limited pediatric ICU evidence).

SAQ 19. Augmentative and assistive communication options.

  • Passy-Muir valve (speaking).
  • Picture/letter boards.
  • Yes/no pain scales.
  • Eye-gaze technology (Tobii, EyeCommand).
  • Tablet apps (Proloquo2Go).
  • Family interpretation of nonverbal cues.

SAQ 20. Top 5 pitfalls in PICU early mobility.

  • Defaulting to deep sedation "for safety" without daily arousal assessment.
  • Delaying rehab consult past 72 h.
  • Failing to screen for delirium (missed PICU-AW and PICS-p risk).
  • Not engaging family in mobility goals.
  • Ignoring sleep promotion (sleep loss worsens delirium and weakness).

7 · 20 PICU Cases — Zimmerman Ch 69

Case 1. 8-year-old day 6 of mechanical ventilation for ARDS, deeply sedated on midazolam + fentanyl.

  • Consult PT/OT for passive ROM and splinting.
  • Daily arousal assessment; target SBS –1 to 0.
  • Transition midazolam to dexmedetomidine if possible to reduce delirium risk.
  • CAPD daily screen.
  • Prone positioning for ARDS already aligns with VAP bundle.

Case 2. 3-month-old post-cardiac surgery, day 5 ventilated.

  • OT for oral motor stimulation, in-utero positioning.
  • Kangaroo care ≥ 1 h/day when hemodynamically stable.
  • Rotate head position to prevent plagiocephaly.
  • Minimize benzodiazepines; consider dexmedetomidine.
  • Synactive Theory approach — neurodevelopmentally supportive care.

Case 3. 12-year-old day 10 ICU, becoming agitated and pulling at lines; CAPD score 12.

  • Delirium positive.
  • Minimize benzodiazepines; consider quetiapine or risperidone if severe.
  • Optimize sleep (clustered care, day-night cues).
  • Family presence at bedside.
  • Treat underlying cause (pain, infection, withdrawal).
  • Early mobility advancement to engagement and play.

Case 4. 5-year-old recovering from severe pneumonia, ventilator weaning, family asking when child can sit up.

  • Apply early mobility safety checklist.
  • If stable: progress to Level 3 (edge of bed sitting).
  • PT to lead progression; respiratory therapy to manage ventilator during transfer.
  • Document mobility goal at multidisciplinary rounds.
  • Engage family in supporting mobility.

Case 5. 14-year-old post-TBI, tracheostomized day 14, attempting to communicate but unable to speak.

  • SLP consult for Passy-Muir valve trial.
  • Deflate cuff under monitoring.
  • If tolerated → phonation restored.
  • Alternative: communication board, eye-gaze system if cognition intact.
  • Family education on assisted communication tools.

Case 6. 9-year-old day 20 PICU, severe weakness post-prolonged paralysis.

  • ICU-acquired weakness.
  • Discontinue paralytic ASAP; minimize sedation.
  • Aggressive PT/OT — passive then active ROM.
  • Optimize nutrition and glucose.
  • Anticipate inpatient rehab placement after extubation.
  • PICS-p education for family.

Case 7. 6-month-old post-RSV bronchiolitis, day 14 ICU, oral aversion.

  • OT for oral motor stimulation to address aversion.
  • Transition to oral feeding gradually with SLP and OT support.
  • Continue kangaroo care.
  • Minimize benzodiazepines.
  • Address sleep disruption.

Case 8. 7-year-old post-asthma exacerbation now ventilator-free, hemodynamically stable, ready for advancement.

  • Advance to Level 5 (standing, marching in place) per safety checklist.
  • PT for endurance and gait training.
  • Discharge planning includes outpatient PT follow-up.
  • School re-entry plan.

Case 9. 11-year-old comatose after severe HIE, prolonged ICU stay anticipated.

  • Early rehab consult — passive ROM and positioning.
  • Splinting to prevent contractures.
  • Sensory stimulation (Synactive Theory).
  • Family involvement in care and rituals.
  • Multidisciplinary discharge planning to rehab facility if survives.

Case 10. 2-year-old delirious on midazolam infusion, CAPD 14.

  • Severe delirium.
  • Wean midazolam; transition to dexmedetomidine.
  • Sleep promotion strategies.
  • Family at bedside.
  • Consider antipsychotic (quetiapine, risperidone) if persistent severe agitation.
  • Treat underlying triggers (infection, pain, electrolyte).

Case 11. 15-year-old severe TBI, family concerned about loss of muscle mass during prolonged ICU stay.

  • Acknowledge concern; explain ICU-AW.
  • Early rehab plan: PT, OT, SLP, physiatry.
  • Aggressive early mobility once medically stable.
  • Nutrition consult for adequate protein.
  • Family education on PICS-p framework.

Case 12. 4-year-old post-meningitis, awakening after coma, struggling to communicate.

  • SLP for cognitive-communication assessment.
  • Augmentative communication board.
  • Family interpretation of cues.
  • Plan for cognitive rehabilitation.
  • Outpatient SLP follow-up.

Case 13. 10-year-old severe asthma + status, on midazolam and rocuronium.

  • Minimize NMB duration; daily holiday assessment.
  • Once off paralysis: aggressive PT for ICU-AW prevention.
  • Transition to dexmedetomidine.
  • cEEG if seizures suspected.
  • Plan for early mobility progression once stable.

Case 14. 6-year-old severe sepsis day 8, family asking about long-term outcomes.

  • Explain PICS-p framework: physical, cognitive, emotional, social, family domains.
  • Multidisciplinary follow-up after discharge.
  • Functional and developmental assessment at 3, 6, 12 months.
  • School support plan; mental health screening.
  • Family resource referral.

Case 15. 9-year-old chronic ventilator-dependent, transitioning out of acute PICU.

  • Long-term ventilator and rehab plan.
  • Trach training for family.
  • Daily mobility program.
  • School re-entry assessment.
  • Quality of life focus; engage palliative care if applicable.

Case 16. 8-year-old morbidly obese on ventilator, mobility challenges.

  • Bariatric equipment (heavy-duty lifts, wider chairs).
  • Two-person transfers minimum.
  • PT plan adapted to body habitus.
  • Coordinate with nutrition for weight management.
  • Address skin breakdown risk with frequent positioning.

Case 17. 13-year-old anxious adolescent on prolonged ventilation, refusing PT sessions.

  • Psychology consult for anxiety/PTSD.
  • Family engagement; peer support if possible.
  • Modify rehab sessions to short, goal-directed activities.
  • Music therapy, video games, distraction.
  • Address sleep and delirium.

Case 18. 3-year-old with severe burns, prolonged sedation for procedures.

  • OT for splinting (anti-contracture) early.
  • Pain management focus.
  • Mobility within tolerance of dressing changes.
  • SLP if oral airway burns.
  • Anticipate inpatient rehab; coordinate with burn rehabilitation team.

Case 19. 2-month-old infant, post-ECMO, plagiocephaly developing.

  • OT for repositioning protocol.
  • Tummy time when medically stable.
  • Avoid prolonged supine.
  • Consider cranial molding helmet if persistent.
  • Kangaroo care as feasible.

Case 20. 11-year-old pre-discharge from PICU, family meeting on transition.

  • FSS or WeeFIM functional assessment.
  • Multidisciplinary discharge plan: home, ward, rehab unit.
  • PICS-p education.
  • School re-entry plan with neuropsychology assessment.
  • Outpatient rehab follow-up.
  • Family mental health screening; PICS-Family resources.

8 · One-Page Infographic — Zimmerman Ch 69

Why early mobility matters

  • Bed rest is the dominant modifiable harm in PICU.
  • 20% of children > 72 h in PICU are completely immobile.
  • Early mobility reduces delirium, ICU-AW, ventilator days, PICS-p.

Team & Timing

  • 4-discipline team: PT, OT, SLP, physiatry.
  • Consult within 72 hours regardless of acuity.
  • Daily mobility goals at multidisciplinary rounds.

Bundle (ABCDEF)

  • A — Assess/prevent/manage pain.
  • B — Both awakening + breathing trials.
  • C — Choice of analgesia/sedation (dex over BZD).
  • D — Delirium screen daily (CAPD ≥ 9).
  • E — Early mobility (7-level ladder).
  • F — Family engagement.

Special Populations

  • Infants < 3 mo: in-utero flexion, kangaroo care, oral motor stimulation.
  • Trach: Passy-Muir valve for phonation.
  • Severe weakness: anticipate inpatient rehab.

Outcome framework: PICS-p

  • Physical, Cognitive, Emotional, Social, Family.
  • Measure FSS, WeeFIM, 6MWT, Vineland.

Exam pearls

  • 72-hour rule for rehab consult.
  • RESTORE: pediatric targeted sedation is safe.
  • CAPD ≥ 9 = delirium positive.
  • VAP bundle includes HOB 30°.
  • BZD → delirium risk; dex over midazolam where possible.
