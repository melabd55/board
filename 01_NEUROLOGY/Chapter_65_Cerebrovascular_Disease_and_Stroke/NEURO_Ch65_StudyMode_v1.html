1 · Structured Rogers Mirror

KEY POINTS (pages 3407–3408)
  • Stroke and cerebrovascular disease (CVD) are major causes of childhood morbidity and mortality.
  • "Time is brain" — delays in recognition and intervention compromise tissue viability.
  • Emergency MRI is preferred over CT when feasible — superior tissue characterization and lower radiation.
  • Multidisciplinary care is essential (pediatric neurology, neurosurgery, hematology, ICU, radiology, rehab).
  • Intracranial hypertension is uncommon but life-threatening — medical and surgical pathways must be ready.
  • Approximately 50% of pediatric stroke occurs in children with previously recognized predisposing conditions (CHD, sickle cell, hematologic, vasculopathy).
  • Silent infarction in >20% of children with sickle cell disease on MRI.
ICU layer: Triage every focal neurologic deficit as potential stroke — image first, do not anchor on "migraine" or "Todd's paralysis."
RCPSC angle: Examiners expect mortality and disability figures, and recognition that pediatric stroke time-to-imaging lags adult systems badly — quality improvement is a frequent question.
PRESENTATION, EPIDEMIOLOGY, OUTCOME, AND COSTS (pages 3408–3416)
  • WHO stroke definition: rapidly developing focal (or global) cerebral dysfunction lasting ≥24 h or causing death, with vascular cause.
  • TIA: symptoms resolving within 24 h — yet many have infarction on MRI (revised definition uses imaging not duration).
  • Common presentations: acute hemiparesis, aphasia, seizure, headache, altered consciousness, ataxia, cranial nerve deficits.
  • Coma is recognized presentation in SAH, ICH, large MCA territory infarction, vertebrobasilar stroke, CSVT, bilateral border-zone ischemia, PRES.
  • Incidence: 2–13 per 100,000 children/year (varies by region, including neonatal stroke ~1/1600–1/4000 live births).
  • Outcome: ~20% mortality; 50–80% disability; recurrence ~20% (lifetime).
  • Costs include long-term rehab, special education, family impact, and lost productivity.
Trap: Misattributing hemiparesis to Todd's paralysis after seizure — neuroimaging mandatory in any new focal deficit.
DIFFERENTIAL DIAGNOSIS IN CHILDREN PRESENTING WITH ACUTE FOCAL NEUROLOGIC DEFICIT (page 3411, Table 65.1)
  • Arterial ischemic stroke (AIS).
  • Hemorrhagic stroke (intracerebral, subarachnoid, intraventricular).
  • Cerebral sinovenous thrombosis (CSVT).
  • Hemiplegic migraine.
  • Todd's paralysis (postictal).
  • Focal seizure with motor manifestations.
  • Demyelinating disease (ADEM, MS).
  • Encephalitis (HSV, autoimmune).
  • Tumor (sudden hemorrhage, mass effect).
  • Hypoglycemia / metabolic stroke (mitochondrial — MELAS).
  • PRES/RPLS (reversible posterior encephalopathy).
  • Drug intoxication (cocaine, sympathomimetics).
  • Functional neurologic disorder.
ETIOLOGY & RISK FACTORS (pages 3416–3430)

Arterial ischemic stroke (AIS) — major risk categories

  • Cardioembolic (~25–30%): congenital heart disease, post-cardiac surgery, cardiomyopathy, infective endocarditis, mechanical valves, ECMO, paradoxical embolus (PFO/ASD).
  • Sickle cell disease (~10–15%): vasculopathy with progressive large-artery narrowing; HbSS highest risk.
  • Focal cerebral arteriopathy (FCA, ~25%): unilateral large artery stenosis; post-varicella angiopathy classic example; transient form often improves.
  • Moyamoya disease/syndrome: progressive bilateral ICA narrowing with "puff of smoke" collaterals; primary (East Asian, RNF213 mutation) and secondary (NF1, sickle cell, Down syndrome, radiation).
  • Dissection (~10–20%): spontaneous or post-traumatic; carotid (anterior circulation) and vertebral (posterior circulation, often atlantoaxial); risk factors include connective tissue disease (Ehlers-Danlos, Marfan), fibromuscular dysplasia.
  • Vasculitis: primary CNS angiitis (cPACNS — small and large vessel), systemic vasculitis (Takayasu, PAN), infection-related (TB, fungi, bacterial meningitis).
  • Prothrombotic states: Protein C/S deficiency, antithrombin deficiency, factor V Leiden, prothrombin G20210A, antiphospholipid syndrome, hyperhomocysteinemia, MTHFR variants.
  • Iron deficiency anemia: independent risk factor — examiner favorite.
  • Other: COVID-19, MIS-C, drug-induced (cocaine, methamphetamine), oral contraceptives, recent infection (transient inflammation).

Hemorrhagic stroke (HS) etiology

  • Arteriovenous malformation (AVM) — most common pediatric cause of spontaneous ICH.
  • Cavernous malformations.
  • Aneurysm (1–2% of pediatric ICH; bacterial endocarditis, trauma, familial).
  • Coagulopathy (hemophilia, ITP, leukemia, drug-induced).
  • Hypertension (uncommon in children, suggest renovascular or coarctation).
  • Hemorrhagic conversion of AIS.
  • Tumor hemorrhage.

Cerebral sinovenous thrombosis (CSVT)

  • Local: head/neck infection (mastoiditis, otitis, sinusitis, meningitis), trauma, dehydration.
  • Systemic: nephrotic syndrome, IBD, malignancy, prothrombotic state, oral contraceptives in adolescents.
  • Neonatal: birth-related, perinatal asphyxia, dehydration.
EMERGENCY IMAGING FOR CHILDHOOD STROKE (pages 3430–3445)
  • MRI preferred when available: DWI/ADC for hyperacute infarction (positive within minutes), FLAIR, SWI/GRE for hemorrhage, T1/T2 for tissue, MRA for vessel, MRV for sinuses, contrast-enhanced sequences for inflammation/dissection.
  • "Rapid stroke MRI" protocols — 6–10 minute sequences (DWI, FLAIR, GRE, MRA) to mirror adult systems.
  • CT — if MRI unavailable; non-contrast head CT excludes hemorrhage; CTA for vasculature; CT venography for sinus thrombosis. Faster but radiation and less sensitive for early ischemia.
  • Carotid/vertebral artery imaging — CTA or MRA neck for dissection if mechanism or focal signs suggest.
  • Echocardiogram (TTE then TEE if needed) — looking for cardiac source, PFO, ASD, mural thrombus, vegetations.
  • Transcranial Doppler (TCD) — surveillance in sickle cell disease per STOP protocol (elevated TCD >200 cm/s indicates high stroke risk).
  • Conventional angiography — reserved for endovascular intervention, problem-solving when MRA/CTA inconclusive, vasculitis confirmation.
Figure 65.1 (described): Multi-panel MRI series showing different stroke patterns — acute MCA infarct on DWI, hemorrhage on GRE/SWI, sinus thrombosis on MRV, moyamoya pattern on MRA.
ICU layer: Establish institutional Code Stroke pathway for pediatrics — door-to-imaging target <30 min, door-to-needle <60 min if thrombolysis considered.
TREATMENT — ACUTE MANAGEMENT (pages 3445–3470)

General resuscitation

  • ABCs — protect airway if GCS ≤8 or risk of aspiration.
  • Avoid hypoxia (SpO₂ >94%); avoid hyperoxia.
  • Maintain normocapnia (PaCO₂ 35–40 mm Hg).
  • Blood pressure: do NOT routinely lower in acute AIS; cerebral perfusion to ischemic penumbra is pressure-dependent. In ICH/SAH, control extreme hypertension to prevent rebleed (target SBP <160 mm Hg or per protocol).
  • Normoglycemia (avoid <3.3 and >10 mmol/L).
  • Normothermia; treat fever aggressively.
  • Treat seizures per Chapter 64 algorithm; load levetiracetam.
  • Head of bed 0–30° (controversial in AIS — flat may improve flow; 30° in ICH/edema).

Thrombolysis and antithrombotic therapy

  • No randomized data for IV tPA in pediatric AIS (TIPS feasibility trial closed early).
  • Consider IV tPA case-by-case in older children (>2 y), within 4.5 h of onset, with confirmed large-vessel AIS, NIHSS ≥4, after multidisciplinary discussion.
  • Endovascular thrombectomy: increasing pediatric case series support efficacy for large-vessel occlusion within 6–24 h.
  • Antithrombotic: aspirin 3–5 mg/kg/day as default first-line for AIS (extrapolated from adult and pediatric guidelines).
  • Anticoagulation (LMWH or unfractionated heparin) — selected indications: cardioembolic (mechanical valve, cardiomyopathy), dissection (extracranial), CSVT, prothrombotic states.

Hemorrhagic stroke

  • Reverse coagulopathy if present (vitamin K, PCC, FFP, platelets).
  • BP control as above.
  • Neurosurgical consult for evacuation in expanding hematoma or impending herniation.
  • EVD if hydrocephalus from IVH.
  • Vascular workup (CTA, conventional angiogram) for AVM/aneurysm; definitive treatment via surgery, embolization, or stereotactic radiosurgery.

CSVT

  • Anticoagulation (LMWH or UFH) for 3–6 months even with hemorrhagic infarct.
  • Hydration; treat underlying cause (infection, dehydration).
  • Repeat imaging at 3 months.

Decompressive craniectomy

  • Indication: malignant MCA infarction with mass effect and impending herniation despite tier-1 measures.
  • Pediatric data supports early intervention (within 48 h).
  • Improves survival; outcomes better than equivalent adult patients due to neuroplasticity.
STROKE DUE TO SICKLE CELL DISEASE (pages 3470–3478)
  • Highest stroke risk among inherited disorders: ~11% by age 20 in HbSS without prophylaxis.
  • Mechanisms: large-artery vasculopathy (progressive stenosis of distal ICA, proximal MCA, ACA), small-vessel injury, microvascular sludge.
  • Primary prevention: annual TCD screening 2–16 y; if velocity >200 cm/s, chronic transfusion (STOP trial, NEJM 1998).
  • Acute stroke management: exchange transfusion to reduce HbS <30% urgently; if exchange unavailable, simple transfusion to Hb 10 g/dL (avoid hyperviscosity).
  • Secondary prevention: chronic transfusion (SWiTCH 2012, TWiTCH 2016 — hydroxyurea non-inferior to transfusion for maintaining low TCD velocity).
  • Hematopoietic stem cell transplant — curative option in selected patients.
  • SIT trial (Silent Infarct Transfusion, NEJM 2014): chronic transfusion reduced silent infarct recurrence.
  • Iron overload management critical with chronic transfusion.
SPECIFIC SYNDROMES (pages 3478–3486)

Moyamoya disease and syndrome

  • Progressive bilateral distal ICA stenosis with proliferation of small collateral vessels.
  • Primary moyamoya disease (RNF213 mutation, East Asian).
  • Moyamoya syndrome: NF1, sickle cell, Down syndrome, post-cranial radiation, autoimmune disease.
  • Presents with TIAs (often triggered by crying, hyperventilation), AIS, hemorrhagic stroke in adults more than children.
  • Treatment: revascularization surgery (indirect — encephaloduroarteriosynangiosis [EDAS], pial synangiosis; or direct STA-MCA bypass).
  • Aspirin until revascularization.
  • Avoid hyperventilation, hypotension, dehydration — all reduce flow.

PRES / RPLS

  • Posterior reversible encephalopathy syndrome / reversible posterior leukoencephalopathy syndrome.
  • Presents with headache, seizures, visual changes, altered mental status.
  • MRI: bilateral parieto-occipital vasogenic edema (FLAIR hyperintensity, no DWI restriction).
  • Triggers: hypertension, calcineurin inhibitors (cyclosporine, tacrolimus), eclampsia, sepsis, renal failure.
  • Treatment: BP control, withdraw offending agent, seizure management.
  • Usually reversible if treated promptly.

Reversible cerebral vasoconstriction syndrome (RCVS)

  • Thunderclap headache with reversible segmental vasoconstriction.
  • Triggers: sympathomimetics, post-partum, drug exposure.
  • Diagnosis via MRA/CTA showing "string of beads."
  • Treatment: avoid trigger; nimodipine; supportive.
OUTCOMES & REHABILITATION (pages 3486–3490)
  • Mortality ~20%; recurrence ~20% (lifetime); disability in 50–80%.
  • Higher recurrence in moyamoya, sickle cell, dissection, prothrombotic states.
  • Outcomes generally better than adults due to neuroplasticity — but younger children at injury may have worse cognitive outcomes (Hebb's principle paradox).
  • Long-term sequelae: motor deficit, epilepsy, cognitive impairment, behavioral disorders, language delay.
  • Rehabilitation: pediatric PT/OT/SLP, constraint-induced movement therapy, school support, neuropsychology assessment.
  • Outcome assessment: Pediatric Stroke Outcome Measure (PSOM), GOS-E Peds.

3 · Equations & Dosing Thresholds

Aspirin (first-line antiplatelet for AIS)
3–5 mg/kg/day PO (some protocols up to 5 mg/kg).
IV tPA in pediatric AIS (case-by-case)
0.9 mg/kg (max 90 mg); 10% bolus, remainder over 60 min.
Within 4.5 h of clear onset; multidisciplinary decision.
Anticoagulation (CSVT, dissection, cardioembolic)
LMWH (enoxaparin): 1 mg/kg q12h SC (1.5 mg/kg q12h in <2 mo); anti-Xa target 0.5–1.0 IU/mL.
UFH: 75 IU/kg bolus → 20–28 IU/kg/h infusion; aPTT target 1.5–2.5× control.
Sickle cell acute stroke transfusion targets
Exchange transfusion: HbS < 30% (urgent).
Simple transfusion (if exchange unavailable): Hb to 10 g/dL; avoid >10 (hyperviscosity).
TCD velocity thresholds (sickle cell)
Normal: < 170 cm/s.
Conditional: 170–199 cm/s — repeat in 1–3 months.
Abnormal: ≥ 200 cm/s — chronic transfusion indicated.
BP management thresholds
AIS: permissive HTN until reperfusion; treat only if SBP >220 or DBP >120 (or per institutional protocol).
ICH/SAH: target SBP < 160 mm Hg or MAP < 110 to reduce rebleed; avoid hypotension.
PedNIHSS
Pediatric National Institutes of Health Stroke Scale; 15 items, score 0–42; ≥4 typically meets threshold for considering thrombolysis/thrombectomy.

7 · 40 SAQs — Chapter 65 (STUDY MODE — answers visible)

SAQ 1. State the WHO definition of stroke.

  • Rapidly developing clinical signs of focal (or global) cerebral dysfunction lasting ≥24 h or leading to death, with no apparent cause other than vascular origin.

SAQ 2. List 5 differential diagnoses for acute focal neurologic deficit in a child.

  • AIS / HS / CSVT.
  • Hemiplegic migraine.
  • Todd's paralysis.
  • Focal seizure with motor manifestations.
  • ADEM, encephalitis, tumor with hemorrhage, hypoglycemia, MELAS, PRES, drug intoxication, functional disorder.

SAQ 3. What proportion of pediatric stroke patients die or remain disabled?

  • Mortality ~20%; disability 50–80%; recurrence ~20% lifetime.
  • Among top 10 causes of childhood death.

SAQ 4. Name the 5 dominant etiologic categories of pediatric AIS.

  • Focal cerebral arteriopathy (FCA) ~25%.
  • Cardioembolic ~25–30%.
  • Sickle cell disease ~10–15%.
  • Dissection ~10–20%.
  • Prothrombotic / vasculitis / cryptogenic.

SAQ 5. First-line imaging in pediatric stroke.

  • MRI when feasible: DWI, FLAIR, GRE/SWI, MRA, MRV.
  • "Rapid stroke MRI" 6–10 min protocol.
  • CT + CTA as alternative if MRI unavailable.
  • Echo (TTE then TEE) for cardiac source.

SAQ 6. DWI/ADC pattern of hyperacute infarction.

  • DWI bright (high signal); ADC dark (low signal).
  • Positive within minutes of infarction.
  • Persists for ~7–10 days; pseudonormalizes thereafter.

SAQ 7. What is FCA and one classic example?

  • Focal cerebral arteriopathy: unilateral, monophasic, large-artery stenosis affecting distal ICA/proximal MCA.
  • Often transient — improves over months.
  • Post-varicella angiopathy is classic example; also other viral inflammatory triggers.
  • Treatment: aspirin ≥12 months; serial MRA.

SAQ 8. Moyamoya pattern on angiography.

  • Progressive bilateral stenosis of distal ICA / proximal MCA/ACA.
  • Multiple small collateral vessels forming "puff of smoke" pattern.
  • Suzuki staging I-VI describes progression.

SAQ 9. Moyamoya secondary causes.

  • NF1.
  • Sickle cell disease.
  • Down syndrome.
  • Post-cranial radiation.
  • Autoimmune (Graves, SLE).
  • Primary form: RNF213 mutation, East Asian predominance.

SAQ 10. Moyamoya treatment.

  • Surgical revascularization — indirect (EDAS, pial synangiosis) or direct (STA-MCA bypass).
  • Aspirin until revascularization.
  • Avoid hyperventilation, hypotension, dehydration — all reduce CBF.

SAQ 11. STOP trial result.

  • Adams NEJM 1998 — RCT in HbSS children 2–16 y with TCD >200 cm/s.
  • Chronic transfusion reduced first stroke risk by >90% vs observation.
  • Annual TCD screening became standard of care.

SAQ 12. TCD velocity categories in SCD.

  • Normal: <170 cm/s.
  • Conditional: 170–199 cm/s (repeat).
  • Abnormal: ≥200 cm/s → chronic transfusion.

SAQ 13. SWiTCH and TWiTCH trials.

  • SWiTCH (Ware 2012) — secondary prevention: hydroxyurea INFERIOR to transfusion.
  • TWiTCH (Ware Lancet 2016) — primary prevention after stable TCD: HU non-inferior to chronic transfusion.
  • Practical: continue transfusion after stroke; can transition to HU in stable primary-prevention patients.

SAQ 14. Acute management of stroke in SCD.

  • Emergent exchange transfusion to HbS <30%.
  • Simple transfusion to Hb 10 g/dL if exchange unavailable (avoid >10 — hyperviscosity).
  • Standard stroke supportive care.
  • Avoid tPA — increased bleeding risk.

SAQ 15. Indications for pediatric tPA in AIS.

  • No high-quality RCT data (TIPS feasibility trial closed early).
  • Consider case-by-case in older children (>2 y).
  • Within 4.5 h of clear onset.
  • Confirmed large-vessel AIS with significant deficit (PedNIHSS ≥4).
  • Multidisciplinary decision.
  • Dose 0.9 mg/kg, max 90 mg.

SAQ 16. Pediatric mechanical thrombectomy.

  • Increasing case series support efficacy in large-vessel occlusion.
  • Time window: 6–24 h for selected cases (mismatch imaging).
  • Should be performed at experienced centers.
  • Multidisciplinary stroke team decision.

SAQ 17. Default antiplatelet for pediatric AIS.

  • Aspirin 3–5 mg/kg/day PO.
  • Used in FCA, cryptogenic, dissection (intracranial), moyamoya pre-surgery.
  • Duration depends on etiology — minimum 12 months for FCA.

SAQ 18. When is anticoagulation indicated in pediatric stroke?

  • CSVT (3–6 months).
  • Extracranial dissection (3–6 months).
  • Cardioembolic (mechanical valve, mural thrombus, cardiomyopathy).
  • Prothrombotic state with high recurrence.
  • Selected paradoxical embolus.

SAQ 19. LMWH dosing for children.

  • Enoxaparin 1 mg/kg q12h SC (1.5 mg/kg q12h in <2 mo).
  • Anti-Xa target 0.5–1.0 IU/mL (peak 4 h post-dose).

SAQ 20. CSVT — common predisposing factors.

  • Local: mastoiditis, otitis, sinusitis, meningitis, trauma, dehydration.
  • Systemic: nephrotic syndrome, IBD, malignancy, hypercoagulable state.
  • Adolescent: oral contraceptives.
  • Neonatal: birth-related, perinatal asphyxia, dehydration.

SAQ 21. CSVT — imaging modality.

  • MRV is preferred (no contrast required for TOF; contrast-enhanced MRV more sensitive).
  • CTV as alternative.
  • Look for absence of flow void or filling defect in sinuses.

SAQ 22. CSVT management even with hemorrhagic infarct.

  • Anticoagulation still indicated (LMWH or UFH) — deVeber NEJM 2001.
  • Hemorrhage typically venous and limited; outcomes worse if untreated.
  • Continue 3–6 months; repeat imaging.
  • Treat underlying cause (infection, dehydration).

SAQ 23. AVM — most common pediatric cause of spontaneous ICH.

  • Yes — AVM is the most common cause of nontraumatic ICH in children.
  • Diagnosed on CTA, MRA, or DSA.
  • Treatment: surgery, embolization, or stereotactic radiosurgery, depending on Spetzler-Martin grade.

SAQ 24. When is decompressive craniectomy considered in pediatric stroke?

  • Malignant MCA infarction with mass effect and impending herniation.
  • Within 48 h of stroke onset for best outcomes (adult RCT data — DECIMAL, DESTINY, HAMLET).
  • Pediatric series suggest better outcomes than adults due to neuroplasticity.

SAQ 25. BP management in acute AIS.

  • Permissive hypertension — do not routinely lower.
  • Treat only if SBP >220 or DBP >120 (or per institutional protocol).
  • Cerebral perfusion to ischemic penumbra is pressure-dependent.
  • Avoid hypotension.

SAQ 26. BP management in acute ICH/SAH.

  • Target SBP <160 mm Hg or MAP <110 (rebleed prevention).
  • Avoid extremes — both hypotension and severe HTN harmful.
  • Use short-acting agents (labetalol, nicardipine).

SAQ 27. PRES/RPLS clinical features and imaging.

  • Headache, seizures, visual changes, altered mental status.
  • MRI: bilateral parieto-occipital vasogenic edema (FLAIR hyperintensity, NO DWI restriction).
  • Triggers: HTN, calcineurin inhibitors (cyclosporine, tacrolimus), eclampsia, sepsis, renal failure.
  • Reversible with BP control and trigger withdrawal.

SAQ 28. RCVS — features and treatment.

  • Thunderclap headache with reversible segmental cerebral vasoconstriction.
  • Triggers: sympathomimetics, postpartum, drug exposure.
  • MRA/CTA shows "string of beads."
  • Treatment: nimodipine; avoid trigger; supportive.

SAQ 29. Dissection — vessels affected and imaging.

  • Extracranial carotid (anterior circulation) and vertebral (posterior circulation, especially atlantoaxial).
  • Risk factors: trauma, connective tissue disease (Ehlers-Danlos, Marfan), fibromuscular dysplasia.
  • Imaging: CTA or MRA neck; T1 fat-sat MRI shows intramural hematoma "crescent sign."
  • Conventional angiography for problem-solving or intervention.

SAQ 30. Dissection treatment.

  • Extracranial: anticoagulation for 3–6 months.
  • Intracranial: antiplatelet preferred (rupture risk with anticoagulation in dissection of intracranial artery).
  • Follow-up imaging at 3–6 months.

SAQ 31. Common cardioembolic sources in pediatric AIS.

  • Congenital heart disease (cyanotic, post-Fontan).
  • Cardiomyopathy.
  • Infective endocarditis.
  • Mechanical valves.
  • ECMO complications.
  • Patent foramen ovale (PFO) / ASD — paradoxical embolus.
  • Mural thrombus.

SAQ 32. Prothrombotic disorders associated with pediatric stroke.

  • Protein C, Protein S, antithrombin deficiency.
  • Factor V Leiden.
  • Prothrombin G20210A mutation.
  • Antiphospholipid syndrome.
  • Hyperhomocysteinemia / MTHFR variants.
  • JAK2 V617F (rarely in children).

SAQ 33. Iron deficiency anemia and stroke risk.

  • Independent risk factor for pediatric AIS and CSVT.
  • Hagstrom 2007, Maguire 2007.
  • Mechanism: thrombocytosis, hyperviscosity, endothelial dysfunction.
  • Examiner favorite — always check ferritin in cryptogenic stroke.

SAQ 34. Workup for cryptogenic AIS.

  • Echo with bubble study (PFO, ASD, mural thrombus).
  • Hypercoagulability panel (proteins C/S, antithrombin, F V Leiden, prothrombin G20210A, antiphospholipid antibodies, homocysteine).
  • Lipid panel; HbA1c if metabolic syndrome.
  • Vasculitis panel (ESR, CRP, ANA, ANCA, complement).
  • Drug screen.
  • Genetic testing (Fabry, CADASIL, NF1) if suggestive.
  • Lumbar puncture (cPACNS).

SAQ 35. PedNIHSS — purpose.

  • Pediatric National Institutes of Health Stroke Scale.
  • 15 items; score 0–42.
  • Quantifies severity and tracks neurologic change.
  • PedNIHSS ≥4 typically meets threshold to consider thrombolysis/thrombectomy.

SAQ 36. Long-term outcome of pediatric stroke.

  • Mortality ~20%; recurrence ~20%; disability 50–80%.
  • Younger age at injury may carry worse cognitive outcomes (Hebb's paradox).
  • Common sequelae: hemiparesis, epilepsy, cognitive impairment, language delay, behavioral disorders.
  • Measured by Pediatric Stroke Outcome Measure (PSOM), GOS-E Peds.

SAQ 37. Rehabilitation strategies post-stroke.

  • Pediatric PT, OT, SLP.
  • Constraint-induced movement therapy.
  • Robot-assisted therapy / functional electrical stimulation.
  • Botulinum toxin for spasticity.
  • School support, neuropsych assessment.
  • Annual follow-up with pediatric stroke team.

SAQ 38. Why might bedside hyperventilation worsen moyamoya?

  • Hyperventilation lowers PaCO₂ → cerebral vasoconstriction → reduced CBF.
  • Moyamoya already has limited reserve.
  • Can precipitate TIA or stroke.
  • Other triggers: dehydration, hypotension, crying (Valsalva drops MAP).

SAQ 39. Neonatal stroke considerations.

  • Incidence ~1/1600–1/4000 live births.
  • Often presents with seizures in 24–72 h of life.
  • MRI DWI for diagnosis.
  • Etiology: birth-related, perinatal asphyxia, CHD, prothrombotic.
  • Generally do not anticoagulate or thrombolyze in neonates (unless CSVT or significant cardioembolic risk).
  • Long-term: hemiplegia (left-MCA classic), epilepsy, cognitive delay.

SAQ 40. Top 5 examiner traps in pediatric stroke.

  • Attributing focal deficit to Todd's paralysis without imaging.
  • Lowering BP in acute AIS.
  • Giving tPA in SCD.
  • Hyperventilating moyamoya patient.
  • Withholding anticoagulation in CSVT due to hemorrhagic infarct (it is still indicated).

Case 1. 8-year-old previously well with sudden right hemiparesis and aphasia 90 minutes ago.

  • Activate Code Stroke.
  • Rapid MRI: DWI/FLAIR/GRE/MRA within 30 min.
  • PedNIHSS scoring.
  • Echo and labs (CBC, coags, ferritin, hypercoag panel).
  • Confirm AIS → start ASA 3–5 mg/kg/day; tPA case-by-case if <4.5 h.
  • Thrombectomy team consult if LVO.

Case 2. 10-year-old HbSS, abrupt left hemiparesis 2 h ago.

  • Emergent exchange transfusion to HbS <30%.
  • Avoid tPA (increased bleeding in SCD).
  • Standard supportive care (normothermia, normoglycemia, BP).
  • Plan chronic transfusion for secondary prevention (SWiTCH).
  • HSCT discussion.

Case 3. 3-year-old with sudden ataxia and vomiting after minor neck trauma.

  • Suspect vertebral artery dissection.
  • MRI brain + MRA neck + T1 fat-sat (look for crescent of intramural hematoma).
  • If extracranial: anticoagulation 3–6 months.
  • If intracranial: antiplatelet.
  • Connective tissue disease workup (Ehlers-Danlos, Marfan).

Case 4. 5-year-old recovering from varicella now with abrupt right hemiparesis.

  • Post-varicella focal cerebral arteriopathy likely.
  • MRI + MRA: unilateral basal ganglia infarct + ipsilateral M1/distal ICA narrowing.
  • ASA 3–5 mg/kg/day for ≥12 months.
  • Repeat MRA at 3–6 months — often improves.
  • Acyclovir if active varicella.

Case 5. 12-year-old with mastoiditis and headache, now with seizures and altered mental status.

  • Suspect transverse sinus thrombosis.
  • MRV or CTV.
  • If confirmed: anticoagulation (LMWH); IV antibiotics for mastoiditis.
  • ENT consult for mastoidectomy if needed.
  • Hydration; treat seizures.

Case 6. 7-year-old on tacrolimus post-transplant, presents with headache, seizures, BP 180/110.

  • PRES/RPLS.
  • MRI: bilateral parieto-occipital FLAIR hyperintensity (vasogenic edema).
  • BP control with labetalol/nicardipine.
  • Reduce or hold tacrolimus.
  • Seizure management (lev).

Case 7. 2-day-old neonate with focal seizures.

  • Suspect neonatal AIS.
  • Phenobarbital 20 mg/kg IV.
  • MRI DWI for diagnosis.
  • Echo for CHD; hypercoag panel.
  • Do NOT anticoagulate or thrombolyze in neonates (unless specific indication).
  • Long-term follow-up for hemiplegia, epilepsy.

Case 8. 15-year-old post-tonsillectomy with thunderclap headache.

  • Differential: SAH, RCVS.
  • Non-contrast CT for SAH; CTA for aneurysm.
  • If RCVS suspected: MRA shows "string of beads."
  • If SAH: neurosurgery consult; control BP; reverse coag if applicable.
  • RCVS treatment: nimodipine, avoid triggers.

Case 9. 6-year-old with NF1, recurrent TIAs.

  • Moyamoya syndrome.
  • MRA confirms bilateral distal ICA stenosis with collaterals.
  • Surgical revascularization referral (EDAS, pial synangiosis).
  • ASA pre-op.
  • Avoid dehydration, hyperventilation, hypotension.

Case 10. 9-year-old with infective endocarditis develops aphasia.

  • Septic embolic AIS likely.
  • MRI brain.
  • Continue IV antibiotics; cardiology consult.
  • Anticoagulation generally avoided (mycotic aneurysm hemorrhage risk).
  • Surgical vegetectomy may be indicated for recurrent emboli.

Case 11. 11-year-old with massive left MCA infarct, GCS 9, midline shift 7 mm.

  • Malignant MCA infarct.
  • Intubate; head of bed 30°.
  • Hyperosmolar therapy (3% NaCl).
  • Emergent decompressive craniectomy within 48 h.
  • Pediatric outcomes generally better than adults due to plasticity.

Case 12. 14-year-old with hereditary hemorrhagic telangiectasia, sudden severe headache.

  • HHT — AVM risk in brain and lung.
  • Non-contrast CT for ICH; CTA for AVM.
  • Neurosurgery consult for AVM management (surgery, embolization, SRS per Spetzler-Martin grade).
  • Screen for pulmonary AVM (CT chest).

Case 13. 5-year-old with cryptogenic AIS; basic workup negative.

  • Echo with bubble study (PFO/ASD).
  • Hypercoagulability panel — proteins C/S, antithrombin, factor V Leiden, prothrombin G20210A, antiphospholipid, homocysteine.
  • Ferritin (iron deficiency anemia).
  • Vasculitis panel (ESR, CRP, ANA, ANCA, complement).
  • Genetic testing for Fabry, NF1, CADASIL.
  • LP for cPACNS.

Case 14. 3-year-old with sudden hemiparesis 6 h ago; CTA shows M1 MCA occlusion.

  • Multidisciplinary stroke team consultation.
  • Consider mechanical thrombectomy at experienced center.
  • Time window 6–24 h with mismatch imaging.
  • Standard supportive care; ASA after intervention.

Case 15. 10-year-old with nephrotic syndrome, headache, papilledema.

  • Suspect CSVT (hypercoagulable state from urinary protein loss including AT III).
  • MRV / CTV.
  • Anticoagulation 3–6 months.
  • Treat underlying nephrotic syndrome; volume status.

Case 16. 8-year-old with cyanotic CHD, post-Fontan, develops focal seizures.

  • High AIS risk — paradoxical embolus, slow circulation.
  • MRI DWI.
  • Anticoagulation per cardiology protocol.
  • Echo and CT for cardiac mural thrombus.
  • Seizure management.

Case 17. 13-year-old with thunderclap headache and altered mental status; CT shows SAH.

  • Activate neurosurgery; CTA for aneurysm or AVM.
  • BP control: SBP <160.
  • Nimodipine for vasospasm prophylaxis (60 mg q4h PO).
  • EVD if hydrocephalus from IVH.
  • Coiling or clipping based on lesion anatomy.

Case 18. 2-year-old with hemiparesis, MRI normal but symptoms persisted >24 h.

  • Reconsider differential: hemiplegic migraine, ADEM, focal seizure with prolonged postictal, conversion disorder.
  • Repeat MRI in 24–48 h to detect delayed DWI.
  • EEG.
  • LP for ADEM/encephalitis.

Case 19. 6-year-old HbSS on chronic transfusion develops fever and headache.

  • Workup for sepsis (functional asplenia risk — encapsulated organisms).
  • Empiric antibiotics covering pneumococcus.
  • MRI brain if neurologic concern; consider stroke or abscess.
  • Continue transfusion regimen.

Case 20. 9-year-old with cerebral AVM and recurrent headaches.

  • MRI and DSA for definitive characterization.
  • Spetzler-Martin grading.
  • Multidisciplinary discussion: surgery, embolization, stereotactic radiosurgery.
  • Aspirin generally avoided in unruptured AVM.

Case 21. 12-year-old with vasculitis presenting with multiple stroke territories.

  • cPACNS (primary CNS angiitis) or systemic vasculitis (Takayasu, PAN).
  • MRA/CTA — beaded or stenotic vessel pattern.
  • LP — pleocytosis, elevated protein.
  • High-dose steroids + IVIG; rituximab or cyclophosphamide for second-line.
  • Rheumatology/neurology consult.

Case 22. 4-year-old with MELAS mitochondrial disease, "stroke-like episode."

  • Not true stroke — does not respect vascular territory.
  • MRI shows cortical/subcortical hyperintensity often crossing vascular boundaries.
  • L-arginine 0.5 g/kg IV bolus + infusion may abort episode.
  • Avoid valproate (mitochondrial toxicity).
  • Genetic confirmation; long-term coenzyme Q10, carnitine, riboflavin.

Case 23. 7-year-old after radiation therapy for craniopharyngioma develops AIS.

  • Post-radiation vasculopathy — chronic moyamoya-like changes.
  • MRA for confirmation.
  • ASA; consider revascularization if recurrent TIA.
  • Annual MRA surveillance.

Case 24. 15-year-old with cocaine use, sudden severe headache.

  • Differential: ICH, SAH, RCVS, AIS, dissection.
  • Non-contrast CT then CTA/MRA.
  • BP control, drug counseling, social work.
  • RCVS pattern likely if reversible vasoconstriction; nimodipine.

Case 25. 3-month-old with bilateral cortical infarcts.

  • Consider: prothrombotic state, CHD, sepsis, AHT, metabolic.
  • Hypercoagulability panel; echo; metabolic workup.
  • MRI brain + MRA + MRV.
  • Hematology consult.

Case 26. 9-year-old SCD, TCD velocity 215 cm/s, no prior stroke.

  • Initiate chronic transfusion (STOP protocol).
  • Target HbS <30%.
  • After 1 y of stable normal TCD: consider transition to hydroxyurea (TWiTCH).

Case 27. 2-year-old MELAS with status epilepticus during "stroke-like episode."

  • Treat SE per algorithm (BZD then lev — avoid valproate).
  • L-arginine for stroke-like episode.
  • cEEG; MRI.

Case 28. 14-year-old taking oral contraceptives presents with headache, vomiting, papilledema.

Case 29. 5-year-old after a high-speed MVC develops AIS within 24 h.

Case 30. 8-year-old with strep meningitis develops AIS.

Case 34. 3-year-old with cerebellar AIS.

Case 35. 15-year-old with thunderclap headache, MRI shows convexal SAH.

11 · One-Page Infographic — Chapter 65

Pathophysiology Flow

  • AIS: arterial occlusion → ischemic core + penumbra → cell death.
  • HS: vessel rupture → hematoma → mass effect + secondary ischemia.
  • CSVT: venous outflow obstruction → edema → venous infarct ± hemorrhage.
  • PRES: BP/drug-induced endothelial dysfunction → vasogenic edema.

Diagnostic Algorithm

  • Rapid MRI: DWI/FLAIR/GRE/MRA/MRV.
  • CT/CTA if MRI unavailable.
  • Echo with bubble study for cardiac source.
  • SCD: TCD screening 2–16 y.
  • Cryptogenic: full hypercoag + vasculitis + genetic workup.

Critical Thresholds & Doses

  • ASA 3–5 mg/kg/day for AIS.
  • tPA 0.9 mg/kg (max 90 mg), <4.5 h, case-by-case.
  • Enoxaparin 1 mg/kg q12h; anti-Xa 0.5–1.0 IU/mL.
  • SCD exchange transfusion to HbS <30%.
  • TCD >200 cm/s = chronic transfusion.
  • BP in AIS: permissive HTN; treat only >220/120.
  • BP in ICH/SAH: target SBP <160.

Red Flags

  • Acute focal deficit — always image, never assume Todd's.
  • Bilateral SDH in infant — consider AHT (Ch 63).
  • Recurrent TIA + bilateral ICA stenosis = moyamoya.
  • Thunderclap headache = SAH or RCVS until proven otherwise.
  • Bilateral parieto-occipital edema = PRES.
  • Cervical SDH + AIS = consider dissection.

Exam Pearls

  • Time is brain — Code Stroke pathway saves tissue.
  • MRI preferred over CT when feasible.
  • SCD acute stroke = exchange transfusion, NOT tPA.
  • Moyamoya = avoid hyperventilation, dehydration, hypotension.
  • CSVT = anticoagulate even with hemorrhagic infarct.
  • Iron deficiency = independent stroke risk — always check ferritin.
  • Decompressive craniectomy: malignant MCA infarction within 48 h.
  • PRES: bilateral posterior edema, FLAIR ↑, no DWI restriction — reversible.
  • Pediatric tPA is case-by-case; no RCT data (TIPS feasibility only).
