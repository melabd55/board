1 · Clinical Recognition of Rising ICP — Stages of Rostrocaudal Deterioration

Early subtle signs (often missed)
  • Infants: poor feeding, vomiting, irritability progressing to lethargy and coma; seizures; full fontanelle; scalp vein distention; false localizing signs (CN VI palsy).
  • Older children: anorexia, nausea, vomiting, lethargy progressing to coma; seizures; CN VI palsy; behavior change; morning headache.
  • Vital sign changes: hypertension; later bradycardia and irregular respirations (Cushing's triad).
  • Pulmonary edema — neurogenic pulmonary edema can complicate severe ICH.
Trap: CN VI palsy is a false localizing sign — does NOT necessarily indicate brainstem pathology; reflects generalized raised ICP stretching the long abducens nerve.
Plum & Posner — Central Syndrome (Rostrocaudal Deterioration)
  • Stepwise downward functional collapse of brain levels as ICP rises and central herniation progresses.
  • Examination tracks four levels: consciousness, respiration, pupils, oculocephalic/oculovestibular, posture/tone.

Diencephalic stage (early to late)

  • Consciousness: agitation → drowsiness → stupor.
  • Respiration: deep sighs, yawns, periodic Cheyne-Stokes breathing.
  • Pupils: small (1–3 mm) but reactive.
  • Eye movements: conjugate, brisk oculocephalic/oculovestibular.
  • Tone: normal or slightly increased; possible decorticate posturing late in this stage.

Midbrain to upper pontine stage

  • Consciousness: coma.
  • Respiration: central neurogenic hyperventilation.
  • Pupils: midposition (3–5 mm), sluggish reaction.
  • Eye movements: dysconjugate.
  • Posture: decorticate progressing to decerebrate.

Lower pontine to upper medullary stage

  • Consciousness: deep coma.
  • Respiration: variable (apneustic, cluster).
  • Pupils: midposition, fixed.
  • Eye movements: absent oculocephalic/oculovestibular.
  • Posture: flaccid; bilateral extensor plantar; occasional flexor responses in lower limbs.

Medullary (terminal) stage

  • Consciousness: deep coma.
  • Respiration: irregular gasps then terminal apnea.
  • Pupils: may be unequal.
  • Eye movements: absent.
  • Posture: flaccid.
  • Cardiac arrest imminent unless reversed.
RCPSC angle: Knowing the Plum-Posner stages helps you anticipate the next step and intervene before terminal deterioration. Examiners may walk you through changing exam findings and ask the stage.
Herniation Patterns
  • Transtentorial (uncal): unilateral temporal lobe herniates through tentorial notch → ipsilateral CN III palsy (blown pupil), contralateral hemiparesis (or ipsilateral via Kernohan's notch phenomenon).
  • Central transtentorial: bilateral or diffuse supratentorial swelling → diencephalon then brainstem driven downward → Plum-Posner rostrocaudal sequence.
  • Cingulate (subfalcine): medial frontal lobe under falx → ipsilateral ACA compression with contralateral leg weakness.
  • Tonsillar (cerebellar — downward): cerebellar tonsils through foramen magnum → medullary compression → respiratory irregularity, lower cranial nerve palsies, sudden cardiorespiratory arrest.
  • Upward cerebellar: uncommon — vermis through tentorial notch → midbrain compression, fixed dilated pupils, paralysis of upward gaze.
  • Transcalvarial (external): brain extruding through skull defect (post-craniectomy or skull fracture).

2 · Physiology of the Intracranial Vault

Monro-Kellie doctrine refresher
  • Skull is fixed compartment containing brain (~80%), blood (~10%), CSF (~10%).
  • An increase in one compartment must be compensated by reduction in another or ICP rises.
  • Initial compensation: CSF displacement to spinal subarachnoid space; venous outflow increase.
  • When compensation exhausted, ICP rises steeply (exponential pressure-volume curve).
  • Infants with open fontanelle have additional compliance but eventually decompensate.
Davson equation for CSF pressure
  • ICP_CSF = (Resistance to CSF outflow) × (CSF formation rate) + (Sagittal sinus pressure).
  • Highlights three modifiable variables:
    • Reduce CSF formation (acetazolamide — chronic; limited acute role).
    • Reduce outflow resistance (EVD, ETV).
    • Reduce sagittal sinus pressure (HOB elevation, treat venous obstruction).
ICP waveform components and pulsatility
  • ICP waveform reflects arterial pulsation modulated by intracranial compliance and venous outflow.
  • Three peaks:
    • P1 (percussion wave) — arterial pulsation transmitted to CSF.
    • P2 (tidal wave) — reflects intracranial compliance.
    • P3 (dicrotic wave) — aortic valve closure.
  • Normal: P1 > P2 > P3.
  • Reduced compliance / rising ICP: P2 > P1 (rounded morphology).
  • Pulse amplitude (PA = max − min within cardiac cycle) increases with reduced compliance.
  • RAP index (correlation of mean ICP and PA): > 0.6 = exhausted compliance.
Lundberg waveforms (slow waves)
  • A waves (plateau): sustained increases > 50 mm Hg lasting 5–20 min — pathologic, herniation risk.
  • B waves: 0.5–2 cycles/min, oscillations of 5–25 mm Hg — variable significance.
  • C waves: 4–8 cycles/min, low amplitude — typically physiologic (Traube-Hering-Mayer).
  • A waves are an emergency indication for tier-2 escalation.

3 · Multimodal Monitoring Integration

Modalities and what each adds
  • ICP / CPP: threshold-based management; CPP optimization (CPPopt) via PRx.
  • PRx (Pressure Reactivity Index): moving correlation between slow waves of MAP and ICP; positive = autoregulation impaired (pressure-passive); negative = intact.
  • PbtO₂ (brain tissue oxygen): regional measure; < 10 mm Hg severe hypoxia, < 15 mm Hg concerning, target > 20.
  • Jugular venous oxygen saturation (SjvO₂): global oxygen extraction; < 50% = ischemia, > 75% = hyperemia or impaired extraction.
  • Cerebral microdialysis: lactate, pyruvate, glucose, glutamate, glycerol; lactate/pyruvate ratio > 25 = metabolic crisis.
  • Transcranial Doppler (TCD): CBF velocity, Lindegaard ratio (MCA/extracranial ICA velocity) > 3 = vasospasm; can estimate ICP noninvasively (Kashif, Fanelli).
  • Near-infrared spectroscopy (NIRS): regional cerebral oxygenation (rSO₂); easy to deploy but less specific.
  • Continuous EEG (cEEG): nonconvulsive seizures, burst suppression for barbiturate coma.
  • Pupillometry: quantitative pupil reactivity (NPi); detects trends before clinical herniation.
Noninvasive ICP estimation
  • Hydrodynamic/electrical-analog models use TCD-derived MCA velocity + ABP waveform to estimate ICP.
  • Kashif 2012 (Sci Transl Med): mean error 1.6 mm Hg in adult severe TBI.
  • Fanelli 2019 (Neurocrit Care): pediatric & young adult cohort (2–25 y), bias 1.0 mm Hg, RMSE 5.2 mm Hg.
  • Approach is promising for resource-limited settings or when invasive monitoring is contraindicated.
  • Limitations: still investigational; not yet routine clinical use.
  • Other noninvasive approaches: optic nerve sheath diameter (ONSD) via ultrasound > 5.5 mm suggests raised ICP; CT/MRI-based estimates.
When to escalate multimodal monitoring
  • Persistent ICP > 20 despite tier-1 measures.
  • Hemodynamic instability complicating standard CPP targets.
  • Refractory edema requiring barbiturate coma.
  • Concern for ischemic vs hyperemic injury → PbtO₂ or SjvO₂ differentiation.
  • Subarachnoid hemorrhage with vasospasm risk → TCD daily.
  • Suspected NCSE → cEEG.

4 · Equations & Targets

Davson equation (CSF pressure)
ICP_CSF = (Resistance to CSF outflow × CSF formation rate) + Sagittal sinus pressure.
Cerebral perfusion pressure
CPP = MAP − ICP. Age-appropriate targets per BTF 2019: ≥ 40 infant, ≥ 50 school age, ≥ 60 adolescent.
Pressure Reactivity Index (PRx)
Moving Pearson correlation between slow waves of MAP and ICP. Positive = pressure-passive (autoregulation impaired); negative = intact.
RAP index
Correlation of mean ICP and ICP pulse amplitude. > 0.6 = exhausted intracranial compliance.
Lindegaard ratio
MCA mean velocity ÷ extracranial ICA velocity. > 3 = vasospasm; > 6 = severe vasospasm.
Lactate/pyruvate ratio (microdialysis)
> 25 = metabolic crisis; > 40 = severe anaerobic metabolism.
Optic nerve sheath diameter (ONSD)
Ultrasound 3 mm posterior to globe; > 5.5 mm in older children suggests raised ICP.

7 · SAQs (20)

SAQ 1. Name the four Plum-Posner stages of central syndrome.

  • Diencephalic (early to late).
  • Midbrain to upper pontine.
  • Lower pontine to upper medullary.
  • Medullary (terminal).

SAQ 2. Diencephalic stage clinical features.

  • Agitation → drowsiness → stupor.
  • Deep sighs, yawns, Cheyne-Stokes breathing.
  • Small (1–3 mm) reactive pupils.
  • Brisk oculocephalic/oculovestibular responses.
  • Normal or slightly increased tone.

SAQ 3. Lundberg A wave significance.

  • Sustained ICP > 50 mm Hg lasting 5–20 min.
  • Pathologic — herniation risk.
  • Emergency tier-2 escalation indicated.

SAQ 4. ICP waveform: normal vs reduced compliance morphology.

  • Normal: P1 > P2 > P3.
  • Reduced compliance: P2 > P1 (rounded peak).
  • Pulse amplitude rises with reduced compliance.

SAQ 5. Davson equation components.

  • ICP_CSF = (CSF outflow resistance × CSF formation) + sagittal sinus pressure.
  • Therapeutic targets: reduce outflow resistance (EVD), reduce formation (limited), reduce venous pressure (HOB 30°).

SAQ 6. PRx definition and use.

  • Pressure Reactivity Index — moving correlation between slow waves of MAP and ICP.
  • Positive PRx = pressure-passive (impaired autoregulation).
  • Negative PRx = intact autoregulation.
  • CPPopt = CPP at which PRx is most negative; guide individualized CPP target.

SAQ 7. Uncal herniation clinical features.

  • Ipsilateral CN III palsy (blown pupil).
  • Contralateral hemiparesis.
  • Kernohan's notch phenomenon: ipsilateral hemiparesis (false localizing).
  • Progressing to coma and brainstem signs.

SAQ 8. Tonsillar herniation features and urgency.

  • Cerebellar tonsils through foramen magnum.
  • Medullary compression → respiratory irregularity, lower CN palsies.
  • Sudden cardiorespiratory arrest possible.
  • Surgical emergency.

SAQ 9. PbtO₂ targets.

  • < 10 mm Hg = severe brain tissue hypoxia.
  • < 15 mm Hg = concerning.
  • Target > 20 mm Hg.
  • Regional measure — placement location matters.

SAQ 10. SjvO₂ interpretation.

  • Global oxygen extraction measure (jugular bulb).
  • < 50% = ischemia (oxygen extraction high).
  • > 75% = hyperemia OR impaired extraction (also low CMRO₂).
  • Combine with CPP, PbtO₂ for full interpretation.

SAQ 11. Cerebral microdialysis metabolic crisis threshold.

  • Lactate/pyruvate ratio > 25 = metabolic crisis.
  • > 40 = severe anaerobic metabolism.
  • Additional analytes: glucose (low = ischemia), glutamate (excitotoxicity), glycerol (membrane breakdown).

SAQ 12. TCD Lindegaard ratio.

  • MCA mean velocity ÷ extracranial ICA velocity.
  • > 3 = vasospasm.
  • > 6 = severe vasospasm.
  • Daily TCD in SAH for vasospasm screening.

SAQ 13. Noninvasive ICP estimation approaches.

  • TCD-based hydrodynamic models (Kashif 2012, Fanelli 2019).
  • Optic nerve sheath diameter (ONSD) ultrasound > 5.5 mm.
  • CT/MRI-based estimates.
  • Pupillometry trends.
  • Investigational — not yet routine standard.

SAQ 14. CN VI palsy as false localizing sign.

  • Long course makes CN VI vulnerable to stretch with generalized raised ICP.
  • Does NOT necessarily indicate brainstem pathology.
  • Examiner trap.

SAQ 15. Monro-Kellie doctrine implication for infants.

  • Open fontanelle and unfused sutures provide compliance.
  • Hydrocephalus can develop chronically without acute symptoms.
  • Once compensation exhausted, decompensation can be rapid.
  • Open fontanelle does NOT prevent herniation.

SAQ 16. Neurogenic pulmonary edema mechanism.

  • Massive sympathetic surge from severe ICH or brainstem injury.
  • Pulmonary capillary leak.
  • Acute hypoxemic respiratory failure.
  • Treatment: control ICP; supportive ventilation; usually resolves with ICH resolution.

SAQ 17. When to add multimodal monitoring beyond ICP.

  • Persistent ICP > 20 despite tier 1.
  • Hemodynamic instability complicating CPP targets.
  • Concern for ischemic vs hyperemic injury.
  • Refractory edema requiring barbiturate coma (cEEG for burst suppression).
  • SAH or post-aneurysm clipping (TCD daily).

SAQ 18. Hyperventilation risks in raised ICP.

  • Lowers PaCO₂ → cerebral vasoconstriction → decreased CBF.
  • Risk of secondary ischemia, especially in first 24 h post-injury.
  • Use only as brief rescue for impending herniation, with multimodal monitoring.

SAQ 19. Why is ONSD elevated in raised ICP?

  • Optic nerve sheath is continuous with subarachnoid space.
  • CSF pressure transmitted to nerve sheath.
  • > 5.5 mm in older children (measured 3 mm posterior to globe) suggests raised ICP.
  • Bedside ultrasound — useful screen when invasive ICP not available.

SAQ 20. Top 5 examiner traps in ICP monitoring.

  • Treating CPP number without considering autoregulation status (PRx).
  • Missing Plum-Posner sequential signs of rostrocaudal deterioration.
  • Prophylactic hyperventilation without monitoring.
  • Anchoring on ICP number alone without considering compliance (waveform morphology).
  • Assuming infant with open fontanelle is protected from herniation.

8 · 15 PICU Cases

Case 1. 10-year-old severe TBI day 1, GCS 6, ICP 22, waveform shows P2 > P1.

  • Reduced intracranial compliance.
  • Optimize sedation; HOB 30°; 3% NaCl 3–5 mL/kg.
  • Consider adding PbtO₂.
  • Watch for plateau waves.

Case 2. 14-year-old post-arrest, severe HIE, ICP 28 sustained for 10 min.

  • Plateau wave (Lundberg A).
  • Emergent tier-2 measures — barbiturate coma, controlled hyperventilation with PbtO₂, decompressive craniectomy.
  • cEEG for burst suppression titration.
  • Family meeting on prognosis.

Case 3. 7-year-old day 5 SAH, daily TCD shows MCA velocity 240 cm/s, ICA velocity 60 cm/s.

  • Lindegaard ratio 4.0 = vasospasm.
  • Permissive hypertension; nimodipine if available.
  • CTA or DSA for confirmation.
  • Consider intra-arterial vasodilator or angioplasty.

Case 4. 12-year-old TBI day 2, ICP 18, CPP 65, but PbtO₂ 8 mm Hg.

  • Tissue hypoxia despite adequate CPP.
  • Optimize O₂ delivery — Hb, SaO₂, MAP.
  • Recheck PaO₂; consider higher FiO₂ short-term.
  • Sedation depth; reduce metabolic demand.
  • Search for distributive issue (anemia, fever).

Case 5. 3-year-old with severe diffuse cerebral edema, exam progressing from agitation → stupor → decerebrate posturing.

  • Central rostrocaudal deterioration — midbrain stage.
  • Tier-1 and tier-2 measures emergently.
  • Imaging; neurosurgery consult.
  • Likely poor prognosis; family communication.

Case 6. 15-year-old with severe coagulopathy, suspected raised ICP, invasive monitor contraindicated.

  • Optic nerve sheath diameter ultrasound.
  • TCD for noninvasive ICP estimate (if validated tool available).
  • Pupillometry, cEEG.
  • Treat coagulopathy; if reversible, place ICP monitor.

Case 7. 5-year-old hydrocephalus with new vomiting and lethargy.

  • Shunt malfunction.
  • Imaging; neurosurgery; potential EVD.
  • Hyperosmolar therapy as bridge if symptomatic.

Case 8. 9-year-old with severe TBI on barbiturate coma, ICP 12.

  • cEEG for burst suppression target (5–10 sec interburst).
  • Vasopressor support anticipated.
  • Daily echo; watch for ileus and infections.
  • Plan slow wean with continued cEEG observation.

Case 9. 2-yo with neurogenic pulmonary edema after severe TBI.

  • Massive sympathetic surge.
  • Control ICP — tier 1/2 escalation.
  • Supportive lung-protective ventilation (PEEP carefully — minimize ICP rise from high PEEP).
  • Usually resolves with ICH control.

Case 10. 11-year-old microdialysis L/P ratio 35, ICP 12, CPP 70.

  • Metabolic crisis despite normal ICP/CPP.
  • Review O₂ delivery, glucose, temperature.
  • Consider CPPopt via PRx.
  • Investigate seizures (cEEG).
  • Hyperthermia control.

Case 11. Infant with bulging fontanelle and irritability.

  • Open fontanelle: still potential for ICH and herniation.
  • Urgent imaging.
  • Neurosurgery for hydrocephalus or hematoma.
  • Do NOT be reassured by fontanelle alone.

Case 12. 13-year-old post-craniotomy for tumor, develops new aphasia day 2.

  • Imaging for hematoma, edema, infarct.
  • Continuous monitoring; cEEG for NCSE.
  • Steroid escalation if edema; neurosurgery if mass effect.

Case 13. 8-year-old PbtO₂ probe placed in penumbral region after focal TBI.

  • Regional measure — interpret with placement in mind.
  • Combine with global measure (SjvO₂ or model-based).
  • Trends matter more than single value.

Case 14. 14-year-old with refractory ICP on barbiturate coma, considering decompressive craniectomy.

  • Tier-2 measure for malignant ICH.
  • Pediatric Taylor RCT supports use in refractory cases.
  • Multidisciplinary decision; family discussion.
  • Cranioplasty at 6–12 weeks.

Case 15. 9-year-old uncal herniation suspected (left blown pupil, right hemiparesis).

  • Immediate hyperosmolar bolus.
  • Brief controlled hyperventilation as rescue.
  • Emergent CT and neurosurgery for craniotomy ± decompression.
  • Cushing's triad may follow — anticipate.

9 · One-Page Infographic — Zimmerman Ch 63

Clinical Recognition

  • Plum-Posner stages: diencephalic → midbrain → lower pontine → medullary.
  • Track consciousness, respiration, pupils, eye movements, motor, tone.
  • Herniation: uncal, central, subfalcine, tonsillar, upward cerebellar.

Waveform Reading

  • P2 > P1 morphology = ↓ compliance.
  • Lundberg A wave (sustained > 50) = emergency.
  • RAP > 0.6 = exhausted compliance.

Multimodal Monitoring

  • ICP + CPP backbone; add PbtO₂ for tissue hypoxia detection.
  • SjvO₂ for global oxygen extraction.
  • Microdialysis L/P > 25 = metabolic crisis.
  • TCD for vasospasm and noninvasive ICP estimate.
  • cEEG for NCSE and burst suppression.
  • Pupillometry for quantitative trends.

Noninvasive ICP

  • ONSD > 5.5 mm.
  • TCD-based models (Kashif 2012, Fanelli 2019).
  • CT/MRI features.

Exam pearls

  • CN VI palsy is a FALSE localizing sign.
  • Open fontanelle does NOT prevent herniation.
  • Treat CPP number with autoregulation context (PRx).
  • Combine ICP + multimodal for tailored therapy.
