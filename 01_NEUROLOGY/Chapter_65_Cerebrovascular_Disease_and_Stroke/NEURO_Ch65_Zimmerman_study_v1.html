1 · Cerebral Arteriopathy — Zimmerman's Framework

VIPS study — landmark for pediatric stroke arteriopathy
  • Vascular Effects of Infection in Pediatric Stroke (VIPS) study — multicenter prospective cohort.
  • Definite or possible arteriopathy on vascular imaging in 46% of all pediatric AIS patients.
  • 55% of previously healthy children (no other risk factor identified) had arteriopathy.
  • Arteriopathy is a major predictor of primary and recurrent stroke in childhood.
  • Reinforces that vascular imaging (MRA, CTA, or DSA) is essential in EVERY pediatric AIS workup.
ICU layer: Even when a child has "another" risk factor (e.g., CHD), arteriopathy may still be present and drive recurrence — image the vessels.
Cerebral arteriopathy spectrum
  • Focal cerebral arteriopathy (FCA): ~25% of childhood AIS arteriopathies; discrete intracranial stenosis (typically distal ICA/proximal MCA); often unilateral.
    • Strongly associated with varicella zoster virus (VZV) infection.
    • Transient cerebral arteriopathy (TCA) — FCA that improves or stabilizes by 6 months.
    • Progressive FCA — uncommon; may evolve toward moyamoya pattern.
  • Cervicocephalic arterial dissection (CCAD): extracranial carotid or vertebral; spontaneous or post-traumatic; ~10–20% of pediatric AIS.
  • Moyamoya disease/syndrome: progressive bilateral distal ICA stenosis with collateral "puff of smoke."
  • Primary CNS angiitis (cPACNS): small or large vessel; biopsy may be needed for small-vessel form.
  • Secondary vasculitis: systemic disease (SLE, Takayasu, PAN, Behçet's, Susac).
  • Post-infectious vasculopathy: bacterial meningitis, TB, fungal, mycoplasma; varicella; HIV.
  • Sickle cell-related vasculopathy: progressive large-vessel disease.
  • Radiation-induced vasculopathy: chronic; after cranial irradiation.
  • Genetic vasculopathies: NF1, ACTA2, COL4A1, fibromuscular dysplasia.
Risk factor framework (Zimmerman Box 66.1)
  • Cerebral arteriopathy: CCAD, moyamoya, FCA, cPACNS, secondary vasculitis, varicella-associated.
  • Hematologic: sickle cell, iron deficiency anemia, thrombocytosis, leukemia.
  • Heart disease: congenital (especially cyanotic, post-Fontan), acquired.
  • Infection: bacterial meningitis, varicella zoster, mycotic and TB meningitis.
  • Hypercoagulable state: protein C/S deficiency, antithrombin III deficiency, antiphospholipid syndrome, hyperhomocysteinemia, factor V Leiden, prothrombin G20210A, elevated lipoprotein(a).
  • Drugs: asparaginase (oncology), estrogen, cocaine, methamphetamine, cannabis (synthetic).
  • Inflammatory/autoimmune: SLE, rheumatoid arthritis, vasculitis.
  • Approximately half of all pediatric AIS have multifactorial etiology.

2 · Acute Stroke Pathophysiology — Penumbra Concept

Core, penumbra, oligemia
  • Ischemic core: CBF below critical threshold → cell death; "no-flow" region; not salvageable.
  • Penumbra: surrounding area with reduced CBF but viable; salvageable with prompt reperfusion.
  • Oligemia: mildly reduced CBF, no functional impairment; vulnerable to subsequent insult.
  • Goal of reperfusion (tPA, thrombectomy): rescue penumbra by restoring blood flow before it becomes core.
  • Infarct size depends on: extent and duration of ischemia, CPP maintenance, collateral circulation.
  • Fever increases infarct size — aggressive normothermia.
  • Energy failure causes neuronal hyperexcitability → seizures (including status epilepticus); cEEG monitoring; no prophylactic ASM but treat seizures aggressively.

3 · Initial Workup — Zimmerman's Approach

Clinical assessment
  • History: predisposing factors, time last seen well (LSW), recent trauma or infection, medications, family history.
  • Baseline neurologic exam with PedNIHSS (Pediatric NIH Stroke Scale).
  • Anterior circulation signs: hemiparesis, hemisensory loss, aphasia, visual field cuts with gaze preference, neglect.
  • Posterior circulation signs: ataxia, vomiting, vertigo, dysarthria, diplopia, dysmetria; cerebellar stroke nonspecific (HA, vomiting).
  • 10–20% of pediatric AIS heralded by seizures — new deficit after seizure may be mistaken for Todd's paresis.
Imaging
  • Rapid stroke MRI protocol (20–30 min): DWI/ADC, FLAIR, GRE/SWI, MRA head and neck, MRV if CSVT suspected.
  • FLAIR less useful in < 2 y due to immature myelination.
  • MRA detects arteriopathy in 50–75% of acute AIS — but cannot resolve smaller arteries.
  • Vessel wall imaging — emerging; evaluates wall inflammation rather than lumen.
  • CTA — alternative; faster but radiation and sedation.
  • Catheter angiography (DSA) — gold standard for small-vessel arteriopathy; also therapeutic (thrombectomy).
  • Sedation considerations: maintain MAP to optimize brain perfusion in setting of arteriopathy.
Laboratory workup (Zimmerman Box 66.2)
  • Initial urgent: CBC, electrolytes, BUN, creatinine, glucose, PT/PTT/INR, ESR, blood gas, type & screen.
  • If on anticoagulation: LMWH anti-Xa, warfarin INR.
  • If thrombolysis considered: fibrinogen.
  • Clinically directed: pregnancy test (childbearing age), toxicology screen.
  • SCD-specific: HbS percentage, reticulocyte count, type for sickle-negative antigen-matched blood.
  • Hypercoagulable workup (delayed if not urgent): protein C/S, antithrombin III, factor V Leiden, prothrombin G20210A, antiphospholipid antibodies (ACL IgM/IgG/IgA, anti-β2GP1, lupus anticoagulant), homocysteine, lipoprotein(a), MTHFR (if homocysteine elevated).
  • Vasculitis workup: ESR, CRP, ANA, ANCA, complement, lupus inhibitor.
  • LP: if cPACNS suspected; delay if raised ICP risk.
  • Echo (TTE → TEE if needed): for cardioembolic source.

4 · Acute Stroke Treatment — Zimmerman's Detail

Supportive care principles
  • PICU admission for ≥ 48 h for neurologic and medical monitoring.
  • Hourly pediatric GCS + pupils by bedside nurse; serial PedNIHSS by medical staff.
  • Intubate if airway threatened, inadequate respiratory drive, or for procedures.
  • HOB flat may optimize CBF (controversial in some settings); HOB 30° in raised ICP.
  • Permissive hypertension in AIS — transient HTN often compensatory; treat only if extreme (SBP > 220, DBP > 120 or per institutional protocol).
  • Treat clinical and electrographic seizures aggressively; no prophylactic ASM.
  • Aggressive fever control to normothermia — but NO cooling blankets in SCD (precipitate sickling).
  • Urgent imaging for any neurologic deterioration.
Recanalization therapies — Zimmerman's nuances
  • AHA/ASA: "hyperacute therapies for AIS remain controversial" in pediatrics.
  • IV tPA:
    • Use with great caution < 2 years.
    • Avoid in neonates.
    • Older children/adolescents: case-by-case after multidisciplinary review.
    • Risk of symptomatic ICH following IV tPA in appropriately selected children appears low.
    • Time window 4.5 h (extrapolated from adult).
  • Mechanical thrombectomy:
    • Standard of care in adults for proximal LVO within 6 h (or up to 24 h with mismatch).
    • Increasingly performed in children with reports of good outcome (no RCT).
    • AHA 2019: reasonable to limit to children with LVO and disabling deficit, performed by experienced pediatric team.
    • Time window for pediatric thrombectomy not formally defined.
    • Particularly consider in older adolescents and basilar artery occlusion.
SCD acute stroke management — Zimmerman emphasis
  • Emergent erythrocyte transfusion: exchange to HbS < 30% target.
  • Simple transfusion if exchange unavailable, to Hb 10 g/dL (avoid > 13 — hyperviscosity risk).
  • O₂ saturation > 93% through completion of transfusion and first night (nocturnal hypoxemia risk).
  • Cardiology consultation if cardiac dysfunction suspected before exchange.
  • NO cooling blankets — can precipitate sickling.
  • tPA in SCD: no pediatric data; pathophysiology may not respond to thrombolysis; generally avoid.
  • Risk factors for SCD stroke: elevated BP, low Hb, high WBC, prior TIA, history of meningitis, presentation with seizure, surgery, priapism, recent acute chest syndrome, recent transfusion, history of overt or silent stroke.
Antithrombotic therapy — Zimmerman pharmacology detail
  • Unfractionated heparin (UFH): continuous IV infusion; titrate by aPTT (1.5–2.5× baseline) or anti-Xa; reversible by protamine.
  • LMWH (enoxaparin): more predictable pharmacokinetics; reduced HIT risk; renal clearance — adjust in renal impairment. Anti-Xa monitoring 4 h post-dose; therapeutic 0.5–1.0 IU/mL.
  • Warfarin: difficult pharmacokinetics in children; multiple drug-diet interactions; INR target 2–3 (or 2.5–3.5 for mechanical valve).
  • DOACs: not yet widely used in pediatric stroke; ongoing studies.
  • Aspirin: 3–5 mg/kg/day for stroke prevention; doses below Reye syndrome risk.
  • Heparin-induced thrombocytopenia (HIT): antibody-mediated complication; check platelet count; switch to direct thrombin inhibitor (argatroban, bivalirudin) if HIT confirmed.
Indications by etiology (Chest guidelines / AHA 2019)
  • Cardioembolic: anticoagulation typically initiated (mechanical valve, mural thrombus, cardiomyopathy).
  • Cervical artery dissection (extracranial): anticoagulation 3–6 months per Chest guidelines; AHA considers controversial — antiplatelet alternative.
  • Intracranial dissection: antiplatelet preferred (rupture risk with anticoagulation).
  • Hypercoagulable state (antiphospholipid syndrome, persistent recurrence): anticoagulation.
  • FCA/post-varicella, cryptogenic: aspirin.
  • Moyamoya: aspirin until surgical revascularization.
  • CSVT: anticoagulation 3–6 months even with hemorrhagic infarct (deVeber NEJM 2001).

5 · Decompressive Hemicraniectomy in Pediatric AIS

Indication
  • Malignant MCA infarction with mass effect and impending herniation.
  • Urgent neurosurgical consultation as soon as malignant pattern emerging.
  • Ojemann's perspective: ICP monitoring not helpful and may DELAY surgical treatment.
  • Adult RCT pooled data (DECIMAL/DESTINY/HAMLET) support within 48 h (Vahedi 2007).
  • Pediatric outcomes generally better than adults due to neuroplasticity.
  • Suboccipital craniectomy with dural expansion for cerebellar infarct with mass effect.
Technique notes
  • Wide hemicraniectomy (≥ 12 cm diameter) with duraplasty.
  • Preserve bone flap (sterile freezer or abdominal subcutaneous pocket) for cranioplasty 6–12 weeks later.
  • Postoperative: monitor for swelling through defect; expect mass effect resolution; cranioplasty timing depends on swelling resolution.

6 · Cerebral Sinus Venous Thrombosis — Zimmerman Detail

Venous anatomy involved
  • Dural sinuses: superior sagittal, transverse, sigmoid.
  • Deep cerebral venous system: internal cerebral veins, vein of Galen, straight sinus.
  • Cortical veins.
  • Multiple sinuses involved in most pediatric cases.
  • Transverse sinus most commonly affected, followed by sagittal sinus.
Complications of CSVT
  • Venous infarction (often hemorrhagic — does NOT preclude anticoagulation).
  • Intracranial hypertension.
  • Cranial nerve palsies (especially CN VI from raised ICP).
  • Cerebral hemorrhage.
  • Seizures (especially with parasagittal cortical involvement).
  • Vision loss from prolonged papilledema.
Predisposing factors
  • Local: mastoiditis, sinusitis, otitis, meningitis, trauma, dehydration.
  • Systemic hypercoagulable state.
  • Nephrotic syndrome (loss of antithrombin III in urine).
  • IBD, malignancy.
  • Estrogen-containing oral contraceptives.
  • Neonates: birth-related, dehydration, perinatal asphyxia.
  • Recent intracranial surgery.
  • Polycythemia.

7 · Intracerebral Hemorrhage — Surgical Perspective

Etiologies of pediatric ICH
  • AVM — most common cause of spontaneous pediatric ICH.
  • Cavernous malformation.
  • Aneurysm (1–2% of pediatric ICH); often mycotic from endocarditis, traumatic, or familial.
  • Coagulopathy (hemophilia, ITP, leukemia, drug-induced).
  • Hypertensive — uncommon in pediatrics; suggests renovascular disease or coarctation.
  • Hemorrhagic conversion of AIS.
  • Tumor hemorrhage.
  • Vascular malformations other than AVM (DAVF, capillary telangiectasia, developmental venous anomaly).
Acute management
  • ABCs, isotonic IVF, BP control: target SBP < 160 mm Hg or institutional protocol; avoid hypotension.
  • Reverse coagulopathy: vitamin K, PCC, FFP, platelets; specific reversal for DOACs.
  • EVD if hydrocephalus from IVH.
  • Antifibrinolytics controversial in pediatric ICH (CRASH-3 evidence is for TBI).
  • Surgical evacuation: consider for expanding hematoma, mass effect, posterior fossa hematoma with brainstem compression.
  • Vascular workup: CTA acutely; conventional angiogram for definitive workup if AVM/aneurysm suspected.
AVM management (neurosurgery emphasis)
  • Spetzler-Martin grading (1–5): based on size, eloquence of brain, deep venous drainage.
  • Grade 1–3: surgical resection often feasible.
  • Grade 4–5: combination embolization + radiosurgery + selective surgery.
  • Stereotactic radiosurgery (Gamma Knife, CyberKnife): for small-to-medium deep AVMs; obliteration over 2–3 years.
  • Endovascular embolization: adjunct to surgery or radiosurgery; rarely curative alone.
  • Rupture risk per year ~2–4%; cumulative lifetime risk significant in young children.
  • ARUBA trial (adult): unruptured AVM medical management vs intervention — controversial; pediatric data limited.
Cavernous malformation
  • "Popcorn" lesion on MRI with surrounding hemosiderin (best seen on SWI/GRE).
  • Sporadic or familial (KRIT1/CCM1, CCM2, PDCD10/CCM3 mutations).
  • Surgical resection for symptomatic lesions (seizures, hemorrhage, focal deficit).
  • Annual rehemorrhage risk after first bleed: 4–5%.
Aneurysm in pediatrics
  • Rare; often arise differently than adults (more posterior circulation, more giant aneurysms).
  • Causes: mycotic (endocarditis), traumatic, familial (5–10%), connective tissue disease (Marfan, Ehlers-Danlos, fibromuscular dysplasia).
  • Treatment: endovascular coiling (often preferred), microsurgical clipping, flow diversion, or combination.
  • Vasospasm management: nimodipine, induced hypertension, intra-arterial vasodilators.

11 · SAQs (15)

SAQ 1. VIPS study — key findings.

  • Vascular Effects of Infection in Pediatric Stroke (multicenter cohort).
  • Arteriopathy in 46% of all pediatric AIS; 55% of previously healthy children.
  • Recent minor infection (URI within 1 week) tripled stroke risk.
  • Justifies vascular imaging in every pediatric AIS workup.

SAQ 2. Define focal cerebral arteriopathy and its post-varicella variant.

  • FCA: discrete intracranial stenosis, unilateral, typically distal ICA/proximal MCA.
  • ~25% of pediatric AIS arteriopathies.
  • Post-VZV/varicella common trigger.
  • Transient cerebral arteriopathy (TCA): subset that improves/stabilizes by 6 months.
  • Treatment: ASA ≥ 12 months; serial MRA.

SAQ 3. When is IV tPA considered in pediatric AIS per Zimmerman/AHA?

  • Older children/adolescents, case-by-case after multidisciplinary review.
  • Within 4.5 h of LSW; large-vessel AIS; PedNIHSS-quantified deficit.
  • Avoid < 2 years; avoid neonates; avoid SCD.
  • Risk of symptomatic ICH appears low in appropriately selected patients.

SAQ 4. SCD acute AIS — Zimmerman targets and cautions.

  • Emergent exchange transfusion to HbS < 30%.
  • Simple transfusion to Hb 10 g/dL if exchange unavailable (NOT above 10 — hyperviscosity).
  • O₂ saturation > 93% through completion and first night (nocturnal hypoxemia).
  • NO cooling blanket — precipitates sickling.
  • Avoid tPA.

SAQ 5. Decompressive hemicraniectomy — Ojemann emphasis.

  • Indicated for malignant MCA infarct with mass effect.
  • ICP monitoring not helpful and may DELAY surgery — do not let it delay.
  • Wide hemicraniectomy ≥ 12 cm + duraplasty.
  • Within 48 h (DECIMAL/DESTINY/HAMLET pooled adult data).
  • Pediatric outcomes better than adults (neuroplasticity).
  • Cranioplasty 6–12 weeks later.

SAQ 6. Penumbra concept.

  • Core: no flow, cell death, not salvageable.
  • Penumbra: low flow, viable, salvageable with prompt reperfusion.
  • Oligemia: mild flow reduction, functional, vulnerable.
  • tPA/thrombectomy aim to rescue penumbra.
  • Modifiers: CPP, collaterals, fever, ischemia duration.

SAQ 7. LMWH advantages over UFH in pediatric anticoagulation.

  • More predictable pharmacokinetics.
  • Less risk of HIT.
  • Easier outpatient administration (SC).
  • Anti-Xa monitoring straightforward.
  • Disadvantages: renal clearance (adjust in CKD); not as rapidly reversible.

SAQ 8. Heparin-induced thrombocytopenia (HIT).

  • Antibody-mediated complication of UFH (and rarely LMWH).
  • Thrombocytopenia + thrombosis (paradoxical).
  • 4T score for clinical probability.
  • Stop heparin immediately; switch to direct thrombin inhibitor (argatroban, bivalirudin).
  • Avoid warfarin acutely (warfarin-induced skin necrosis with HIT).

SAQ 9. CSVT venous anatomy commonly involved.

  • Multiple sinuses involved in most pediatric cases.
  • Transverse sinus most common, then sagittal sinus.
  • Deep venous system: internal cerebral veins, vein of Galen, straight sinus.
  • Cortical veins.
  • Complications: venous infarct, ICH, raised ICP, cranial nerve palsies, vision loss.

SAQ 10. Spetzler-Martin AVM grade components.

  • Size: 1 point < 3 cm; 2 points 3–6 cm; 3 points > 6 cm.
  • Eloquence of brain: 0 non-eloquent; 1 eloquent.
  • Deep venous drainage: 0 no; 1 yes.
  • Total grade 1–5; higher grades = higher operative risk.

SAQ 11. Pediatric aneurysm features distinct from adult.

  • Rare in children.
  • More common posterior circulation than adults.
  • More giant aneurysms (> 25 mm).
  • Causes: mycotic (endocarditis), traumatic, familial (5–10%), connective tissue disease (Marfan, Ehlers-Danlos, FMD).
  • Treatment: endovascular coiling often preferred; clipping; flow diversion.

SAQ 12. Why aggressive fever control in AIS?

  • Fever increases metabolic demand on penumbra.
  • Increases infarct size.
  • Worsens outcome.
  • Target normothermia; avoid cooling blankets in SCD.

SAQ 13. When can anticoagulation be considered despite hemorrhagic conversion or hemorrhagic infarct?

  • CSVT with hemorrhagic venous infarct — anticoagulation still indicated (deVeber NEJM 2001).
  • Cardioembolic stroke with limited petechial hemorrhage may still warrant anticoagulation after multidisciplinary discussion.
  • Withhold anticoagulation acutely in significant ICH; reassess after stability.

SAQ 14. Cavernous malformation MRI signature.

  • "Popcorn" lesion on T2/SWI/GRE.
  • Surrounding hemosiderin (blooming on SWI).
  • Often multiple in familial form.
  • Annual rehemorrhage risk after first bleed: 4–5%.

SAQ 15. Top 5 pitfalls in pediatric AIS care.

  • Missing the diagnosis — attributing to migraine, postictal paresis, or "behavioral."
  • Aggressive BP lowering in acute AIS (worsens penumbra).
  • Cooling blankets in SCD patient.
  • Delaying decompressive surgery while obtaining ICP monitor for malignant MCA.
  • Failing to obtain vascular imaging — missing arteriopathy that predicts recurrence.

12 · PICU Cases (15)

Case 1. 7-year-old previously healthy with sudden right hemiparesis 2 h ago; MRA shows distal left ICA narrowing.

  • FCA — likely post-VZV; ask varicella history.
  • ASA 3–5 mg/kg/day for ≥ 12 months.
  • Repeat MRA at 3–6 months.
  • Acyclovir if active VZV; check VZV PCR.
  • Hypercoag panel; vasculitis panel.

Case 2. 10-year-old HbSS with new aphasia.

  • Exchange transfusion to HbS < 30% emergently.
  • Avoid tPA; avoid cooling blanket.
  • Maintain O₂ > 93% through transfusion and first night.
  • Chronic transfusion plan; HSCT discussion.

Case 3. 14-year-old post-football tackle, ataxia + vertigo.

  • Vertebral artery dissection.
  • MRA neck + T1 fat-sat (crescent sign).
  • Anticoagulation 3–6 months (Chest) — discuss with neurosurgery if intracranial extension.
  • Connective tissue disease workup (EDS, Marfan).

Case 4. 11-year-old post-Fontan with new focal seizure and right hemiparesis.

  • Cardioembolic AIS.
  • MRI + MRA.
  • Anticoagulation per cardiology.
  • Echo with bubble study.
  • Treat seizures; cEEG.

Case 5. 8-year-old M1 occlusion on CTA at 5 h post-LSW with NIHSS 16.

  • LVO eligible for thrombectomy at experienced center.
  • tPA window now closed (> 4.5 h).
  • Multidisciplinary stroke team discussion.
  • Perfusion imaging for penumbra assessment.

Case 6. 12-year-old malignant left MCA infarct with midline shift 7 mm at 24 h.

  • Emergent decompressive hemicraniectomy + duraplasty.
  • Do NOT delay for ICP monitor.
  • Wide craniectomy ≥ 12 cm.
  • Cranioplasty at 6–12 weeks.
  • Aggressive rehab.

Case 7. 15-year-old thunderclap headache; CT shows SAH; CTA shows 7 mm anterior communicating aneurysm.

  • Activate neurosurgery; BP < 160.
  • Endovascular coiling vs surgical clipping discussion.
  • Nimodipine for vasospasm prophylaxis.
  • EVD if hydrocephalus from IVH.
  • Watch days 4–14 for delayed cerebral ischemia.

Case 8. 6-year-old with cerebellar infarct + mass effect on 4th ventricle.

  • Surgical emergency — posterior fossa decompression with suboccipital craniectomy + dural expansion.
  • EVD for hydrocephalus.
  • Aspirin post-op per etiology.
  • MRA neck for vertebral dissection.

Case 9. 10-year-old with nephrotic syndrome, headache + papilledema.

  • CSVT — MRV.
  • Anticoagulation 3–6 months even with hemorrhagic infarct (deVeber).
  • Treat nephrotic syndrome (loss of AT III).
  • Hydration; treat raised ICP.

Case 10. 9-year-old with sudden severe headache; CT shows lobar ICH; CTA shows AVM Spetzler-Martin grade 3.

  • Stabilize; BP < 160.
  • Reverse coagulopathy if applicable.
  • Surgical evacuation if mass effect.
  • AVM management: multidisciplinary plan — surgery + embolization + radiosurgery.
  • Long-term follow-up.

Case 11. 13-year-old with bacterial endocarditis, mycotic aneurysm.

  • Septic embolic AIS + mycotic aneurysm risk.
  • Antibiotics for endocarditis.
  • Endovascular treatment of aneurysm if accessible.
  • Anticoagulation generally AVOIDED (mycotic aneurysm hemorrhage risk).
  • Cardiology + neurosurgery + ID multidisciplinary.

Case 12. 15-year-old female on OCP with thunderclap headache.

  • Differential: SAH (aneurysm), RCVS, CSVT.
  • CT then CTA/MRA; MRV for CSVT.
  • Discontinue OCP.
  • If CSVT: anticoagulation.
  • If RCVS: nimodipine, supportive.

Case 13. 14-year-old with antiphospholipid syndrome and second AIS.

  • Switch from ASA to anticoagulation (warfarin or LMWH).
  • Confirm: lupus anticoagulant, anti-β2GP1, anticardiolipin — three positive on two occasions 12 weeks apart.
  • Rheumatology + hematology consult.

Case 14. 3-year-old with multifocal AIS, raised inflammatory markers, lymphocytic CSF.

  • cPACNS (primary CNS angiitis).
  • Empiric high-dose steroids + IVIG.
  • Brain + meningeal biopsy if small-vessel form suspected and diagnosis uncertain.
  • Cyclophosphamide or rituximab for large-vessel form; rituximab/MMF maintenance.
  • Long-term rheumatology follow-up.

Case 15. 7-year-old with multiple cavernous malformations on family history; new seizure.

  • Familial cavernous malformation (KRIT1/CCM1, CCM2, PDCD10).
  • Genetic counseling for family.
  • Annual MRI surveillance.
  • Surgical resection for symptomatic lesions.
  • Antiepileptic therapy.

Arteriopathy emphasis

  • VIPS: arteriopathy in 46% of AIS; 55% of previously healthy.
  • Vascular imaging mandatory in every pediatric AIS.
  • FCA most common arteriopathy (~25%); VZV trigger common.

Acute care

  • Rapid MRI (20–30 min protocol).
  • Permissive HTN; aggressive normothermia.
  • cEEG if seizures; no prophylactic ASM.
  • PICU ≥ 48 h with PedNIHSS, GCS, pupils hourly.

Reperfusion

  • tPA case-by-case < 4.5 h in older children; avoid < 2 y, avoid SCD.
  • Thrombectomy for LVO at experienced pediatric center.
  • SCD: exchange transfusion to HbS < 30%.

Antithrombotic by etiology

  • Cardioembolic / dissection (extracranial) / CSVT / APS: anticoagulation.
  • Intracranial dissection / FCA / moyamoya / cryptogenic: ASA.
  • SCD: transfusion (not antithrombotic).

Surgical (Ojemann)

  • Malignant MCA: hemicraniectomy < 48 h; don't wait for ICP monitor.
  • Cerebellar infarct: suboccipital decompression.
  • ICH: surgical evacuation for mass effect; CTA for AVM/aneurysm.
  • Spetzler-Martin grade guides AVM management.

Exam pearls

  • Vascular imaging in EVERY pediatric AIS.
  • SCD: exchange to HbS < 30%, NOT tPA, NOT cooling.
  • Anticoagulation in CSVT even with hemorrhagic infarct.
  • Don't delay decompressive surgery for ICP monitoring.
  • Fever increases infarct size — aggressive normothermia.
