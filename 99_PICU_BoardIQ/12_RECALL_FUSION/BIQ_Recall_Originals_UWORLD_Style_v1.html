Question 1 — TBI & Therapeutic Hypothermia (BTF 2019)

Q1. Child with severe TBI. According to current TBI guidelines, list TWO recommendations about therapeutic hypothermia.
Tested concept: BTF 2019 Tier 2 — role of hypothermia in pediatric severe TBI. Knowledge of Hutchison 2008 and Cool Kids 2013 negative trials.
Hidden clue: "Two recommendations" implies a guideline statement, not pathophysiology. Examiners want explicit BTF language.
Step-by-step reasoning:
  • Hutchison 2008 (NEJM): hypothermia 32.5°C × 24 h after pediatric severe TBI — no mortality/outcome benefit; trend toward harm.
  • Adelson 2013 (Cool Kids): hypothermia 32–33°C × 48 h — stopped early; no benefit.
  • BTF 2019 (Kochanek PCCM 2019) therefore recommends: (a) prophylactic hypothermia is NOT recommended for neuroprotection; (b) moderate hypothermia (32–34°C) may be used as a Tier 2 option for refractory ICH only; (c) if used, slow rewarming < 0.5°C/h.
  • Aggressive normothermia (36–37°C) IS recommended — fever worsens secondary injury.
Best answer (any two):
  • Prophylactic hypothermia is NOT recommended to improve neurologic outcome in pediatric severe TBI.
  • Moderate hypothermia (32–34°C) may be considered for refractory intracranial hypertension as a Tier 2 measure (ICP control only).
  • If used, rewarming should be slow (< 0.5°C per hour) to avoid rebound ICH.
  • Strict avoidance of hyperthermia (fever) — targeted normothermia 36–37°C — is recommended.
Common wrong answers:
  • "Cool to 32°C for 48 h for neuroprotection" — directly contradicted by Hutchison/Cool Kids.
  • "Hypothermia improves mortality" — false; trials are negative.
  • "Rewarm rapidly to euthermia" — risks rebound ICH; must be slow.
Rogers/Zimmerman anchor: Rogers Ch 62 (Head and Spinal Cord Trauma) + Zimmerman Ch 118 (TBI). BTF 2019 Tier 2.
Exam trap: Conflating cardiac-arrest TTM evidence (THAPCA — neutral) with TBI hypothermia evidence (negative). Hypothermia is fever control, NOT neuroprotection in pediatric TBI.
Pearl: "Cool to control ICP; warm to control nothing." In pediatric TBI, target strict normothermia — never use hypothermia as primary neuroprotection.

Confidence: HIGH

Easier variant

Harder SEAP variant

Question 2 — Pseudocholinesterase Deficiency

Q2. Postoperative patient suspected to have pseudocholinesterase deficiency. What (aside from physical exam) can you do in the ICU to test?
Tested concept: Diagnosis of plasma cholinesterase deficiency producing prolonged paralysis after succinylcholine or mivacurium.
Hidden clue: "Postoperative" + "aside from physical exam" → the examiner wants a laboratory or bedside test, not just clinical observation.
Reasoning:
  • Suspect when prolonged paralysis (> 8–10 min) after sux or mivacurium.
  • Bedside: train-of-four (TOF) with peripheral nerve stimulator — confirms ongoing neuromuscular blockade (fade pattern).
  • Laboratory: plasma pseudocholinesterase (BChE) activity assay.
  • Definitive: dibucaine number (% inhibition of BChE by dibucaine; normal ~80, heterozygote ~50, homozygote ~20). Also fluoride number.
  • Genotyping (BCHE gene) confirms and counsels family.
Best answer:
  • Peripheral nerve stimulator (TOF) at bedside — documents residual NMB.
  • Plasma pseudocholinesterase activity (serum BChE level).
  • Dibucaine number to identify atypical variants.
  • Genetic testing (BCHE gene) for confirmation and family counseling.
Wrong answers:
  • "Sugammadex reversal trial" — does NOT reverse depolarizing (sux) blockade; only aminosteroid agents.
  • "Edrophonium test" — that diagnoses myasthenia, not BChE deficiency.
  • "CK level" — non-specific; rules out malignant hyperthermia / rhabdo, not BChE.
Anchor: Rogers anesthesia/PNS chapters; Zimmerman pharmacology. RCPSC PICU pharmacology yield.
Trap: Confusing with malignant hyperthermia (also OR emergency but different — rigidity, hyperthermia, hypercarbia; treat with dantrolene). Pseudocholinesterase deficiency = paralysis only, no metabolic crisis.
Pearl: Prolonged sux paralysis with normal vital signs = BChE deficiency. Supportive ventilation + sedation until block resolves (often 3–8 h); send BChE assay + dibucaine number.

Confidence: HIGH

Easier variant

Question 3 — Serotonin Syndrome

Q3. Patient with myoclonus, hyperreflexia, autonomic instability, on an SSRI. Most likely diagnosis? Name one non-supportive therapy.
Tested concept: Recognition and pharmacotherapy of serotonin syndrome (Hunter criteria).
Hidden clue: Triad of neuromuscular excitation (myoclonus, clonus, hyperreflexia) + autonomic + mental status on SSRI = serotonin syndrome. Examiners want cyproheptadine.
Reasoning:
  • Hunter criteria: serotonergic agent + ONE of spontaneous clonus / inducible clonus + agitation or diaphoresis / ocular clonus + agitation or diaphoresis / tremor + hyperreflexia / hypertonia + temp > 38 + clonus.
  • Differentiate from NMS (rigidity, hyporeflexia, neuroleptic exposure) — opposite reflex pattern.
  • Acute care: stop serotonergic agents; sedate with BZD; aggressive cooling and IV fluids; secure airway if needed.
  • Non-supportive (disease-modifying) therapy: cyproheptadine — 5-HT2A antagonist, 4–8 mg PO/NG q4–6 h up to 32 mg/day.
  • Avoid bromocriptine (worsens), avoid dantrolene (no role unless concomitant rhabdo), avoid succinylcholine if intubating (use roc).
Best answer: Cyproheptadine (5-HT2A antagonist), 4–8 mg via NG q4–6 h.
Wrong answers:
  • Bromocriptine — used in NMS, worsens serotonin syndrome.
  • Dantrolene — for malignant hyperthermia / severe NMS, not serotonin syndrome.
  • Physostigmine — for anticholinergic toxicity.
  • Plasmapheresis — not indicated.
Anchor: Rogers Ch on toxicology + neuro emergencies. Zimmerman serotonin physiology.
Trap: Confusing with NMS or malignant hyperthermia. Key discriminator: hyperreflexia + clonus + lower-limb prominent = serotonin; rigidity + hyporeflexia = NMS.
Pearl: "Clonus + clonus + clonus + SSRI → cyproheptadine." Lower-limb clonus more prominent than upper.

Confidence: HIGH

Question 4 — Paradoxical Breathing in Neuromuscular Disease

Q4. Explain the pathophysiology of paradoxical breathing in a child with neuromuscular disease.
Tested concept: Mechanics of diaphragm vs intercostal weakness; abdominal–thoracic dissociation.
Hidden clue: "Paradoxical" = abdomen and chest move out of phase during inspiration. Examiner wants a physiology answer, not a list.
Reasoning:
  • Normal inspiration: diaphragm descends → abdominal contents pushed down → abdomen rises; simultaneously intercostals expand rib cage outward → chest rises (synchronous outward motion).
  • Diaphragm weakness (eg phrenic injury, high cervical cord, advanced NMD): intercostals still contract creating negative thoracic pressure, but the weak diaphragm gets sucked upward into the chest → abdomen retracts inward during inspiration → paradox.
  • Intercostal weakness (eg low-cord injury, DMD): diaphragm contracts; intercostals fail to stabilize rib cage → chest wall retracts inward while abdomen rises (Hoover sign in COPD analog).
  • Position-dependent worsening: supine posture loads abdominal contents on diaphragm → worse paradox supine.
  • Hallmark of respiratory failure imminent; signals need for NIV or intubation.
Best answer: Loss of synchronous coordination between diaphragm and intercostals due to focal weakness. When the diaphragm is weak, intercostal-generated negative intrathoracic pressure pulls the diaphragm cephalad, causing the abdomen to retract inward during inspiration; when intercostals are weak, diaphragmatic descent without rib-cage stabilization causes inward chest-wall motion. Both reflect impending hypoventilation and tidal-volume failure.
Anchor: Rogers respiratory mechanics + neuromuscular chapters; Zimmerman pulmonary physiology.
Trap: Assuming all paradoxical breathing is upper-airway obstruction. In NMD, it is muscular dyssynchrony, not airway resistance.
Pearl: Supine-worsened paradoxical breathing in NMD = bilateral diaphragm weakness; needs urgent NIV or intubation.

Confidence: HIGH

Easier variant

Question 5 — Avoiding Vasopressors in Recovering TBI

Q5. Child with TBI, days out, ICP < 15, good markers of CO. Staff wants to start vasopressor — you do not. Give two reasons.
Tested concept: CPP/MAP targets are floors, not goals; vasopressor harms when ICP and CO are controlled.
Hidden clue: ICP is controlled (< 15) — so CPP is already adequate. Excess MAP would harm.
Reasoning:
  • CPP = MAP − ICP. With ICP 15 and age-appropriate MAP, CPP is already at or above target.
  • Driving MAP higher pushes more CPP than needed → risks hyperemia, BBB disruption, vasogenic edema, hemorrhagic conversion, ARDS if vasopressor with fluids.
  • Vasopressors carry direct risks: arrhythmia, peripheral ischemia, increased myocardial oxygen demand, splanchnic ischemia.
  • In recovery phase, autoregulation may be returning — overshoot now causes pressure-passive flow swings.
  • Evidence (Lewis pediatric TBI cohorts; Adelson 2003) — supraphysiologic CPP does not improve outcome and may cause ARDS (especially with adult-pattern hyperosmolar + vasopressor combinations).
Best answer (any two):
  • CPP is already adequate (ICP 15 + age-appropriate MAP) — no physiologic benefit from further pressor escalation.
  • Supraphysiologic CPP risks hyperemic edema, BBB disruption, hemorrhagic conversion, and worsens secondary injury.
  • Vasopressor toxicity — arrhythmia, peripheral ischemia, increased myocardial work, splanchnic hypoperfusion.
  • Risk of ARDS (especially with concurrent volume + pressor in the recovering brain).
  • Possible interference with returning autoregulation — pressure-passive flow swings.
Wrong reasons given by candidates:
  • "Hyperkalemia risk" — not specific to vasopressor.
  • "Renal injury" — generic; not the strongest answer.
Anchor: Rogers Ch 59 (ICP monitoring), Zimmerman Ch 63 supplement (ICP/multimodal monitoring), BTF 2019.
Trap: Belief that "higher CPP = better outcome." CPP target is a floor, not a goal. Above ~70 in adults / age-appropriate in children, harm rises.
Pearl: Don't chase CPP for its own sake. If ICP is controlled and CO is good, your CPP is adequate.

Confidence: HIGH

Question 6 — Post-op Brain Tumor + GTC Seizure on LR

Q6. Post-partial-resection brain tumor on Ringer's lactate has a generalized tonic-clonic seizure. List 3 possible reasons and 2 stat investigations.
Tested concept: Differential of post-craniotomy seizure — fluid choice + metabolic + structural causes.
Hidden clue: "Straight Ringer's lactate" = hypotonic-relative (Na 130, no dextrose) — sets up hyponatremia and hypoglycemia.
Reasoning:
  • LR is mildly hypotonic (Na 130) and contains no dextrose — risks of hyponatremia (especially with SIADH from tumor/surgery) and hypoglycemia (especially in infant/young child).
  • Post-surgical seizure differential: (a) metabolic — hyponatremia, hypoglycemia, hypocalcemia, hypomagnesemia; (b) structural — post-op hematoma, tension pneumocephalus, infarction, residual tumor; (c) infectious/inflammatory — meningitis, brain abscess; (d) drug — withdrawal of AEDs, anesthetic-related, opioid-related; (e) tumor — recurrence, edema.
Best answers — 3 reasons:
  • Hyponatremia (SIADH or LR-induced) — most common.
  • Post-op hematoma or cerebral edema causing cortical irritation.
  • Hypoglycemia (LR contains no dextrose; young child).
  • (Alternates: pneumocephalus, AED withdrawal, hypocalcemia, infection.)
Two stat investigations:
  • Stat point-of-care blood glucose and electrolytes (Na, Ca, Mg).
  • Stat non-contrast CT head — look for hematoma, pneumocephalus, hydrocephalus, edema.
  • (Add EEG and serum AED levels next.)
Wrong answers:
  • "MRI" — not stat option; CT is first.
  • "LP" — contraindicated until CT excludes mass effect / shift.
Anchor: Rogers Ch 61 (Neurosurgical Conditions) + Zimmerman Ch 59 (Neurosurgery supplement).
Trap: Forgetting that LR is functionally hypotonic in the post-op brain → drives free water into edematous tissue.
Pearl: Post-craniotomy maintenance fluid is isotonic with dextrose (eg D5NS), not LR or D5½NS. Hypotonic fluids are contraindicated in the post-op or injured brain.

Confidence: HIGH

Question 7 — Guillain-Barré: Indications to Intubate; Weaning Modes

Q7. GBS patient. (a) Two indications to intubate. (b) Two ventilator modes for weaning.
Tested concept: "20-30-40 rule" for NMD respiratory monitoring; supportive ventilation strategies.
Hidden clue: Examiners want measurable bedside thresholds, not vague clinical impressions.
Reasoning — intubation indications:
  • VC < 20 mL/kg (or < 50% predicted).
  • MIP / NIF less negative than –30 cmH₂O (loss of force generation).
  • MEP < 40 cmH₂O (loss of cough).
  • Bulbar dysfunction — inability to swallow secretions, aspiration risk.
  • Hypoxia or hypercapnia (PaCO₂ > 50) on ABG.
  • Rapid progression (worsening between exams), autonomic instability.
Weaning modes:
  • Pressure support ventilation (PSV) with daily SBT — allows the patient to generate the work of breathing while the ventilator augments.
  • SIMV with pressure support — backup rate as muscles recover.
  • NIV (BiPAP) as a bridge after extubation in cooperative patients.
Best answers:
  • Intubate (any two): VC < 20 mL/kg; MIP / NIF less negative than –30 cmH₂O; bulbar dysfunction with aspiration; CO₂ retention > 50 mm Hg; rapid progression.
  • Weaning modes (any two): PSV + SBT; SIMV+PS; NIV/BiPAP post-extubation.
Wrong choices:
  • SaO₂ < 90% on supplemental O₂ — late, not the trigger for elective intubation in NMD (ventilation fails before oxygenation).
  • Volume-control AC for weaning — doesn't let the patient build respiratory muscle work.
Anchor: Rogers Ch 60 (neuromuscular disorders); Zimmerman PNS chapter.
Trap: Waiting for hypoxia in NMD respiratory failure — ventilation fails first; oxygenation drops late (often only when atelectasis or aspiration adds).
Pearl: Memorize "20-30-40" — VC < 20 mL/kg, NIF > –30, MEP < 40 cmH₂O → intubate electively before crisis.

Confidence: HIGH

Easier variant

Question 8 — VC Postural Change in Spinal Cord Injury & Bilateral Diaphragm Paresis

Q8. Patient with C5 spinal cord injury — how does VC change from upright to supine? Same question for bilateral diaphragm paresis.
Tested concept: Postural diaphragmatic mechanics in tetraplegia vs phrenic palsy.
Hidden clue: Counterintuitive — C5 tetraplegia improves supine; bilateral diaphragm paresis worsens supine.
Reasoning:
  • C5 tetraplegia: phrenic nerve (C3–C5) preserved → diaphragm functional. Upright: intercostal/abdominal muscles paralyzed → abdominal contents droop, diaphragm flattens → mechanical disadvantage → VC lower upright. Supine: gravity pushes abdominal contents up into thorax → diaphragm sits at higher (more efficient) length → VC improves. Typical: VC increases 15–25% supine in tetraplegic patient. Apply abdominal binder upright to mimic supine improvement.
  • Bilateral diaphragm paresis: diaphragm cannot contract. Upright: gravity assists by pulling abdominal contents away from thorax (passive thoracic expansion as accessory muscles work). Supine: abdominal contents fall onto the paralyzed diaphragm, which moves cephalad with each inspiration (paradoxical), reducing tidal volume. Result: VC drops markedly supine (orthopnea, supine hypoventilation, sleep-disordered breathing).
Best answer:
  • C5 cord injury: VC increases from upright to supine (diaphragm operates from more advantageous resting length). Abdominal binder helps when upright.
  • Bilateral diaphragm paresis: VC decreases from upright to supine (loss of gravity assistance; abdominal contents oppose paralyzed diaphragm).
Anchor: Rogers Ch on spinal cord injury and neuromuscular disease; Zimmerman pulmonary/PNS physiology.
Trap: Reversing the two — assuming all NMD VC worsens supine. Diaphragm-intact tetraplegia is the exception.
Pearl: "Diaphragm intact → supine better. Diaphragm paralyzed → supine worse." A > 25% supine drop in VC = bilateral diaphragm dysfunction until proven otherwise.

Confidence: HIGH

Easier variant

Question 9 — Teen With ↓LOC, HAGMA, HCO3 3 → Toxic Alcohol/DKA

Q9. Teen with GCS 5, stable vitals, HCO3 3, pH 7.10, given osmoles. Most likely Dx + 3 treatments.
Tested concept: Severe HAGMA differential in adolescent — DKA vs toxic alcohol (methanol, ethylene glycol) vs salicylate. Osmolar gap discriminator.
Hidden clue: "Given osmoles" → osmolar gap matters → toxic alcohol ingestion suspected.
Reasoning:
  • Severe HAGMA with HCO3 3 — calculate anion gap (likely > 20).
  • Differentiate: DKA (glucose ↑, ketones, no osmolar gap), toxic alcohol (osmolar gap ↑ early, anion gap ↑ late), salicylate (mixed respiratory alkalosis + HAGMA), lactic acidosis (sepsis/shock — vitals unstable usually).
  • Stable vitals + low GCS + osmolar gap → methanol or ethylene glycol.
  • Methanol: visual disturbance, retinal toxicity (formate); ethylene glycol: oxalate crystals, AKI, hypocalcemia, "snowstorm urine."
Best diagnosis: Toxic alcohol ingestion (methanol or ethylene glycol) — most likely given osmolar gap.
Alternative if no osmolar gap: severe DKA.
Treatment (3):
  • Fomepizole 15 mg/kg IV load (or ethanol infusion if unavailable) — ADH inhibition to prevent formate/oxalate formation.
  • Hemodialysis — removes parent alcohol and toxic metabolites; indicated for severe acidosis, end-organ damage, high level.
  • Sodium bicarbonate infusion — buffer pH; aim pH > 7.30.
  • Adjuncts: methanol — folinic acid 1 mg/kg q4h (enhances formate metabolism). Ethylene glycol — pyridoxine + thiamine cofactors. Calcium for hypocalcemia (EG). Airway protection (intubation given GCS 5).
Wrong:
  • "Insulin infusion" — only if DKA confirmed (glucose, ketones, no osmolar gap).
  • "Activated charcoal" — does not bind alcohols.
  • "Naloxone" — for opioid; not relevant here unless co-ingestant.
Anchor: Rogers toxicology, neuro emergencies; Zimmerman ICU pharmacology.
Trap: Anchoring on DKA in a teen with ↓LOC and HAGMA. The osmolar gap clue is the discriminator — don't miss it.
Pearl: HAGMA + osmolar gap + visual symptoms = methanol. HAGMA + osmolar gap + AKI + hypocalcemia = ethylene glycol. Treat with fomepizole + dialysis.

Confidence: HIGH (assuming osmolar gap was abnormal)

Question 10 — GBS: When to Intubate, Treatment, Weaning

Q10. GBS patient. (a) 4 things that help you decide when to intubate. (b) 2 treatment options. (c) 2 modes to ventilate.
Tested concept: Decision-making in NMD respiratory failure; disease-modifying therapy; ventilator selection.
Reasoning — intubation decision factors:
  • VC < 20 mL/kg (or < 50% predicted, declining).
  • MIP/NIF less negative than –30 cmH₂O.
  • MEP < 40 cmH₂O — impaired cough.
  • Bulbar dysfunction (dysphagia, pooled secretions, aspiration).
  • Rate of progression (rapid worsening between checks).
  • Autonomic instability (BP swings, arrhythmia).
  • Hypercapnia > 50 mm Hg, hypoxia on supplemental O₂.
  • Anticipation of long transport, sedation, or procedures.
Treatment options:
  • IVIG 0.4 g/kg/day × 5 days (most commonly used).
  • Plasma exchange (PLEX) — equivalent efficacy; reserve for IVIG-refractory or contraindicated.
  • Supportive: DVT prophylaxis, autonomic monitoring, pain control (gabapentin, opioids), nutrition, PT/OT, mood support.
  • Steroids are NOT effective in classical GBS.
Ventilator modes:
  • SIMV with PS — backup rate as patient recovers.
  • PSV alone for weaning, augmented spontaneous breaths.
  • NIV (BiPAP) post-extubation bridge.
Answers:
  • Intubation: any 4 of VC, NIF, MEP, bulbar function, ABG, progression rate, autonomic instability.
  • Treatment: IVIG and plasma exchange.
  • Ventilation: SIMV+PS and PSV (alternates: AC volume, NIV).
Trap: Picking steroids — proven ineffective in GBS (CIDP responds, GBS does not).
Pearl: GBS: IVIG OR PLEX (not both — equivalent). Steroids do NOT work.

Confidence: HIGH

Anchor: Rogers Ch 60 (neuromuscular).

Question 11 — Hyponatremia in CNS Tumor with Seizures

Q11. Patient with CNS tumor and seizures, Na 120. List 3 reasons for hyponatremia.
Tested concept: Neuro-hyponatremia triad — SIADH vs CSW vs iatrogenic hypotonic fluids.
Reasoning — 3 reasons:
  • SIADH — ectopic/inappropriate ADH from tumor, hypothalamic involvement, or surgery; euvolemic hyponatremia, concentrated urine, normal/elevated UNa, low serum osm, high urine osm. Treat with fluid restriction + hypertonic saline if symptomatic.
  • Cerebral salt wasting (CSW) — hypovolemic hyponatremia from CNS-mediated natriuresis (ANP/BNP excess); polyuria, weight loss, hemoconcentration. Treat with salt replacement + isotonic/hypertonic fluids.
  • Iatrogenic hypotonic fluid administration — D5W, ½NS, or LR maintenance especially in postoperative or ill child; preventable.
  • Adjuncts: adrenal insufficiency (if pituitary involvement), thiazide diuretics, vomiting, psychogenic polydipsia.
Answer: SIADH; CSW; iatrogenic hypotonic fluids. (Alternate: adrenal insufficiency from pituitary apoplexy.)
Wrong if simply "dehydration": — too vague; examiner wants mechanism.
Anchor: Rogers Ch 61 (neurosurgery) + Zimmerman Ch 59 supplement.
Trap: Treating SIADH like CSW (fluid resuscitation makes SIADH worse) or vice versa (fluid restriction makes CSW worse). Volume status is the discriminator.
Pearl: "Wet → SIADH (restrict). Dry → CSW (replace)." Volume status + urine sodium + weight trajectory.

Confidence: HIGH

Question 12 — DMD + Succinylcholine → VT/VF Arrest

Q12. DMD intubated with succinylcholine, VT arrest → VF. (a) Why? (b) 3 reasons defibrillation has been unsuccessful.
Tested concept: Sux contraindication in NMD; hyperkalemic arrest mechanism; refractory VF differential.
Hidden clue: DMD + sux is a "never event" — pathognomonic for hyperkalemic cardiac arrest.
Reasoning — why VT arrest:
  • DMD has upregulated extrajunctional fetal-type acetylcholine receptors in muscle.
  • Succinylcholine depolarizes ALL receptors → massive K⁺ efflux → acute hyperkalemia (often K > 7–10).
  • Hyperkalemic VT/VF → arrest within minutes of induction.
  • Avoid sux in any disorder of muscle: DMD, BMD, denervation (cord injury > 48 h, burns > 24 h, prolonged immobility, GBS, MS, sepsis-induced myopathy).
3 reasons defibrillation fails:
  • Severe hyperkalemia — myocardium unresponsive until K⁺ corrected (give calcium chloride/gluconate first, insulin + glucose, β-agonist, bicarbonate, dialysis if refractory).
  • Severe acidosis (from arrest + hyperkalemia) — must correct pH.
  • Profound hypothermia, hypocalcemia, hypomagnesemia — uncorrected metabolic milieu.
  • Persistent precipitant — DMD-related cardiomyopathy, ongoing K⁺ release.
  • Inadequate energy or pad placement.
Answer: Hyperkalemic cardiac arrest from succinylcholine-induced massive K⁺ efflux in DMD muscle.
Failed defibrillation: hyperkalemia (give calcium + insulin/glucose/β-agonist), severe acidosis, electrolyte abnormalities (Ca, Mg), underlying DMD cardiomyopathy.
Wrong:
  • "Malignant hyperthermia" — DMD has elevated MH risk too, but the immediate VT/VF in this scenario is hyperkalemia, not MH.
  • "Tension pneumothorax" — possible but not the headline answer.
Anchor: Rogers Ch 60 (neuromuscular). Zimmerman PNS chapter. AHA PALS — H's and T's.
Trap: Calling MH every time DMD goes bad. The IMMEDIATE arrest with sux = hyperkalemia. MH presents later with rigidity, hypercarbia, hyperthermia.
Pearl: "Sux is a sin in known or suspected muscle disease." Use rocuronium 1.2 mg/kg + reverse with sugammadex.

Confidence: HIGH

Question 13 — Thiopental Coma: Complications & Post-infusion Coma

Q13. Refractory SE → barbiturate (thiopental) coma. (a) 4 complications. (b) Seizures stopped, infusion off 6 h, patient still comatose — why?
Tested concept: Barbiturate pharmacology — toxicity profile and pharmacokinetics with prolonged infusion.

  • Electrophysiologic: reduced CMAP and SNAP amplitudes with preserved conduction velocity (axonal pattern); fibrillations + positive sharp waves.
  • Pathologic: axonal degeneration with Wallerian-like changes; minimal demyelination.
Myopathy findings:
  • Clinical: predominantly proximal weakness; sensation preserved; reflexes preserved or reduced.
  • Electrophysiologic: normal SNAP; reduced CMAP; short-duration low-amplitude MUAP; ABNORMAL direct muscle stimulation (key discriminator from CIP).
  • Pathologic: selective loss of myosin (thick-filament myopathy); type II fiber atrophy; occasional myofiber necrosis.
Answer: See best-answer summary above.
Anchor: Rogers Ch 60; Zimmerman Ch 69 supplement.
Trap: Confusing CIM and CIP — direct muscle stimulation discriminates (abnormal in CIM, normal in CIP).
Pearl: "Sensory loss + axonal EMG = CIP. Preserved sensation + abnormal direct muscle stim = CIM."

Confidence: HIGH

Question 31 — VAP in a Neurologic ICU Patient

Q31. 5-y-o intubated 6 days with neurologic injury, increased vent support. (a) 4 findings suggesting VAP. (b) Common pathogens early vs late.
Tested concept: Diagnosis and microbiology of VAP — temporal pattern.
4 findings suggesting VAP:
  • Worsening oxygenation (PEEP/FiO2 escalation), increasing tracheal secretions (purulent), new fever.
  • New or progressive infiltrate on CXR.
  • Leukocytosis or leukopenia; left shift; rising procalcitonin/CRP (non-specific).
  • Microbiology: tracheal aspirate or BAL with bacterial growth above thresholds (≥ 10⁴ CFU/mL BAL; ≥ 10⁶ CFU/mL tracheal aspirate).
  • Clinical Pulmonary Infection Score (CPIS) > 6.
Early VAP (< 5 d) — community/early hospital flora:
  • Streptococcus pneumoniae.
  • Haemophilus influenzae.
  • MSSA (methicillin-sensitive Staphylococcus aureus).
  • Enterics (E. coli).
Late VAP (≥ 5 d) — nosocomial/MDR:
  • Pseudomonas aeruginosa.
  • MRSA.
  • Klebsiella, Acinetobacter, Enterobacter (often MDR).
  • Stenotrophomonas in select cohorts.
4 findings: new fever; new infiltrate; purulent secretions; positive tracheal culture above threshold; worsening O2/PEEP.
Early: S. pneumoniae, H. influenzae, MSSA. Late: Pseudomonas, MRSA, Acinetobacter/Klebsiella.
Anchor: Rogers Ch on respiratory infections.
Trap: Choosing empiric narrow-spectrum coverage for late VAP — MDR risk demands pseudomonal + MRSA coverage (eg piperacillin-tazobactam or cefepime + vancomycin ± aminoglycoside) pending culture.
Pearl: "Early VAP = community bugs; late VAP = ICU bugs (Pseudomonas, MRSA, Acinetobacter)." De-escalate when culture allows.

Confidence: HIGH

Question 32 — Sarin Gas Mechanism

Q32. How does sarin gas work?
Tested concept: Organophosphate / nerve agent mechanism — irreversible acetylcholinesterase inhibition.
Reasoning:
  • Sarin (GB) is an organophosphate nerve agent that irreversibly inhibits acetylcholinesterase at neuromuscular junctions, autonomic synapses, and CNS.
  • Result: acetylcholine accumulates → over-stimulation of muscarinic and nicotinic receptors and CNS.
  • Muscarinic syndrome (SLUDGE/Killer Bs): Salivation, Lacrimation, Urination, Defecation, GI upset, Emesis / Bronchorrhea, Bronchospasm, Bradycardia.
  • Nicotinic effects: muscle fasciculations, weakness, paralysis, mydriasis (rarely), tachycardia.
  • CNS: seizures, coma, central respiratory failure.
  • Aging: organophosphate–AChE bond becomes irreversible over time ("aging"); pralidoxime must be given before aging completes (varies by agent: sarin aging ~5 hours).
  • Treatment: airway/ventilation, decontamination, atropine (titrate to dry secretions), pralidoxime (2-PAM) to regenerate AChE before aging, benzodiazepines for seizure.
Answer: Sarin is an organophosphate that irreversibly inhibits acetylcholinesterase, causing acetylcholine accumulation and over-stimulation of muscarinic, nicotinic, and CNS receptors → SLUDGE/B syndrome, paralysis, seizures, central respiratory failure. Treatment: atropine + pralidoxime + benzodiazepine + supportive ventilation; decontamination is critical.
Anchor: Rogers Ch toxicology; PALS/AHA disaster preparedness.
Trap: Forgetting decontamination of staff and removing clothing — secondary exposure risk to providers.
Pearl: "Atropine + pralidoxime + BZD — and decontaminate first." Atropine dose: 0.05 mg/kg up to 2 mg, repeat every 5 min until secretions dry.

Confidence: HIGH

PATTERN RECOGNITION — Themes Repeated Across This Set

Theme 1: Neuromuscular Disease Respiratory Mechanics (Q4, 7, 8, 10, 17, 19)

  • "20-30-40 rule" for intubation: VC < 20 mL/kg, NIF > –30, MEP < 40.
  • Position-dependent VC (improves supine if diaphragm intact; worsens supine if diaphragm paralyzed).
  • NIV bridge post-extubation in cooperative patients with reversible disease.
  • Avoid sux + aminoglycosides in NMD.
  • Disease-modifying: IVIG, PLEX, BabyBIG, pyridostigmine.

Theme 2: Pediatric TBI / Hypothermia Negative Trials (Q1, 5, 18, 22, 29)

  • Hutchison 2008 + Cool Kids 2013 = NO benefit; possible harm.
  • BTF 2019: hypothermia for ICP control only (Tier 2); aggressive normothermia for everyone.
  • CPP target: age-stratified (40/50/60); never chase supraphysiologic CPP.
  • First hypotension or hypoxia doubles mortality.

Theme 3: Neuro Endocrine/Electrolyte Crises (Q6, 9, 11)

  • Hyponatremia post-CNS surgery: SIADH vs CSW vs iatrogenic hypotonic fluid.
  • HAGMA with osmolar gap = toxic alcohol → fomepizole + dialysis.
  • Volume status determines treatment (SIADH = restrict; CSW = replace).

Theme 4: Brain Death Pediatric Specifics (Q23)

  • Two exams, two examiners, age-stratified interval.
  • No determination < 37 weeks GA; term newborns must be ≥ 48 hours.
  • Ancillary testing required when clinical or apnea cannot be completed.
  • Confounders rigorously excluded.

Theme 5: ICU-Acquired Weakness (Q20, 30)

  • Sepsis is the strongest driver.
  • Direct muscle stim discriminates CIM (abnormal) from CIP (normal).
  • Treatment is preventive: sepsis bundle + glycemic control + minimize sedation/NMB + early mobility.

Theme 6: Image Recognition (Q15, 28)

  • Pneumocephalus; AHT subdurals; ventriculomegaly.
  • Marshall classification for TBI CT.
