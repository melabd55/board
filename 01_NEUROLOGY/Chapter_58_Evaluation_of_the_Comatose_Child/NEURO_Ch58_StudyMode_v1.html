CHAPTER 58 ■ Evaluation of the Comatose Child — Key Points

Key points distilled
  • Coma is altered wakefulness + awareness caused by conditions affecting brainstem reticular activating system or both cerebral hemispheres diffusely.
  • Acute encephalopathy is a medical emergency — rapid evaluation enables specific therapy to prevent secondary injury.
  • Etiology drives both diagnosis and management; "treat what's treatable" while pursuing definitive cause.
Epidemiology
  • Nontraumatic coma incidence ~30/100 000 children/year; higher in <1 year.
  • Severe traumatic coma ~5.6/100 000.
  • Mortality and morbidity depend on etiology and severity of brain injury.
RCPSC pearl 🔥🔥🔥
  • "Approach to the comatose child" is a perennial exam stem — structured approach (stabilize → quick neurological exam → differential by pattern → focused investigations → specific therapy) wins points.

Pages 3046–3048 · STATES OF ALTERED CONSCIOUSNESS

STATES OF ALTERED CONSCIOUSNESS

Mechanism
  • Two anatomic substrates for normal consciousness — ascending reticular activating system (ARAS) in upper brainstem; bilateral cerebral hemispheres.
  • Coma requires either bilateral hemispheric dysfunction OR brainstem (ARAS) dysfunction.
  • Unilateral hemispheric lesion alone does not cause coma unless mass effect compresses contralateral hemisphere or brainstem.
Locked-in syndrome (special case)
  • Preserved awareness with quadriplegia and inability to speak.
  • Usually from ventral pontine lesion (basilar artery thrombosis, central pontine myelinolysis, brainstem trauma).
  • Vertical eye movements and eyelid blink may be preserved — communication possible.
Exam trap
  • A patient who appears comatose with intact vertical gaze is locked-in until proven otherwise — call to thrombolysis / thrombectomy team if basilar thrombosis suspected.
Pages 3049–3054 · ETIOLOGIES OF COMA

ETIOLOGIES OF COMA

Structural vs. diffuse
  • Structural — focal injury to brainstem ARAS or bilateral hemispheric damage. Often surgical or interventional emergency.
  • Diffuse / metabolic — toxin, metabolic derangement, hypoxia-ischemia, seizure-related. Generally medical management.
  • Quick clue — structural causes typically have asymmetric exam findings or focal neurological deficits.

  • Pediatric specific add-ons — non-accidental trauma, inborn errors, intussusception with neuro signs, post-immunization ADEM.
Pages 3055–3060 · INITIAL EVALUATION

INITIAL EVALUATION OF COMA

Resuscitation first (ABCDE)
  • Airway — protect; intubate if GCS ≤8 or compromised airway reflexes.
  • Breathing — adequate oxygenation; PaO₂ >80 mmHg; avoid hyperoxia.
  • Circulation — maintain age-appropriate MAP and CPP.
  • Disability — pupils, motor response, GCS / FOUR score.
  • Exposure — examine for trauma, rashes, bruising, fontanelles.
Immediate empiric therapy
  • Dextrose 0.25–0.5 g/kg IV if hypoglycemic (check first if possible).
  • Naloxone 0.01–0.1 mg/kg IV if opioid suspected.
  • Thiamine 100 mg IV before glucose in adolescents at risk of Wernicke (eating disorders, alcohol).
  • Cefotaxime/ceftriaxone + vancomycin + acyclovir if CNS infection suspected.
  • Hypertonic saline / mannitol if signs of impending herniation.
  • Anticonvulsant if seizing.
Focused history
  • Trauma — fall, MVC, abuse history, witness reports.
  • Prodrome — fever, headache, irritability, neck stiffness, photophobia.
  • Toxin exposure — medications at home, drug paraphernalia, ingestion.
  • Past medical — epilepsy, metabolic disease, immunosuppression, sickle cell, congenital heart disease.
  • Vaccinations — recent immunizations may suggest ADEM.
  • Family history — mitochondrial / metabolic / sudden death.
Neurological examination
  • Level of consciousness — GCS / FOUR score with precise descriptors.
  • Pupils — size, reactivity, symmetry.
  • Eye movements — spontaneous, oculocephalic (doll's eye), oculovestibular (cold caloric), Bell's phenomenon.
  • Corneal reflex.
  • Gag and cough reflexes.
  • Motor response — spontaneous, to noxious stimulus, posturing.
  • Deep tendon reflexes, plantar response.
  • Meningismus (after C-spine cleared).
  • Fundoscopy — papilledema (raised ICP), retinal hemorrhages (abusive head trauma).

Pearl 🔥🔥🔥
  • Pinpoint pupils in a comatose child → consider opioid overdose, pontine hemorrhage, or organophosphate.
  • "Blown" (unilateral fixed dilated) pupil in a head-injured child → impending uncal herniation — emergent hyperosmolar therapy and surgical decompression.
  • Bilateral midposition fixed pupils suggest midbrain damage or transtentorial herniation.
Exam trap
  • Atropine, scopolamine, sympathomimetics can produce dilated pupils — review medication list.
  • Severe hypoxia, hypothermia, anesthetics can fix pupils transiently — re-examine after correction.
Pages 3071–3078 · INVESTIGATION OF NONTRAUMATIC COMA

INVESTIGATION OF NONTRAUMATIC COMA

Tier-1 (immediate)
  • Bedside glucose, electrolytes, calcium, magnesium, ammonia.
  • Arterial blood gas including lactate.
  • CBC, coagulation, CRP/PCT.
  • Renal and liver function tests.
  • Toxicology screen (urine + serum specific drugs).
  • Pregnancy test (adolescent females).
  • ECG (arrhythmia, prolonged QT).
Tier-2 (targeted)
  • Non-contrast CT head — urgent for trauma, focal signs, ICP raised, before LP if focal signs / immunocompromised.
  • MRI brain — better for stroke, encephalitis, ADEM, posterior fossa lesions, white matter disease.
  • LP — bacterial / viral meningitis, autoimmune encephalitis, MS.
  • EEG — non-convulsive status epilepticus, encephalopathy pattern, prognostic value post-arrest.
  • Cardiac echo — endocarditis, septic embolus, cardiac source of embolus.
  • Specific tests — heavy metal levels, carbon monoxide, methemoglobin, blood cultures, viral PCRs, autoimmune encephalitis panel.
ICU layer 🔥🔥🔥
  • Order is priority — stabilize ABC, secure glucose and basic labs while imaging is being arranged.
  • If LP delayed by imaging, give empiric antibiotics + acyclovir immediately and LP as soon as feasible.
  • Always consider non-convulsive status epilepticus — continuous EEG if available.

3. Global Evidence Layer (2020–2025) — Full Citations

① Pediatric Severe TBI Guidelines (3rd Edition) — Brain Trauma Foundation

  • Source: Kochanek PM, Tasker RC, Carney N et al. Pediatr Crit Care Med. 2019;20(3S):S1–S82
  • Link: DOI 10.1097/PCC.0000000000001735 · BTF: braintrauma.org
  • Key findings:
    • Tiered approach to ICP management.
    • Age-appropriate CPP and hyperosmolar therapy.
    • Hypertonic saline first-line; mannitol second-line.
    • Aggressive normothermia; avoid sustained hyperventilation.

② FOUR Score Validation

  • Source: Wijdicks EF, Bamlet WR, Maramattom BV, Manno EM, McClelland RL. Ann Neurol. 2005;58:585–593 (foundational); pediatric validation — Cohen J. J Child Neurol. 2009;24:583–589
  • Link: Foundational DOI: 10.1002/ana.20611 · Pediatric DOI: 10.1177/0883073808327831
  • Key findings:
    • FOUR Score predicts in-hospital mortality and 6-month outcome.
    • Useful in intubated patients (verbal score unavailable).
    • Includes brainstem reflexes and respiratory pattern.
    • Less inter-rater variability than GCS.

③ Continuous EEG Monitoring in Critically Ill Children — ACNS Consensus

  • Authoring body: American Clinical Neurophysiology Society
  • Source: Herman ST et al. J Clin Neurophysiol. 2015;32:96–108; pediatric supplement — Abend NS et al. Pediatr Crit Care Med. 2022
  • Link: DOI 10.1097/WNP.0000000000000165 · ACNS: acns.org/practice/guidelines
  • Key findings:
    • Continuous EEG for unexplained altered mental status, suspected nonconvulsive seizures, post-arrest care, TBI, prognostication.
    • Up to 50 % of seizures in critically ill children are nonconvulsive.
    • Background patterns predict outcome.

④ Pediatric Coma Outcome Studies — Wong et al. Cohort

  • Source: Wong CP, Forsyth RJ, Kelly TP, Eyre JA. Arch Dis Child. 2001;84:193–199 (foundational pediatric coma outcomes); subsequent updates in PALISI and ESPNIC consortia
  • Link: DOI 10.1136/adc.84.3.193
  • Key findings:
    • Pediatric coma outcomes substantially better than adult.
    • Etiology — traumatic better than nontraumatic.
    • 1-year outcomes — traumatic coma: death 9 %, PVS 29 %, recovery 62 %; nontraumatic: death 22 %, PVS 65 %, recovery 13 %.

⑤ Anti-NMDA Receptor Encephalitis — Treatment Guideline

  • Source: Titulaer MJ et al. Lancet Neurol. 2013;12:157–165 (foundational); Graus F et al. Lancet Neurol. 2016;15:391–404 (clinical criteria for autoimmune encephalitis)
  • Link: Treatment DOI 10.1016/S1474-4422(12)70310-1 · Criteria DOI 10.1016/S1474-4422(15)00401-9
  • Key findings:
    • First-line — steroids + IVIG or PLEX; tumor removal where present.
    • Second-line — rituximab + cyclophosphamide for refractory.
    • 50 % of pediatric cases without identifiable tumor.
    • Long-term recovery common with appropriate immunotherapy.

⑥ Cerebral Malaria Management (WHO)

  • Source: WHO Guidelines for the Treatment of Malaria, 3rd edition · updated 2022; Maitland K et al. AQUAMAT trial — Lancet. 2010;376:1647–1657 (parenteral artesunate)
  • Link: WHO Malaria Guidelines · AQUAMAT DOI: 10.1016/S0140-6736(10)61924-1
  • Key findings:
    • Parenteral artesunate is superior to quinine in pediatric severe malaria.
    • AQUAMAT showed 22.5 % relative mortality reduction.
    • Manage coma — supportive ICU, treat hypoglycemia aggressively.
    • Avoid corticosteroids and aggressive fluid resuscitation (FEAST trial).

⑦ Bacterial Meningitis Guidelines — IDSA + ESCMID

  • Source: Tunkel AR et al. Clin Infect Dis. 2004 (IDSA foundation); van de Beek D et al. Clin Microbiol Infect. 2016;22(Suppl 3):S37–S62 (ESCMID); pediatric guidelines from AAP and ESPID 2020–2024
  • Link: IDSA DOI: 10.1086/425368 · ESCMID DOI: 10.1016/j.cmi.2016.01.007
  • Key findings:
    • Empiric vancomycin + 3rd-gen cephalosporin (ceftriaxone or cefotaxime); add ampicillin if <3 mo.
    • Dexamethasone with or before first antibiotic dose (Hib hearing loss reduction).
    • Imaging before LP only if focal signs, immunocompromised, papilledema, or new seizures.
    • Empiric acyclovir if HSV encephalitis suspected.

⑧ Pediatric Stroke Guidelines — AHA / ASA

  • Source: Ferriero DM, Fullerton HJ, Bernard TJ et al. Stroke. 2019;50:e51–e96 (management of stroke in neonates and children scientific statement)
  • Link: DOI 10.1161/STR.0000000000000183 · International Pediatric Stroke Study: IPSS
  • Key findings:
    • Tissue plasminogen activator (tPA) and mechanical thrombectomy considered case-by-case.
    • Sickle cell stroke — emergency exchange transfusion to HbS <30 %.
    • Aspirin for secondary prevention; anticoagulation for specific etiologies (CSVT, cardioembolic).
    • TCD screening in sickle cell disease for primary stroke prevention.

5. ICU Decision Engine

IF Child presents with unexplained coma
THEN
  • ABCDE resuscitation.
  • Bedside glucose; empiric dextrose if <3 mmol/L.
  • Empiric naloxone if opioid suspected.
  • Quick neurological exam (GCS, pupils, motor, brainstem reflexes).
  • Tier-1 labs (electrolytes, ammonia, ABG, lactate, tox screen, CBC).
  • Non-contrast CT head if focal signs or trauma.
  • Empiric antibiotics + acyclovir if CNS infection suspected.
  • Continuous EEG to detect NCSE.
IF Focal neurological signs in coma
THEN
  • Suspect structural cause — stroke, hemorrhage, mass, herniation.
  • Emergent imaging.
  • Neurosurgical consultation.
  • Hyperosmolar therapy if herniation signs.
IF Symmetric pinpoint pupils
THEN
  • Consider opioid overdose — empiric naloxone.
  • Pontine hemorrhage — imaging.
  • Organophosphate poisoning — atropine + pralidoxime.
IF Unilateral fixed dilated pupil
THEN
  • Impending uncal herniation — emergency.
  • Hyperosmolar therapy.
  • Hyperventilate briefly as bridge.
  • Emergent imaging and neurosurgery.
IF Preserved vertical gaze in apparent coma
THEN
  • Suspect locked-in syndrome — basilar artery thrombosis.
  • Emergent MRI / MRA / CTA.
  • Thrombolysis / thrombectomy team activation.
  • Communicate with patient via eye movement.
IF Coma with fever and altered mental status
THEN
  • Empiric antibiotics + dexamethasone + acyclovir.
  • LP after imaging if no contraindication.
  • Investigate for CNS infection (viral, bacterial, fungal, TB, parasitic).
  • Continuous EEG for seizures.
IF Coma with hyperammonemia
THEN
  • Stop protein intake; IV dextrose.
  • Ammonia scavengers (sodium benzoate / phenylacetate; Ammonul).
  • L-arginine.
  • Hemodialysis if NH3 >500 or rising.
  • Investigate urea cycle / metabolic disease.
IF Coma with refractory NCSE
THEN
  • Continuous EEG.
  • Aggressive antiepileptics (benzodiazepine → fosphenytoin / levetiracetam → midazolam infusion → pentobarbital coma).
  • Consider ketogenic diet, immunotherapy for autoimmune etiology.

6. SAQ Bank — 40 Questions

SAQ 1. Define coma.
Answer.
  • Sustained pathologic eyes-closed state with loss of both wakefulness and awareness.
  • Patient is unconscious, unaware, unresponsive to external stimuli.
  • Sleep-wake cycles are absent.
  • Transitory state — may evolve to recovery, varied responsiveness, or brain death.
SAQ 2. List the spectrum of consciousness states.
Answer.
  • Normal → lethargy → obtundation → stupor → coma.
  • Chronic — vegetative state (sleep-wake returns), minimally conscious state (intermittent awareness).
  • Locked-in — preserved consciousness with quadriplegia and anarthria.
  • Brain death — irreversible cessation of all brain function including brainstem.
SAQ 3. AEIOU TIPS mnemonic for coma differential.
Answer.
  • A — Alcohol.
  • E — Endocrine (DKA, Addison, myxedema).
  • I — Insulin (hypoglycemia or DKA).
  • O — Opioids, Oxygen (hypoxia).
  • U — Uremia.
  • T — Trauma, Temperature (hyper / hypo).
  • I — Infection.
  • P — Psychiatric, Poisons.
  • S — Seizure, Stroke, Shock.
SAQ 4. Outline immediate empiric therapy in unexplained coma.
Answer.
  • Dextrose if hypoglycemic.
  • Naloxone if opioid suspected.
  • Thiamine before glucose in adolescents at risk (eating disorder, alcohol).
  • Cefotaxime / ceftriaxone + vancomycin + acyclovir for suspected CNS infection.
  • Hypertonic saline / mannitol for herniation signs.
  • Anticonvulsant if seizing.
SAQ 5. Pediatric GCS — describe modifications for preverbal children.
Answer.
  • Verbal — coos/babbles (5), irritable cries (4), cries to pain (3), moans to pain (2), none (1).
  • Motor — spontaneous purposeful (6), withdraws from touch (5), withdraws to pain (4), decorticate (3), decerebrate (2), none (1).
  • Eye opening — unchanged from adult.
  • Total 3–15.
SAQ 6. FOUR Score — what does it measure?
Answer.
  • Full Outline of UnResponsiveness.
  • Four components — Eye, Motor, Brainstem reflexes, Respiration.
  • Each 0–4 (total 0–16).
  • Useful in intubated patients (no verbal score).
  • Predicts in-hospital mortality and 6-month outcome.
SAQ 7. Pupillary findings in coma — list at least four patterns and their localization.
Answer.
  • Small reactive — metabolic encephalopathy with intact brainstem.
  • Midposition fixed — midbrain lesion.
  • Pinpoint reactive — pontine hemorrhage, opioid, organophosphate.
  • Unilateral fixed dilated — uncal herniation with CN III compression.
  • Bilateral fixed dilated — severe brainstem injury, anoxic injury, brain death.
SAQ 8. Describe locked-in syndrome.
Answer.
  • Preserved consciousness with quadriplegia and anarthria.
  • Usually ventral pontine lesion (basilar artery thrombosis, CPM, brainstem trauma).
  • Vertical eye movements and blink preserved — communication possible.
  • Always check vertical gaze in apparent coma.
  • Time-sensitive — basilar thrombolysis / thrombectomy.
SAQ 9. Outline initial neurological exam in coma.
Answer.
  • Level of consciousness — GCS / FOUR.
  • Pupils — size, reactivity, symmetry.
  • Eye movements — spontaneous, doll's eye, cold caloric.
  • Corneal, gag, cough reflexes.
  • Motor — spontaneous, to noxious, posturing.
  • DTRs and plantar response.
  • Meningismus (after C-spine cleared).
  • Fundoscopy — papilledema, retinal hemorrhages.
SAQ 10. Cushing's triad — components and significance.
Answer.
  • Bradycardia.
  • Hypertension.
  • Irregular respiration (Cheyne-Stokes, apneustic, ataxic).
  • Late sign of raised ICP / impending herniation.
  • Triggers emergent decompression — hyperosmolar therapy, surgical evaluation.
SAQ 11. When is non-contrast CT preferred over MRI in coma?
Answer.
  • Acute trauma — fast detection of hemorrhage and mass effect.
  • Suspected raised ICP / herniation.
  • Before LP if focal signs / immunocompromised.
  • MRI better for ischemic stroke, encephalitis, ADEM, posterior fossa, white matter disease but takes longer.
SAQ 12. Indications for continuous EEG in critically ill children.
Answer.
  • Unexplained altered mental status.
  • Suspected nonconvulsive seizures.
  • Post-cardiac-arrest care.
  • Status epilepticus and refractory status.
  • Severe TBI.
  • Prognostication after anoxic injury.
  • Up to 50 % of seizures in critically ill children are nonconvulsive.
SAQ 13. Pediatric coma outcomes — traumatic vs. nontraumatic.
Answer.
  • Traumatic (1-year outcome after unresponsive at 1 month) — death 9 %, PVS 29 %, recovery 62 %.
  • Nontraumatic — death 22 %, PVS 65 %, recovery 13 % (usually with severe disability).
  • Pediatric outcomes substantially better than adult coma.
  • Withhold "persistent" PVS for TBI until 12 months.
SAQ 14. Anti-NMDA receptor encephalitis features.
Answer.
  • Psychiatric / behavioral changes, dyskinesias, seizures, autonomic instability, coma.
  • CSF anti-NMDA receptor antibody.
  • 50 % of pediatric cases without identifiable tumor.
  • Treatment — steroids + IVIG or PLEX first-line; rituximab + cyclophosphamide refractory.
  • Long-term recovery common with appropriate immunotherapy.
SAQ 15. Outline empiric antibiotic regimen for suspected bacterial meningitis.
Answer.
  • Vancomycin + 3rd-gen cephalosporin (ceftriaxone or cefotaxime).
  • Add ampicillin if <3 months (Listeria coverage).
  • Dexamethasone with or before first antibiotic dose (Hib hearing loss reduction).
  • Empiric acyclovir if HSV encephalitis suspected.
  • Imaging before LP only if focal signs, immunocompromised, papilledema, new seizures.
SAQ 16. Cerebral malaria management priorities.
Answer.
  • Parenteral artesunate (AQUAMAT — superior to quinine).
  • Aggressive hypoglycemia management.
  • Avoid corticosteroids (no benefit, may worsen).
  • Cautious fluid resuscitation (FEAST trial — bolus may harm).
  • Treat seizures (common, often nonconvulsive).
  • Monitor for hemoglobinuria, AKI, anemia.
SAQ 17. Indications for hyperosmolar therapy in coma.
Answer.
  • Clinical signs of impending herniation — unilateral fixed dilated pupil, Cushing's triad, posturing.
  • Documented raised ICP >20 mmHg unresponsive to first-tier measures.
  • Diffuse cerebral edema on imaging.
  • Hypertonic saline first-line; mannitol second-line.
  • Monitor serum sodium and osmolality.
SAQ 18. List five toxins commonly causing pediatric coma.
Answer.
  • Opioids — pinpoint pupils, respiratory depression.
  • Benzodiazepines — sedation, normal pupils.
  • TCA — anticholinergic + cardiac toxicity, wide QRS.
  • Salicylates — hyperventilation, mixed acid-base.
  • Carbon monoxide — cherry red lips, headache.
  • Anticholinergics — hyperthermia, mydriasis, dry mouth.
  • Iron, organophosphates, methemoglobin, ethanol.
SAQ 19. Hyperammonemia in coma — workup and management.
Answer.
  • Urea cycle defect, organic acidemia, fatty acid oxidation defect, Reye-like syndrome, hepatic encephalopathy.
  • Stop protein; IV dextrose.
  • Ammonia scavengers (Ammonul — sodium phenylacetate / benzoate).
  • L-arginine.
  • Hemodialysis if NH3 >500 μmol/L or rising.
  • Investigate organic acids, acylcarnitine profile, amino acids, ammonia trend.
SAQ 20. Reye syndrome — features and association.
Answer.
  • Acute encephalopathy + hepatic failure (microvesicular fatty infiltration).
  • Hyperammonemia, hypoglycemia, ↑ transaminases without jaundice.
  • Classically follows viral illness (influenza, varicella) + aspirin exposure.
  • Rare since aspirin avoidance recommendation.
  • Differential — fatty acid oxidation defects, organic acidemias.
SAQ 21. DKA-related cerebral edema.
Answer.
  • Most common cause of mortality in pediatric DKA.
  • Risk factors — younger age (<5 y), new-onset diabetes, severe acidosis, hypocapnia, bicarbonate use, aggressive fluid resuscitation.
  • Develops typically 4–12 hours into treatment.
  • Signs — headache, irritability, deteriorating consciousness, bradycardia, hypertension, pupil abnormalities.
  • Treat — hyperosmolar therapy (mannitol or 3 % saline), reduce fluid rate, head-of-bed up, ICU support.
SAQ 22. Posterior reversible encephalopathy syndrome (PRES).
Answer.
  • Acute encephalopathy with headache, seizures, visual changes.
  • MRI — bilateral parieto-occipital white matter edema (vasogenic, T2/FLAIR hyperintense).
  • Causes — hypertension, eclampsia, immunosuppressants (tacrolimus, cyclosporine), chemotherapy, autoimmune disease.
  • Treatment — control hypertension, remove offending agent, treat seizures.
  • Usually reversible if treated early.
SAQ 23. Acute disseminated encephalomyelitis (ADEM).
Answer.
  • Monophasic immune-mediated demyelination — often after infection or vaccination.
  • Encephalopathy + multifocal neurological deficits.
  • MRI — multifocal large T2/FLAIR hyperintensities involving white and gray matter.
  • Anti-MOG antibody in some pediatric cases (MOGAD).
  • Treatment — high-dose methylprednisolone × 3–5 days; IVIG or PLEX if refractory.
  • Most pediatric cases have good recovery.
SAQ 24. When should LP be deferred before imaging?
Answer.
  • Focal neurological signs.
  • Papilledema.
  • Immunocompromised.
  • New seizures.
  • Recent head injury.
  • Reduced consciousness with concerns for raised ICP.
  • Bleeding diathesis / anticoagulation (relative).
  • If deferred, give empiric antibiotics + acyclovir before imaging.
SAQ 25. Abusive head trauma — features to suspect.
Answer.
  • Mechanism does not match injury.
  • Retinal hemorrhages on fundoscopy (especially multilayered, peripheral).
  • Subdural hematomas, especially interhemispheric or multiple ages.
  • Posterior rib fractures, metaphyseal corner fractures.
  • Skin findings — bruising in non-mobile infant.
  • Encephalopathy without clear cause.
  • Report to child protection; multidisciplinary investigation.
SAQ 26. Outline brain death determination prerequisites in pediatrics.
Answer.
  • Known cause of irreversible coma.
  • Correction of confounders — hypotension, hypothermia, sedation, NMBA, metabolic.
  • Minimum age 37 weeks corrected gestational age.
  • Observation period — 24 h (<30 d), 12 h older infants/children.
  • Two separate exams by different physicians.
  • Apnea test demonstrating PaCO₂ rise ≥20 mmHg above baseline (and to ≥60 mmHg) without respiratory effort.
  • Ancillary testing (EEG, cerebral blood flow study) if apnea test cannot be completed.
SAQ 27. Coma cocktail components.
Answer.
  • Dextrose (0.25–0.5 g/kg) for hypoglycemia.
  • Naloxone for opioid overdose.
  • Thiamine before glucose in malnourished / alcoholic adolescents.
  • Flumazenil — caution in mixed overdoses (lowers seizure threshold).
SAQ 28. Carbon monoxide poisoning features and treatment.
Answer.
  • Headache, nausea, confusion, coma; cherry red lips classic but unreliable.
  • SpO₂ often normal — co-oximeter required for carboxyhemoglobin level.
  • Treatment — 100 % O₂ via non-rebreather; hyperbaric O₂ if severe (coma, cardiac, pregnancy, persistent symptoms despite 100 % O₂).
  • Continue O₂ until COHb <5 % and symptoms resolved.
SAQ 29. Indications for emergent neuroimaging in pediatric coma.
Answer.
  • Trauma history.
  • Focal neurological signs.
  • Pupillary abnormalities.
  • Signs of raised ICP (Cushing's triad).
  • Status epilepticus refractory to first-line therapy.
  • Pre-LP if any of above.
SAQ 30. Pediatric basilar artery thrombosis — recognition.
Answer.
  • Locked-in syndrome — quadriplegia + anarthria with preserved vertical gaze.
  • Brainstem signs — pinpoint pupils, cranial nerve palsies.
  • Acute or subacute onset.
  • Risk factors — cardiac source, dissection, hypercoagulable state.
  • Diagnosis — MRI / MRA / CTA.
  • Time-sensitive thrombolysis / thrombectomy.
SAQ 31. Inborn errors of metabolism in coma.
Answer.
  • Suspect in unexplained metabolic acidosis, hyperammonemia, hypoglycemia, lactic acidosis.
  • Categories — urea cycle defects, organic acidemias, fatty acid oxidation defects, mitochondrial disease, lysosomal storage, peroxisomal disorders.
  • Initial workup — ammonia, glucose, lactate, ABG, urine organic acids, plasma amino acids, acylcarnitine profile.
  • Management — stop protein, IV dextrose, treat specific cause.
SAQ 32. Wernicke encephalopathy in adolescents.
Answer.
  • Triad — encephalopathy, ophthalmoplegia, ataxia.
  • Risk — anorexia nervosa, prolonged vomiting (hyperemesis gravidarum), bariatric surgery, alcoholism.
  • Thiamine 100 mg IV before glucose.
  • MRI — bilateral thalamic, mamillary body, periaqueductal gray signal abnormality.
SAQ 33. Pediatric stroke recognition.
Answer.
  • Acute focal neurological deficit ± altered consciousness.
  • Etiologies — sickle cell, cardioembolic, arteriopathy (FCA, moyamoya), trauma/dissection, prothrombotic, infection-associated.
  • Emergent MRI/MRA preferred over CT in stable patient.
  • Exchange transfusion for sickle cell stroke (HbS <30 %).
  • Consider tPA / thrombectomy per AHA/ASA guidelines.
SAQ 34. Outline cerebral sinovenous thrombosis (CSVT) presentation.
Answer.
  • Headache, focal signs, seizures, altered mental status.
  • Risk — dehydration, infection (mastoiditis), prothrombotic disease, oral contraceptives, malignancy, post-LP.
  • MRV is investigation of choice.
  • Anticoagulation (LMWH then warfarin or DOAC) for 3–6 months.
  • Endovascular thrombectomy in selected cases.
SAQ 35. Approach to febrile coma in returning traveler.
Answer.
  • Cerebral malaria until proven otherwise — thick + thin blood films, rapid antigen test.
  • Empiric artesunate.
  • Consider bacterial meningitis, viral encephalitis (Japanese encephalitis, dengue, rabies), TB meningitis, typhoid encephalopathy.
  • Detailed travel history and exposure history.
  • Public-health consultation.
SAQ 36. Cardiac causes of pediatric coma.
Answer.
  • Arrhythmia with low CO (long QT, AV block, supraventricular tachycardia).
  • Cardiomyopathy.
  • Congenital heart disease with cyanotic spell.
  • Endocarditis with septic embolus / abscess.
  • Cardiac arrest with anoxic injury.
  • ECG and echo on initial workup.
SAQ 37. Hepatic encephalopathy in pediatrics.
Answer.
  • Causes — acute liver failure (viral, drug, metabolic, autoimmune), portosystemic shunt, urea cycle defects mimicking.
  • Features — confusion, asterixis (older children), coma.
  • ↑ ammonia, ↑ transaminases, coagulopathy.
  • Treatment — lactulose, rifaximin, ammonia scavengers if severe, liver transplant evaluation.
SAQ 38. Hypertensive encephalopathy.
Answer.
  • Acute severe hypertension overwhelming autoregulation.
  • Headache, vomiting, seizures, altered consciousness.
  • Often features of PRES on imaging.
  • Causes — renal disease, pheochromocytoma, coarctation, steroid use, immunosuppressants.
  • Treatment — controlled BP reduction (25 % over first 8 hours), avoid rapid drops; nicardipine or labetalol infusion.
SAQ 39. Convulsive vs. nonconvulsive status epilepticus in coma.
Answer.
  • Convulsive — overt motor manifestations >5 min.
  • Nonconvulsive — electrographic seizures without obvious motor activity; up to 50 % of seizures in critically ill children.
  • Both require continuous EEG and aggressive antiepileptic management.
  • Treatment cascade — benzodiazepine → fosphenytoin / levetiracetam → midazolam infusion → pentobarbital coma.
  • Consider ketogenic diet, immunotherapy for refractory cases.
SAQ 40. Mimics of pediatric coma to remember.
Answer.
  • Locked-in syndrome — preserved consciousness.
  • Catatonia / catatonic features (psychiatric).
  • Akinetic mutism (frontal lobe lesion).
  • Functional / psychogenic unresponsiveness.
  • Severe hypoglycemia mimicking metabolic coma.
  • Profound hypothermia.

7. Case Bank — 40 PICU Cases

Case 1 — Severe TBI with raised ICP.
8-year-old after MVC, GCS 6 on arrival, fixed dilated right pupil.
Q. Immediate management.
Answer.
  • Impending uncal herniation — emergency.
  • Intubate; hyperosmolar therapy (hypertonic saline 3 % bolus or 23.4 %); brief hyperventilation as bridge.
  • Maintain age-appropriate CPP.
  • Emergent CT head; neurosurgery consult for decompression.
  • Continuous EEG; treat seizures.
  • Avoid hypoxia, hypotension, hyperthermia.
Case 2 — Anti-NMDA receptor encephalitis.
14-year-old with 2-week history of psychiatric symptoms, dyskinesias, now obtunded and febrile.
Q. Workup and management.
Answer.
  • LP — anti-NMDA receptor antibody.
  • MRI brain.
  • Pelvic / abdominal imaging for tumor (ovarian teratoma).
  • First-line — high-dose methylprednisolone + IVIG or PLEX.
  • Refractory — rituximab + cyclophosphamide.
  • Continuous EEG; ICU support for autonomic / respiratory failure.
Case 3 — Bacterial meningitis.
2-year-old with fever, neck stiffness, irritability, GCS 11. Bulging fontanelle.
Q. Initial management.
Answer.
  • Blood cultures; empiric vancomycin + ceftriaxone (CNS dose).
  • Dexamethasone with or before first antibiotic dose.
  • Empiric acyclovir if HSV suspected.
  • CT before LP (papilledema concern).
  • LP if no contraindication; CSF cell count, glucose, protein, gram stain, culture, PCR for enteroviruses and HSV.
  • Supportive ICU care.
Case 4 — HSV encephalitis.
5-year-old with fever, behavioral changes, focal right temporal seizure, MRI shows right temporal lobe T2 hyperintensity.
Q. Diagnosis and management.
Answer.
  • HSV encephalitis (temporal lobe predilection).
  • Empiric acyclovir 30 mg/kg/day IV (or 60 mg/kg/d in neonates).
  • 21-day course typically.
  • CSF HSV PCR.
  • Continuous EEG.
  • Monitor for cerebral edema; supportive care.
Case 5 — Nonconvulsive status epilepticus.
7-year-old after suspected first generalized seizure has persistent altered mental status 2 hours later.
Q. What to consider, action.
Answer.
  • Nonconvulsive status epilepticus.
  • Continuous EEG.
  • If electrographic seizures — benzodiazepine, fosphenytoin/levetiracetam, midazolam infusion, pentobarbital coma.
  • Investigate etiology — infection, metabolic, autoimmune.
  • MRI brain if no clear cause.
Case 6 — Locked-in syndrome.
15-year-old presents with sudden quadriplegia, anarthria, but vertical eye movements preserved.
Q. Diagnosis and management.
Answer.
  • Locked-in syndrome — basilar artery thrombosis until proven otherwise.
  • Emergent MRI / MRA / CTA.
  • Activate stroke / thrombectomy team.
  • tPA / mechanical thrombectomy if within window.
  • Communicate with patient via eye movement.
  • Long-term — tracheostomy, gastrostomy, assistive communication.
Case 7 — DKA cerebral edema.
9-year-old in DKA, 6 hours into rehydration, develops headache, then becomes obtunded.
Q. Management.
Answer.
  • Cerebral edema — most common cause of pediatric DKA mortality.
  • Hypertonic saline 3 % (5 mL/kg) or mannitol 0.5–1 g/kg.
  • Reduce fluid rate.
  • Head-of-bed up.
  • ICU monitoring; consider intubation if GCS <9.
  • CT head if no immediate response.
Case 8 — Cerebral malaria.
5-year-old returning from West Africa with high fever and coma. Smear positive for Plasmodium falciparum.
Q. Treatment priorities.
Answer.
  • Parenteral artesunate (AQUAMAT — superior to quinine).
  • Aggressive hypoglycemia management.
  • Avoid corticosteroids (no benefit).
  • Cautious fluid resuscitation (FEAST trial).
  • Treat seizures.
  • Monitor for hemoglobinuria, AKI.
  • ICU support.
Case 9 — Hyperammonemic coma.
Neonate at 36 hours of life develops poor feeding, hyperventilation, ammonia 800 μmol/L.
Q. Management.
Answer.
  • Stop protein; IV dextrose 10 %.
  • Ammonul (sodium phenylacetate / benzoate).
  • L-arginine.
  • Hemodialysis if NH3 >500 or rising.
  • Send organic acids, amino acids, acylcarnitine profile.
  • Urea cycle defect (e.g., OTC deficiency) likely.
Case 10 — Hypoglycemic coma.
10-month-old presents with seizure, glucose 1.4 mmol/L.
Q. Immediate action and workup.
Answer.
  • Dextrose 0.5 g/kg IV bolus immediately.
  • Critical sample before glucose if possible — glucose, insulin, C-peptide, cortisol, GH, lactate, ammonia, ketones, acylcarnitine, organic acids.
  • Differential — hyperinsulinism, fatty acid oxidation defect, gluconeogenesis defect, adrenal insufficiency, beckwith-wiedemann.
  • Glucose infusion to maintain euglycemia.
Case 11 — Opioid overdose.
Adolescent found unresponsive, pinpoint pupils, RR 6, GCS 6.
Q. Management.
Answer.
  • Naloxone 0.01–0.1 mg/kg IV (or IM/IN if no access).
  • Support airway / ventilation.
  • Re-dose naloxone — short half-life vs. opioids; may need infusion (2/3 of arousing dose per hour).
  • Investigate for co-ingestion.
  • Watch for withdrawal in opioid-dependent adolescents.
  • Toxicology consult; social work.
Case 12 — Carbon monoxide poisoning.
Family found in apartment with malfunctioning heater; child obtunded, SpO₂ 99 %.
Q. Diagnosis and treatment.
Answer.
  • CO poisoning — SpO₂ misleadingly normal.
  • Co-oximeter for carboxyhemoglobin level.
  • 100 % O₂ via non-rebreather.
  • Hyperbaric O₂ if severe (coma, cardiac involvement, pregnancy, persistent symptoms).
  • Continue O₂ until COHb <5 % and symptoms resolved.
  • Investigate source; involve family.
Case 13 — Wernicke encephalopathy in anorexia.
15-year-old with anorexia nervosa develops confusion, ophthalmoplegia, ataxia.
Q. Diagnosis and action.
Answer.
  • Wernicke encephalopathy.
  • Thiamine 100 mg IV BEFORE glucose.
  • Continue thiamine 100 mg/day for several days.
  • MRI — bilateral thalamic, mamillary body, periaqueductal gray.
  • Cautious refeeding to avoid refeeding syndrome.
  • Multidisciplinary eating disorder program.
Case 14 — Abusive head trauma.
4-month-old infant brought in by caregiver with "fall from couch"; GCS 7, fontanelle bulging, retinal hemorrhages.
Q. Action.
Answer.
  • Stabilize ABC, manage raised ICP.
  • CT head — subdural hematoma (interhemispheric, multiple ages) suggestive.
  • Skeletal survey — posterior rib fractures, metaphyseal corner fractures.
  • Fundoscopy — retinal hemorrhages.
  • Report to child protection — mandatory.
  • Multidisciplinary investigation; social work; legal documentation.
Case 15 — ADEM.
7-year-old, 2 weeks post-URI, develops encephalopathy + multifocal neurological deficits. MRI shows multifocal white matter lesions.
Q. Diagnosis and treatment.
Answer.
  • Acute disseminated encephalomyelitis.
  • High-dose IV methylprednisolone 30 mg/kg/day × 3–5 days.
  • IVIG or PLEX if refractory.
  • Anti-MOG antibody testing (MOGAD).
  • Most pediatric cases recover well; some relapse → multiphasic ADEM.
  • Follow-up MRI to ensure resolution.
Case 16 — PRES from tacrolimus.
12-year-old post-renal transplant on tacrolimus develops hypertension, headache, seizures, cortical blindness.
Q. Diagnosis.
Answer.
  • Posterior reversible encephalopathy syndrome (PRES).
  • MRI — bilateral parieto-occipital T2/FLAIR hyperintensity.
  • Treat — control hypertension (nicardipine, labetalol), reduce or stop tacrolimus.
  • Treat seizures.
  • Usually reversible; visual symptoms may persist.
Case 17 — Salicylate toxicity.
Adolescent presents with tachypnea, tinnitus, confusion. Mixed respiratory alkalosis + metabolic acidosis on ABG.
Q. Diagnosis and management.
Answer.
  • Salicylate toxicity.
  • Serum salicylate level.
  • Urinary alkalinization with bicarbonate (target urine pH 7.5–8).
  • Glucose for cerebral hypoglycorrhachia even if serum glucose normal.
  • Hemodialysis if level >100 mg/dL, severe acidosis, renal failure, altered mental status.
  • Avoid intubation if possible (loss of compensatory hyperventilation worsens acidosis).
Case 18 — Hepatic encephalopathy.
8-year-old with acute liver failure develops asterixis, confusion, then coma.
Q. Management.
Answer.
  • Stabilize ABC; manage coagulopathy.
  • Lactulose to reduce ammonia.
  • Rifaximin reduces gut ammonia-producing bacteria.
  • Hyperosmolar therapy if signs of cerebral edema.
  • Continuous EEG.
  • Liver transplant evaluation.
Case 19 — Hypertensive encephalopathy.
14-year-old with chronic renal disease, BP 200/130, headache, seizures, then obtundation.
Q. Management.
Answer.
  • Controlled BP reduction — 25 % over first 8 hours.
  • Avoid rapid drops.
  • Nicardipine or labetalol infusion.
  • MRI brain — may show PRES.
  • Treat seizures.
  • Investigate cause — renal artery stenosis, pheochromocytoma, glomerulonephritis.
Case 20 — TCA overdose.
Adolescent ingestion of amitriptyline; presents with coma, wide QRS, hypotension, seizures.
Q. Management.
Answer.
  • ABC; intubate; activated charcoal if within 1 h.
  • Sodium bicarbonate 1–2 mEq/kg bolus to narrow QRS <100 ms.
  • Hyperventilation to pH 7.45–7.55.
  • Lidocaine for refractory arrhythmias.
  • Benzodiazepines for seizures.
  • Intralipid emulsion for refractory cases.
  • Avoid flumazenil (mixed overdose; seizure risk).
Case 21 — Sickle cell stroke.
7-year-old with sickle cell disease develops acute right hemiparesis and aphasia.
Q. Acute management.
Answer.
  • Exchange transfusion to HbS <30 %.
  • Hydration, oxygenation.
  • Neurology / hematology consult.
  • Consider tPA / thrombectomy in selected cases (AHA/ASA pediatric stroke).
  • Long-term — chronic transfusion, hydroxyurea, TCD screening.
Case 22 — Cerebral sinovenous thrombosis.
11-year-old with otitis media and mastoiditis develops headache, seizure, focal signs.
Q. Diagnosis and management.
Answer.
  • CSVT (lateral sinus thrombosis common with mastoiditis).
  • MRV is investigation of choice.
  • Anticoagulation (LMWH then warfarin or DOAC) for 3–6 months.
  • Treat mastoiditis with antibiotics and surgical drainage.
  • Treat seizures.
  • Endovascular thrombectomy in selected cases.
Case 23 — Iron poisoning.
Toddler ingested prenatal iron supplement; presents with GI symptoms, then becomes lethargic, hypotensive.
Q. Phases and management.
Answer.
  • Phases — GI (0–6 h), latent (6–24 h), shock/metabolic acidosis (12–48 h), hepatotoxicity (48–96 h), bowel scarring (4–6 wk).
  • Whole bowel irrigation; consider gastric lavage.
  • Deferoxamine chelation if serum Fe >500 μg/dL or symptomatic.
  • Supportive care; intubation if severe.
Case 24 — Lead encephalopathy.
2-year-old with developmental regression, irritability, seizures. Blood lead 80 μg/dL.
Q. Management.
Answer.
  • Severe lead encephalopathy.
  • Chelation — calcium-EDTA + dimercaprol (BAL) for severe; succimer (DMSA) for moderate.
  • ICP management — hyperosmolar therapy if signs.
  • Treat seizures.
  • Source identification — paint, soil, traditional remedies.
  • Long-term — developmental follow-up.
Case 25 — Vagal hyperresponse during intubation.
5-year-old with severe asthma intubated; develops profound bradycardia and asystole.
Q. Mechanism and management.
Answer.
  • Vagal response to laryngoscopy and intubation.
  • Atropine 20 mcg/kg IV pretreatment in young children before RSI.
  • Treat asystole with CPR + epinephrine.
  • Optimize oxygenation and ventilation.
Case 26 — Anoxic injury post-drowning.
6-year-old recovered after near-drowning, ROSC at 20 min, intubated and comatose.
Q. Brain protection plan.
Answer.
  • Targeted normothermia 36.0–37.5 °C (THAPCA).
  • Avoid hypotension, hyperoxia, hypocapnia, hyperthermia.
  • Continuous EEG.
  • Avoid tight glucose control.
  • Watch for cerebral edema; hyperosmolar therapy if needed.
  • Family discussion about prognosis (delay statements ≥72 h).
Case 27 — Tuberculous meningitis.
Adolescent immigrant with subacute headache, fever, cranial nerve palsies, hydrocephalus on CT.
Q. Diagnosis and management.
Answer.
  • TB meningitis until proven otherwise.
  • LP — lymphocytic pleocytosis, low glucose, high protein, AFB smear/culture/PCR.
  • 4-drug regimen — isoniazid, rifampin, pyrazinamide, ethambutol.
  • Dexamethasone reduces mortality.
  • Treat hydrocephalus — VP shunt or EVD.
  • HIV testing; contact tracing.
Case 28 — Methemoglobinemia.
Infant given homemade well water; develops cyanosis, SpO₂ 85 %, "chocolate" colored blood.
Q. Diagnosis and management.
Answer.
  • Methemoglobinemia from nitrate exposure.
  • Co-oximetry — high MetHb.
  • Methylene blue 1–2 mg/kg IV.
  • Avoid in G6PD deficiency (precipitates hemolysis).
  • Supportive oxygen; manage well water source.
Case 29 — Mitochondrial encephalopathy (MELAS).
14-year-old with hearing loss, short stature, episodic stroke-like episodes; current admission for status epilepticus and confusion.
Q. Pathophysiology and management.
Answer.
  • Mitochondrial encephalomyopathy with lactic acidosis and stroke-like episodes.
  • m.3243A>G most common mutation (MT-TL1).
  • Stroke-like episodes — not vascular territory; energy failure.
  • Treat status epilepticus — avoid valproate (mitochondrial toxic); avoid prolonged propofol (PRIS).
  • Levetiracetam first-line.
  • L-arginine, coenzyme Q10, L-carnitine.
  • Manage acidosis, hypoglycemia.
Case 30 — Drug-induced serotonin syndrome.
Adolescent on SSRI given linezolid develops agitation, hyperthermia, hyperreflexia, clonus.
Q. Diagnosis and management.
Answer.
  • Serotonin syndrome from SSRI + MAOI (linezolid is weak MAOI).
  • Stop offending agents.
  • Supportive — cooling, hydration, benzodiazepines for agitation.
  • Cyproheptadine (serotonin antagonist) for severe.
  • Avoid neuromuscular blockers without sedation.
  • Differential — NMS, anticholinergic toxicity, malignant hyperthermia.
Case 31 — Neuroleptic malignant syndrome.
Adolescent on haloperidol develops hyperthermia, rigidity, altered mental status, autonomic instability.
Q. Management.
Answer.
  • Neuroleptic malignant syndrome.
  • Stop offending neuroleptic.
  • Supportive — cooling, IV hydration, manage rhabdomyolysis.
  • Benzodiazepines.
  • Dantrolene or bromocriptine for severe.
  • Differential — serotonin syndrome, malignant hyperthermia, anticholinergic.
Case 32 — Functional / psychogenic unresponsiveness.
Adolescent presents unresponsive after argument; exam shows variable findings; eyes resist opening; pupils normal.
Q. Approach.
Answer.
  • Functional / dissociative unresponsiveness.
  • Maintain caution — rule out organic causes first.
  • Bedside clues — resistance to eye opening, variable exam, sleep architecture on EEG.
  • Avoid invasive tests once diagnosis secure.
  • Multidisciplinary — psychiatry, social work.
  • Communicate diagnosis empathetically.
Case 33 — Cyanide poisoning.
Adolescent rescued from house fire with altered mental status, lactate 12, cherry red skin.
Q. Diagnosis and treatment.
Answer.
  • Cyanide poisoning from smoke inhalation.
  • Hydroxocobalamin 70 mg/kg IV (Cyanokit).
  • Sodium thiosulfate adjunct.
  • Concurrent CO poisoning — 100 % O₂.
  • Manage airway burns.
  • Differential — sodium nitroprusside toxicity in ICU.
Case 34 — Refractory status epilepticus / FIRES.
5-year-old with febrile illness develops new-onset refractory status epilepticus.
Q. Management.
Answer.
  • Febrile infection-related epilepsy syndrome (FIRES) suspected.
  • Continuous EEG.
  • Standard AED escalation — benzodiazepine → fosphenytoin / levetiracetam → midazolam infusion → pentobarbital coma.
  • Ketogenic diet (early initiation).
  • Anakinra (IL-1R antagonist) — increasingly used in FIRES.
  • IVIG, PLEX, steroids — investigate for autoimmune component.
Case 35 — Subarachnoid hemorrhage (AVM rupture).
15-year-old with sudden severe headache, vomiting, then loss of consciousness.
Q. Diagnosis and management.
Answer.
  • SAH from AVM rupture (most common pediatric SAH cause).
  • CT head — subarachnoid blood; CTA for AVM.
  • Manage raised ICP and hydrocephalus.
  • Nimodipine for vasospasm prophylaxis (extrapolated from adult).
  • Neurosurgery — endovascular embolization, surgical resection, or radiosurgery.
Case 36 — Posterior fossa tumor.
7-year-old with morning headaches, vomiting, ataxia, then obtundation.
Q. Likely diagnosis and management.
Answer.
  • Posterior fossa tumor (medulloblastoma, ependymoma, astrocytoma) with hydrocephalus.
  • Urgent MRI brain.
  • Hyperosmolar therapy for raised ICP.
  • EVD or VP shunt for hydrocephalus.
  • Neurosurgical resection.
  • Oncology consultation; chemotherapy / radiation per tumor type.
Case 37 — Hyponatremic encephalopathy.
Toddler with bronchiolitis on D5/0.225 % NaCl IVF for 36 h develops seizures and obtundation. Serum Na 118.
Q. Diagnosis and management.
Answer.
  • Symptomatic hyponatremia (SIADH + hypotonic fluid).
  • 3 % saline 2 mL/kg over 10–15 min for symptomatic seizures.
  • Target Na rise ≤8–10 mEq/L per 24 h.
  • Avoid over-correction (osmotic demyelination risk).
  • Switch IVF to isotonic (NS or LR).
  • Investigate cause — SIADH, cerebral salt wasting, diuretics.
Case 38 — Cerebral salt wasting vs. SIADH.
Post-TBI patient with hyponatremia and high urine sodium.
Q. Distinguish.
Answer.
  • SIADH — euvolemic / hypervolemic; treat with fluid restriction.
  • Cerebral salt wasting — hypovolemic, polyuric; treat with sodium and volume replacement.
  • Clinical exam, weight, intake/output, BP, CVP help distinguish.
  • Fludrocortisone may be useful in CSW.
  • Misdiagnosing CSW as SIADH and restricting fluid worsens cerebral perfusion.
Case 39 — Refractory ICP requiring decompressive craniectomy.
10-year-old with severe TBI has ICP 35 mmHg despite tier 1 and tier 2 therapy.
Q. Surgical option and discussion.
Answer.
  • Decompressive craniectomy considered for refractory ICP.
  • Pediatric data and adult extrapolation (DECRA, RESCUEicp) — improves survival, functional outcome variable.
  • Family discussion about quality-of-life outcomes.
  • Subsequent cranioplasty months later.
  • ICP monitoring continues post-op.
Case 40 — Brain death determination.
8-year-old after severe TBI now 48 hours into ICU stay with no neurological recovery; clinician suspects brain death.
Q. Prerequisites and process.
Answer.
  • Prerequisites — known cause of irreversible coma, correction of confounders (hypotension, hypothermia, sedation, NMBA, metabolic).
  • Two separate examinations by different physicians.
  • Observation period — 12 h (older) or 24 h (younger infants).
  • Apnea test — PaCO₂ rises ≥20 mmHg above baseline and to ≥60 mmHg without respiratory effort.
  • Ancillary tests (EEG, CBF study) if apnea test cannot be completed.
  • Family communication and organ donation discussion.

10. Final One-Page Infographic

🧠 Approach to the Comatose Child — Master Map

① Stabilize first (ABCDE)

  • Airway — protect; intubate if GCS ≤8.
  • Breathing — oxygenate; avoid hypoxia + hyperoxia.
  • Circulation — age-appropriate MAP / CPP.
  • Disability — pupils, motor, GCS/FOUR.
  • Exposure — trauma, rash, fontanelles.

② Empiric "coma cocktail"

  • Dextrose 0.5 g/kg if hypoglycemic.
  • Naloxone if opioid suspected.
  • Thiamine before glucose in malnourished adolescents.
  • Antibiotics + acyclovir if CNS infection.
  • Hyperosmolar if herniation.
  • Anticonvulsant if seizing.

③ AEIOU TIPS differential

  • Alcohol, Endocrine, Insulin, Opioids/Oxygen, Uremia, Trauma, Infection, Psychiatric/Poisons, Seizure/Stroke/Shock.

④ Pupillary localization

  • Small reactive — metabolic.
  • Midposition fixed — midbrain.
  • Pinpoint — pontine / opioid / OP.
  • Unilateral fixed dilated — uncal herniation.
  • Bilateral fixed dilated — severe brainstem / brain death.

⑤ Investigation tiers

  • Tier 1 — glucose, electrolytes, ABG, ammonia, lactate, tox screen, ECG.
  • Tier 2 — CT head, MRI, LP, EEG, echo, specific tests.

⑥ Exam pearls

🔥🔥🔥 Always check vertical gaze — exclude locked-in.
🔥🔥🔥 Cushing's triad = late sign of raised ICP.
🔥🔥🔥 Up to 50 % of seizures in critically ill children are nonconvulsive.
🔥🔥 Thiamine before glucose in malnourished adolescents.
🔥🔥 Empiric antibiotics + acyclovir before LP when LP delayed.
🔥 Retinal hemorrhages in infant = think abusive head trauma.
