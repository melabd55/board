1 · Guidelines Lineage & ADAPT Trial

BTF pediatric severe TBI guidelines
  • 2003 — First edition pediatric severe TBI guidelines.
  • 2012 — Second edition update.
  • 2019 — Third edition (Kochanek PCCM 2019) with consensus/evidence-based algorithm by pediatric severe TBI committee.
  • Algorithm includes: baseline care, treatment of herniation, first-tier treatment of intracranial hypertension, second-tier for refractory ICH.
  • Reflects insufficient evidence (few pediatric RCTs) — much of practice based on adult extrapolation + observational studies.
ADAPT — Approaches and Decisions in Acute Pediatric TBI
  • Largest pediatric TBI comparative effectiveness study to date.
  • Completed enrollment + 1-year follow-up; major findings:
    • Despite general adherence to BTF guidelines, therapy is markedly heterogeneous across PICU programs nationally and internationally.
    • Specific subanalyses on hyperosmolar choice, hyperventilation, barbiturates, decompressive craniectomy.
  • Has not generated new prescriptive recommendations but informs precision medicine direction.
Precision medicine direction
  • Pediatric TBI endophenotypes — based on age, mechanism, imaging pattern, biomarker profile, genetic polymorphisms.
  • Genetic polymorphisms relevant to cerebral edema and outcome being identified (e.g., AQP4, IL-1, BDNF Val66Met).
  • Tailored monitoring and management plans rather than one-size-fits-all.

2 · Pathophysiology — Three Categories

Primary vs secondary injury
  • Primary injury — direct mechanical disruption of brain parenchyma at moment of impact; not preventable post-injury.
  • Secondary injury — biochemical, cellular, and molecular cascade evolving over hours to days; partially modifiable.
  • Three Zimmerman mechanism categories:
    1. Ischemia, excitotoxicity, energy failure, cell death cascades.
    2. Secondary cerebral swelling.
    3. Axonal injury (traumatic axonal injury, TAI).
Cell death cascade — excitotoxicity to apoptosis
  • Primary insult → ATP depletion → membrane depolarization.
  • Glutamate release → NMDA + AMPA overactivation.
  • Ca²⁺ influx → mitochondrial Ca²⁺ overload → mitochondrial permeability transition pore (MPTP) opening.
  • Mitochondrial collapse → bioenergetic failure + cytochrome c release.
  • Activation of caspases → apoptosis.
  • Activation of necroptosis pathways (RIPK1/3/MLKL).
  • Reactive oxygen and nitrogen species (ROS/RNS) during reperfusion.
  • Lipid peroxidation; DNA damage; PARP activation; ferroptosis emerging concept.
  • NMDA antagonism: caution in infants — preclinical evidence that NMDA antagonists may induce apoptotic neurodegeneration in immature brain.
Cerebral swelling mechanisms
  • Cerebral swelling peaks between 24 and 72 hours after injury (variable; often earlier and more pronounced in pediatric TBI).
  • Cerebral blood volume (CBV) contribution: early hyperemia in some patients (CBF > metabolic demand); vasoparalysis.
  • Cytotoxic edema: intracellular water accumulation from Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase failure; astrocyte swelling predominant; AQP4 channels.
  • Vasogenic edema: BBB disruption with extravasation of plasma into extracellular space.
  • Osmotic edema: osmolar shifts after dilutional resuscitation or hyponatremia.
  • Diffuse swelling more common in children than adults — pediatric vulnerability.
Traumatic axonal injury (TAI)
  • Shearing forces injure axons rather than direct disruption.
  • Mechanisms:
    • Mitochondrial permeability transition pore opening → mitochondrial failure.
    • Calpain-mediated proteolysis of microtubules.
    • Failure of axoplasmic transport.
    • Accumulation of proteins (amyloid precursor protein, APP).
    • Direct axolemmal poration.
  • Detected by susceptibility-weighted imaging (SWI), diffusion tensor imaging (DTI).
  • Underlies diffuse axonal injury (DAI) and post-concussion symptoms.

3 · Initial Resuscitation

Priorities
  • Complete and rapid physiologic resuscitation is the first priority.
  • Identify and correct airway obstruction, inadequate ventilation, and shock BEFORE detailed neurologic assessment.
  • Brain-specific interventions in absence of herniation signs are NOT routinely recommended early.
  • Mannitol/hypertonic saline use during initial resuscitation can be counterproductive — reverse osmolar gradient may develop if BBB disrupted.
  • Hypotension and hypoxemia are critical secondary insults — increased morbidity and mortality.
Intubation criteria (Zimmerman Box 118.1)
  • GCS ≤ 10.
  • Decrease in GCS > 3 points, independent of initial GCS.
  • Anisocoria > 1 mm.
  • Cervical spine injury compromising ventilation.
  • Apnea.
  • Hypercarbia (PaCO₂ > 45 mm Hg).
  • Loss of pharyngeal reflex.
  • Spontaneous hyperventilation causing PaCO₂ < 25 mm Hg.
Rapid-sequence induction (RSI) in TBI
  • Goals: analgesia, amnesia, and neuromuscular blockade rapidly achieved.
  • Bag-valve-mask ventilation avoided unless hypoxia or impending herniation.
  • Cervical spine immobilization throughout.
  • Orotracheal intubation under direct vision is recommended route; avoid nasotracheal.
  • FiO₂ 1.0 pre-oxygenation before laryngoscopy.
  • Endotracheal tube secured without circumferential neck tape (avoid impeding cerebral venous return).
  • Hemodynamically unstable: fentanyl 2–4 µg/kg + lidocaine 1 mg/kg + rocuronium 1 mg/kg or vecuronium 0.3 mg/kg.
  • Hemodynamically stable: add midazolam 0.1–0.2 mg/kg; etomidate 0.1–0.3 mg/kg (concerns regarding adrenal suppression).
  • Lidocaine 1 mg/kg blunts catecholamine surge from laryngoscopy.
  • Thiopental availability curtailed; propofol an alternative in stable patients.
Circulatory stabilization
  • Posttraumatic hypoperfusion assumed hemorrhagic until proven otherwise.
  • Cardiac contusion less common in children than adults but possible.
  • Fluid: 20 mL/kg isotonic crystalloid (0.9% NaCl) as initial bolus.
  • NO hypotonic fluids in TBI resuscitation.
  • Subsequent: isotonic crystalloid or packed RBCs titrated to BP, perfusion, hematocrit.
  • Lactated Ringer is slightly hypotonic — controversy, but generally accepted.
  • 3% saline can be used as maintenance fluid (Fisher) — first-tier hyperosmolar strategy.
  • Albumin/colloid trials negative in adult TBI (NIH trials halted for futility); albumin associated with INCREASED mortality.

4 · Herniation Pathway

Recognition triggers
  • Pupillary dilation.
  • Systemic hypertension.
  • Bradycardia (Cushing's triad).
  • Extensor posturing.
  • Sudden GCS decline.
Emergency treatment (Zimmerman Fig. 118.6)
  • Brief hyperventilation with FiO₂ 1.0 — titrate to reverse pupillary dilation.
  • Mannitol 0.5–1.0 g/kg OR hypertonic saline (3% 1–3 mL/kg up to 250 mL; OR 23.4% 0.5 mL/kg up to 30 mL).
  • Open EVD to continuous drainage if in place.
  • Ensure appropriate sedation/analgesia (avoid stimulating ICP spikes).
  • To CT scan or surgery (emergent decompression / hematoma evacuation).
Pediatric-specific herniation considerations
  • Diffuse cerebral swelling more common than focal mass lesions in pediatric TBI.
  • Status epilepticus may precipitate herniation in already-vulnerable brain.
  • Iatrogenic hypoxemia/hypercarbia or excessive fluid administration can trigger.
  • AHT particularly associated with severe diffuse swelling.

5 · Imaging — Marshall Classification and Beyond

Marshall CT classification (Table 118.3, adult-derived; pediatric extrapolated)
  • Diffuse injury I: no visible intracranial pathology.
  • Diffuse injury II: cisterns present with shift 0–5 mm or lesion densities present; no high or mixed density lesion > 25 mL.
  • Diffuse injury III (swelling): cisterns compressed or absent with shift 0–5 mm.
  • Diffuse injury IV (shift): shift > 5 mm; no high or mixed density > 25 mL.
  • Evacuated mass lesion: any surgically evacuated lesion.
  • Nonevacuated mass: high or mixed density > 25 mL.
  • Brain dead: no brainstem reflexes; flaccid; fixed/non-reactive pupils; apnea with normal PaCO₂.
Pediatric-specific CT considerations
  • AHT: subdural interhemispheric and convexity hemorrhages, preexisting lesions — 2–3× more common than accidental TBI.
  • Accidental TBI: epidural hematomas more common.
  • Normal CT does NOT exclude raised ICP (Zimmerman emphasis).
  • Approximately 15% of adults with normal CT after severe TBI develop clinically significant ICH.
  • Children with normal initial CT but hypotension or abnormal posturing have similar ICH risk as those with abnormal CT.
  • Routine reimaging at 24–48 h is NOT supported by literature; reimage based on clinical change or rising ICP.
MRI in pediatric TBI
  • Diffusion-weighted imaging (DWI) — cerebral edema.
  • Susceptibility-weighted imaging (SWI) — microhemorrhages and DAI.
  • Diffusion tensor imaging (DTI) — white matter integrity.
  • High-definition fiber tractography (HDFT) — research applications.
  • MR spectroscopy (NAA, lactate) — neuronal viability.
  • Functional MRI — research.
  • Used for prognostic information and to characterize TAI not visible on CT.

6 · Intracranial Pressure Monitoring

ICP monitoring debate
  • No pediatric RCT establishes ICP monitoring efficacy in altering outcome.
  • Forsyth UK multicenter database (> 500 pediatric TBI cases): ICP > 20 mm Hg AND lack of ICP monitoring independently associated with death before discharge.
  • Gerber et al. (NY State 2001–2009): mortality decreased from 23% to 13% with concurrent increase in guidelines-based ICP monitoring from 56% to 75%.
  • Chesnut RCT (Latin America, 324 adults): no outcome difference between ICP-directed care vs imaging + clinical exam protocol.
  • Bennett et al. propensity-matched analysis in > 3,000 children: no benefit of ICP monitoring (limitations of analysis acknowledged).
  • In practice: ICP monitoring rates remain low — only ~33% of US infants/toddlers with severe TBI underwent ICP monitoring in 2005.
  • Therapies in absence of monitoring inevitably extrapolated from monitor-guided studies — caveat for clinical practice.
ICP monitor placement considerations
  • EVD (external ventricular drain) — gold standard; allows therapeutic CSF drainage; risk of infection (1–10%).
  • Parenchymal monitor (intraparenchymal fiber-optic) — easier insertion; no therapeutic drainage; tip drift over time.
  • Subdural/subarachnoid bolts — less accurate; rarely used now.
  • Coagulopathy is the main contraindication; correct INR < 1.5 and platelets > 100 before insertion when feasible.
  • In infants with unossified skull, bolt may fracture — use cautiously.

7 · BTF Tier 1 — First-Tier Therapies for Intracranial Hypertension

Baseline care (all severe TBI patients)
  • Head of bed elevation 30°; midline neck position.
  • Adequate sedation and analgesia.
  • Normocapnia (PaCO₂ 35–40 mm Hg); SpO₂ > 94%; avoid hyperoxia.
  • Normothermia (avoid fever).
  • Normoglycemia (avoid hypoglycemia < 3.3 mmol/L and severe hyperglycemia > 10 mmol/L).
  • CPP target by age (≥ 40 infant, ≥ 50 school-age, ≥ 60 adolescent).
  • Seizure prophylaxis: levetiracetam 7 days post-injury for severe TBI.
  • VTE prophylaxis with mechanical devices; pharmacologic per protocol after bleeding ruled out.
Tier 1 interventions for ICP > 20
  • Confirm/deepen sedation and analgesia.
  • Neuromuscular blockade trial if cough/shiver-driven spikes.
  • CSF drainage via EVD if in place.
  • Hyperosmolar therapy:
    • 3% NaCl 2–5 mL/kg bolus over 10–20 min; continuous infusion targeting Na 150–160; osmolality ≤ 360.
    • Mannitol 0.25–1 g/kg; osmolality ceiling 320 mOsm/kg; risk of AKI.
  • Maintain MAP for CPP at age-appropriate target.
  • Continuous EEG monitoring for nonconvulsive seizures.

8 · BTF Tier 2 — Refractory Intracranial Hypertension

When to escalate
  • Refractory ICH despite optimal Tier 1.
  • Rapid neurologic deterioration.
  • Multidisciplinary discussion required.
Tier 2 options
  • Controlled mild hyperventilation (PaCO₂ 30–35) with PbtO₂ or SjvO₂ monitoring; avoid prophylactic hyperventilation.
  • Barbiturate coma (pentobarbital): titrate to burst suppression on cEEG; vasopressor support anticipated; daily echo; watch for ileus, infections.
  • Decompressive craniectomy — pediatric Taylor 2001 RCT supportive for refractory cases; weigh ICP reduction against severe disability survival.
  • Therapeutic hypothermia — Cool Kids RCT negative; not recommended as routine. Strict normothermia recommended.
  • Multidisciplinary family meeting at this stage.

9 · Advanced Neuromonitoring

Multimodal integration
  • ICP/CPP: backbone; threshold-based.
  • PRx (pressure reactivity index): CPPopt for individualized targets.
  • PbtO₂ (brain tissue O₂): regional measure; target > 20 mm Hg; detects ischemia despite "adequate" CPP.
  • SjvO₂: global oxygen extraction; < 50% ischemia, > 75% hyperemia.
  • Cerebral microdialysis: lactate/pyruvate ratio > 25 = metabolic crisis; glucose, glutamate, glycerol.
  • Transcranial Doppler (TCD): CBF velocity; vasospasm detection; noninvasive ICP estimation.
  • NIRS: regional cerebral O₂ saturation.
  • cEEG: NCSE detection; burst suppression titration.
  • Pupillometry: quantitative NPi; trends before clinical herniation.

10 · Special Considerations

Age-related differences (pediatric vs adult TBI)
  • Children 5–15 years generally have FAVORABLE outcomes vs adults at matched GCS.
  • Children < 4 years (especially < 2 years) have WORSE outcomes than older children and adults — AHT contributes significantly.
  • Diffuse swelling more common in children.
  • Pediatric vulnerability to hypoxia/hypotension narrower BP-autoregulation range.
  • NMDA antagonist caution in infants (apoptotic neurodegeneration in immature brain).
  • Open fontanelle delays but does NOT prevent herniation.
Abusive head trauma (AHT) — Zimmerman emphasis
  • Leading cause of severe TBI in infants.
  • Mechanisms: shaking, blunt impact, hypoxia-ischemia.
  • Late presentation common — already established edema, secondary insults.
  • Higher mortality and morbidity than noninflicted TBI at matched GCS.
  • Rate increasing with economic stressors.
  • Coordinate with safeguarding team, social work, CPS, forensic medicine.
  • Detailed coverage in Rogers Ch 63 and Zimmerman_67 supplement.
Penetrating brain injury
  • Gunshot wounds most common cause of penetrating injury.
  • Carry high mortality and morbidity.
  • Acute management: airway, hemodynamic stabilization, antibiotics (broad-spectrum), antiseizure prophylaxis 7 days.
  • Image findings: bullet/fragment trajectory, hematoma, pneumocephalus, intracranial fragments.
  • Neurosurgical debridement, hemostasis, dural repair.
  • Forensic evidence preservation (chain of custody).
Coagulopathy and TBI
  • Trauma-induced coagulopathy from tissue factor release, hyperfibrinolysis, dilution.
  • Worsens secondary hemorrhage and outcome.
  • Correct INR < 1.5, platelets > 100 before invasive procedures.
  • Use TEG/ROTEM for goal-directed therapy.
  • Tranexamic acid (TXA) within 3 hours per CRASH-3 (extrapolated pediatric data).
  • Avoid hypofibrinogenemia.
Rehabilitation linkage
  • Early rehabilitation engagement improves outcomes after severe TBI.
  • Engage PT, OT, SLP, physiatry within 72 hours regardless of acuity (Zimmerman Ch 69).
  • Even with deeply sedated patient: positioning, splinting, sensory stimulation.
  • Outcomes measured by GOS-E Peds, Pediatric Stroke Outcome Measure (PSOM), FSS, WeeFIM.
  • School re-entry planning; family education on PICS-p.

12 · Equations, Doses, and Targets

Hyperosmolar therapy
3% NaCl: bolus 2–5 mL/kg over 10–20 min PRN; continuous infusion targeting Na 150–160 mmol/L; osmolality ceiling ≤ 360 mOsm/kg.
Mannitol: 0.25–1 g/kg IV; osmolality ceiling ~320 mOsm/kg; risk of AKI and rebound ICH at higher osmolality.
For herniation: mannitol 0.5–1 g/kg OR 3% NaCl 1–3 mL/kg (max 250 mL) OR 23.4% NaCl 0.5 mL/kg (max 30 mL).
RSI drugs in head-injured child (Zimmerman Table 118.2)
Unstable: fentanyl 2–4 µg/kg + lidocaine 1 mg/kg + rocuronium 1 mg/kg or vecuronium 0.3 mg/kg.
Stable: add midazolam 0.1–0.2 mg/kg; etomidate 0.1–0.3 mg/kg (adrenal suppression concern).
CPP targets (BTF 2019)
Infant ≥ 40; school age ≥ 50; adolescent ≥ 60 mm Hg.
Avoid CPP < 40 at any age.
Fluid resuscitation
20 mL/kg isotonic crystalloid (0.9% NaCl) bolus; titrate to BP/perfusion/hematocrit.
NO hypotonic fluids in TBI.
Avoid albumin in adult TBI (associated with increased mortality).
Hyperventilation thresholds
Brief rescue for impending herniation: PaCO₂ 30–35 with FiO₂ 1.0.
Avoid PaCO₂ < 30 unless documented hyperemia with multimodal monitoring.
Avoid prophylactic hyperventilation, especially in first 24 hours.
Seizure prophylaxis
Levetiracetam 40–60 mg/kg load → 20–30 mg/kg q12h × 7 days post-injury for severe TBI.
Preferred over phenytoin for tolerability.

SAQ 1. Three Zimmerman categories of secondary injury after TBI.

  • Ischemia, excitotoxicity, energy failure, cell death cascades.
  • Cerebral swelling (CBV hyperemia, cytotoxic edema, vasogenic edema, osmotic).
  • Axonal injury (traumatic axonal injury, TAI).

SAQ 2. List the Zimmerman Box 118.1 intubation criteria.

  • GCS ≤ 10.
  • Decrease in GCS > 3 points.
  • Anisocoria > 1 mm.
  • Cervical spine injury compromising ventilation.
  • Apnea.
  • PaCO₂ > 45 (hypercarbia).
  • Loss of pharyngeal reflex.
  • Spontaneous hyperventilation with PaCO₂ < 25.

SAQ 3. RSI drugs for hemodynamically unstable head-injured child.

  • Fentanyl 2–4 µg/kg.
  • Lidocaine 1 mg/kg (blunts catecholamine surge).
  • Rocuronium 1 mg/kg OR vecuronium 0.3 mg/kg.
  • FiO₂ 1.0 preoxygenation; cervical spine immobilization.

SAQ 4. RSI drugs for hemodynamically stable child.

  • Etomidate 0.1–0.3 mg/kg (adrenal suppression concern) OR fentanyl 2–4 µg/kg.
  • Lidocaine 1 mg/kg.
  • Midazolam 0.1–0.2 mg/kg.
  • Rocuronium 1 mg/kg or vecuronium 0.3 mg/kg.

SAQ 5. Why avoid nasotracheal intubation in TBI?

  • Risk of cribriform plate fracture and intracranial passage in basilar skull fracture.
  • Hemorrhage and aspiration risk.
  • Orotracheal route under direct vision is preferred.

SAQ 6. Hyperosmolar therapy for impending herniation.

  • Mannitol 0.5–1.0 g/kg IV OR
  • 3% NaCl 1–3 mL/kg (max 250 mL) OR
  • 23.4% NaCl 0.5 mL/kg (max 30 mL).
  • Brief hyperventilation with FiO₂ 1.0 as rescue bridge.

SAQ 7. Marshall CT classification — Diffuse Injury III vs IV.

  • III (swelling): cisterns compressed/absent; shift 0–5 mm.
  • IV (shift): shift > 5 mm; no lesion > 25 mL.
  • Both: no high/mixed density lesion > 25 mL.

SAQ 8. Does a normal CT scan exclude raised ICP?

  • NO. ~15% of adults with normal CT after severe TBI develop clinically significant ICH.
  • Children with normal initial CT but hypotension or abnormal posturing have similar ICH risk.
  • BTF 2019 explicitly states normal CT does NOT exclude raised ICP.

SAQ 9. When NOT to routinely reimage after pediatric TBI.

  • Routine repeat CT at 24 or 48 h is NOT supported by literature.
  • Reimage based on clinical deterioration or rising ICP.

SAQ 10. ADAPT trial key conclusion to date.

  • Despite general adherence to BTF guidelines, therapy is markedly heterogeneous across PICU programs.
  • No new prescriptive recommendations yet but informs precision medicine direction.

SAQ 11. Cool Kids trial result and implication.

  • Hutchison NEJM 2008 — RCT of therapeutic hypothermia (32–33°C) vs normothermia in pediatric severe TBI.
  • NEGATIVE; signal of harm in hypothermia arm.
  • Therapeutic hypothermia NOT recommended as routine.
  • Strict normothermia (avoid fever) is the standard.

SAQ 12. CRASH-3 trial finding for TBI.

  • TXA within 3 h of TBI reduced head-injury related death; signal weaker in severe TBI but benefit if given early.
  • Dose: 1 g IV load + 1 g over 8 h (adult); pediatric weight-based ~15 mg/kg.
  • Do NOT give > 3 h post-injury (signal of harm).

SAQ 13. Pediatric Taylor 2001 decompressive craniectomy RCT.

  • Pediatric severe TBI with refractory ICH randomized to early decompressive craniectomy vs medical management.
  • Decompression group had better outcomes.
  • Supports use in selected refractory pediatric cases.

SAQ 14. Acute fluid resuscitation choice in TBI.

  • 20 mL/kg isotonic crystalloid (0.9% NaCl) bolus initially.
  • NO hypotonic fluids.
  • Subsequent isotonic crystalloid or PRBC titrated to BP, perfusion, Hct.
  • Avoid albumin (SAFE study — increased mortality in adult TBI).

SAQ 15. Why avoid albumin in TBI resuscitation?

  • SAFE study post-hoc analysis showed increased mortality in adult TBI subgroup.
  • Possibly related to leak into injured brain via BBB.
  • NIH-funded trials of hypertonic saline ± dextran-70 in TBI halted for futility.

SAQ 16. Excitotoxicity cascade — order of events.

  • Primary injury → ATP depletion.
  • Membrane depolarization → glutamate release.
  • NMDA + AMPA receptor overactivation.
  • Ca²⁺ influx → mitochondrial Ca²⁺ overload → MPTP opening.
  • Mitochondrial collapse → cytochrome c release → caspase activation → apoptosis.
  • ROS/RNS during reperfusion → lipid peroxidation, DNA damage.

SAQ 17. Cerebral swelling peak timing post-TBI.

  • Generally peaks between 24 and 72 hours post-injury.
  • Often earlier and more pronounced in pediatric TBI.
  • Diffuse swelling more common in children than adults.

SAQ 18. Traumatic axonal injury (TAI) mechanisms.

  • Shearing forces — not direct disruption.
  • Mitochondrial permeability transition pore (MPTP) opening → mitochondrial failure.
  • Calpain-mediated proteolysis of microtubules.
  • Failure of axoplasmic transport.
  • APP accumulation.
  • Direct axolemmal poration.

SAQ 19. Best imaging modality to detect TAI.

  • Susceptibility-weighted imaging (SWI) — microhemorrhages.
  • Diffusion tensor imaging (DTI) — white matter integrity.
  • High-definition fiber tractography (HDFT) — research.
  • CT often underestimates TAI extent.

SAQ 20. BTF tier-1 ICP threshold and key interventions.

  • ICP > 20 mm Hg sustained > 5 min.
  • HOB 30°; sedation; NMB if needed; CSF drainage; hyperosmolar 3% NaCl or mannitol; CPP optimization.

SAQ 21. Pediatric CPP targets by age.

  • Infant ≥ 40; school age ≥ 50; adolescent ≥ 60 mm Hg.
  • Avoid CPP < 40 at any age.

SAQ 22. Why is hyperventilation harmful in early TBI?

  • Lowers PaCO₂ → cerebral vasoconstriction → reduced CBF.
  • CBF already depressed in first 24 h after injury.
  • Risk of secondary ischemia.
  • Brief rescue only for impending herniation, with multimodal monitoring (PbtO₂ or SjvO₂).

SAQ 23. Forsyth UK ICP monitoring data.

  • > 500 pediatric TBI cases.
  • Both ICP > 20 mm Hg AND lack of ICP monitor independently associated with death before discharge.

SAQ 24. Chesnut BEST TRIP RCT finding.

  • 324 adults with severe TBI randomized to ICP-directed care vs imaging + clinical exam-based protocol.
  • NO outcome difference.
  • Major challenge to monitoring-mandated care, although applicability debated.

SAQ 25. Gerber NY State data on ICP monitoring.

  • 2001–2009 mortality 23% → 13%.
  • Concurrent increase in guidelines-based ICP monitoring 56% → 75%.
  • Supports population-level benefit of ICP monitoring.

SAQ 26. Pediatric-specific factors causing herniation.

  • Diffuse cerebral swelling (more common in children).
  • Status epilepticus post-TBI.
  • Iatrogenic hypoxemia/hypercarbia.
  • Excessive fluid administration.
  • Expanding mass lesions (epidural, subdural).

SAQ 27. AHT — pediatric TBI subgroup with worse outcomes.

  • Leading cause of severe TBI in infants.
  • Mechanisms: shaking, blunt impact, hypoxia-ischemia.
  • Late presentation common — established edema and secondary insults at time of recognition.
  • Higher mortality/morbidity vs noninflicted TBI at matched GCS.
  • Coordinate safeguarding, social work, CPS.

SAQ 28. Why NMDA antagonists caution in infants.

  • Preclinical evidence that NMDA antagonists may induce apoptotic neurodegeneration in immature brain.
  • Caution with ketamine high-dose or prolonged in infants.
  • Risk-benefit weighed in clinical scenarios.

SAQ 29. Multimodal monitoring beyond ICP.

  • PbtO₂: regional > 20 target.
  • SjvO₂: global < 50 ischemia, > 75 hyperemia.
  • Microdialysis L/P > 25 = metabolic crisis.
  • PRx for autoregulation and CPPopt.
  • TCD vasospasm + noninvasive ICP.
  • NIRS; pupillometry; cEEG.

SAQ 30. Pediatric vs adult TBI outcome differences.

  • 5–15 y generally favorable vs adults.
  • < 4 y (especially < 2 y) WORSE than older children and adults.
  • AHT major contributor to poor infant outcomes.
  • Diffuse swelling more common in children.
  • Narrower autoregulation range in young children.

SAQ 31. Genetic polymorphisms in TBI outcome.

  • AQP4 — cerebral edema.
  • IL-1 family — inflammatory response.
  • BDNF Val66Met — plasticity.
  • APOE — outcome after TBI.
  • Emerging field for precision medicine.

SAQ 32. Coagulopathy management in TBI.

  • Trauma-induced coagulopathy common.
  • Reverse INR < 1.5, platelets > 100 before invasive procedures.
  • TEG/ROTEM for goal-directed therapy.
  • TXA within 3 h (CRASH-3).
  • Avoid hypofibrinogenemia.

SAQ 33. Pediatric TBI seizure prophylaxis.

  • Levetiracetam 40–60 mg/kg load → 20–30 mg/kg q12h × 7 days.
  • Preferred over phenytoin (tolerability).
  • Continue if seizures occur.
  • Goal: prevent early post-traumatic seizures.

SAQ 34. Cervical spine in TBI.

  • Trauma above clavicles → assume both cranial AND cervical injury.
  • In-line cervical immobilization during intubation.
  • Normal lateral C-spine X-ray does NOT rule out injury.
  • CT C-spine and clinical clearance per pediatric protocol.

SAQ 35. Penetrating brain injury management.

  • Airway, hemodynamic stabilization.
  • Broad-spectrum antibiotics (e.g., vancomycin + ceftriaxone + metronidazole).
  • Antiseizure prophylaxis 7 days.
  • Neurosurgical debridement, hemostasis, dural repair.
  • Forensic evidence preservation.

SAQ 36. PbtO₂ rationale and threshold.

  • Regional brain tissue oxygenation.
  • Target > 20 mm Hg; < 15 concerning; < 10 severe hypoxia.
  • Detects ischemia despite adequate CPP.
  • Useful for individualized therapy.

SAQ 37. Steroid use in TBI.

  • NOT recommended (CRASH trial 2005 — increased mortality).
  • Includes children — extrapolated.
  • Dexamethasone has no role in acute TBI management.

SAQ 38. VTE prophylaxis in TBI.

  • Mechanical (sequential compression devices) first-line.
  • Pharmacologic (LMWH) per institutional protocol once bleeding controlled (typically 24–48 h post-injury, with stable imaging).
  • Adolescents particularly at risk.

SAQ 39. Early rehabilitation in severe TBI.

  • Engage PT, OT, SLP, physiatry within 72 h regardless of acuity.
  • Even deeply sedated: positioning, splinting, sensory stimulation.
  • Improves outcomes; reduces ICU-AW and contractures.
  • Outcome measures: GOS-E Peds, FSS, WeeFIM, PSOM.

SAQ 40. Top 5 examiner traps in pediatric TBI.

  • Prophylactic hyperventilation in stable severe TBI.
  • Steroids in TBI (CRASH negative).
  • Routine hypothermia (Cool Kids negative).
  • Hypotonic fluids in resuscitation.
  • Assuming open fontanelle prevents herniation.

Case 1. 10-yo MVC, GCS 6, anisocoria, bradycardia, HTN.

  • Impending herniation.
  • RSI hemodynamically stable algorithm (fentanyl + lidocaine + midazolam + rocuronium).
  • Brief hyperventilation FiO₂ 1.0; mannitol 1 g/kg or 3% NaCl 3–5 mL/kg.
  • Urgent CT; neurosurgery; emergent decompression if mass effect.

Case 2. 3-yo AHT, GCS 4, bilateral fixed pupils.

  • AHT — leading cause of severe pediatric TBI.
  • RSI; full BTF tier 1; activate Child Protection Team.
  • Safeguarding workup (skeletal survey, ophthalmology, MRI brain + spine).
  • Mandatory reporting; ICU monitoring; cEEG.

Case 3. 14-yo polytrauma, hypotensive, GCS 7.

  • Resuscitate hypovolemic shock first (20 mL/kg isotonic).
  • NO hypotonic; correct hypotension to maintain CPP.
  • RSI hemodynamically unstable: fentanyl + lidocaine + rocuronium.
  • TXA within 3 h post-injury per CRASH-3.
  • Coordinated trauma + neuro management.

Case 4. 8-yo severe TBI day 1, ICP 22 despite sedation.

  • Tier 1: confirm sedation depth; add NMB if cough/shiver-driven.
  • 3% NaCl 3–5 mL/kg bolus.
  • CSF drainage via EVD.
  • Optimize MAP for CPP ≥ 50.
  • Continuous EEG.

Case 5. 11-yo refractory ICH on tier 1; what next?

  • Tier 2 escalation.
  • Controlled mild hyperventilation PaCO₂ 30–35 with PbtO₂/SjvO₂.
  • Barbiturate coma (pentobarbital) with cEEG burst suppression.
  • Decompressive craniectomy.
  • Multidisciplinary family meeting.

Case 6. 5-yo on barbiturate coma, hypotensive on epinephrine.

  • Anticipated complication of pentobarbital.
  • Add norepinephrine or vasopressin.
  • Stress-dose hydrocortisone if refractory.
  • Echo for cardiac function.
  • Daily echocardiogram during coma.

Case 7. 2-yo AHT, day 5 develops Na 158, polyuria.

  • Central DI from hypothalamic injury.
  • DDAVP IV; replace free water deficit over 48 h.
  • Screen anterior pituitary axes.
  • Likely worsening intracranial pathology — repeat imaging.

Case 8. 15-yo isolated severe TBI 90 min post-injury.

  • Consider TXA per CRASH-3 (within 3 h).
  • 1 g IV load + 1 g over 8 h (or ~15 mg/kg pediatric).
  • Standard BTF tier 1.
  • Continue all neuroprotection.

Case 9. 7-yo severe TBI on cEEG — electrographic seizures.

  • Load levetiracetam 40–60 mg/kg.
  • Continue prophylactic dose 7 d.
  • Add second-line ASM if persistent (fosphenytoin, valproate).
  • Anesthetic infusion if refractory.

Case 10. 12-yo severe TBI, CT shows large epidural with midline shift 7 mm.

  • Excellent prognosis if treated early.

Case 11. 3-mo AHT, suspected mixed-age subdurals.

  • Mandatory reporting; safeguarding sibling assessment.

Case 12. 9-yo TBI with INR 1.8, platelets 65, fibrinogen 1.0.

  • Trauma-induced coagulopathy.
  • Vitamin K, FFP, platelets, cryoprecipitate per TEG/ROTEM.
  • TXA within 3 h.
  • Reverse before invasive procedures.

Case 13. 11-yo severe TBI day 2, MRI shows extensive DAI on SWI.

  • Traumatic axonal injury — poor prognostic indicator.
  • Multidisciplinary family meeting integrating clinical, EEG, MRI.
  • Continue BTF management; long-term rehab planning.

Case 14. 6-yo penetrating GSW to head.

  • Airway, hemodynamic stabilization.
  • Broad-spectrum antibiotics.
  • Antiseizure prophylaxis 7 days.

  • Forensic evidence preservation.

Case 15. 13-yo severe TBI, multimodal monitoring shows ICP 18, CPP 65, PbtO₂ 8.

Pathophysiology

  • Primary injury (impact) + secondary cascade (excitotoxicity, swelling, axonal injury).
  • Modifiable secondary insults: hypoxia, hypotension, hypercarbia, hyperthermia, hyperglycemia, seizures, anemia, raised ICP.

Initial Management

  • ABCs first; complete and rapid physiologic resuscitation.
  • Intubate per Box 118.1 criteria; neuro-protective RSI.
  • NO hypotonic fluids; 20 mL/kg isotonic crystalloid initial.
  • Avoid hypotension and hypoxemia at all costs.

Herniation pathway

  • Pupil dilation, HTN/bradycardia, posturing.
  • Hyperventilate FiO₂ 1.0; mannitol/3% NaCl; EVD drainage; sedation; CT/surgery.

BTF 2019 Algorithm

  • Tier 1: HOB 30°, sedation, NMB, CSF drain, hyperosmolar, CPP.
  • Tier 2: controlled HV with PbtO₂; barbiturate coma; decompressive craniectomy.
  • Strict normothermia (Cool Kids negative); no steroids.

Special considerations

  • AHT — leading cause severe TBI in infants; CPS, safeguarding.
  • CRASH-3 TXA < 3 h.
  • Coagulopathy: TEG/ROTEM goal-directed.
  • NMDA antagonist caution in infants.
  • ADAPT trial framework — heterogeneous practice; precision medicine direction.

Exam pearls

  • Box 118.1 intubation criteria.
  • Marshall CT classification.
  • RSI drugs by hemodynamic status.
  • Normal CT does NOT exclude raised ICP.
  • Hyperventilation: brief rescue only with monitoring.
  • Cool Kids negative — maintain normothermia.
  • Taylor 2001 — pediatric decompressive craniectomy supportive.
